# Corporal Joliffe

**Corporal Joliffe** is the bustling, well-meaning, and spectacularly inefficient "Corporal in charge of details" in Jules Verne's *The Fur Country*. He is one of the novel's most reliable sources of comedy — a man of boundless energy and negligible organisational ability, whose every effort at management is silently corrected by his far more competent wife. Together with [[mrs-joliffe|Mrs. Joliffe]], he forms one of the most endearing domestic partnerships in Verne's Arctic fiction.

## In the Novel

### The Great Hall Transformation

Joliffe bursts into the narrative at [[captain-craventy|Captain Craventy]]'s soirée on the evening of 17th March 1859, where he has "completely transformed" the great hall of [[fort-reliance|Fort Reliance]] — or so he believes.

> [001-003] "In fact, under the auspices of Corporal Joliffe, the large room on the ground-floor was completely transformed. The floor, of rough joists of wood laid parallel with each other, had been carefully swept by Corporal Joliffe."

In reality, as the narrator gently informs us, every detail of the transformation was overseen by his wife:

> [001-026] "So he called himself the 'Corporal in charge of details,' but he would have lost himself a hundred times amongst these details, had not little Mrs. Joliffe guided him with a firm hand. So it came to pass, that Corporal Joliffe obeyed his wife without owning it."

This dynamic — the husband who takes the credit while the wife does the work — is treated by Verne with affectionate humour rather than satire. Joliffe is never portrayed as lazy or dishonest; he genuinely believes he is in charge, and his energy is real even if his competence is not.

> [001-006] "'But honour to whom honour is due, Mrs. Joliffe deserves part of your commendation; she assisted me in everything.'"

### Reviving the Frozen Astronomer

Joliffe's most memorable scene comes when [[thomas-black|Thomas Black]], the frozen astronomer, arrives at [[fort-reliance|Fort Reliance]] encased in ice. It is Joliffe who takes charge of the rescue — stripping the unconscious man, rubbing him with snow, and administering an unconventional remedy.

> [003-026] "Joliffe undressed him, and turned him rapidly on to his face and back again."

When conventional methods fail to revive the astronomer, Joliffe's ingenuity takes a characteristically liquid turn.

> [003-028] "But the visitor who had arrived in so strange a manner showed no signs of returning life, and Corporal Joliffe could think of no better means to restore the lost vital heat than to give him a bath in the bowl of hot punch."

With [[sergeant-long|Sergeant Long]]'s assistance, Joliffe administers a rubbing "such as he had probably never before received" (PN 003-031). The treatment, against all odds, succeeds — the astronomer stirs back to life, and Joliffe has his moment of genuine, if accidental, heroism.

### The Overland Journey

During the expedition's march to the Arctic coast, the Joliffes bring up the rear in their own sledge (PN 005-009). The journey provides a running domestic comedy: the Corporal, having perhaps "drunk too often to the success of the expedition on starting," disputes with his wife as they travel.

> [005-033] "Perhaps the gallant Corporal had too often drunk to the success of the expedition on starting; for, strange to say, he was disputing with his little wife."

Throughout the journey, Joliffe "exerted himself without producing any very tangible results" (PN 004-057) — a perfect summary of his contribution. Yet his wife's devotion and usefulness more than compensate, and [[paulina-barnett|Paulina Barnett]] takes an immediate liking to the couple.

### Command at Fort Hope

At [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]], Joliffe settles into his role as self-appointed master of details. When [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Hobson]] must leave the fort, command nominally falls to the Corporal — but everyone knows where the real authority lies.

> [026-010] "During the absence of the Lieutenant and the Sergeant, the command of the fort fell to Corporal Joliffe, or rather to his little wife, and Hobson knew that he could trust her."

He also serves as a kind of comic chorus. When the eclipse fails to occur as predicted, it is Joliffe who steps forward with the shrewd observation that they might not be where they think they are — though he requires his wife's nudge to find the courage to speak.

> [024-010] "But Joliffe hesitated, and his little wife nudged his elbow."

## Character and Traits

Joliffe is one of Verne's most carefully balanced comic figures — he could easily be a mere buffoon, but several qualities save him from that fate:

- **Genuine energy**: Joliffe never stops moving. He sweeps floors, arranges furniture, undresses frozen astronomers, and bustles about the fort with inexhaustible vigour. His activity is real even when its results are negligible.
- **Complete lack of self-awareness**: The comedy of Joliffe depends on his total inability to perceive that his wife is the competent one. This blindness is presented not as stupidity but as a quirk of personality — he is simply incapable of seeing what everyone else sees.
- **Occasional usefulness**: As the rescue of Thomas Black demonstrates, Joliffe's willingness to act — however unconventionally — sometimes produces results that a more cautious person would never achieve.
- **Domestic devotion**: Despite their comic dynamic, the Joliffes' marriage is clearly a happy one. The Corporal praises his wife publicly, and she supports him loyally. Their partnership, however asymmetrical, is built on genuine affection.

At [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]], the domestic routine continues: "Mrs. Joliffe, assisted by Mrs. Rae, and with the Corporal always at her heels, presided in the kitchen" (PN 018-009). The image of the bustling Corporal "always at her heels" — present, active, and entirely in the way — is Joliffe in a single phrase.
