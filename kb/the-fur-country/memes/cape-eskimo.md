# Cape Eskimo

Cape Eskimo is a prominent coastal feature on the eastern side of [[victoria-island|Victoria Island]], named by [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Jaspar Hobson]]'s colonists after an Eskimo (Inuit) family who established a temporary encampment there. It became one of the most frequently visited landmarks on [[the-ice-island|the drifting island]].

## Naming and Discovery

The cape received its name from the Eskimo family discovered camping there during one of the colonists' exploratory excursions:

> [019-045] The next day was fine, and accompanied by Madge, Lieutenant Hobson, and a few soldiers, well armed in case any bears should be prowling about, Mrs. Barnett set out for "Cape Eskimo," as they had named the western point of the bay.

The Eskimo family remained encamped near the cape for eight days, during which the men devoted twelve hours out of every twenty-four to hunting morses (walruses) with remarkable patience.

## Strategic Importance

As the ice-island drifted westward, Cape Eskimo served as a crucial observation point. From its summit, Hobson would scan the horizon for any sign of approaching land or changes in the island's course:

> [026-037] Before starting Hobson once more looked round him from the summit of Cape Eskimo; but seeing nothing worthy of notice, he rejoined Mrs. Barnett and Sergeant Long.

The cape was also a reference point for measuring the island's drift and the gradual erosion of its coastline. [[sergeant-long|Sergeant Long]] remarked on the seasonal changes:

> [026-029] "They will come, Lieutenant," said Long; "have patience, in another two months the waves will no longer break upon the shores of Cape Eskimo."

## Geological Changes

As the ice-island drifted into warmer waters, Cape Eskimo suffered significant erosion. [[paulina-barnett|Mrs. Barnett]] and [[madge|Madge]] observed the deterioration during a visit:

> [031-046] About ten o'clock Mrs. Barnett and Madge had reached the rising ground above Cape Eskimo, but at least two miles inland, for they had found it impossible to follow the coast, worn away as it was by the waves.

The cape's fate mirrored that of the entire [[victoria-island|Victoria Island]] — it was destined to melt and disappear into the [[arctic-ocean|Arctic Ocean]] as the ice foundation succumbed to warmer waters.
