# Coronation Gulf

Coronation Gulf is a major Arctic estuary on the northern coast of North America, situated at the mouth of the [[coppermine-river|Coppermine River]]. It marks the point where Hobson's overland expedition first reached the [[arctic-ocean|Polar Sea]] and represents the culmination of Samuel Hearne's pioneering journey of 1772.

## Geography

The gulf is a vast, irregularly-shaped body of water set deep into the North American continent:

> [011-002] Coronation Gulf, the large estuary dotted with the islands forming the Duke of York Archipelago, which the party had now reached, was a sheet of water with irregular banks, let in, as it were, into the North American continent.

At its western angle, the [[coppermine-river|Coppermine River]] empties into the gulf. To the east, Bathurst Inlet extends into the mainland. Beyond the mouth of the Coppermine, the coast turns sharply northward, terminating at [[cape-krusenstern|Cape Krusenstern]].

## Strategic Position

[[fort-confidence|Fort Confidence]], built on the northern extremity of the [[great-bear-lake|Great Bear Lake]], lay at least two hundred miles from Coronation Gulf. The overland journey from the lake to the gulf represented the final push of Hobson's expedition to reach the Arctic coast:

> [010-007] According to the advice of the Indian chief, Hobson determined to get to the coast by the shortest route, and to take a northeasterly direction. After consulting his map, it seemed best to him to descend the valley of the Coppermine, a large river which flows into Coronation Gulf.

## Historical Context

The gulf was first sighted by Europeans through the explorations of **Samuel Hearne**, the [[hudsons-bay-company|Hudson's Bay Company]] agent who discovered the [[coppermine-river|Coppermine River]] in July 1772 after three grueling attempts:

- First attempt (1769): Forced back by cold and hunger
- Second attempt (1770): Survived seven days on wild fruit, old leather, and burnt bones
- Third attempt (1770–1772): Successfully reached the river's mouth after nineteen months

Hearne was the first European to penetrate to the northern coast of America, preceding Hobson's fictional expedition by nearly ninety years.
