# Kalumah

**Kalumah** is a young Eskimo girl who appears in the second half of Jules Verne's *The Fur Country*. She befriends [[paulina-barnett|Paulina Barnett]] and the colonists of [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]], becoming a daily and beloved presence at the fort during the long Arctic winter. Her story takes a dramatic and deeply moving turn when, months after her family's departure, she is discovered stranded on [[the-ice-island|the drifting ice island]] — having crossed two hundred miles of frozen sea in a perilous journey to keep a promise to her European friend. Kalumah represents one of Verne's most sympathetic and fully realised portrayals of an indigenous character, elevated from a minor figure to one of the novel's most memorable by the extraordinary courage and loyalty of her final act.

## In the Novel

Kalumah arrives with her family at the vicinity of [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]] during the deepening winter. The Eskimo encampment — a large cone of snow, an *igloo* in their language, constructed with great rapidity and admirably suited to the climate — is established near the cape. Kalumah hastens forward to meet [[paulina-barnett|Mrs. Barnett]], pointing to the hut with an air of pride. She is immediately drawn to the European woman, and the two — one a celebrated traveller, the other a young Eskimo girl — form a bond that transcends barriers of language and culture.

> [019-043] "The name of the young girl was Kalumah, and she seemed to have taken a great fancy to Mrs. Barnett. But sociable as she was, she appeared to feel no regret at having left the government of her own country for the somewhat monotonous life of the factory."

Kalumah's daily visits to the fort become a fixture of the colonists' winter routine. She explores every room, watches [[mrs-joliffe|Mrs. Joliffe]] at her cooking or sewing, and asks the English name of everything. She talks for hours together with Mrs. Barnett — if the term "talking" can be applied to an exchange of words after long deliberation on both sides. When Mrs. Barnett reads aloud, Kalumah listens with rapt attention, although she likely understands nothing of what she hears. She brings a warmth of human connection to the isolated settlement, a reminder of the world beyond the frozen walls.

> [019-053] "Kalumah came to the fort every day in spite of the severity of the weather. She was never tired of going through the different rooms, and watching Mrs. Joliffe at her cooking or sewing. She asked the English name of everything, and talked for hours together with Mrs. Barnett, if the term 'talking' can be applied to an exchange of words after long deliberation on both sides. When Mrs. Barnett read aloud, Kalumah listened with great attention, although she probably understood nothing of what she heard."

## The Farewell

When Kalumah's family must move on, the parting is genuinely emotional. The exchange of gifts — Kalumah's copper ring for Barnett's necklace of black beads — is a quiet moment of mutual recognition that transcends the colonial framework of the novel. [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Hobson]] gives the departing family a good stock of provisions for their journey. Kalumah promises to return to Fort Hope in the summer.

> [019-075] "On the 20th December the Eskimo family came to take leave of the occupants of the fort. Kalumah was sorry to part with Mrs. Barnett, who would gladly have retained her in her service; but the young native could not be persuaded to leave her own people; she promised, however, to return to Fort Hope in the summer."

> [019-076] "Her farewell was touching. She presented Mrs. Barnett with a copper ring, and received in exchange a necklace of black beads, which she immediately put on. Hobson gave the poor people a good stock of provisions, which they packed in their sledge; and after a few words of grateful acknowledgment from Kalumah, the whole party set out towards the west, quickly disappearing in the thick fogs on the shore."

## Kalumah's Adventures on the Ice Island

### Journey on the Drifting Island

The most remarkable chapter of Kalumah's story occurs months later, when the colonists — now adrift on [[the-ice-island|the ice island]] — make a shocking discovery. On the desolate frozen surface, two hundred miles from the American coast, [[madge|Madge]] approaches an inanimate body and looks long and fixedly at it.

> [031-088] "It was the young Eskimo girl Kalumah!"

> [032-002] "Kalumah on the floating island, two hundred miles from the American coast. It was almost incredible!"

Through patient care, Mrs. Barnett and [[madge|Madge]] revive the exhausted girl, who had eaten nothing for forty-eight hours. Kalumah, seeing Mrs. Barnett, her face brightened — the same name again burst from her lips, and painfully raising her hand she let it fall on that of her friend.

> [032-006] "But at last Kalumah's breast heaved with a faint sigh, her hands moved feebly, and presently she opened her eyes, and recognising her preserver she murmured—"

Kalumah then told her story. She had kept her promise: when the long Polar night ended in May, she set out from Russian America, accompanied by one of her brothers-in-law, to return to Fort Hope. She travelled for weeks across the frozen wilderness — only to find that the cape had vanished. The entire peninsula had broken away from the mainland and become a floating island.

> [032-015] "The long Polar night being over, and the month of May having come round, Kalumah set out to fulfil her pledge. She was on her way to Fort Hope."

Kalumah understood what had happened. In an act of extraordinary courage and loyalty — one that surpasses anything attempted by any other character in the novel — she crossed the frozen sea to reach the drifting island and find her friends. She had voluntarily sought the floating island, hoping to find her old European friends on it.

> [032-018] "Kalumah understood what had happened. Either the whole of the peninsula had been swallowed up by the waves, or it was floating away as an island, no one knew whither!"

The reunion is deeply moving. Kalumah, seated on the sand between Mrs. Barnett and Madge, overwhelmed them with thanks and expressions of attachment — a young girl who risked her life crossing the Arctic ice to keep a promise to a friend.

> [032-012] "The anxious care of the two women soon revived Kalumah, whose extreme exhaustion arose not only from fatigue but also from hunger."

> [032-013] "Before starting, however, Kalumah, seated on the sand between Mrs. Barnett and Madge, overwhelmed them with thanks and expressions of attachment."

## Narrative Significance

Kalumah represents one of Verne's more sympathetic and fully realised portrayals of an indigenous character. Unlike the stereotypical "noble savage" or the threatening native, she is depicted as a distinct individual — curious, affectionate, loyal, and courageous. Her friendship with [[paulina-barnett|Barnett]] demonstrates that genuine human connection is possible across the widest cultural divides. Her perilous journey to find her friends on the ice island elevates her from a minor character to one of the novel's most memorable figures: a young girl whose loyalty and determination bring her across two hundred miles of Arctic wilderness to keep a promise. As Mrs. Barnett herself reflects, she had always held "dear little Kalumah in friendly remembrance" — but even she could never have anticipated the depth of devotion that remembrance would inspire.
