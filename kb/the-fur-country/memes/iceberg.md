# Iceberg

Icebergs and ice fields are the dominant physical realities of the Arctic world in *The Fur Country*, and they play a role far beyond mere scenery. The novel's central revelation — that [[victoria-island|Victoria Land]] is itself a colossal ice field disguised by centuries of accumulated earth — makes ice the literal foundation of the entire narrative.  Icebergs feature prominently in the novel's Arctic seascape, serving as both navigational hazards and — in a characteristically Vernean twist — as deceptive analogues to the ice island itself. The bergs' massive scale and hidden depths mirror the central deception of Victoria Island, where solid ground proves to be nothing more than frozen water.

## The Ice Island

### Overview

The most extraordinary iceberg in the novel is [[victoria-island|Victoria Island]] itself — a sheet of ice five hundred square miles in extent, veneered with enough soil and vegetation to support forests, a lake, a river, and a full [[hudsons-bay-company|Hudson's Bay Company]] fort. The ice foundation was discovered when the colonists dug a reindeer pit and found solid ice ten feet below the surface:

> [024-027] "Beneath the lake, beneath the soil of earth and sand — in a word, beneath our feet is a foundation of ice, which floats upon the water by reason of its being specifically lighter than it. Yes, it is a sheet of ice which bears us up, and is carrying us away."

## Ice as Threat

Beyond the island itself, icebergs posed constant danger to the colonists and travellers. The Arctic seas were described as crowded with ice formations of fantastic shapes:

| Type | Description | Threat Level |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| Ice fields | Vast frozen plains, up to 100 miles long, 50 wide | Medium — impede navigation |
| Icebergs | Towering formations up to 500 feet high | High — destructive collisions |
| Hummocks | Piled-up ridges and uneven formations | Low — obstacles to travel |
| Ice floes | Smaller drifting sheets | Medium — unstable footing |

Navigators like Parry, Penny, and [[franklin|Franklin]] had encountered ice fields of staggering dimensions in the Polar seas. Captain [[kellet|Kellet]] abandoned his boat on an ice field measuring at least three hundred square miles.

## Seasonal Cycle

The ice governed the Arctic year with absolute authority:

- **Winter**: The sea froze solid, turning [[victoria-island|Victoria Island]] into an immobile part of a vast frozen plain. Travel by sledge became possible.
- **Spring thaw**: Ice fields broke up; icebergs "drew back to the north." The island began to drift.
- **Summer**: Open water surrounded the island. Waves eroded its ice-shores from below.

Hobson's plan for survival depended on the winter freeze "soldering" the island to the Arctic ice field, allowing the colonists to cross hundreds of miles of ice to reach the mainland.
