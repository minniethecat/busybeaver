# Norman

**Norman** — always referred to as "old Norman" — is an experienced boatman of the [[hudsons-bay-company|Hudson's Bay Company]] in Jules Verne's *The Fur Country*. He appears in a single sustained sequence — the crossing of the [[great-bear-lake|Great Bear Lake]] and the subsequent storm — but his brief presence on the page is one of the novel's most vivid portraits of frontier competence. In an expedition filled with soldiers, hunters, and officers, Norman represents a quieter kind of expertise: the knowledge of wind and water that only decades on northern lakes can confer. His taciturn authority, economy of speech, and unwavering grip on the tiller make him one of Verne's most memorable minor characters.

## In the Novel

Norman is introduced at the little harbour near [[fort-enterprise|Fort Confidence]], where [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Hobson]] and [[paulina-barnett|Paulina Barnett]] find him "ready to embark." The boat is a modest open fishing vessel, six feet long, rigged like a cutter, which one man could easily manage. [[sergeant-long|Sergeant Long]] accompanies the travellers to the harbour, and [[sergeant-felton|Sergeant Felton]] takes his leave with apologies for being unable to accompany them in the absence of his chief.

> [009-003] "The two travellers were accompanied by Sergeant Long as far as the little harbour, where they found old Norman ready to embark. Their little vessel was but an open fishing-boat, six feet long, rigged like a cutter, which one man could easily manage."

The crossing to the Indian encampment at the northwest angle of the lake proceeds smoothly. But the return journey is another matter entirely. As they prepare to push off, Norman is seized by a sudden presentiment of danger — an instinct honed by years of reading northern weather. He looks anxiously at the fog above his head and cries out for haste.

> [009-003] "'Let us start without losing an instant,' cried old Norman, looking anxiously at the fog above his head."

> [009-008] "But just as they were pushing off, old Norman, as if possessed by some sudden presentiment, murmured—"

When [[paulina-barnett|Mrs. Barnett]] asks if he fears a storm, Norman's reply is characteristically terse — he does not elaborate, does not explain his reasoning. He simply knows. Throughout the ordeal that follows, Norman's competence is defined by this same economy of speech and action. He does not explain, or reassure, or speculate. He reads the weather, adjusts the sail, clutches the tiller, and — when the boat is finally driven ashore on an unknown coast — simply admits that he has no idea where they are. His honesty is part of his authority.

> [009-033] "Neither Norman nor the Lieutenant had any idea of where they were, or whither they were going."

## The Storm on Great Bear Lake

When the squall descends, Norman's seamanship is tested to its limits. He tightens the sail to get ahead of the wind while keeping the boat under control — a delicate balance that requires both strength and judgment. The Lieutenant urges him to turn back toward the southern shore, but Norman replies bluntly that it is no longer possible.

> [009-019] "'I should be glad enough to go back to the north, Lieutenant,' replied Norman, 'if it were still possible.'"

> [009-021] "'We are scarcely advancing at all,' said the Lieutenant to old Norman. The latter, who saw the danger, cried, 'Tighten the sail!' so as to get his boat ahead of the wind, whilst keeping her under control of the helm."

> [009-025] "Norman screamed above the roaring of the storm."

As the situation deteriorates, the halliards become entangled and Norman, unable to leave the helm, cannot take in the sail. [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Hobson]] seizes Norman's knife and cuts the halyard like a harp-string, but the wet cordage jams in the pulleys and the yard remains attached to the mast. Unable to make headway against the wind, Norman makes the dangerous decision to tack south — running before the wind with waves pursuing at double speed.

> [009-026] "The Lieutenant, his cap blown away and his eyes blinded by the spray, seized Norman's knife and cut the halliard like a harp-string; but the wet cordage no longer acted in the grooves of the pulleys, and the yard remained attached to the top of the mast."

> [009-027] "Norman, totally unable to make head against the wind, now resolved to tack about for the south, dangerous as it would be to have the boat before the wind, pursued by waves advancing at double its speed."

Throughout the ordeal, the two passengers watch their pilot's face for any sign — and find only grim determination. Neither yielded to despair; clinging to the sides of the boat, wet to the skin, chilled to the bone by the cutting blast, they strove to gaze through the thick mist and fog.

> [009-029] "Now and then the two travellers looked inquiringly into old Norman's face, who, with teeth set and hands clutching the tiller, tried to keep his boat as much as possible under wind."

## The Final Wave

The storm reaches its climax when Norman spots an enormous wave bearing down on the boat — a wave from which there is no escape. In the novel's most chilling moment, Mrs. Barnett's last conscious thought is a question: "Where was Norman?"

> [009-034] "Norman, looking round, saw its approach; and Mrs. Barnett and the Lieutenant, with eyes fixed and staring, awaited in fearful suspense the blow from which there was no escape."

> [009-036] "Where was Norman?"

Though the boat is wrecked, Norman and his passengers survive — washed ashore on an unknown coast, beginning the next phase of their journey. Norman's fate after the wreck is not elaborated in the novel, but his role in delivering Hobson and Barnett safely through the storm cements his place as a memorable minor character: the old boatman whose skill and courage held against the fury of the northern lake.
