# Scurvy

Scurvy was one of the most feared threats to any Arctic expedition in the nineteenth century. In *The Fur Country*, Lieutenant [[jaspar-hobson|Jaspar Hobson]] took careful precautions against the disease, recognising that the safety of his colonists depended as much on nutrition as on protection from cold and wild animals.

## The Disease

Scurvy is caused by a deficiency of vitamin C (ascorbic acid), which the human body cannot synthesise. Without fresh fruits and vegetables, symptoms develop within weeks to months:

| Stage | Symptoms |
|-------|----------|
| Early | Fatigue, weakness, joint pain |
| Advanced | Bleeding gums, loose teeth, skin haemorrhages |
| Terminal | Organ failure, death |

Arctic explorers were particularly vulnerable because their diet consisted almost entirely of preserved meat, dried fish, and biscuits — all lacking in vitamin C. The disease had decimated earlier expeditions and remained a persistent threat.

## Hobson's Preparations

Knowing the dangers, Hobson stocked [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]] with multiple antiscorbutic provisions:

> [014-033] Knowing the scarcity of vegetables, Jaspar Hobson had plenty of seeds with him, chiefly sorrel and scurvy-grass (Cochlearia), the antiscorbutic properties of which are invaluable in these latitudes.

The fort's dispensary also contained:

| Antiscorbutic | Form | Source |
|--------------|------|--------|
| Lemon juice | Casks | Imported |
| Lime juice | Casks | Imported |
| Sorrel (*Rumex*) | Seeds → cultivated | Grown at [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]] |
| Scurvy-grass (*Cochlearia*) | Seeds → cultivated | Grown at [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]] |

The sheltered site chosen for [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]], protected from the "keen blasts which shrivel vegetation like a fire," gave the seeds every chance of yielding a crop. This foresight was critical: when the colonists later faced their ordeal on [[the-ice-island|the drifting island]], these provisions helped sustain them.

## Historical Context

By 1859, the connection between fresh vegetables and scurvy prevention was well established in practical terms, even though vitamin C itself would not be isolated until the 1930s. The British Royal Navy had adopted lemon juice rations in the late eighteenth century, earning British sailors the nickname "limeys." Arctic explorers of the nineteenth century, learning from the tragedies of earlier expeditions, made antiscorbutic provisions a standard part of their supplies.
