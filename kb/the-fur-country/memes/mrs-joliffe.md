# Mrs. Joliffe

**Mrs. Joliffe** is the capable and sharp-eyed wife of [[corporal-joliffe|Corporal Joliffe]] in Jules Verne's *The Fur Country*. While her husband bustles about with great energy and little direction, it is Mrs. Joliffe who quietly runs the household, manages the fort's domestic affairs, and — by universal acknowledgment — holds the real authority in the [[corporal-joliffe|Joliffe]] partnership. She is perhaps the novel's most fully realised portrait of domestic competence in an Arctic setting.

## In the Novel

### The Pyramidal Pudding

Mrs. Joliffe is introduced at the opening soiree at [[fort-reliance|Fort Reliance]] on 17th March 1859. Her first notable contribution is culinary — a monumental dessert that serves as the evening's centrepiece.

> [001-012] "On the table rose a pyramidal pudding made by Mrs. Joliffe's own hands; it was an immense truncated cone, composed of flour, fat, reindeer venison, and musk beef."

The pudding is a triumph, and when [[captain-craventy|Captain Craventy]] commends [[corporal-joliffe|the Corporal]] for the evening's arrangements, Joliffe himself concedes the true source of the credit: "honour to whom honour is due, Mrs. Joliffe deserves part of your commendation; she assisted me in everything" (PN 001-006). The irony — that she "assisted" him, rather than the reverse — is, as always, lost on the Corporal. The narrator notes that "Mrs. Joliffe served out slice after slice with liberal hands, yet there remained enough and to spare" (PN 001-012), and that "endless were the compliments paid to the Joliffes that evening, but they deserved them; how zealously they waited on the guests, with what easy grace they distributed the refreshments!" (PN 001-013). Later that same evening, the Corporal announces "with Mrs. Joliffe's assistance" that he is about to mix the punch — with the sugar "measured out by Mrs. Joliffe" (PN 002-051).

### The Real Authority

The essential dynamic of the Joliffe marriage is established in a single devastating sentence:

> [001-026] "So he called himself the 'Corporal in charge of details,' but he would have lost himself a hundred times amongst these details, had not little Mrs. Joliffe guided him with a firm hand. So it came to pass, that Corporal Joliffe obeyed his wife without owning it."

This is Verne at his most delicately satirical. The observation is sharp but never cruel; the Joliffes' arrangement works because both parties are genuinely devoted to each other, even if only one of them understands how it actually functions. The narrator completes the portrait with a literary flourish, observing that the Corporal, "doubtless thinking to himself, like the philosopher Sancho, 'a woman's advice is no such great thing, but he must be a fool who does not listen to it'" (PN 001-026), has arranged his life around a truth he cannot quite bring himself to state aloud.

### The Overland Journey

During the expedition's march northward, the Joliffes travel together in their own sledge, bringing up the rear of the convoy (PN 005-009). While the Corporal disputes with his wife — perhaps having "drunk too often to the success of the expedition on starting" (PN 005-033) — Mrs. Joliffe maintains her practical composure. Her usefulness on the journey is noted by the narrator: "his wife was most useful and devoted; and Mrs. Paulina Barnett had already struck up a great friendship with the brisk little Canadian woman, whose fair hair and large soft eyes were so pleasant to look at" (PN 004-057).

### Domestic Command at Fort Hope

At [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]], Mrs. Joliffe's authority becomes institutionalised. She is assigned quarters in the principal house alongside [[jaspar-hobson|Hobson]], [[paulina-barnett|Barnett]], [[thomas-black|Thomas Black]], and [[madge|Madge]] (PN 013-008). She presides over the kitchen, assisted by Mrs. Rae — "with the Corporal always at her heels" (PN 018-009) — and manages the fort's domestic economy with quiet efficiency.

When [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Hobson]] must leave the fort on a critical mission, he knows exactly where true command resides:

> [026-010] "During the absence of the Lieutenant and the Sergeant, the command of the fort fell to Corporal Joliffe, or rather to his little wife, and Hobson knew that he could trust her."

The phrase "or rather to his little wife" is the novel's definitive verdict on the Joliffe partnership. Hobson does not trust the Corporal; he trusts Mrs. Joliffe, and the distinction is both comic and entirely serious. In an emergency, competence matters more than rank. This trust manifests practically later when, after the ice island disaster, Hobson "sent for Mrs. Joliffe, and asked her if plenty of provisions had been left in the house" (PN 041-015). Her reply is characteristically brisk: "Oh, yes, there was plenty to eat in the pantry and kitchen" (PN 041-016) — as well as water and rum (PN 041-018).

### The Garden at Fort Hope

Mrs. Joliffe's ambition extends far beyond the kitchen. She "took a great interest in 'economic' botany" (PN 014-030), scouring the barren land around the fort for edible plants. The pickings were slim — she found only a wild leek whose "bulb, very difficult to classify, because its leaves fall off just at the flowering season, turned out to be a wild leek, and yielded a good crop of onions, each about the size of an egg" (PN 014-031), and the ubiquitous Labrador tea that grew abundantly on the shores of the lagoon (PN 014-032). But Mrs. Joliffe had grander plans.

In the last days of September, "before the ground should be covered with snow, Mrs. Joliffe was busy sowing the seeds of Cochlearia (scurvy grass) and sorrel, in the hope that as they were very hardy, and would be well protected from the rigour of the winter by the snow itself, they would come up in the spring" (PN 017-007). Her garden was no window-box — it consisted "of several acres hidden behind the cliff of the cape" (PN 017-007). This was serious, provisioning-minded horticulture: antiscorbutic plants that would protect the colony against the vitamin deficiencies that plagued Arctic expeditions.

The gamble paid off. "Early in May the snow had disappeared in several places, and a scanty vegetation clothed the soil. Tiny mosses and slender grasses timidly pushed up their stems above the ground, and the sorrel and cochlearia seeds which Mrs. Joliffe had planted began to sprout" (PN 022-044). The garden required constant vigilance against birds and rodents, and "this arduous and important task was confided to the worthy Corporal, who acquitted himself of it with the zeal and devotion of a scarecrow in a kitchen garden" (PN 022-044). By summer, "[t]here was now a fine crop of sorrel and scurvy-grass — thanks to the Corporal's unwearying exertions to keep off the birds of every kind, which congregated by hundreds" (PN 028-019). [[paulina-barnett|Mrs. Barnett]], "seeing what an interest Mrs. Joliffe took in her plants, joined her every day in the garden" (PN 028-019).

Mrs. Joliffe's horticultural foresight proved medically critical. During the long, damp second polar night, "the constant damp was very unhealthy, and likely to lead to scorbutic affections amongst the colonists, but fortunately, although the lime juice and lime lozenges were running short, and no fresh stock had been obtained, the scurvy-grass and sorrel had yielded a very good crop, and, by the advice of Lieutenant Hobson, a portion of them was eaten daily" (PN 034-032). The garden was not decorative; it was a survival technology.

The fact that gardening continued even into the second season — "the Corporal and his little wife had also sown fresh seeds, encouraged by the success of the last in the warm season" (PN 033-016), preparing the ground for scurvy-grass and Labrador tea because "it was important that there should be no lack of these valuable antiscorbutics" (PN 033-016) — is a testament to their persistence and foresight. Even when the island was doomed and the fort was to be abandoned, the Corporal fought desperately to protect the crop from the hordes of rodents and reindeer that besieged it (PN 039-020). [[paulina-barnett|Mrs. Barnett]] "could not help laughing at this absurd speech, and sent the Corporal to his little wife, who had long ago resigned herself to the loss of her sorrel, scurvy grass, and other medicinal herbs" (PN 039-023). Mrs. Joliffe's resignation, in contrast to her husband's theatrical despair, speaks to her hard-won pragmatism. As the ice island drifted into warmer latitudes, "Mrs. Joliffe's garden was wonderfully successful" (PN 042-033) — a small, strange triumph in the midst of catastrophe.

### The Christmas Celebration and Maintaining Morale

Mrs. Joliffe's role as keeper of the colony's morale is most visible during the holiday seasons that punctuate the long Arctic winter. At Christmas 1859, though the weather was brutal — "the Fahrenheit thermometer fell to 18 degrees below zero, and the gale roared like a volley of musketry" (PN 020-003) — she ensured the traditions of home were observed. "Christmas Day, the day of home-gatherings so dear to the heart of all Englishmen, was kept with due solemnity. The colonists returned thanks to God for preserving them through so many perils; and the workmen, who had a holiday in honour of the day, afterwards assembled with their masters and the ladies round a well-filled board, on which figured two huge Christmas puddings" (PN 020-004). That evening "a huge bowl of punch flamed in the centre of the table; the lamps were put out, and for a time the room was lighted only by the livid flames of the spirit" (PN 020-005).

The Christmas puddings of 1859 silently echo the pyramidal pudding with which she was introduced — Mrs. Joliffe's signature contribution to communal life is always the meal, the shared table, the food that transforms a group of isolated colonists into a community. On New Year's Day, "Lieutenant Hobson wished Mrs. Barnett a happy new year, and complimented her on the courage and good temper with which she endured the miseries of this northern winter" (PN 020-020), a recognition that applied equally to all the women who kept the fort's domestic machinery running.

Her work in maintaining normalcy is equally apparent at smaller celebrations. On 11th November, during the second year, "something of a fete was held at Fort Hope. Mrs. Joliffe served up a few extras at dinner, for it was the anniversary of the birth of little Michael Mac-Nab. He was now a year old, and was the delight of everybody" (PN 034-030). She provided the reindeer milk for her daily cooking from the domesticated herd: "the females provided Mrs. Joliffe with plenty of milk for her daily culinary needs" (PN 033-015). Even when the fort was reduced to a fragment of ice and the situation was hopeless, Mrs. Joliffe continued performing her function: "Mrs. Joliffe was preparing some food" while the men and women gathered "in gloomy silence in the rough shelter still remaining to them" (PN 045-019). That she kept cooking — serving salt-meat to people who could not bring themselves to eat, the night before their expected destruction (PN 046-005) — is the novel's final, implicit tribute to her unshakeable routine.

### The Alert Observer

Mrs. Joliffe is not merely a domestic manager; she is also one of the colony's sharpest observers. During the winter, when strange sounds echo through the frozen night, it is Mrs. Joliffe who suddenly exclaims, drawing attention to something amiss (PN 018-026). Her vigilance complements her husband's obliviousness.

When the Corporal hesitates to voice an important observation about the fort's position, it is Mrs. Joliffe who nudges his elbow — literally pushing him to speak what she has already figured out (PN 024-010). Behind every one of the Corporal's insights stands his wife, feeding him the lines.

### The Salt Water Discovery

Mrs. Joliffe is, remarkably, the person who first discovers that Fort Hope has become an island. On 1st June, "Hope, one of the soldiers, went to fetch some water from the lake for culinary purposes, and when Mrs. Joliffe tasted it, she found that it was salt. She called Hope, and said she wanted fresh, not salt water" (PN 044-017). It is a moment of pure, practical detection — she is simply doing her job, tasting the water meant for cooking, when her senses alert her to something catastrophically wrong.

"The man replied that he had brought it from the lake as usual, and as he and Mrs. Joliffe were disputing about it, the Lieutenant happened to come in. Hearing Hope's repeated assertions that he had fetched the water from the lake, he turned pale and hurried to the lagoon" (PN 044-018). Hobson's immediate verification confirms what Mrs. Joliffe's tongue had already told her: "the waters were quite salt; the bottom of the lake had evidently given way, and the sea had flowed in" (PN 044-019). The discovery that the very ground beneath them had become a floating island — the central crisis of the novel's second half — is initiated by Mrs. Joliffe tasting her cooking water and refusing to accept a bad explanation.

## Character and Traits

Mrs. Joliffe is one of Verne's most successful supporting characters — a woman whose competence is so complete that it requires no advertisement. Her traits form a portrait of quiet authority:

- **Practical competence**: Where others plan and debate, Mrs. Joliffe simply does. The pyramidal pudding, the kitchen management, the garden — all testify to a woman who knows how to make things work. When Hope brings salt water from the lake, everyone else would have accepted it; Mrs. Joliffe tastes, detects the anomaly, and demands an explanation. Her competence is sensory and immediate.
- **Scientific curiosity in a practical frame**: Her interest in botany is not academic but nutritional — "economic botany" (PN 014-030). She seeks out wild leeks and Labrador tea, sows cochlearia specifically as an antiscorbutic (PN 033-016), and her garden of "several acres" (PN 017-007) is conceived as a food-security project. This is applied science at its most essential.
- **Long-range planning**: Sowing seeds in September for a spring harvest that may arrive only in May — and repeating the process in a second season even as the island drifts toward destruction — demonstrates extraordinary patience and faith. Mrs. Joliffe plans meals, gardens, and celebrations months in advance, creating the rhythms that make an Arctic winter survivable.
- **Tactical invisibility**: She never challenges her husband's self-image. She nudges his elbow rather than contradicting him (PN 024-010); she lets him take the credit while she does the work. This is not weakness but wisdom — she understands that managing the Corporal requires managing his ego. The Sancho Panza allusion (PN 001-026) is exactly right: she rules a kingdom without ever sitting on the throne.
- **Loyalty without illusion**: Mrs. Joliffe loves her husband without being blind to his limitations. Her support is unwavering but never uncritical; she nudges, guides, and corrects, all while maintaining the fiction that he is in charge. When the Corporal makes theatrical speeches about abandoning the garden, she has "long ago resigned herself to the loss" (PN 039-023) — she accepts reality while he performs grief.
- **Community anchor**: Alongside [[mrs-mac-nab|Mrs. Mac-Nab]] and [[mrs-rae|Mrs. Rae]], Mrs. Joliffe forms the backbone of the fort's domestic life — cooking, sewing, nursing, and maintaining the routines that make survival possible. The Christmas puddings, the Michael birthday feast, the daily meals that continued even as the island melted beneath their feet — these are the rituals through which a colony becomes a community, and Mrs. Joliffe is their presiding genius.
- **Resilience in catastrophe**: After the earthquake, after the salt water discovery, after the breakup of the island reduced them to a fragment of ice — Mrs. Joliffe is still cooking. "The men and women were gathered together in gloomy silence in the rough shelter still remaining to them, and Mrs. Joliffe was preparing some food" (PN 045-019). It does not matter that "no one would touch the salt-meat served round by Mrs. Joliffe" (PN 046-005); she makes it available, because that is what she does. Her response to the unthinkable is to carry on.
