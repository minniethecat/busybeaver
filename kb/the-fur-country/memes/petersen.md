# Petersen

**Petersen** is a Canadian soldier of the [[hudsons-bay-company|Hudson's Bay Company]] in Jules Verne's *The Fur Country*. He is twice singled out by the narrator — once, at the outset, as "the Canadian," marking his distinct origin among the predominantly English and Scottish company men; and again when he and [[kellet|Kellet]] vanish for two days in the Arctic wilderness, a disappearance that the novel records with characteristic brevity. Among [[mac-nab|Mac-Nab]]'s carpenter's men, Petersen is the reliable, unobtrusive soldier who appears at every critical moment — building [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]], hunting walruses, volunteering for dangerous reconnaissance, and finally constructing the escape boat on [[the-ice-island|the drifting ice island]].

## In the Novel

Petersen first appears during the overland journey from [[fort-reliance|Fort Reliance]], travelling in the third sledge with [[thomas-black|Thomas Black]], the astronomer. The narrator introduces him with a single telling epithet: "the Canadian, Petersen."

> [005-009] "Thomas Black and one of the soldiers, the Canadian, Petersen, occupied the third sledge."

This epithet sets Petersen apart from his comrades. Where the other soldiers are identified by their trades — [[rae|Rae the blacksmith]], [[mac-nab|Mac-Nab]] the carpenter — Petersen is identified by his origin. The designation suggests that his value to the expedition lies in his familiarity with the North American wilderness rather than any particular craft. He is, in a sense, the expedition's native guide among the soldiers — a man who knows the land in a way the European-born company men do not.

> [013-004] "Petersen, Belcher, Rae, Garry, Pond, Hope, and Kellet formed a body of clever, zealous carpenters, under the able superintendence of Mac-Nab, a Scotchman from Stirling, who had had considerable experience in the building both of houses and boats."

At [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]], Petersen is absorbed into [[mac-nab|Mac-Nab]]'s team of carpenters, participating in the construction of the fort, the hunting expeditions, and the scouting missions — the anonymous, essential labour that sustains the colony through its first Arctic year.

## The Walrus Hunt

Petersen's most active sequence comes during the walrus hunting expedition, when [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Hobson]] selects him alongside Hope and Kellet as companions. Stationed as a sentinel on a little promontory, Petersen watches for danger while [[paulina-barnett|Mrs. Barnett]], Hobson, and [[sergeant-long|Long]] climb the cliff to survey the coast.

> [015-003] "The Lieutenant chose as his other companions, Sergeant Long, and the soldiers Petersen, Hope, and Kellet, and the party set off at eight o'clock in the morning in two sledges, each drawn by six dogs."

> [015-016] "Petersen, Hope, and Kellet, took up their position as sentinels on the little promontory, whilst Mrs. Barnett, Hobson, and Long, after promising not to lose sight of their comrades, and to be on the lookout for their signal, proceeded to climb the cliff."

When the hunters close in on the walrus herd, Petersen joins the coordinated assault — Hobson and Kellet rushing from one side, the Sergeant, Petersen, and Hope from the other. In a swift and decisive action, they dispatch two walruses before the rest of the herd plunges into the sea.

> [015-035] "As the hunters drew near the sentinels took alarm, and raising their heads looked searchingly around them; but before they could warn their companions of danger, Hobson and Kellet rushed upon them from one side, the Sergeant, Petersen, and Hope from the other, and after lodging a ball in each of their bodies, despatched them with their spears, whilst the rest of the herd plunged into the sea."

## Scout and Volunteer

Petersen's reliability as a scout is demonstrated most clearly in two episodes. In the first, during the foggy month of May, he and [[kellet|Kellet]] "once caused their companions grave anxiety by disappearing for forty-eight hours." They had lost their way in the thick fog, turning to the south when they thought they were near Walruses' Bay. They came back exhausted and half dead with hunger — but they came back. The novel's laconic report leaves the reader to imagine the silent drama of those two days: how they survived, what they saw, what passed between the Canadian soldier and his companion in the blank white fog.

> [022-048] "Petersen and Kellet once caused their companions grave anxiety by disappearing for forty-eight hours. They had lost their way, and turned to the south when they thought they were near to Walruses' Bay. They came back exhausted and half dead with hunger."

In the second episode, Petersen volunteers for a dangerous ice-field reconnaissance mission alongside [[belcher|Belcher]], [[pond|Pond]], and Kellet — all four men declaring their readiness to start without hesitation, a testament to the courage and loyalty that Hobson had cultivated among his men.

> [023-034] "The four soldiers elected to accompany him were Belcher, Pond, Petersen, and Kellet, who declared their readiness to start."

## The Boat on the Ice Island

When the colony finds itself adrift on [[the-ice-island|the ice island]], Petersen is once again among [[mac-nab|Mac-Nab]]'s men, working zealously at the construction of a boat — a difficult task likely to occupy them for several months, and one on which the colonists' survival may depend. The Lieutenant lets things go on as if the future of the factory were not compromised, persevering in keeping the men in ignorance of their true situation. Petersen and his fellow carpenters work on, unaware that the boat they are building may be their only hope of escape.

> [028-006] "Mac-Nab and his men — Petersen, Belcher, Garry, Pond, and Hope — worked zealously at the construction of a boat, a difficult task, likely to occupy them for several months."
