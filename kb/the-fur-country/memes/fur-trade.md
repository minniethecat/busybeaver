# Fur Trade

The fur trade was the primary economic activity driving European and Canadian exploration and settlement of the North American Arctic throughout the 18th and 19th centuries. In Jules Verne's novel _The Fur Country_, the trade in pelts—particularly those of bears, foxes, beavers, and other [[arctic-wildlife|Arctic animals]]—forms the backdrop against which the [[hudsons-bay-company|Hudson's Bay Company]] establishes its network of forts and trading posts.

## The Hudson's Bay Company's Commercial Empire

The [[hudsons-bay-company|Hudson's Bay Company]] operated an extensive network of forts and factories across the Canadian territories, each serving as a local hub for the collection, sorting, and shipment of furs. These furs were destined for European markets where they commanded premium prices (002-001). The Company's commercial dominion extended from the Atlantic to the Pacific, and northward into the [[arctic-circle|Arctic region]]s where [[fort-reliance|Fort Reliance]] and other outposts were established.

The Company's agents and factors managed the trade at each post, dealing directly with native trappers who supplied the vast majority of furs. These exchanges were conducted according to established rates and customs, with [[hudsons-bay-company|the Company]] exercising a virtual monopoly over the trade in its chartered territories (002-002).

## The Fur Bazaar at Fort Reliance

At the opening of the novel, [[captain-craventy|Captain Craventy]] hosts a soirée at [[fort-reliance|Fort Reliance]] where the great hall is decorated with a magnificent display of furs:

> From the beams hung magnificent furs admirably arranged, the equal of which could not be seen in the more favoured regions of the globe. The eye wandered from the furs of wolves, grey bears, polar bears, otters, wolverines, beavers, muskrats, water polecats, ermines, and silver foxes; above these were displayed muslin, cottons, and cloths of various colours, and from the centre of the hall were suspended magnificent lustres (001-001).

This display served not merely as decoration but as a tangible representation of the wealth generated by the fur trade—a living catalogue of the commodities that justified [[hudsons-bay-company|the Company]]'s presence in these remote northern latitudes.

## Types of Furs and Their Sources

The [[arctic-circle|Arctic region]]s yielded a remarkable variety of valuable pelts. Among the most prized were:

- **Polar bear** furs, valued for their size, warmth, and the prestige associated with hunting such dangerous game in the extreme north (021-001)
- **Silver fox** and **ermine** pelts, which commanded the highest prices in European luxury markets
- **Beaver** pelts, the staple of the Canadian fur trade since its inception in the 17th century
- **Wolf**, **wolverine**, and **otter** furs, sought after for their durability and warmth

Each species required different trapping techniques and was hunted in different seasons, creating a year-round cycle of economic activity that structured life at [[hudsons-bay-company|the Company]]'s forts (001-001).

## The Factory System

The Company organized its operations around "factories"—fortified trading posts where furs were received, graded, and stored pending shipment. At [[fort-reliance|Fort Reliance]], the factory served as the administrative and commercial center for a vast surrounding territory. The factor (chief trader) managed accounts, negotiated with native trappers, and maintained [[hudsons-bay-company|the Company]]'s interests against rival traders and the harsh environment (004-001).

## Role of Native Peoples

The native populations—Eskimos (Inuit) and various Indian tribes—were essential partners in the fur trade. They possessed the knowledge of the land, the hunting skills, and the seasonal mobility required to harvest furs across the vast Arctic territories. In exchange for pelts, they received European goods including firearms, metal tools, textiles, and other manufactured items (001-003).

The relationship between [[hudsons-bay-company|the Company]] and native trappers was complex—partnership, dependency, and exploitation intertwined. The Company relied on native expertise for survival in the Arctic, yet the trade fundamentally altered traditional economies and social structures.
