# Lieutenant Jaspar Hobson

**Jaspar Hobson** is the central protagonist of Jules Verne's novel *The Fur Country*. A lieutenant in the service of the [[hudsons-bay-company|Hudson's Bay Company]], he is entrusted with leading an ambitious expedition north of the Great Slave Lake to establish a new trading post near the Arctic coast. His leadership, determination, and devotion to [[hudsons-bay-company|the Company]]'s interests drive the narrative forward through both triumph and catastrophe.

## In the Novel

Jaspar Hobson appears as the commanding officer of the expedition to the Arctic coast, responsible for establishing what becomes [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]] on the shores of the [[great-bear-lake|Great Bear Lake]]. His journey takes him from [[fort-reliance|Fort Reliance]] northwest across the Slave Lake, through the [[arctic-circle|Arctic Circle]], and to the edge of the [[arctic-ocean|Arctic Ocean]]. When the catastrophic discovery is made that the expedition's settlement sits not on a peninsula but on an ice island adrift in the ocean, Hobson's leadership is tested to its limits.

Throughout the novel, Hobson embodies the virtues of the ideal Company officer: methodical planning, unwavering courage, and a deep sense of duty. Yet he is not merely a stock military figure -- his relationships with [[paulina-barnett|Paulina Barnett]], [[sergeant-long|Sergeant Long]], and the eccentric astronomer [[thomas-black|Thomas Black]] reveal a man capable of warmth, humour, and genuine friendship.

> [001-024] "Jaspar Hobson was a man of forty years of age. He was short and slight, with little muscular power; but a force of will which carried him successfully through all trials, and enabled him to rise superior to adverse circumstances. He was 'a child of the Company.' His father, Major Hobson, an Irishman of Dublin, had for forty years commanded a troop of soldiers in the service of the Hudson's Bay Company, and Jaspar was born on the very day of the Union."

> [002-010] "It is, madam, but Jaspar Hobson has never yet drawn back from a task imposed upon him, however formidable it may have appeared."

### The Tempest Episode

Hobson's first great test of leadership comes during the overland journey north, when the expedition is caught in a violent Arctic tempest while traversing a narrow mountain pass. As the party entered a gorge between towering icebergs, Hobson, reading the signs of the sky with practiced eye, foresaw the coming storm and ordered immediate preparations for shelter [007-028]. His judgment proved correct: the hurricane descended with fearful fury, and pieces of ice broken off by the wind were hurled into the pass while avalanches threatened to crush the sledges and their occupants [007-033].

With travel impossible, Hobson ordered snow-houses to be hollowed out of the frozen masses -- holes in which each person could shelter until the storm abated. He shared a hut with [[paulina-barnett|Mrs. Barnett]] and [[madge|Madge]], while [[thomas-black|Thomas Black]] and [[sergeant-long|Sergeant Long]] occupied another [007-036]. For forty-eight hours the tempest continued unabated, and Hobson had to ensure the openings of the shelters were cleared of drifting snow every half-hour. Throughout the ordeal he remained vigilant, warning [[paulina-barnett|Mrs. Barnett]] that bears were prowling about the pass, drawn by the commotion [007-037]. So violent was the storm that even the Lieutenant and his soldiers could scarcely set foot outside [007-036]. When the tempest suddenly ceased under the influence of intense cold at dawn on the third day, Hobson immediately assessed the damage and pressed the journey onward, his authority and composure having held the party together through the ordeal [007-038].

A second, more personal trial of endurance occurred on the Great Bear Lake, when Hobson and [[paulina-barnett|Mrs. Barnett]] set out alone across the water in a fishing-boat guided by the old boatman Norman. A sudden squall capsized the vessel, and in the darkness and battering waves, Hobson proved himself a strong swimmer: "Hobson was a strong swimmer, and struck out with one arm, supporting his companion with the other" [009-060]. His physical courage matched his moral resolve, and both were saved by the timely intervention of Indians on the shore. It was during this ordeal that "Lieutenant Hobson now for the first time realised with how intrepid a woman fate had thrown him" [009-044].

### The Discovery of the Ice Island

From the moment Hobson selected the site for [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]] on the plateau behind Cape Bathurst, subtle anomalies troubled his methodical mind. He remarked to [[paulina-barnett|Mrs. Barnett]] that there was not a single rock, stone, flint, or pebble to be seen anywhere on the cape -- the soil consisted entirely of earth and sand [013-004]. More disturbingly, the tides of the Arctic Ocean, which all navigators agreed were distinctly marked in Polar seas, failed to materialise: Hobson watched them attentively throughout a full month and found they varied no more than a foot [015-008]. He had made bench marks to estimate vertical displacement, but the combined solar and lunar influence appeared almost absent -- "a fact," Hobson reflected, "quite inexplicable" [017-003].

The climactic revelation came not through geology but astronomy. When [[thomas-black|Thomas Black]]'s long-awaited total solar eclipse of 18 July 1860 failed to occur at the predicted moment, Hobson knew at once that something fundamental had changed: their latitude was no longer what it had been when Fort Hope was founded [023-072]. Taking new observations with the sextant, he discovered the truth -- Fort Hope had moved. The settlement was not on a peninsula but on an ice island adrift in the Arctic Ocean. "And so Fort Hope, founded by Lieutenant Hobson on the borders of the Polar Sea, had drifted!" [024-002].

Hobson's first decision as commander was strategic: he determined not to acquaint the men with the truth [024-004]. He judged correctly that the knowledge might provoke panic among the soldiers and their families, and instead resolved to maintain the routine of the fort while secretly studying their situation and searching for a means of escape. Only [[paulina-barnett|Mrs. Barnett]], [[sergeant-long|Sergeant Long]], and [[thomas-black|Thomas Black]] were admitted to the secret. Hobson initiated the secret construction of a large boat, ostensibly for coastal exploration, and ordered a chart of the Arctic Ocean currents to be brought to his room, where the inner circle studied their possible drift trajectories [025-018].

### Crisis Management: The Polar Night and Bear Siege

The polar winter at Fort Hope presented Hobson with crises that tested the limits of his command. When the temperature plunged to catastrophic lows, he grew increasingly uneasy [020-022] and urged his companions to resist the intense drowsiness induced by extreme cold, knowing that sleep in such conditions meant death [020-019]. He distributed lime juice and lime lozenges to guard against scurvy [019-016] and ordered the approaches to the enceinte cleared of snow so the fort could better withstand wild beasts [019-018].

The most desperate crisis combined both elemental and animal threats. When a party of large polar bears climbed the palisades and attacked the house, Hobson immediately ordered the passage window barricaded [021-006]. The bears climbed onto the roof and their great claws caught in the laths -- there was real danger they could tear away the woodwork [021-021]. Hobson and his men rushed to the attic but were driven back by the cold [021-022]. The siege became a blockade: the besieged could not leave the house without freezing, and could not stay indefinitely without fuel.

When the fuel ran out and the stoves failed, Hobson faced an agonizing choice: "My friends, two courses only are open to us -- we can risk our lives to get a fresh supply of fuel, or we can burn the benches, beds, partition walls, and everything in the house to feed our stoves for a few days longer" [021-033]. He chose the former, and in an act of cool leadership selected [[sergeant-long|Sergeant Long]] for the perilous mission, accompanying him to the outer door himself [021-041, 021-045].

The crisis reached its climax when the bears broke the chimney pipes, filling the house with thick, acrid smoke. "Hobson now saw that he must leave the house if he wished to escape suffocation, but to leave the house would be to perish with cold" [021-075]. Seizing a hatchet, he shouted "To the bears, my friends!" and led the charge up through the trapdoor into the attic [021-075, 021-077]. The colonists fired into the smoke-filled darkness, and through the gaps in the separated wall beams they saw the terrified bears fleeing into the night [021-078]. Hobson had converted a catastrophe into a breakout.

### The Bond with Paulina Barnett

From their earliest meetings at [[fort-reliance|Fort Reliance]], Hobson formed an intellectual and emotional bond with [[paulina-barnett|Paulina Barnett]] that deepened throughout the expedition. Before departure, "many a long talk did she have with Lieutenant Hobson, many were the projects they formed, and firmly were they both determined to allow no obstacle to check their advance" [004-025]. He named a stream Paulina River and a harbour Port Barnett in her honour, gestures that delighted the celebrated traveller [012-026].

Their relationship was forged in shared danger. When their boat capsized on the Great Bear Lake, Hobson struck out with one arm while supporting Barnett with the other [009-060]. When Hobson resolved to make his perilous reconnaissance to Cape Michael during the great hurricane, it was Barnett who waited to press his hand before he slipped away [030-004]. He confided his deepest fears about the island's fate to her, and she in turn did her best "to cheer the disheartened Lieutenant" [027-045]. After the earthquake, he renamed the surviving body of water Lake Barnett [022-013].

Hobson's admiration of Barnett was open and profound. "Lieutenant Hobson's admiration of her character was unbounded" [039-010]. He praised her "grandeur of character, moral courage and imperturbable good temper" [022-023]. At the New Year, he complimented her on "the courage and good temper with which she endured the miseries of this northern winter" [020-020]. Yet their bond was conducted with Victorian restraint -- there were moments of charged silence. When the decision to abandon the island was announced, "for some minutes Mrs. Barnett and Hobson were left alone, but neither of them spoke" [034-048]. It was a partnership of equals: the soldier of the Company and the intrepid lady traveller, bound by mutual respect and shared peril in the white wilderness.

## Leadership and Character

Jaspar Hobson represents Verne's ideal of the officer-explorer: a man in whom technical competence, moral courage, and personal decency are fused. His leadership style is defined by several consistent traits.

**Methodical Prudence.** Hobson "determined with almost excessive prudence to have the Factory enclosed before the winter set in" [014-011]. He planned every stage of the expedition with the rigor of a military campaign -- provisions, arms, tools, seeds for antiscorbutic plants, and winter garments were all arranged before departure [004-050, 004-052, 014-033]. His foresight repeatedly saved the colony: he laid in stores of walrus oil, ordered the construction of a large magazine, and kept a detailed daily journal recording weather, temperature, and the appearance of meteors [018-008, 020-003].

**Strategic Secrecy.** When Hobson learned that Fort Hope was adrift on an ice island, his instinct was to contain the information. "Although Hobson had no doubt of the moral and physical courage and determination of his companions, he determined not to acquaint them with the truth" [024-004]. This was not cowardice but command judgment: he knew the soldiers could endure hardship better without the psychological burden of knowing they were floating helplessly toward the Bering Strait. He maintained this secrecy for months, taking his bearings in solitude to avoid arousing suspicion [028-049], and when the truth finally had to be told, his men responded not with panic but with tears of loyalty [035-003].

**Physical Courage.** Despite his slight build, Hobson never shrank from physical danger. He swam a half-frozen crevasse to scout a route for his party [036-027]. He braved the hurricane on Cape Bathurst, "crouching upon the ground, clutching at the shrubs like a drowning man at a spar, lashed by the wind, drenched by the rain and the spray" [029-020]. He faced bears, avalanches, and the dissolving ice island itself with a steadiness that inspired his followers. Even in what seemed the final hours, "Hobson, whose heroic courage did not even now fail him, shouted" orders to his companions [046-072].

**Devotion to the Company.** Hobson was "a child of the Company" -- born into HBC service, his entire identity was defined by the fur-trading enterprise. His "zeal was beyond all praise" [004-057]. Yet this devotion never blinded him to human realities. He economised the rum and whisky, "firmly resolved to economise these spirits, so injurious to the health in cold latitudes" [004-052]. He put the lives of his people before the Company's property when the two conflicted, authorizing the burning of furniture and the sacrifice of furs when survival demanded it [035-038].

**Emotional Reserve and Its Limits.** Hobson was a master of the composed exterior expected of an officer, but Verne allows us glimpses of the man beneath. He "pressed his subordinate's hand, well knowing the poor fellow's devotion" [020-034]. When his men revealed they had known the truth about the island for weeks, "tears of emotion stood in Hobson's eyes, and he made no attempt to conceal them" [035-003]. He was "very silent and thoughtful" when troubled by the appearance of rival trappers [016-055], and "painfully moved" by bad news "and being anxious to conceal his emotion, he shut himself up in his own room and did not appear again that day" [031-025].

Jaspar Hobson is thus not a superhuman hero but a recognizably human commander -- one who feels fear, grief, and doubt, but whose force of will consistently overcomes them for the sake of those in his charge.

Jaspar Hobson was literally born into the [[hudsons-bay-company|Hudson's Bay Company]] -- his birth coincided with the Act of Union between Great Britain and Ireland (1 January 1801), and his father served as a major in [[hudsons-bay-company|the Company]] for four decades. This lineage made him, in the novel's phrase, "a child of [[hudsons-bay-company|the Company]]." His entire identity and career are inseparable from the fur-trading enterprise.
