# Bering Sea

The **Bering Sea** (often spelled Behring Sea in Verne's text) is the body of water that connects the Pacific Ocean to the [[arctic-ocean|Arctic Ocean]] via the Bering Strait. In Jules Verne's *The Fur Country*, it represents both a strategic asset and a distant hope: the sea route by which the [[hudsons-bay-company|Hudson's Bay Company]] plans to resupply [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]], and the direction in which the colonists ultimately look for rescue.  The sea represents both a destination and a threat in the novel's geography — the ultimate southern limit of the ice island's drift, where warmer waters would inevitably melt the frozen foundation beneath Fort Hope. Its position between Russian America and the Asian continent places it at the crossroads of the narrative's climatic and geographical tensions.

## In the Novel

### Overview

The Bering Sea enters the narrative early, as part of [[captain-craventy|Captain Craventy]]'s strategic briefing. The Company's plan for its Arctic factory depends on a crucial geographical fact: the sea north of Bering Strait is open for much of the year, allowing vessels to reach the new post:

> [011-006] "'There the sea, open for a great part of the year, will allow the vessels from Bering Strait to come right up to the fort, bringing us fresh provisions and taking away our commodities.'"

This assumption — that the [[arctic-ocean|Arctic Ocean]] above the seventieth parallel would be navigable — was, in Verne's day, a subject of active debate among geographers. The novel's plot is, in part, a dramatisation of that debate: the colonists stake their lives on a theory of Arctic navigation, and the theory fails them.

> [002-013] "'The Admiralty is about to send a vessel which will coast along the North American continent, from Bering Strait to Coronation Gulf, on the eastern side of which the new fort is to be built.'"

## Geography

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Connection** | Pacific Ocean to [[arctic-ocean|Arctic Ocean]] via Bering Strait |
| **Width of strait** | Approximately 53 miles at narrowest point |
| **Borders** | Siberia (west), Alaska (east) |
| **Ice conditions** | Seasonal; navigable summer–autumn |
| **Role in novel** | Resupply route; direction of eventual hope |

The Bering Sea's significance shifts as the novel progresses. At first, it is a commercial lifeline — the route by which provisions will arrive and furs will be shipped out. Later, when [[the-ice-island|the ice island]] begins to drift, it becomes a direction: the colonists watch the sea with new urgency, hoping to see the sails of a rescue vessel approaching from the west.

> [013-027] "The supply of sea-fish was not so good; and though many a grampus and whale passed by in the offing, the latter probably flying from the harpoons of the Bering Strait fishermen."
