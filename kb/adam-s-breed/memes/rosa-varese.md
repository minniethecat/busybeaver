## Overview

Rosa Varese is the second wife of [[fabio-boselli]] and a maternal presence in the childhood of the protagonist [[gian-luca-boselli]]. The daughter of [[nerone|Nerone]] Varese, Rosa enters the Boselli household following the death of [[olga-boselli]] and becomes one of [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s primary caregivers during his early years. Characterized by her emotional openness — she "spoke seldom but cried very often" [010-001] — Rosa represents the warmth and vulnerability that temper the sternness of [[teresa-boselli]]'s matriarchal household.

## Biography

### Entry into the Boselli Household

Rosa is introduced as "the concrete creature" [009-002], a phrase that suggests both her physical reality and the role she fills in the wake of [[olga-death-and-gian-luca-birth|Olga's death]]. She marries [[fabio-boselli|Fabio]] and brings her son [[mario-boselli|Mario]] from a previous relationship into the Boselli home. Her husband is a waiter at a restaurant in the Soho [[italian-diaspora|Italian community]] [010-004].

### Caregiver to Gian-Luca

Rosa becomes a significant figure in [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s early childhood. She shares a bed with [[fabio-boselli|Fabio]] and keeps a cradle beside it for the infant [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]], who comes to like her presence [010-003]. Her interactions with the child are marked by a tenderness that contrasts with [[teresa-boselli|Teresa]]'s more austere approach: when [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] hurts himself, Rosa weeps and murmurs "Poveretto!" — a refrain that recurs throughout her appearances [010-009]. Her emotional expressiveness, including tearful reconciliations with [[mario-boselli|Mario]] after their quarrels [010-005], creates an atmosphere of warmth in the household.

### Gian-Luca's Weaning

As [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] grows older, a deliberate effort is made to wean him from Rosa's constant care — a transition that coincides with his first ventures into the wider world of the [[old-compton-street|Soho streets]] [012-002]. Even after this separation, Rosa remains a peripheral but steady presence, sometimes staying to play with the boy and always watching over the domestic spaces she maintains [014-022].

## Relationships

### Fabio Boselli
Rosa's marriage to [[fabio-boselli]] is portrayed as a practical and affectionate partnership. They share a bed, work in the restaurant trade, and together provide a stable home for their blended family [010-004].

### Gian-Luca Boselli
Rosa is one of the few nurturing figures in [[gian-luca-boselli]]'s childhood. Her tearful pity for the motherless boy and her willingness to play with him offer a gentleness that balances the severity of [[teresa-boselli|Teresa]]'s grandmotherly discipline. When [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] is eventually weaned from her care, the separation marks his first step toward the independence and [[loneliness-and-alienation|alienation]] that will define his adult life [012-002].

### Mario
Rosa's relationship with her biological son [[mario-boselli|Mario]] is characterized by dramatic emotional swings — quarrels followed by soft-murmured reconciliations — that reveal the texture of domestic life in the Boselli household [010-005].

## Significance

Rosa Varese serves as the novel's embodiment of maternal warmth in its most uncomplicated form. Unlike [[teresa-boselli|Teresa]], whose love is fierce and morally exacting, Rosa offers simple pity and tears. Her refrain of "Poveretto!" — poor little one — expresses a compassion that needs no theological framework. In a novel preoccupied with sin, guilt, and spiritual crisis, Rosa represents the possibility of love without conditions, and her eventual fading from the narrative parallels [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s loss of that simple warmth as he moves deeper into the [[loneliness-and-alienation|isolation]] that defines his adult life.
