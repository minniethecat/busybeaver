## Description

The Macaroni Factory was [[teresa-boselli|Teresa Boselli]]'s most ambitious business venture — a fresh pasta-making operation established in a room behind the [[casa-boselli|Casa Boselli]]'s new shop in London. It was the first enterprise of its kind in England, producing freshly-made pasta daily rather than importing aged product from Italy.

## Origins

The idea for the factory originated from [[francesco-millo|Millo]]'s complaint about the quality of imported pasta. He told [[teresa-boselli|Teresa]]: "The pasta is such a trouble, I would like them to eat it fresh; I am more than a little ashamed of my pasta. If only the English could make macaroni! But no doubt they would make it very badly" (079-004). [[teresa-boselli|Teresa]] took this as a challenge, declaring: "You shall have your fresh pasta; the [[casa-boselli|Casa Boselli]] will make it" (079-004). She considered this instinct for seizing opportunity to be "the genius for business" (079-004).

> "I am sure he is pleased with our macaroni factory." (086-008)

The machinery required for pasta-making had to be imported from Milan at great expense — an investment so large that [[fabio-boselli|Fabio]] "trembled whenever he thought about it" (079-004). The process involved mixing enormous quantities of flour — "sixty pounds with an egg to each pound" — which was then fed into a rotund machine for kneading before being passed through large wooden rollers repeatedly, becoming "cool to the touch and flawless in texture" until "almost transparent, so elegant had it become" (079-004, 120-005).

## Symbol of Success

For [[teresa-boselli|Teresa]], the Macaroni Factory represented the pinnacle of her achievement — "the symbol of success, her unique macaroni factory" (120-005). On her price list she proclaimed: "The [[casa-boselli|Casa Boselli]] will make your macaroni; no need to eat it many months old, we make it fresh every day!" (079-004). The venture gave her aging heart a sense of youth and purpose; "the spring that was thrusting the sap along the branches" also made "[[teresa-boselli|Teresa]]'s old heart feel young because of her new macaroni factory" (108-006).

> The macaroni factory! All night long she lay awake, as straight and as stiff as a corpse, beside Fabio; scarcely twitching a muscle in case she should rouse him, in case he might talk, disturbing her thoughts. All night the thoughts whirred like wheels in her head — like the wheels of the macaroni factory — wheels made of steel that turned faster and faster, stretching their leather bands tighter and tighter — and the bands were somehow a part of her head. (120-006)

## Decline

During the First World War, the factory faced insurmountable challenges. The expert Francesco and the other skilled workers were called up for military service, leaving "one poor, bewildered Anglo-Saxon" to struggle "resentfully to cope with the pasta" (121-009). The flour supply deteriorated into "a dreadful, grey mixture of wheat, barley, maize, and Heaven alone knew what other disgusting adulteration; a gravelly, husky, unpalatable outrage" that "the very machines spewed forth" (121-009).

> For the macaroni factory was threatened on all sides, the forces of disruption were closing in upon it. (121-009)

[[teresa-boselli|Teresa]] paced her office at night, thinking: "If only we could get our supplies, if only we could get our supplies!" She would murmur "My pasta — my wonderful pasta —" with "a voice almost tremulous with sadness" (122-003). By March, the last worker had "thrown up his job, preferring to make munitions," and "now indeed [[teresa-boselli|Teresa]] had her back against the wall" (122-003). In desperation, she turned to old [[fabio-boselli|Fabio]], remembering that "as a lad he had worked in a macaroni factory, he had told her so many times in the past" (122-003).

## Significance

The rise and fall of the Macaroni Factory mirrors the arc of the [[casa-boselli|Casa Boselli]] itself — from immigrant enterprise and innovation through wartime privation to near-ruin. [[teresa-boselli|Teresa]]'s fierce determination to protect it — "I would pickle them with capers, I would surely contrive to make them the fashion, rather than let our business go bankrupt" (120-007) — embodies the immigrant will to survive through sheer force of character.
