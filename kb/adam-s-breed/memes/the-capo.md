## Overview

Il Capo — simply "the Capo" or "the Boss" — is the padrone of the Capo di Monte,
a restaurant at the center of the Boselli family's working life in Soho. More
than an employer, the Capo embodies the aspirational spirit of London's Italian
restaurant trade: his relentless drive to elevate his establishment from a modest
Dean Street dining room into a fashionable destination mirrors the broader
immigrant ambition for respectability and success in a foreign land.

## The Restaurant

[[capo-di-monte|The Capo]] di Monte is situated in Dean Street, Soho, "the whole of a tall,
blistered house that had once been painted brown" (053-001). On either side
of its swing glass doors sit "two white [[capo-di-monte|Capo di Monte]] spaniels" (053-001),
ceramic figures whose smiles and plump contours proclaim, to the hungry of
Dean Street, "the food at the Capo was good" (053-001).

The restaurant employs multiple members of the Boselli family. [[rosa-varese|Rosa]]'s husband
is a waiter there (010-004), as is [[mario-boselli|Mario Boselli]], who speaks of the
establishment with pride and proprietary enthusiasm. [[capo-di-monte|The Capo]] presides over
this domain with the authority of a patriarch, his very title — *Il Capo* —
conveying the absolute nature of his command within the restaurant's walls.

## Ambition and Aspiration

[[capo-di-monte|The Capo]] harbors grand ambitions for his restaurant. [[mario-boselli|Mario]] reports that
"[[the-padrone|the Padrone]] had sworn to bring all London to the Capo di Monte" (031-005).
The restaurant's evolution is marked by incremental refinements: "We have red
cotton curtains at the window now, but soon they will be silk" (031-005).
[[capo-di-monte|The Capo]]'s strategy is to build reputation steadily: "One of these days we
become the fashion, then we put up our prices" (031-005).

[[mario-boselli|Mario]]'s faith in the Capo's vision is unshakeable. When challenged by [[nerone|Nerone]],
[[mario-boselli|Mario]] insists: "How often have I told you that the Capo will be famous, very
famous, one of these days?" (045-020). This conviction extends to his plan
to bring young [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] into the restaurant trade. [[capo-di-monte|The Capo]] thus becomes not
merely an employer but a symbol of the deferred promise that sustains the
immigrant working class — the belief that hard work and patience will
eventually yield prosperity.

## Gian-Luca at the Capo

[[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] begins his working life at the Capo di Monte after leaving his
grandfather's shop. He tells [[fabio-boselli|Fabio]]: "I think I would rather go to the Capo,
[[mario-boselli|Mario]] likes it so" (053-004). The decision is a turning-point — the Capo
becomes the gateway through which [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] enters the restaurant trade that
will define his career.

At the Capo, [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] is envisioned in the restaurant's uniform: "Already
I can see him in a neat white waistcoat and a little black satin tie"
(045-027). [[capo-di-monte|The Capo]]'s establishment thus serves as the training ground
from which [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] will eventually graduate to the more prestigious Doric.

## The Capo as Patriarchal Figure

The padrone is addressed not by name but by title — *Il Capo*, the Boss —
reflecting the hierarchical structure of the Italian restaurant world.
His authority is absolute within the restaurant's walls, yet it is a
benevolent authority invested in the collective project of the
establishment's rise. [[capo-di-monte|The Capo]]'s private motto, revealed late in the novel,
is disarmingly simple: "I have got [[gemma|Gemma]]" (062-009) — suggesting that
beneath the commercial ambition lies a deeply personal satisfaction.

[[nerone|Nerone]], however, remains skeptical of the Capo's pretensions. He sneers
at [[mario-boselli|Mario]]: "are you not well over thirty, and still at your [[capo-di-monte|Capo di Monte]]?"
(045-019), dismissing the restaurant as a place of stalled ambition. This
tension — between the Capo's vision of ascendancy and the reality of
restaurant work as servitude — echoes throughout the novel, questioning
whether the immigrant dream of upward mobility can ever be fully realized
within the constraints of the restaurant trade.

## The Capo and the Restaurant Breed

[[capo-di-monte|The Capo]] belongs to what the novel terms "the restaurant breed" — those
whose identities are inseparable from the trade. His restaurant is not
merely a business but an extension of self, a vehicle for transforming
immigrant marginality into civic presence. [[capo-di-monte|The Capo]]'s story is thus the
story of an institution: the Italian restaurant as a site of cultural
negotiation, where the labour of immigrants becomes visible to, and valued
by, the English public they serve.
