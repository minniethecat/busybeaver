## Biography

Lucrezia is a cook who lived and worked in the Soho [[italian-diaspora|Italian community]] before her death, which occurred prior to the main events of the novel. She exists in the story only through the memories of others, remembered primarily for her culinary excellence and her risotto.

She is first mentioned by [[the-padrone|the Padrone]], who recalls her with fond exaggeration: "My Lucrezia, what a cook! What a marvelous risotto!" [011-025]. This extravagant praise is followed by an offer: "Then you send us your Lucrezia, and we will show her zabaione" — a playful challenge that treats her as both a prized possession and a point of communal pride [011-026].

When Lucrezia died, [[fabio-boselli|Fabio Boselli]] sent a wreath — a gesture that speaks to the depth of feeling she inspired in the community [011-027]. [[fabio-boselli|Fabio]] had been kind to her in her lifetime, part of his general benevolence toward "even new arrivals" [024-006]. She is warned by others to "never take the lira," suggesting the financial precariousness in which she lived [024-007].

## Character

Lucrezia is defined by absence — she is a character constructed entirely through recollection. This narrative technique gives her a mythic quality: she is not a person so much as an idea of a person, the archetype of the good cook whose memory outlasts her life. The Padrone's possessive "My Lucrezia" suggests the proprietary affection the community feels toward its memorable figures.

Her risotto functions as a symbol of community identity. It is not merely a dish but a cultural artifact, something that defines "Italianness" for the expatriates who gather around [[the-padrone|the Padrone]]'s table. Lucrezia's risotto is the standard against which all other [[food-and-cuisine|cooking]] is measured.

## Role in the Narrative

Lucrezia serves a dual purpose in the novel's world-building. First, she establishes the Soho community's culture of food as a primary social currency — [[food-and-cuisine|cooking]] is how people express love, assert status, and maintain connection to their homeland. Second, her death and the community's response to it ([[fabio-boselli|Fabio]]'s wreath, the warnings she received) reveal the network of mutual care and concern that binds the neighborhood together.

By making Lucrezia a memory rather than a present character, the novel also establishes the theme of loss that runs through the narrative. The community is defined as much by who is gone as by who remains.
