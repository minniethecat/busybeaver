## Biography

Rocca is the butcher of the Soho [[italian-diaspora|Italian community]], a former soldier who served as a corporal in the Italian army. He keeps a shop in the neighborhood and is known for his loud, commanding voice and his goats, particularly one named [[nerone|Nerone]] [018-012][018-013].

His shop serves as a social hub of the community — people naturally gather there, and it is said that "you could not pass Rocca's, the butcher's" without stopping [021-011]. He is highly regarded by his neighbors: "Rocca was much esteemed for his meat" — a phrase that carries a double meaning, referring both to his professional skill and his character [018-014].

Rocca's relationship with his wife, [[signora-rocca|Signora Rocca]], is marked by affectionate conflict. She is religious and frequently invokes divine judgment, which pricks Rocca's "tardy conscience" [023-003]. Rocca, for his part, swings between irreverence toward priests and genuine religious feeling: he "jeered at priests, but he confessed his sins" [024-023]. When his wife lectures him, he silences her by shouting "Giurabbacco!" — an oath that she, being devout, cannot answer [024-021][024-022].

## Character

Rocca embodies a particular type of Italian masculinity — the ex-soldier whose military bearing never quite left him. He addresses people with a "voice of a corporal" and takes visible pride in his physical goods, demanding admiration for his meat with a characteristic "Belli, eh?" — "Beautiful, aren't they?" [018-015][018-021].

Despite his bluster, Rocca possesses a capacity for tenderness. At one point, [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] observes "something worth loving" in him, a quality that the young man finds surprising and moving [029-006]. In the later chapters, "even Rocca grew poetical," showing that the rhythms of community life can draw out unexpected depths in even the most apparently coarse characters [032-018].

## Role in the Narrative

Rocca appears primarily in the early and middle sections of the novel, serving as a fixed point in the Soho neighborhood's social landscape. His shop is one of the landmarks that define [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s world, and his interactions with his wife provide comic relief while also illustrating the texture of immigrant community life — the blend of old-country [[catholic-faith|Catholicism]] with worldly pragmatism [023-001][024-020].

In the broader thematic arc, Rocca represents the earthy, unreflective vitality that [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] both admires and ultimately cannot share. Rocca's simple pleasures — his goats, his meat, his shouted arguments with his wife — stand in contrast to [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s deepening [[spiritual-crisis|spiritual alienation]].
