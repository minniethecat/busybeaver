## Biography

Milady is a regular patron of the Doric restaurant, known to the staff by her title alone — never by a personal name. She is consistently described as "little and lovely" (135-020) and "greedy," a woman whose appetites, both culinary and romantic, are insatiable.

[[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] routinely reserved her usual table in the corner by the window, arranging flowers by her plate (134-010). The chef [[henri|Henri]] knew her tastes intimately and prepared dishes specifically for her. When [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] called to order a lobster, [[henri|Henri]] teased him: "a nice, fat lobster for your pretty Milady? You are always so fussy, and she is extremely gourmande, she will eat it up shell and all!" (135-019). [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s weary reply reveals both familiarity and judgment: "She is little and lovely — they are always the greediest kind" (135-020). Yet privately, [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] did not select the fat lobster for Milady — he just stood there, making no special effort (135-020).

## The Doria Luncheon

Milady appears most prominently as the dining companion of [[ugo-doria|Ugo Doria]] during his visit to the Doric. She arrived ahead of him at the door, "the little Milady," followed by the white-haired poet removing pale grey gloves (142-001). A man dining nearby had left his chair pulled out because it was close to Milady — or rather, to her elegant presence (142-002).

[[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] greeted her with professional formality: "It is ready — this way please, Milady" (142-005). Throughout the meal, she laughed softly at Doria's conversation while her white hands rested on the table; every few minutes her laugh rippled through the dining room (142-007). She and Doria "ate and drank hugely" while the waiters filled their glasses, and Milady said, still laughing, that she had lost her appetite (142-009).

The narrator's judgment is explicit and damning:

> Milady, who was little and lovely, was as greedy in passion as in food. (142-011)

She lied to Doria about having read his "Gioia della Luce," though the aging poet's face remained "terribly old" as he drank to impress her (142-013). Her attention drifted: while Doria's "lustful, adoring eyes" dwelt on her, Milady's "eyes wandered away at moments to someone who sat near the door" — a younger man whom Doria's gaze "sought the young man's eyes in turns whenever they dwelt on Milady" (142-017, 142-019).

When the meal concluded, the bill was paid with scarcely a glance. Milady "got up, and as she did so, she smiled and nodded at [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]" (142-022).

## Gian-Luca's Assessment

In his private rage after the luncheon, [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] included Milady in his condemnation of Doria and all that the Doric's clientele represented:

> "I know her, the Milady — and all the while your eyes are on her, and your eyes make Gian-Luca go mad with anger." (143-006)

He saw her as emblematic of the Doric's patrons: beautiful, greedy consumers who used the restaurant and its staff as props for their vanities. Later, as [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s mental state deteriorated, he would sometimes forget to reserve her table, causing friction at the restaurant and leading to a list of complaints in which Milady's table being forgotten "twice" featured prominently (160-003, 160-009).

## Later References

After [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s departure from the Doric, Milady became part of his retrospective inventory of what his life had been: the table where [[ugo-doria|Ugo Doria]] had "feasted beside the little Milady" now seemed a long time ago (175-014). In his final days, as he sought [[loneliness-and-alienation|solitude]] in nature, the narrator compares the simplicity of birds to what [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] had once endured: "he had waited upon Milady's caprices" (189-004). Near the end, [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] recalls "its ignoble service — the little Milady, [[jane-coram|Jane Coram]] and the others" as a litany of what he has left behind (196-007).

## Characterization

Milady functions in the novel as a type rather than a fully individuated character — she is "the Milady," never given a proper name, representing a class of wealthy, idle patrons whose appetites the Doric exists to serve. Her pairing with the aging [[ugo-doria|Ugo Doria]] exemplifies the novel's critique of the transaction between aging male desire and youthful female greed, both staged within the theatre of the restaurant. The detail of her eyes wandering to a younger man while Doria's gaze sought the same man's eyes creates a triangulation of desire that exposes the emptiness beneath the restaurant's surface elegance.
