## Overview

Il Padrone is the Italian proprietor of [[the-capo|the Capo]] di Monte restaurant in Dean Street, Soho, where much of the novel's early London action unfolds. A young, ambitious businessman with a volatile personality, he presides over a tightly controlled establishment where his whims dictate the rhythm of daily life. His relationship with his staff — particularly [[mario-boselli|Mario Boselli]] and later [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] — reflects the paternalistic yet exploitative nature of immigrant labour relations in early 20th-century London's Little Italy.

## Biography

The Padrone was, at the time of the novel's opening, "still quite young in years, but already successful enough to have the feelings of pride and importance" (053-002). He owned and operated [[the-capo|the Capo]] di Monte in Dean Street, which he regarded as his personal domain — he "did not encourage visits from his underlings" to the upstairs residential quarters (053-012).

His business practices were marked by a combination of shrewdness and pettiness. [[mario-boselli|Mario Boselli]] noted that "the Padrone was sly" (031-004) and that "the Padrone had sworn to bring all London to [[the-capo|the Capo]] di Monte" (031-005), revealing his grandiose commercial ambitions. He took pride in small refinements, such as his use of French phrases — "'Sapristi!' — the Padrone was proud of his French" (032-005) — and purchased French pasta, prompting [[berta|Berta]]'s remark that "[[mario-boselli|Mario]]'s Padrone is buying French pasta, because it is cheaper" (028-028).

When [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] first presented himself for work at [[the-capo|the Capo]] di Monte, the Padrone was initially suspicious — "'And who may you be?'" (054-006) — but relented and hired him. His management style was imperious and demanding: he once "was called to apologize" after a customer complaint (056-002), and he decided unilaterally to extend the restaurant's hours, causing his staff to "work half the night because the Padrone has decided to serve suppers" (041-008).

The Padrone was married to [[signora-rocca|Signora Rocca]] (055-025), who also lived above the restaurant. His relationship with his employees ranged from gruff authority to moments of grudging accommodation, as when he agreed to hire [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] at [[mario-boselli|Mario]]'s urging: "'Let me ask the Padrone to take him at [[the-capo|the Capo]],' [[mario-boselli|Mario]] said" (045-025).

## Relationships

The Padrone's most significant relationship was with **[[mario-boselli|Mario Boselli]]**, his senior waiter, who navigated the Padrone's moods with a mixture of deference and behind-the-back commentary. [[mario-boselli|Mario]] whispered gossip about the Padrone and observed his "many little ways" (053-009), maintaining a careful balance between loyalty and self-preservation.

With **[[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca Boselli]]**, the Padrone had a distant, transactional relationship. As [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] grew dissatisfied with [[the-capo|the Capo]] di Monte, the Padrone's establishment became a symbol of the limited horizons he sought to transcend. The Padrone's authority was a structural fact of [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s early London life rather than a personal connection.

With **[[signora-rocca|Signora Rocca]]**, his wife, the Padrone shared the domestic and commercial life of [[the-capo|the Capo]] di Monte. She was "married to the Padrone; three facts which she found rather inconvenient at times" (055-025), suggesting the strains of combining marriage and business.

With **[[schmidt|Schmidt]]**, the Swiss waiter, the Padrone maintained a professional but tense relationship. When a customer complained about [[schmidt|Schmidt]]'s temper, the Padrone was compelled to intervene (056-002).

## Significance

The Padrone embodies the self-made Italian immigrant entrepreneur, a figure who has achieved modest success through ambition and control. His character illustrates the precarious position of immigrant business owners in London's ethnic enclaves — simultaneously proud and defensive, aspiring to refinement while resorting to petty economies. [[capo-di-monte|The Capo]] di Monte under his rule functions as a microcosm of Italian immigrant society, where reputation, hierarchy, and personal loyalty govern daily life. His mercurial authority represents the old-world paternalism that characters like [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] eventually outgrow, making the restaurant a launching point for upward mobility rather than a destination.
