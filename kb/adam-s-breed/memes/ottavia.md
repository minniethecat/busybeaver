## Overview

Aunt Ottavia is [[maddalena-boselli|Maddalena Boselli]]'s aunt, a resident of Little Italy in London
who plays a significant role in the domestic life of [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] and [[maddalena-boselli|Maddalena]]
after their marriage. Voluble, worldly, and irrepressibly good-natured, she
embodies the warmth and resilience of the established Italian immigrant community
in London — a foil to the darker, more brooding characters who dominate the
novel's emotional landscape.

## Home and Character

Aunt Ottavia lives in Coldbath Square, in the Italian quarter of London.
[[maddalena-boselli|Maddalena]] describes the area to [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]: "Aunt Ottavia lived in Little Italy,
and here [[maddalena-boselli|Maddalena]] smiled at [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]. 'But it is not little at all,' she said;
'it is large and gloomy and very dirty'" (088-029). Aunt Ottavia runs a
lodging-house, and she is willing to "turn out all her lodgers" (092-001) to
make room for the newly married couple — a gesture that reveals both her
generosity and her pragmatic approach to life.

Her personality is marked by irrepressible volubility. "Aunt Ottavia never
stopped talking; failing an audience she would talk to the cat" (092-001).
She is described as "all blacks and whites like a magpie, and quite as voluble"
(092-001). Yet she is also worldly-wise: "Aunt Ottavia knew life, very
thoroughly she knew it — for the most part it only made her laugh" (092-002).
This laughter, born of experience rather than innocence, distinguishes her from
characters who meet life's trials with anguish or withdrawal.

## Role in Gian-Luca and Maddalena's Marriage

Aunt Ottavia becomes an early supporter of [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] and [[maddalena-boselli|Maddalena]]'s
relationship. [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] expresses a desire to meet her: "I would like to know
Aunt Ottavia, for I want you and me to be friends" (088-032). She in turn
visits [[teresa-boselli|Teresa Boselli]] and is "properly impressed" (089-001) by the Boselli home,
suggesting her role as a social bridge between the two families.

When the couple marries, [[maddalena-boselli|Maddalena]] would have preferred to live with Aunt
Ottavia (092-001), but [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] decides against it after closer acquaintance
with Coldbath Square, finding it "anything but clean, in spite of its hopeful
name" (092-001). His rejection of Ottavia's world — the dense, communal,
slightly shabby life of Little Italy — foreshadows his lifelong inability to
settle comfortably into any community.

## The Wedding

Aunt Ottavia attends [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] and [[maddalena-boselli|Maddalena]]'s wedding celebration. At the
feast she is "very merry, as she always was with men," asking [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] to
dance with her (094-001). She is among the many guests — including [[the-padrone|the Padrone]],
[[gemma|the Padrona]], [[francesco-millo|Millo]], old [[nerone|Nerone]], [[rocca|Rocca]], and others — who fill the Boselli home
for the occasion (093-002). Her presence at the wedding anchors the union in
the wider [[italian-diaspora|Italian community]], lending it a legitimacy and warmth that the
novel's more solitary moments lack.

## Religious Life

Aunt Ottavia is Roman Catholic, and her confessor is [[father-antonio|Father Antonio]] (091-001).
[[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]], preparing for his marriage, must "talk with [[father-antonio|old Father Antonio]],
Aunt Ottavia's confessor" (091-001), suggesting her role in facilitating the
religious formalities of the wedding. Her [[catholic-faith|Catholicism]] is presented as a natural,
unquestioned part of her identity — another expression of her rootedness in a
community and tradition that [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]], for all his searching, can never fully
claim.
