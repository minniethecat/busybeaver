## Description

Modane is a French town in the Savoie region, situated near the Italian frontier in the Alps. In the novel, it functions as the border crossing where trains stop for customs inspection and passengers transition between the French and Italian rail systems. The narrator captures the scene at Modane with vivid sensory detail: "noise and confusion," "shouting officials running backwards and forwards," "dignified English folk talking Italian learnt from inadequate handbooks" (173-011).

The platform at Modane becomes a theatre of cultural transition. Passengers snatch hurried refreshments — "a hasty sandwich or an orange, or an apple, or a bun — not a fat currant bun, but its thin Latin cousin containing no currants at all" (173-011). The very food marks the passage from one world to another: the Anglo-Saxon bun gives way to its thinner, plainer Mediterranean counterpart.

> The train had jolted itself into Modane, and all was noise and confusion. Shouting officials running backwards and forwards, dignified English folk talking Italian learnt from inadequate handbooks. There were people snatching a hasty sandwich or an orange, or an apple, or a bun — not a fat currant bun, but its thin Latin cousin containing no currants at all. Presently the train was ready to start: "*In vagone! In vagone! Partenza!*" (173-011)

## Historical Context

In the early twentieth century, Modane was a significant railway junction on the Mont Cenis route connecting Paris to Turin and Rome. The town's position at the entrance to the Fréjus Rail Tunnel made it a natural stopping point for international trains. During the 1920s, the period of [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s journey, crossing at Modane represented the physical and psychological threshold between northern and southern Europe — precisely the transition the novel dramatizes.

## Narrative Role

### The Threshold Moment

Modane's narrative function is as a symbolic threshold: the place where [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] and [[maddalena-boselli|Maddalena]] leave the English world behind and enter the Italian one. As the train approached the frontier, [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] looked at his wife and saw that "the tears were rolling down her cheeks — she, who so seldom wept" (173-002). [[maddalena-boselli|Maddalena]]'s tears, rare and therefore significant, mark the emotional cost of their departure. [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] responded with uncharacteristic tenderness, taking her hand and stroking it with his fingers.

> When they came near the frontier at Modane the next day Gian-Luca looked at his wife; Maddalena was gazing out of the window and he saw that the tears were rolling down her cheeks — she, who so seldom wept. Then he took her hand and stroked it with his fingers, while the other passengers pretended to read — they were all English people who were fond of Italians, and they thought this a quarrel between lovers. (173-002)

The English passengers' polite misreading of the scene — they "thought this a quarrel between lovers" — adds a layer of quiet comedy to an otherwise poignant moment. It also underscores the cultural distance that will only widen as the train carries the couple further south.

### Sensory Transformation

Modane is the first point at which the novel's sensory register shifts decisively from English to Italian. The cries of "*In vagone! In vagone! Partenza!*" (173-011) replace the polite murmurs of the English dining room; the thin Latin bun replaces the fat English one; and the disciplined order of the London restaurant gives way to the exuberant chaos of the Italian railway platform. This sensory transformation prefigures [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s own internal journey — his gradual shedding of the English self he has constructed and his return to something more elemental.

## Thematic Significance

Modane embodies the novel's preoccupation with borders and thresholds — not only geographical but psychological and spiritual. [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] has spent his adult life crossing borders: from Italy to England, from boyhood to adulthood, from waiter to manager, from [[catholic-faith|Catholicism]] to spiritual emptiness. Modane is the last border he will cross, and the journey it initiates will lead not to a new life but to his final renunciation and death. The "noise and confusion" of the station, the tears of his wife, the inadequacy of the phrasebooks — all suggest that the crossing is not merely geographical but existential.
