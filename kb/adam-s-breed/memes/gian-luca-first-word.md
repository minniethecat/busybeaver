## Summary

In Book I, Chapter II, the infant [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] speaks his first word: "Dog" [010-006], to the consternation of his family. [[rosa-varese|Rosa]] tries to correct him — "Doggie!" [010-007] — but [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] repeats "Dog" firmly [010-008]. [[fabio-boselli|Fabio]] reflects that it is a "marvel" that [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] spoke at all [011-001], but [[mario-boselli|Mario]] is troubled that the word was English rather than Italian: "That his first word should be 'doggie' instead of 'Mamma'" [010-009]. The incident is treated by the characters as an early omen of [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s difference — his divergence from the expected path of an Italian immigrant child.

## Detailed Account

### The Word

The scene opens with the infant [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] in the family home above the shop. He fixes his attention on something and says "Dog!" [010-006] — an English word, not the Italian "cane" nor the expected "Mamma." [[rosa-varese|Rosa]], speaking as one would to a child, corrects him: "Doggie!" [010-007], but [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] insists: "Dog," repeated firmly [010-008]. The firmness of the repetition suggests a will already forming — the stubbornness that will characterise his adult spiritual crisis.

### Family Reactions

[[fabio-boselli|Fabio Boselli]] is primarily amazed that his son has spoken at all, regarding it as a "marvel" [011-001]. His response is paternal wonder rather than cultural anxiety. [[mario-boselli|Mario]], however, reacts differently upon hearing the news that evening: "That his first word should be 'doggie' instead of 'Mamma'" [010-009]. [[mario-boselli|Mario]]'s concern registers the symbolic weight of the event — an Italian child's first word being English signals a rupture in the transmission of immigrant identity, a foretaste of the assimilation that will shape [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s life.

### Omen and Interpretation

The narrative frames the incident as more than a developmental milestone. [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s choice of "Dog" — an animal, not a person; English, not Italian — prefigures his lifelong tension between inherited identity and adopted culture. The firmness of his repetition ("Dog," not "Doggie") also hints at a personality that resists correction, that insists on its own terms — traits that will define his adult struggles.
