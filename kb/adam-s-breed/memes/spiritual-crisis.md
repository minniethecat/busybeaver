## Definition

Spiritual crisis in *Adam's Breed* is the condition of radical disconnection — from faith, from community, from family, and ultimately from the self. [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s trajectory through the novel traces a progressive stripping away of all the sources of meaning available to him: the [[catholic-faith|Catholicism]] of his grandmother [[rosa-varese|Rosa]], the commercial ambition of his uncle [[mario-boselli|Mario]], the romantic love of [[maddalena-boselli|Maddalena]], and finally the sensual pleasures of food and companionship.

## Manifestation

The novel opens with a scene of existential weight: [[teresa-boselli|Teresa]]'s "whole being, soul, heart and brain" is concentrated on the suffering of the dying [[olga-boselli|Olga]], while the oleograph of the Sacred Heart looks down from the wall [003-002]. This juxtaposition of human anguish with religious iconography establishes the central question: can faith answer suffering? For [[teresa-boselli|Teresa]], the answer is no — after [[olga-death-and-gian-luca-birth|Olga's death]], she declares "I have done with [[catholic-faith|the Church]]" [008-008].

[[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] inherits this question but not its resolution. Unlike [[teresa-boselli|Teresa]], who makes a decisive break, [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] drifts. He attends Mass with [[rosa-varese|Rosa]] as a child but feels no connection. He works at [[the-doric|The Doric]] with professional competence but no ambition. He accepts [[maddalena-boselli|Maddalena]]'s love but cannot return it fully. His condition is not atheism — it is something more unsettling: a pervasive inability to care about the things that are supposed to matter.

## The Failure of Substitutes

Each domain of life that the novel offers as a potential source of meaning is tried and found wanting. [[catholic-faith|Catholicism]] provides ritual without transcendence — [[rocca|Rocca]] jeers at priests while confessing his sins, a posture of ironic semi-belief that [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] cannot even manage [024-023]. The restaurant trade offers success — [[mario-boselli|Mario]]'s ambition to "bring all London" to [[the-capo|the Capo]] di Monte — but [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] sees only the machinery of appetite [031-005]. Romantic love, in the figure of [[maddalena-boselli|Maddalena]], offers connection, but [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] experiences it as another form of obligation.

The novel's genius is to present this spiritual crisis without naming it as such. [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] does not have theological conversations; he simply finds himself unable to eat, unable to love, unable to stay. His crisis is embodied rather than intellectualized.
