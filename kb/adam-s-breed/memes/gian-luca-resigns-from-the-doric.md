## Summary

[[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca Boselli]], after months of declining mental health and a period of medical leave, returns to the Doric restaurant and formally resigns his position as headwaiter. His resignation is received with regret and understanding by [[franco-millo|Franco Millo]], and with sorrow by the staff. The event marks the definitive end of [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s identity as a waiter — the role that had defined him since childhood — and the beginning of his search for meaning beyond service.

## Detailed Account

### Prelude: The Doctor's Orders

Before the resignation, [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s deteriorating performance at the Doric had become impossible to ignore. His orders grew vague, his voice uncertain, and he repeatedly forgot reservations — including those of the [[milady|Milady]] (160-001, 160-003). When [[francesco-millo|Millo]] confronted him, [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] confessed to "a kind of great sadness" that had come over him (160-013). [[francesco-millo|Millo]], recognizing the seriousness of [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s condition, arranged for him to see a doctor and took him off the roster, assuring him that [[daniele|Daniele]] could manage in his absence (160-014).

### The Resignation

On his return, [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] went directly to [[francesco-millo|Millo]] and tendered his resignation "very simply, giving no excuse, for indeed he had none to offer":

> "I cannot come back to the Doric," he told Millo. "I cannot any longer be a waiter — all I can do is to thank you from my heart for your very great kindness and patience." (175-001)

[[francesco-millo|Millo]], who had "long since ceased to be surprised at any queer happening in this queerest of worlds," accepted the resignation with characteristic pragmatism (175-002). When [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] attempted to return four months' wages that had been paid as a retaining fee, [[francesco-millo|Millo]] refused with impatience: "Do not be such an imbecile, [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]! You can give the money to [[maddalena-boselli|Maddalena]]" (175-003).

[[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s explanation was existential rather than professional:

> "I must try to find myself first, signore — I am utterly lost — I must find myself again — and something else that I need." (175-006)

### Millo's Parting Wisdom

Though losing his finest headwaiter, [[francesco-millo|Millo]] offered [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] a parting reflection that crystallizes the novel's worldview:

> "Remember that the world is a very greedy place — it is only an extension of the Doric. To keep pace with the world one must wink at its follies and if necessary pander a little; there is no room for those who want to dig beneath the surface." (175-008)

This statement links the microcosm of the restaurant to the macrocosm of modern civilization — a connection [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] himself had already made and would continue to explore in his subsequent life.

### Farewell to the Staff

After leaving [[francesco-millo|Millo]]'s office, [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] went to the restaurant floor where the morning preparations were underway. He found [[roberto|Roberto]], [[giovanni|Giovanni]], [[daniele|Daniele]], and the other waiters and told them simply:

> "I am leaving the Doric, I have come here to say goodbye." (175-012)

Their incredulous response — "No, no, it cannot be possible!" (175-013) — revealed that "his faults had been forgotten, and only his virtues remembered" (175-014). [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] realized he was genuinely fond of these men, "these patient, uncomplaining servers," and let his gaze rest on each of them as though memorizing their faces (175-014).

[[roberto|Roberto]], the wine-waiter whose eyes were like "a skylark looking through bars," spoke for them all: "We shall miss you, signore, it will not be the same here without you" (175-015).

### The Doric as Monster

[[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s final departure from the restaurant building is rendered in the novel's most potent beast imagery:

> As he went down the tortuous steel-rimmed staircase, he was met by a blast of hot air, for the great, greedy monster was stirring to action, and its breath was already heavy with the food that fumed on its tables of fire. (175-020)

[[the-doric|The Doric]], personified as a living creature, continues its endless feeding even as its most devoted servant walks away.

### Aftermath

News of [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s departure caused "consternation in [[old-compton-street|Old Compton Street]] when the clan learnt that [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] had left the Doric" (179-001). Even [[nerone|Nerone]]'s firm faith in him was shaken. The resignation severed [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] not only from his profession but from the Italian immigrant community's understanding of who he was — a successful, respected headwaiter who had risen through merit.

## Significance

This event represents the culmination of [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s gradual [[loneliness-and-alienation|alienation]] from the world of the Doric. It is not merely a career change but an existential break — the moment when a man who has been defined entirely by his work decides that he can no longer perform that work, even though he cannot articulate what he will do instead. The resignation prefigures his retreat to Italy and ultimately to the forest, completing the arc from civilized servant to natural being.
