## Description

The Octagon Room was one of three dining areas at the Doric restaurant in London. The narrator describes "three verdant pastures for browsing at the Doric; the grill room which did not concern [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]], the large restaurant with its excellent band, and an octagon room which led off the restaurant and which had recently become [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s province" (084-001). True to its name, the room was an octagonal space, smaller and more intimate than the main restaurant.

## Clientele

The Octagon Room attracted a specific type of diner. According to the narrator, it drew "those of [[francesco-millo|Millo]]'s clients who preferred to be far from the music, either because they wished to talk business, or because they were lovers and therefore spoke softly, or because — and this was the most frequent reason — they were people who appreciated food" (084-001). The room thus served as the Doric's connoisseur's corner, where serious diners could focus on cuisine without the distraction of the band.

## Gian-Luca's Domain

The Octagon Room became closely identified with [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca Boselli]] after [[francesco-millo|Millo]] assigned it to him. [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] took great pride in his management of the room, developing what the narrator calls "my special clients" and learning "their little fads" (175-011). He approached his work with military precision:

> Like a general and his chief of staff, he and Roberto would hurry their waiters to the kitchens and cellars, to those vast armories that contained all the weapons wherewith to slay lassitude and boredom. Back they would come, the neat, black-and-white army, very well equipped to slay lassitude and boredom. (101-016)

[[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] would watch his diners with "experienced eyes," monitoring "for a self-controlled face to relax," listening "for the subtle change in a voice, in a laugh." He had developed an instinct for "just how long a time should elapse between the popping of a cork and the coming of the miracle" (101-016). When all went well, he felt "a glow of satisfaction" and "a passing affection for his diners, who were doing him credit, and via him the Doric" — even though he knew they "looked upon him as a machine" (101-016).

## Notable Events

The Octagon Room was the setting for several significant moments in [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s career:

**Doria's Visit**: When the celebrated poet [[ugo-doria|Ugo Doria]] announced he would dine at the Doric, [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] arranged for Doria's table to be placed "in the octagon room, in a quiet corner by the window," with "a red carnation made up as a buttonhole" at each plate (140-002). This event represented the height of [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s professional pride, though the evening ended in disappointment when Doria failed to appear at the appointed hour.

**[[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s Resignation**: When [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] finally decided to leave the Doric, he approached [[francesco-millo|Millo]] with the question that revealed how deeply he identified with his work: "And the octagon room, signore? Who will take charge of my octagon room? It is very important, I have my special clients, I am used to their little fads" (114-005).

> He said: "And the octagon room, signore? Who will take charge of my octagon room? It is very important, I have my special clients, I am used to their little fads." (114-005)

## Significance

The Octagon Room represents [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s professional identity at its fullest expression. It was the space where his craft — the art of service — was practiced at its highest level, and where his investment in his work was most personal. His possessive reference to "my octagon room" (114-005) reveals that the room had become an extension of his sense of self, making his eventual departure from it all the more poignant.
