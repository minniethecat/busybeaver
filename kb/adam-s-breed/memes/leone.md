## Overview

Leone is the 16-year-old son of [[sisto|Sisto]], the bailiff of [[villa-sabelli|Villa Sabelli]], and his wife [[lidia|Lidia]]. He appears in the [[villa-sabelli|Villa Sabelli]] chapters (Book III) as a brutish, casually cruel adolescent whose tormenting of a donkey provokes [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] to a rare act of physical violence. Leone embodies the harsh, unsympathetic side of rural Italian life that contrasts with [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s urban London upbringing.

## Biography

### Life at Villa Sabelli

Leone is introduced when [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]], now a young man, visits the [[villa-sabelli|Villa Sabelli]] estate in Italy. He is "a lumping lad of sixteen" with a "bovine" expression (165-003). His mother [[lidia|Lidia]] dotes on him, and he assists his father [[sisto|Sisto]] with tasks around the estate, such as operating the olive scales (165-022).

Leone quickly becomes a source of irritation to [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]. The narrative notes that certain traits were "more irritating still in Leone" than in others (168-003). He is made to serve Mass, and the text suggests he emulates his father's prideful spirit: "Leone would quickly emulate his father" (169-006).

### The Donkey Incident

Leone's defining act in the novel is his tormenting of a donkey: "The creature Leone loved best to torment was a donkey" (170-001). His cruelty to the animal — a persistent, casual brutality — builds [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s resentment until it reaches a breaking point. "[[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s resentment towards Leone had got well-nigh beyond his control" (172-001).

[[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] erupts in sudden violence, thrashing Leone so quickly that "Leone knew nothing until he was swung" (172-003). Leone's response is abject wailing and blubbering, and his mother [[lidia|Lidia]] rushes to defend him, shrieking: "To beat my Leone because of a donkey!" (172-008). The incident reveals the deep cultural and temperamental divide between [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s sensibilities and the rural Italian household.

## Relationships

- **[[sisto]]** — His father, the bailiff of [[villa-sabelli|Villa Sabelli]], whom Leone emulates in manner and pride (169-006).
- **[[lidia|Lidia]]** — His mother, who fiercely defends him after [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s beating (172-008).
- **[[gian-luca-boselli]]** — His antagonist; [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s resentment of Leone's animal cruelty culminates in a violent beating (172-001).
- **[[maddalena-boselli]]** — His aunt by marriage, present at [[villa-sabelli|Villa Sabelli]] during the incident.

## Significance

Leone functions as a foil to [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]. Where [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] has been shaped by London's streets and the cosmopolitan world of [[the-doric|The Doric]] restaurant, Leone represents an unreflective, rural brutishness. [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s violent reaction to Leone's cruelty — defending a helpless animal — marks one of the few moments of unambiguous moral action in the novel, and it costs him: the incident deepens the estrangement between him and the Italian relatives, reinforcing his [[loneliness-and-alienation|alienation]] from his ancestral culture.
