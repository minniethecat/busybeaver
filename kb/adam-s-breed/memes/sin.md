## Definition

In Radclyffe Hall's *Adam's Breed*, sin operates not merely as a religious concept but as a generational inheritance—a psychological burden passed from grandmother to father to son. Within the devoutly Catholic world of the Boselli household in [[old-compton-street|Soho]], sin is understood through the traditional framework of confession, penance, and absolution, yet the novel consistently questions whether formal religious observance can truly resolve the moral entanglement of human desire. The Italian term *peccato* carries the weight of both transgression and the inescapable condition of fallen humanity that the novel's characters navigate daily.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### Teresa Boselli's Original Sin

The concept of sin enters the novel through [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s grandmother [[teresa-boselli|Teresa]], whose single passionate transgression—"the one hot sin of her youth" (003-005)—defines her subsequent life of penitential devotion. [[teresa-boselli|Teresa]] "had passionately sinned, and had forever after hated sin" (003-019), establishing a pattern of reaction-formation that echoes through the family line. Her sin, implied to be of a sexual nature, is never specified in detail, yet its shadow falls across three generations.

[[teresa-boselli|Teresa]]'s response to her own transgression takes the form of intensified religious observance: "she dropped to her knees, pressing the rosary against her forehead" (003-023), seeking through ritual the absolution that her conscience cannot grant itself. This pattern—transgression followed by ritualized guilt—becomes the Boselli family's emotional template.

### Olga and Maternal Culpability

[[teresa-boselli|Teresa]]'s moral framework extends to her judgment of others, most powerfully in her response to [[olga-death-and-gian-luca-birth|Olga's death]] in childbirth. In a moment of startling theological defiance, [[teresa-boselli|Teresa]] challenges divine justice: "Why should her sin be greater than mine? You who know all things, answer!" (003-024). This cry reveals the inadequacy of the sin/absolution framework when confronted with the arbitrary suffering of the innocent. [[olga-boselli|Olga]], who dies bringing [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] into the world, becomes in [[teresa-boselli|Teresa]]'s reckoning a victim of a cosmic injustice that [[catholic-faith|the Church]]'s categories cannot accommodate.

### Sins of the Flesh and Male Frailty

The novel extends its treatment of sin to the male characters through [[fabio-boselli|Fabio]], [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s father, who is "occasionally weak in regard to the sins of the flesh" (024-004) yet remains a dutiful Catholic. Unlike [[teresa-boselli|Teresa]]'s consuming guilt, [[fabio-boselli|Fabio]]'s relationship with sin is characterized by a pragmatic acceptance of human frailty—a gendered asymmetry in how the novel portrays moral consciousness.

### Gian-Luca's Inherited Guilt

[[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] inherits not specific sins but the *structure* of guilt itself. His adult spiritual crisis, which reaches its climax in Book III, represents a secularized version of [[teresa-boselli|Teresa]]'s religious torment. The [[loneliness-and-alienation|alienation]] that defines his later life is in part the product of a moral sensibility formed in a household where every action was weighed in the balance of sin and absolution.

## Key Passages

> "Remembering the one hot sin of her youth—'Ora pro nobis, ora pro nobis'" (003-005)
> — The novel's first mention of sin, linking Teresa's private transgression to the liturgical plea for intercession. The Latin prayer *ora pro nobis* (pray for us) frames sin as a condition requiring external mediation.

> "Teresa Boselli had passionately sinned, and had forever after hated sin" (003-019)
> — The novel's most concise statement of the psychology of reaction-formation. Teresa's hatred of sin is proportional to her experience of it, establishing a pattern of moral absolutism born of personal failure.

> "Why should her sin be greater than mine? You who know all things, answer!" (003-024)
> — Teresa's challenge to God marks the limit of the sin framework. Confronted with Olga's death, the neat calculus of transgression and punishment collapses into anguished incomprehension.

> "Occasionally weak in regard to the sins of the flesh, he was nevertheless a devout Catholic" (024-004)
> — Fabio's characterization illustrates the novel's recognition that religious identity and moral fallibility coexist, and that the Church's framework accommodates this tension through the sacrament of confession.
