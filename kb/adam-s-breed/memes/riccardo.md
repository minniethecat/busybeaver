## Biography

Riccardo is a headwaiter at the large restaurant, a colleague of [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s, introduced as "the head of the large restaurant" — a phrasing that suggests his professional seniority [088-001]. He is still young enough for military service, and when the First World War breaks out, he is among those called up by Italy.

Riccardo is passionately patriotic. When news of the war reaches the restaurant, his eyes are "unusually bright" and he exclaims, "If only our Italy comes in!" — a fervent wish that the nation would join the conflict [104-003][104-004]. He is angered by [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s silence in the face of this enthusiasm, demanding, "Do you not feel for our country?" [104-005].

He departs for the front in ten days' time after receiving his call-up papers [114-015]. In March, news arrives that Riccardo has been killed [116-005]. His death is framed not as a tragedy but as a fulfillment of a certain logic — he died for his country, which is what he had wanted. [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] reflects on this with characteristic detachment.

## Character

Riccardo represents the uncomplicated patriot — a man whose identity is so thoroughly tied to his nation that he cannot conceive of someone feeling otherwise. His smile, described as "rather unpleasant" during his exchange with [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] about the latter's potential employment at the large restaurant, reveals a petty, competitive streak beneath the grand patriotic rhetoric [114-016].

[[mario-boselli|Mario Boselli]] exploits this rivalry, promising [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] Riccardo's position even before the man has departed: "of course, to which I am to add Riccardo's" [114-011]. The restaurant world is depicted as a place where one man's death is another man's career opportunity.

## Role in the Narrative

Riccardo serves as a foil to [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]. Where Riccardo is passionate about national identity, [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] is indifferent. Where Riccardo eagerly goes to war, [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] stays behind to take his job. Riccardo's death validates his patriotism — he dies as he lived, committed to his country — but the novel treats this commitment with irony, noting that [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] felt "no particular pity" for him [116-006].

In the novel's moral economy, Riccardo's fate illustrates the cost of unexamined enthusiasm. He is a good soldier, a good patriot, a good headwaiter — and he is dead. The novel asks, through [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s eyes, whether such goodness is enough.
