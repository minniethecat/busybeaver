# War and Immigrant Communities

World War I casts a long shadow over the Italian immigrant community in *Adam's Breed*. The war tests loyalties, takes lives, and deepens the **identity fracture** experienced by Italians living in London — men who must choose between fighting for their adopted country or returning to serve Italy.

## The Soldier Heritage

Military service is woven into the fabric of Italian masculinity in the novel. The men of the community are defined, in part, by their soldiering — a legacy that connects them to the old country even as they build new lives in London. [[rocca|Rocca]], the butcher who fought in an earlier war, embodies this martial heritage:

> Rocca had been a soldier and now he was a butcher—he was pleasantly familiar with death; and as the Church, to Rocca, stood more for death than life, he was pleasantly familiar with the Church. [023-019]

[[rocca|Rocca]]'s easy relationship with death — forged in war and reinforced by his trade — represents a generation of Italian men for whom soldiering was a rite of passage. His military titles, used playfully with neighbors, signal that the war identity never fully fades:

> "*Buon giorno, Capitano!*" Rocca had been a good soldier in his day, and now he used military titles for fun. [018-018]

## Loss and Sacrifice

The war extracts a human toll from the immigrant community. The death of [[geppe|Geppe]], a family friend, in combat represents the ultimate sacrifice demanded of these transplanted Italians:

> Geppe who died that Italy might live. [106-032]

[[geppe|Geppe]]'s death — dying for Italy while living in England — crystallizes the **dual allegiance** that defines the immigrant experience. His sacrifice is claimed by the Italian nation, yet mourned within the London [[italian-diaspora|Italian community]], creating an ambiguous grief that has no clear national home.

## Gian-Luca's Father: The Soldier Myth

For [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]], the absent father is constructed as a military hero — a narrative that shapes his understanding of Italian manhood:

> "Your father was also an Italian. Your father was a very great man, he was a soldier; he also owned vineyards, enormous vineyards, from which comes the finest Chianti." [028-032]

This idealized portrait — soldier, landowner, Italian — becomes the **impossible standard** against which [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] measures himself. The father-as-soldier myth links military valor to national identity in ways that complicate [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s own relationship with both Italy and England.

## Identity Under Pressure

War sharpens the question that haunts the immigrant community: to whom do we belong? The Boselli family's naturalization as British subjects coexists uneasily with their Italian loyalties:

> Fabio had already been a naturalized Englishman at the time of his meeting with Teresa in Florence. [003-010]

> He hated the English as Nerone hates them; they are stupid, they have the brains of pigs. [028-030]

This split — legal Britishness versus emotional Italianness — becomes acute during wartime, when nations demand unambiguous loyalty from their citizens.
