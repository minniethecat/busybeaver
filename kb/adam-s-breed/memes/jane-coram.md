## Overview

Jane Coram is a celebrated young socialite in 1920s London, famous enough that other restaurant patrons boast of having seen her: "I saw Jane Coram at luncheon" (146-002). A regular client at [[the-doric|The Doric]] where [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] works as headwaiter, she embodies the glittering facade of London's demi-monde — and its hidden despair. Through [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s observant eyes, the novel reveals the alcoholism and [[loneliness-and-alienation|loneliness]] beneath her public performance.

## Biography

### Public Fame

Jane Coram is introduced as a figure of public fascination, part of the fashionable crowd at [[the-doric|The Doric]]. The other clients, "who could go home and say: 'I saw Jane Coram at luncheon'" (146-002), treat her presence as a social trophy. She arrives with her "satellites" — a court of followers who surround the famous young woman (146-001).

She is "still very young, the famous Jane Coram, and on rare occasions quite sober" (146-002). The qualifier "on rare occasions" is devastating in its understatement, immediately establishing the alcoholism that defines her private life.

### Gian-Luca's Perception

[[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]], in his role as headwaiter, becomes an unusually perceptive observer of Jane Coram. He sees past the public performance to "the thing that cowered behind" — a vision he describes as a "pitiless vision" of a woman with "the body of an athlete" whose inner life is in ruins (157-012). He wants to share this insight, "to tell her about Jane Coram," but the observation remains his private burden (158-004).

His concern for her becomes a recurring preoccupation: "he was worrying over Jane Coram or some other unfortunate creature" (160-003). Her brandy consumption is noted with clinical precision — "Jane Coram's brandy — moderation in all things" (160-009), the irony cutting through the narrative voice.

### The Empty Table

In the novel's final section, Jane Coram's table at [[the-doric|The Doric]] is described as having been "decked" with something — a gesture that marks her absence (175-014). The image of the decorated but empty table becomes a symbol of her fate. [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] imagines "consoling her solitary spirit" (196-017), but the novel offers no resolution to her story — only the haunting image of a woman whose public fame could not fill the private void.

## Relationships

- **[[gian-luca-boselli]]** — The headwaiter who serves her at [[the-doric|The Doric]] and becomes an acutely perceptive observer of her hidden suffering. His concern for her is one of the few instances of genuine empathy in his professional life.
- **[[the-doric|The Doric]] clientele** — Her "satellites" and fellow patrons who treat her presence as social currency without seeing her private despair.

## Significance

Jane Coram serves as one of the novel's most poignant embodiments of its central themes: the gap between public persona and private self, the [[loneliness-and-alienation|alienation]] of modern urban life, and the hidden costs of fame. She is a mirror image of [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] — both are performers in the theatre of [[the-doric|The Doric]], both conceal their true selves behind professional facades, and both are ultimately alone. Her alcoholism and despair represent the dark underbelly of the glamorous London world that the novel so precisely depicts, making her a crucial figure in the novel's moral landscape.
