## Overview

Berta is the toddler daughter of [[rosa-varese]] and [[mario-boselli]], and one of the young children in the crowded Boselli household. A "cross and fat and ugly" baby in the eyes of the young [[gian-luca-boselli]], she is also the object of her mother [[rosa-varese|Rosa]]'s fierce adoration—"*Bella, la mia Berta!*"—and of her father [[mario-boselli|Mario]]'s devoted affection. Her presence in the narrative highlights the theme of parental love as an unreflective "primitive instinct" that [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]], the motherless child, can only observe from outside.

## Biography

### Early Childhood

Berta entered [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s world as an interloper. "[[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] thought that Berta was cross and fat and ugly, and in any case he was rather jealous of her, she took up too much of [[rosa-varese|Rosa]]'s time" [017-013]. Her arrival disrupted the household's rhythms and redirected [[rosa-varese|Rosa]]'s attention away from her foster-child, intensifying [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s sense of displacement.

### A Child of the Household

Despite [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s resentment, Berta was deeply loved by both her parents. [[rosa-varese|Rosa]] would "exclaim in admiration: '*Bella, la mia Berta!*'" [017-012], while [[mario-boselli|Mario]] showed "devotion to Berta and [[geppe|Geppe]]" that [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] recognized as part of the natural order of family feeling—"the love of children was a primitive instinct like that of eating and drinking" [014-009].

### The War Years

As war approached, Berta, like all the Boselli children, was absorbed into the anxious rhythms of the household. The [[signora-rocca|Signora Rocca]], childless herself, "said very many prayers; it was May, the month of Our Lady" [032-019]—prayers that encompassed the children of the community as the shadow of war lengthened.

## Relationships

### Gian-Luca Boselli
[[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s feelings toward Berta were complicated. He "did not like either of them very much"—Berta and her brother [[geppe|Geppe]]—"and yet they had [[mario-boselli|Mario]] for their father, and [[mario-boselli|Mario]] loved them—that was so strange, for he, [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] went unloved" [014-009]. Berta thus becomes a mirror in which [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] sees his own emotional deprivation reflected.

### Rosa and Mario
Berta was the center of her parents' world. [[rosa-varese|Rosa]]'s exclamation "*Bella, la mia Berta!*" and [[mario-boselli|Mario]]'s "infinite, long-suffering patience" with his children represented the novel's ideal of uncomplicated familial devotion—a love [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] could observe and analyze but never quite feel.

## Significance

As a minor character, Berta's narrative function is symbolic rather than dramatic. She represents the next generation of the Boselli family—the Italian-British children who will inherit the world their grandparents and parents built in Soho. Her "howls, rages, and insatiable greed" [014-009] are the sounds of life itself, the noisy, demanding vitality that the novel's protagonist, increasingly hollowed out by [[loneliness-and-alienation|alienation]], can no longer access.
