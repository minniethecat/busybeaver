## Overview

Mario Boselli (later Mario Varese) is [[rosa-varese|Rosa]]'s husband and a waiter at [[the-capo|the Capo]] di Monte restaurant in Soho. A good-hearted, occasionally flawed man of thirty-three with a receding hairline and a perpetual bunion, Mario functions as a stepfather figure to [[gian-luca-boselli]] after the death of [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s mother [[olga-boselli|Olga]]. His earthy warmth, long-suffering patience, and capacity for joy—expressed in laughter at "the sins of his small man-child"—provide one of the few sources of genuine parental affection in [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s childhood.

## Biography

### Marriage and Family

Mario married [[rosa-varese|Rosa Varese]], the daughter of [[nerone]] the tobacconist, and gave her his name in marriage—a contrast to [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s unknown father, who "did not marry [[olga-boselli|Olga]] as Mario married [[rosa-varese|Rosa]]" and "did not wish to give you his name" [024-004]. Together they had two children, [[berta|Berta]] and [[geppe|Geppe]], whose howls and rages filled the household, yet Mario loved them with the "primitive instinct" of a devoted father [014-009].

### Character and Temperament

Mario was "occasionally weak in regard to the sins of the flesh"—at one point making [[rosa-varese|Rosa]] jealous with a barmaid—but was "nevertheless a good son of Holy Church, attending Mass with [[rosa-varese|Rosa]] every Sunday morning" and accompanying her to Confession every Easter for a "spiritual spring-cleaning" [024-004]. He suffered chronically from a bunion that made his work as a waiter difficult; [[rosa-varese|Rosa]] lamented that "he cannot move quickly enough, and that is bad for a waiter" [023-004]. Despite these troubles, Mario possessed "infinite, long-suffering patience" and a quality of joy that made him "laugh sometimes at the sins of his small man-child" [014-009].

### Professional Life

At thirty-three, Mario had broadened out and his dress-suit, once loose, was now rather tight. His cheeks were "redder than ever, and his eyes... now resembled two brown balls of toffee" [101-013]. He was "very much the man of affairs when reposing in the bosom of his family" and would expound at length on the art of dealing with clients: "You assert yourself—but with grace... You expatiate on the food" [101-015].

## Relationships

### Gian-Luca Boselli
Mario held "a good-hearted, pitiful affection for [[rosa-varese|Rosa]]'s foster-child" [[gian-luca-boselli]] [101-013]. Unlike others in the household, "there was someone who always wanted [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]], and that was Mario Varese" [101-013]. His laughter at childish misbehavior stood in contrast to [[fabio-boselli|Fabio]]'s joyless patience.

### Rosa Varese
Mario's relationship with [[rosa-varese]] was warm but occasionally strained. Their reconciliations were marked by "many soft murmurings and kissings" [010-005], and [[rosa-varese|Rosa]] spoke of him with fond exasperation: "he is always so patient and kind, and moreover he suffers with his bunion" [023-004].

### Nerone
Through his marriage to [[rosa-varese|Rosa]], Mario became the son-in-law of [[nerone]] the tobacconist. [[rosa-varese|Rosa]] had "showed Mario to [[nerone|Nerone]]... a long time before they got married," suggesting a courtship conducted with the old man's approval [014-009].

## Significance

Mario embodies the novel's theme of ordinary, flawed humanity as a counterweight to existential suffering. His bunion—a comically mundane affliction—and his occasional moral weakness make him one of Hall's most warmly drawn minor characters. In a narrative marked by [[loneliness-and-alienation|alienation]] and spiritual crisis, Mario's "infinite, long-suffering patience" and his capacity to laugh at a child's small sins represent the redemptive potential of simple, unselfconscious love.
