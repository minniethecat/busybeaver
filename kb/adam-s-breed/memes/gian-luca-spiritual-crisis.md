## Summary

In Book III of *Adam's Breed*, [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] undergoes a sustained spiritual crisis that constitutes the novel's central psychological event. Having achieved professional success at the Doric and married [[maddalena-boselli|Maddalena]], he finds the inherited structures of immigrant Catholic identity — faith, family, trade — inadequate to his inner life. The crisis intensifies through his departure for Italy and culminates during the [[vendemmia-as-symbol|vendemmia]] at [[villa-sabelli|Villa Sabelli]]. The spiritual crisis is the novel's answer to the question posed by the "restaurant breed": what happens when inherited identity no longer suffices for the soul?

## Detailed Account

### Origins of the Crisis

The crisis has roots in [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s lifelong sense of difference from his community. His first word was English [010-006]; he does not enlist when Italy calls [107-003]; and his professional success at the Doric [083-003] brings respect but not belonging. These accumulated experiences of not-quite-fitting crystallise in Book III into a conscious crisis. The narrator describes a "kind of spiritual spring-cleaning" [024-004] — an earlier, milder version of the process — but Book III's crisis is deeper and more destructive.

### The Question of God

Central to the crisis is [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s relationship to Catholic faith. [[fabio-boselli|Fabio]]'s earlier declaration — "I have done with God, too" [008-010] — echoes across the novel as a precedent for [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s own rupture. The [[spiritual-crisis|loss of faith]] is not merely intellectual but visceral: it removes the framework that has given meaning to suffering, community, and identity for generations of Bosellis. Without God, the restaurant breed becomes mere labour; without faith, [[maddalena-boselli|Maddalena]]'s devotion at St. Peter's [137-015] becomes a language [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] can no longer speak.

### Italy and the Vendemmia

[[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s journey to [[villa-sabelli|Villa Sabelli]] and participation in the [[vendemmia-as-symbol|vendemmia]] represent the crisis's turning point. The harvest — elemental, cyclical, connected to soil — offers an alternative to the spiritual emptiness of London's restaurant economy. Yet the resolution remains ambiguous: the novel's epilogue suggests that the crisis has changed [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] irrevocably, leaving him suspended between the Italian Catholic world he has lost and a modern, secular self he cannot fully inhabit.

### Maddalena's Suffering

The crisis is not solitary: [[maddalena-boselli|Maddalena]], "born on the Campagna" [196-005], endures [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s spiritual withdrawal as a personal abandonment. His promise that she will "go back to the Campagna" [173-003] links her longing for home to his search for meaning, but the promise's fulfillment is deferred. The spiritual crisis thus becomes a relational crisis — [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s inability to believe isolates him from the woman whose faith sustains her.
