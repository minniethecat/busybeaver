## Biography

Albert Cole is a young English commercial traveler who becomes [[berta|Berta]]'s suitor and eventual husband. He first enters the narrative through [[geppe|Geppe]], who met him through a friend of [[berta|Berta]]'s at [[madame-germaine|Madame Germaine]]'s establishment and was impressed by his apparent sophistication — "a very smart fellow with plenty of money and a staggering knowledge of the world" [098-001].

[[berta|Berta]]'s heart was "caught on the rebound" — a phrase suggesting that her attachment to Cole follows a previous disappointment — and she wishes to marry him [098-001]. [[nerone|Nerone]] dislikes Albert Cole on sight, partly because of his nationality: "he had the misfortune to be English." [[nerone|Nerone]] is rude whenever Cole calls, which only accelerates [[berta|Berta]]'s determination; she decides "to marry him at once" [098-001].

Cole calls himself an agnostic, a detail that [[rosa-varese|Rosa]] reports with evident distress, her eyes welling with tears [098-001]. In the devout Catholic milieu of the Soho [[italian-diaspora|Italian community]], this self-identification marks Cole as an outsider not merely by nationality but by his entire worldview.

## Character

Albert Cole is defined almost entirely through the reactions he provokes in others. [[geppe|Geppe]] admires him as a model of worldly success — the commercial traveler's life represents freedom from the shop and access to a wider world. [[nerone|Nerone]], by contrast, sees him as an interloper, English and godless, unworthy of his granddaughter. [[berta|Berta]], caught between these perspectives, uses Cole as a vehicle for asserting her independence.

Cole's agnosticism is the most striking detail about him. In a novel where [[catholic-faith|Catholicism]] is the ambient spiritual language of the community — a constant presence even for those, like [[rocca|Rocca]], who jeer at priests — Cole's rejection of religious belief places him entirely outside the community's moral framework. He is not merely a foreigner but a sign of the encroaching modern, secular world.

## Role in the Narrative

Albert Cole appears only in a single paragraph, but his function is significant. He represents the assimilation of the second generation into English life: [[berta|Berta]], the daughter of Italian immigrants, marries an Englishman who does not share her family's faith. This union is presented with a mixture of comedy ([[nerone|Nerone]]'s rudeness, [[berta|Berta]]'s defiance) and pathos ([[rosa-varese|Rosa]]'s tears over the agnosticism).

Cole is also a narrative device for closing [[berta|Berta]]'s arc. She has been depicted throughout the novel as greedy, plain, and somewhat comic; her engagement to Cole gives her story a resolution that is simultaneously conventional (marriage) and transgressive (to an English agnostic).
