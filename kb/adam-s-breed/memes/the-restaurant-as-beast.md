## Manifestation

Hall uses persistent animal and bestial imagery to depict the Doric restaurant, transforming it from a mere workplace into a living, consuming entity. The metaphor operates on multiple levels: the restaurant as a physical "monster," its patrons as feeding "beasts," and the institution itself as a devourer of human spirit.

### The Restaurant as Monster

The novel's most direct personification of the Doric occurs late in the narrative, when the restaurant is depicted as a living creature stirring to its daily feeding:

> As he went down the tortuous steel-rimmed staircase, he was met by a blast of hot air, for the great, greedy monster was stirring to action, and its breath was already heavy with the food that fumed on its tables of fire. (175-020)

Earlier, the Doric is described as possessing a "vast, mysterious heart" that is "throbbing, bubbling, giving forth" (084-006), reinforcing the organic, living metaphor. The restaurant is not merely a building or business but a breathing organism with its own appetites. The staircase itself — "tortuous" and "steel-rimmed" — suggests descent into the belly of the beast.

### Patrons as Feeding Animals

[[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s perspective transforms the diners into creatures driven by base instinct. He observes them with growing disgust:

> Some chewed with a thoughtful, circular motion that suggested an aftermath of grazing, while others nibbled their food very quickly, like rabbits devouring a lettuce. (147-002)

His contempt culminates in explicit condemnation to [[maddalena-boselli|Maddalena]]:

> "Because, Maddalena, that is what I feel for those beasts at the Doric, I hate them; and now I am going to tell you something: that is how I hated Ugo Doria." (147-012)

This conflation of diner and beast strips the Doric's clientele of the social prestige they believe the restaurant confers. In [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s vision, the elegant dining room becomes a feeding pen.

### The Institution as Devourer

Beyond the physical imagery, the restaurant consumes the humanity of those who work within it. [[franco-millo|Franco Millo]], though "a kindhearted, tolerant man," acknowledges that "if a few people went down in the process that was not [[francesco-millo|Millo]]'s fault, but the fault of his times — of the age that demanded a Doric" (085-001). [[the-doric|The Doric]] is an impersonal force that grinds down its servants regardless of individual intention.

## Broader Resonance

The restaurant-as-beast motif connects to the novel's wider critique of modern civilization. [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] extends the metaphor outward:

> They had built up the monster, and had called him Civilization. And now they were sweating great drops of fear. (156-014)

[[the-doric|The Doric]] becomes a microcosm of a society that consumes without conscience, foreshadowing [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s eventual retreat from both the restaurant and the civilized world it represents. This extension of the metaphor from dining room to civilization itself is one of the novel's most ambitious rhetorical moves — the restaurant is not an aberration but the purest expression of modern life's organizing principle.

In his final years in Italy, [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s compassion shifts to literal beasts — farm animals suffering under human cruelty. This transfer of sympathy from restaurant patrons to actual animals completes the thematic arc: the Doric taught him to see the beast in man, and Italy taught him to see the man in the beast. The motif thus serves as both critique and redemption — the same lens that condemned civilization enables [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s final awakening.
