## Overview

Cousin Sisto is a relative of [[maddalena-boselli]] who lives on the family farm near Levanto, on the Ligurian coast. A "broad, squat person of fifty" with grey hair cut *en brosse*, Sisto embodies the rooted Italian life that the Boselli family left behind when they emigrated to London. His warm, voluble welcome to [[maddalena-boselli|Maddalena]] and [[gian-luca-boselli]] when they visit Italy provides one of the novel's rare scenes of uncomplicated familial joy.

## Biography

### Life in Levanto

Sisto was "a person of some importance, *fattore*" on a farmhouse estate in the hills behind Levanto, his grey hair "cut *en brosse*, giving to his large head a square appearance" [164-011]. He met [[maddalena-boselli|Maddalena]] and [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] at the Levanto train station with characteristic Italian *espressività*: "[[maddalena-boselli|Maddalena]], but this is splendid! And [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]], but this is splendid!" [164-010].

### The Visit

Cousin Sisto's reception of the travelers was marked by comic exasperation at Italian inefficiency: "It is late, this train," he remarked "in a voice of incredulous surprise; 'nearly one hour and a half of delay'" [164-012]. He directed the porter [[carlo|Carlo]]—"who groaned and sighed loudly as he heaved the small trunks on to his antiquated barrow"—with the authority of a man accustomed to command [164-013]. His wife [[lidia]], "a simple creature, but kind, oh! intensely kind," received them at the farm gates [162-009].

### Significance to Maddalena

For [[maddalena-boselli|Maddalena]], Sisto represented the family connections she had sacrificed by marrying [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] and moving to London. "[[maddalena-boselli|Maddalena]] told him about her cousins; he had heard it all many times before, but he let her talk on because it made her happy" [162-009]. Sisto's warm embrace of the visiting couple affirmed the bonds of blood and place that the immigrant experience had stretched but not broken.

## Relationships

### Maddalena Boselli
Sisto's relationship with his cousin [[maddalena-boselli|Maddalena]] was affectionate and uncomplicated. His joy at seeing her was unfeigned, and his hospitality—the farm, the food, the voluble conversation—offered her a temporary return to the Italian world she had left behind.

### Gian-Luca Boselli
Sisto extended his welcome to [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] with the same generosity, addressing him directly: "And [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]], but this is splendid!" [164-010]. For [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]], the visit to Levanto was an encounter with a version of Italian identity—rural, rooted, familial—that was utterly different from Soho's urban immigrant experience.

## Significance

Cousin Sisto functions in the novel as a geographic and emotional counterpoint to London. Where the [[italian-diaspora|Soho Italians]] are displaced, struggling, and gradually assimilating, Sisto remains embedded in the Italian soil—a *fattore* whose identity is inseparable from the land he works. His brief appearance reminds the reader—and the protagonists—of what emigration has cost: the loss of an unselfconscious belonging to place and family.
