## Summary

In one of the novel's most dramatic and thematically dense scenes, [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]
Boselli — now headwaiter at the Doric restaurant — serves lunch to the poet
[[ugo-doria|Ugo Doria]] in the Octagon Room. Unknown to Doria, [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] is his biological
son. Unknown to [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s colleagues, Doria is the poet whose verses [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]
has revered since youth. The scene unfolds as a silent, searing confrontation
between father and son, poet and waiter, ideal and reality.

## Detailed Account

### Preparation

[[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] prepares for Doria's arrival with uncharacteristic agitation. At ten
o'clock he puts on his dress-suit, "taking quite a long time in the process"
(141-001). He insists on serving everything himself: "I will open the wine, I
wish to serve everything myself" (141-002), repeating this instruction until
his subordinate [[roberto|Roberto]] is "somewhat nettled" (141-003). His staff notice his
strange behavior: "He is not like this as a rule — today one would think he
was new to the work" (141-009).

But [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s motive is personal, not professional: "today he was going to
be human for once, he was going to be a creature of feeling; today he would
serve for the joy of serving, and his service should be a thing to remember"
(141-010).

### The Arrival

At twenty to two, Doria arrives — late. [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] stiffens as "someone was
coming in at the door, a woman, [[milady|the little Milady]]; and after her followed a
white-haired man who was taking off pale grey gloves" (142-001). [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]
sees his father with terrible clarity: "a bulky, elderly man of sixty with a
self-willed mouth that had weakened and coarsened, with pale eyes that looked
small and unimportant beneath their heavy, pouched lids — [[ugo-doria|Ugo Doria]]" (142-002).

[[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] bows: "Signor Doria's table? It is ready, signore — this way please,
[[milady|Milady]]" (142-003, 142-005).

### The Meal

[[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] serves Doria and [[milady|Milady]] "with artichokes in oil and other delectable
things" (142-006). Doria talks in "broken English, making gestures with his
long white hands" while [[milady|Milady]] laughs (142-007). [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] opens champagne,
pours, serves with a cold smile — "a queer, cruel smile that distorted his
features, twisting the corners of his mouth" (142-020).

Doria, drinking heavily, grows expansively romantic. He tells [[milady|Milady]]: "You are
beautiful like morning, bellezza, you are like my 'Gioia della Luce'"
(142-012) — the very poem [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] has adored since youth. [[milady|Milady]] lies that
she has read it, her eyes wandering "at moments to someone who sat near the
door" (142-017).

[[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] watches impassively: "Tall and silent, but terribly watchful, stood
[[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]], always at his elbow" (142-020). The scene becomes unbearable: "A
large, foolish, lovesick viveur of sixty, greasy with food and wine; hot with
the passing caprice of the moment — a triumph of a poet, a disaster of a man;
this then was [[ugo-doria|Ugo Doria]]" (142-021). Doria drags "his exquisite art in the
mire, using his im[[death-and-mortality|mortality]] as a cloak for a brainless woman to tread on"
(142-021).

### The Departure

Doria pays "with scarcely a glance" and hands [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] a two-pound tip "in
order to impress [[milady|Milady]]" (142-022). [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] bows and holds open the door:
"Buon giorno, signore, e grazie" (142-024). The chapter closes with the
devastating summary: "And that was how [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] served his father; and that
was how Doria was served by his son — neither of them any the wiser" (142-026).

### The Aftermath

That night, while his wife [[maddalena-boselli|Maddalena]] sleeps, [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] burns Doria's books:
"He burnt them deliberately, one by one, and the pound notes that Doria had
given him he burnt also; and the flames that consumed these things were less
searing than the anger within his heart" (143-001). He reviles the burning
pages: "You hypocrites! Here is [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] who believed you all these years...
You say to [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]: 'Come here, my good fellow, and tell me what I must
eat'" (143-003).

He rages against Doria directly: "You beast, you fool, you ridiculous
bladder! Damn you and your children and your children's children!" (143-006).
Then, fearing [[maddalena-boselli|Maddalena]] will think him mad, he collects the burnt paper and
throws it into the street (143-008, 143-009).

## Participants

- **[[gian-luca-boselli]]** — the headwaiter and secret son. His composure
  throughout the meal masks a rage that erupts afterward in the burning of
  Doria's books. The scene represents the collapse of his most sustaining
  ideal — the poet whose verses gave meaning to his waiter's life.

- **[[ugo-doria]]** — the celebrated Italian poet and [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s biological
  father. He is portrayed as a ruin of a man: vain, lecherous, drunken,
  "a triumph of a poet, a disaster of a man" (142-021). He never learns
  that the headwaiter who served him is his son.

## Consequences

The scene precipitates [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s final crisis. The ideal of the poet —
which had sustained him through years of restaurant toil — is destroyed.
Doria is revealed as "small, small, small" (143-003), a man whose "sins are
the sins of an overcharged belly" (143-003). The burning of the books is a
ritual of disenchantment: the verses that once unsealed [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s ears and
eyes are now ash.

More profoundly, the encounter is an inverted recognition scene. In classical
drama, recognition (*anagnorisis*) reunites separated kin and restores order.
Here, father and son meet, touch, exchange money and pleasantries — yet remain
"neither of them any the wiser" (142-026). The non-recognition is more
devastating than any revelation could be: it proves that the bond [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]
has unconsciously sought — through poetry, through the restaurant trade,
through his own restless ambition — does not exist.
