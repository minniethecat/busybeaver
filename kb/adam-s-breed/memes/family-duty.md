## Definition

Family duty in *Adam's Breed* operates as both a moral imperative and a trap. The Italian immigrant community is organized around family as the primary social unit — loyalty to parents, obligation to children, responsibility to the household economy. [[persons-index|Characters]] navigate this web of obligation with varying degrees of success and resentment, and the novel traces the psychological cost of both compliance and rebellion.

## Manifestation

The family economy is most visible in [[boselli-family-shop|Fabio's shop]], where [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] is expected to work as a matter of course. The shop is not merely a business but a family institution — [[fabio-boselli|Fabio]]'s labor supports not only his immediate household but a wider network of dependents. When [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] questions this arrangement, he questions the fundamental logic of the community.

[[mario-boselli|Mario]] represents the successful fulfillment of family duty: he has built a restaurant that supports the extended family, married, and taken on the role of provider. But his success comes at a cost — he manages people as he manages ingredients, and his relationship with [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] is framed in terms of utility rather than affection.

## Generational Patterns

The tension between duty and desire plays out differently across generations. [[nerone|Nerone]], the patriarch, embodies the duty-bound immigrant — he "loved his country, but he lived in England" because that is where the family needed him [024-006]. His sacrifice is absolute and unquestioned.

The second generation — [[fabio-boselli|Fabio]] and [[mario-boselli|Mario]] — internalize duty while seeking modest exits. [[fabio-boselli|Fabio]]'s kindness is a form of duty performed gracefully; [[mario-boselli|Mario]]'s ambition is duty transformed into personal advancement.

The third generation — [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]], [[geppe|Geppe]], [[berta|Berta]] — experiences duty as constraint. [[geppe|Geppe]] wishes to be a commercial traveler, seeing it as "a pleasant, safe way to see life, and one that would release him from the shop" [098-001]. [[berta|Berta]] marries an English agnostic, defying her grandfather's preferences. [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s rebellion is the most radical: he simply stops caring, drifting away from family obligations without ever explicitly rejecting them.
