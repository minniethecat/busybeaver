## Overview

Giovanni is the *trancheur*—the carver—at the Doric restaurant, a once-lighthearted fellow whose personal life collapses when his young lady marries the hall porter "under his very nose." His story illustrates the human cost of [[francesco-millo]]'s professional credo that waiters should have "no hearts and no emotions; no passions—no bodies except to serve" [085-003].

## Biography

### The Jilting

Giovanni's defining crisis arrived when "Giovanni's young lady had married the hall porter under his very nose, and Giovanni, from being a lighthearted fellow, had grown decidedly broody" [085-002]. The betrayal was compounded by its domestic setting—the betrayal occurred within the same building where he worked, making every shift a reminder of his loss.

### Millo's Philosophy

[[francesco-millo|Millo]] addressed Giovanni's heartbreak with his characteristic blend of philosophy and professional discipline. "'Here we have no hearts and no emotions; no passions—no bodies except to serve. Am I not right, my good Giovanni? Is not a ham more important to me than your broken heart?'" [085-003]. Giovanni "had bowed and murmured in agreement, feeling that [[francesco-millo|Millo]] was right; feeling that a ham must be worth more to [[francesco-millo|Millo]] than a man's broken heart" [085-004].

### The War

When war came, Giovanni was among the waiters called up. "A few weeks later went Giovanni, the *trancheur*, and he too confided in [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]. The war was loosening all tongues" [113-003]. His departure was part of the gradual erosion of the Doric's pre-war world.

## Relationships

### Francesco Millo
Giovanni's relationship with [[francesco-millo|Millo]] was defined by the power asymmetry of employer and employee, but also by a kind of brutal honesty. [[francesco-millo|Millo]]'s refusal to pretend concern for Giovanni's personal life was, in its way, more respectful than false sympathy—a recognition that in the Doric's world, professional competence was the only currency that mattered.

### Gian-Luca Boselli
As a fellow waiter, [[gian-luca-boselli]] witnessed Giovanni's decline from lightheartedness to brooding. Their shared experience of [[francesco-millo|Millo]]'s discipline and the war's disruptions made them part of the same threatened world of pre-war restaurant culture.

## Significance

Giovanni's small tragedy—a broken heart in a restaurant carving station—is one of the novel's most telling minor episodes. [[francesco-millo|Millo]]'s pronouncement that "a ham must be worth more... than a man's broken heart" distills the dehumanizing logic of professional service into a single, devastating axiom. Giovanni bows in agreement not because he believes it, but because he has internalized a world in which his feelings have no market value.
