## Overview

Signora Rocca is a member of the Soho [[italian-diaspora|Italian community]] whose life intersects with the Boselli household at moments of moral crisis. Childless—"a reproach among women"—she compensates with an excess of piety, proverbs, and moral indignation. Her enormous eyes, her repertoire of Italian sayings, and her habit of being "appalled" make her one of the novel's most vividly drawn minor characters.

## Biography

### The Scandal

When scandal touched the Boselli household, "[[rosa-varese|Rosa]] was deeply shocked and so was [[nerone]], while the Signora Rocca was appalled" [023-001]. Unlike the others, who directed their anger at [[fabio-boselli|Fabio]], Signora Rocca "announced her intention of calling upon [[teresa-boselli|Teresa]] immediately. She gave [[rosa-varese|Rosa]] to understand that she was about to speak her mind" [023-001]—though, tellingly, she "dared tackle [[teresa-boselli|Teresa]]" no more than anyone else [023-007].

### Proverbs and Piety

Signora Rocca was "versed in proverbs," deploying them as moral weapons: "'You give the lettuce into the keeping of the geese!' she quoted in high, shrill Italian" [023-023]. Yet her moral authority was undermined by her childlessness—a condition the community regarded as a deficiency. At this reproach, "Signora Rocca was forced to be silent; she was childless, a reproach among women" [024-022].

### Prayers in Wartime

During the war years, "the Signora Rocca said very many prayers; it was May, the month of Our Lady" [032-019]. Her piety, like her proverbs, served as both genuine spiritual practice and social performance—a way of asserting her place in a community that otherwise defined women by their children.

## Relationships

### The Boselli Household
Signora Rocca orbited the Boselli family as a kind of moral thermometer—her level of outrage reliably indicating the community's judgment. Her relationship with [[teresa-boselli]], in particular, was defined by the tension between her desire to moralize and her inability to confront the formidable matriarch directly.

## Significance

Signora Rocca is a study in the social dynamics of a tight-knit immigrant community. Her childlessness—"a reproach among women"—marks her as incomplete in the eyes of her world, and her compensatory piety and proverbial wisdom are both armor and performance. She embodies the way a community polices itself through gossip, moral pronouncement, and the weaponization of folk wisdom, even as her own vulnerable position (the childless woman) exposes the cruelty that underlies such social control.
