## Overview

Nonna is [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s great-grandmother and the quiet matriarch of the Boselli household during his early childhood. She runs a small shop, knits constantly, and serves as a figure of both comfort and authority for the young [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]. Her presence defines the domestic world of [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s earliest memories — a world of wool, peppermint, and the steady rhythm of knitting needles.

## Biography

### Early Household Life

Nonna appears throughout [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s earliest years in the Boselli household. [[rosa-varese|Rosa Varese]] frequently invokes Nonna's authority to discipline the young children, warning [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]: "You must not tell Nonna where we have been" (012-002). This establishes Nonna as the ultimate authority figure in the household, someone whose judgment even the adults respect.

The young [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] perceives Nonna with a mixture of reverence and fear. He "suspected the omnipotence of Nonna" and felt that while his grandfather "Nonno might be God," Nonna was "the source from which he sprang" (014-008, 014-009). This quasi-religious awe defines his earliest understanding of power and authority.

### The Shop and Daily Life

Nonna runs a small shop attached to the household. Her constant knitting — "Nonna would go on with her knitting" (014-013) — becomes one of [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s most persistent sensory memories. The knitting serves as a visual symbol of her steady, unchanging presence. She also tends to the children's minor ailments with "the essence of peppermint that Nonna administered" (021-002).

Nonna is notably restrained in her emotional expression. "[[fabio-boselli|Fabio]] had small rages, but Nonna very seldom raised her voice" (014-022). Her authority is exercised through quiet presence rather than overt discipline, making her rare moments of attention or affection all the more significant to [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]].

### Gian-Luca's Attachment

[[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] develops a deep attachment to Nonna, crying out "I want Nonna!" when distressed (020-003). His emotional world revolves around seeking her approval and attention. In a pivotal moment, he brings her fading flowers — a gesture of love that finally earns her acknowledgment: "'Thank you,' said Nonna. 'What very pretty flowers'" (020-027). This exchange marks what the narrative describes as "[[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s miniature triumph; Nonna no longer ignored him" (021-001).

## Relationships

- **[[gian-luca-boselli]]** — Her great-grandson, who views her with a mixture of awe, fear, and desperate affection. She is the primary figure of authority and comfort in his earliest years.
- **[[rosa-varese]]** — Her granddaughter, who invokes Nonna's authority to control the children and whose own relationship with Nonna shapes the household dynamics.
- **[[fabio-boselli]]** — Her grandson-in-law, whose occasional rages contrast with Nonna's characteristic calm (014-022).

## Significance

Nonna represents the domestic stability and traditional authority of the Italian household. Her character anchors [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s earliest memories in a world of sensory richness — wool, peppermint, jam, flowers — and establishes the pattern of longing for approval that shapes his later relationships. She also embodies the novel's theme of generational continuity: as [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s great-grandmother, she connects him to a deeper Italian heritage beyond his immediate parents.

Her knitting — constant, repetitive, productive — serves as a metaphor for the quiet, sustaining labor that holds families together, a contrast to the louder dramas of the male characters. When [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] later faces [[loneliness-and-alienation|alienation]] and displacement, the memory of Nonna's steady presence becomes a touchstone for what he has lost.
