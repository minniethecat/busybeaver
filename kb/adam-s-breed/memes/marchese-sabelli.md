## Overview

The Marchese Sabelli is an Italian nobleman who owns [[villa-sabelli|Villa Sabelli]], the estate where [[sisto|Sisto]] serves as *fattore* (steward). He appears in the novel's later sections when [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] visits [[sisto|Sisto]] at the villa. Described by his servants as a person of importance who enjoys good wine and pretty women, the Marchese remains an off-stage presence whose authority shapes the lives of those on his estate.

## Biography

[[sisto|Sisto]] describes the Marchese as holding a very responsible position and being "a person of great importance" [165-016]. According to [[sisto|Sisto]], the Marchese "is merry, he admires pretty women" [165-017], painting a picture of a worldly, pleasure-loving aristocrat. The Marchese is also "very fond of good wine" [166-001], and the villa's cellars are stocked with his excellent vintages [166-007].

The Marchese's prized possession is his collection of trained *richiami*——ortolans used as decoy birds——which hang from the windows of his bedroom [170-004]. When these birds are stolen, [[sisto|Sisto]] is distraught, knowing that "the Marchese will be furious, he will surely dismiss me" [170-014]. The theft precipitates a crisis for [[sisto|Sisto]], revealing the absolute dependence of the servants on their master's goodwill.

The Marchese is expected home the day after the theft of his birds [172-014], though the novel never shows him directly confronting the situation. His impending return creates tension that underscores the power dynamics of the estate.

## Relationships

- **[[sisto|Sisto]]**: The Marchese employs [[sisto|Sisto]] as his *fattore*, a position of considerable responsibility that [[sisto|Sisto]] takes great pride in [162-009]. [[sisto|Sisto]]'s fear of the Marchese's anger over the stolen richiami reveals the precariousness of a servant's life [170-014].
- **[[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca Boselli]]**: Though they never meet directly, [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] visits [[villa-sabelli|Villa Sabelli]] as [[sisto|Sisto]]'s guest and hears extensive accounts of the Marchese's character and habits [165-016], [166-001].

## Significance

The Marchese Sabelli represents the old Italian aristocratic order——absent, powerful, and arbitrary. His character is defined entirely through the eyes of his servants, particularly [[sisto|Sisto]], whose identity is bound up in serving him. The Marchese's unseen authority over the villa parallels broader themes in the novel of power, dependency, and the immigrant's complicated relationship with the homeland. The episode of the stolen richiami demonstrates how the lives of the lower classes can be upended by the whims and possessions of their social superiors.
