## Overview

Dr. Rossi is the Boselli family's physician, a recurring but unnamed presence whose appearances mark moments of bodily crisis across three generations. From attending [[olga-boselli]]'s death in childbirth at the novel's opening to diagnosing [[fabio-boselli]]'s lumbago in his middle years and being urgently summoned for [[gian-luca-boselli]]'s decline, the doctor serves as a medical witness to the family's physical unraveling.

## Biography

### Olga's Death

The doctor first appears at the novel's most pivotal moment: [[olga-boselli|Olga]], [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s mother, is dying in childbirth. The nurse, finding [[olga-boselli|Olga]]'s condition worsening, declares: "I must send your husband for the doctor" [003-027]. When [[fabio-boselli|Fabio]] is not immediately available, she insists: "You must go for the doctor—any doctor if he's out" [003-030]. The doctor arrives but can do little: "[[olga-boselli|Olga]] was dying—the doctor came and went; he would call again later, he told them" [004-001].

### Fabio's Lumbago

Years later, the doctor treats [[fabio-boselli|Fabio]] for chronic back pain. "The pains had been lumbago, or so the doctor said, and when they caught him, [[fabio-boselli|Fabio]] had to lie flat on his back, sometimes for several days at a stretch" [022-008]. These episodes contributed to [[fabio-boselli|Fabio]]'s premature aging and inability to work steadily.

### Gian-Luca's Illness

In the novel's later sections, the doctor is repeatedly summoned for [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]], who is suffering from an unspecified wasting illness. [[nerone]] protests: "Per Bacco, he is not! I think he is probably ill, our [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]" [137-005]. But [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] resists treatment: "Nothing that [[maddalena-boselli|Maddalena]] could say would persuade him to see a doctor" [147-018]. His employer [[francesco-millo]] urges him: "What you need is a holiday and a bottle of tonic" [160-014].

## Relationships

### The Boselli Family
Dr. Rossi's relationship with the Boselli family spans decades. He is the medical constant in a household marked by premature death, chronic pain, and psychosomatic illness. His inability to prevent [[olga-death-and-gian-luca-birth|Olga's death]] or to reverse [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s decline makes him a figure of well-meaning but limited power.

## Significance

The doctor embodies the limits of medical intervention in the face of the novel's deeper existential ailments. [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s refusal to see the doctor in his final decline is not merely stubbornness but a recognition that his suffering—rooted in [[loneliness-and-alienation|alienation]], identity crisis, and spiritual despair—lies beyond the reach of medical diagnosis. The doctor's recurring presence, always arriving too late or achieving too little, underscores the novel's theme of the body as a site of inevitable dissolution.
