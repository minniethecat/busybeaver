# Franco Millo

Franco "[[francesco-millo|Millo]]" is the director of the [[the-doric|Doric Restaurant]], a celebrated establishment
in post-Armistice London. He is invariably referred to by his surname alone,
a mark of his professional authority and distance from his staff.

## First Appearance

[[francesco-millo|Millo]]'s introduction in the novel is a carefully staged event. "Just after
Christmas when [[francesco-millo|Signor Millo]] marched into [[boselli-family-shop|Fabio's shop]] one morning, [[fabio-boselli|Fabio]] was
overcome by the honor" (070-001). His entrance into the Boselli shop is
described in martial terms — he "marched" — underscoring his presence as a
man of action and status. Hall renders him in full: "[[francesco-millo|Signor Millo]] was a man
of forty-five, of medium height, strongly built, and handsome in a florid,
vivid way" (070-003).

His first meeting with [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] occurs in the shop: "If [[francesco-millo|Francesco Millo]] had
a palate for good food, he also had an eye for a fine figure of a man, and
his bold, brilliant glance swept over [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]" (070-018). [[francesco-millo|Millo]]'s appraisal
is both professional and personal, and the brief exchange establishes the
dynamic of patronage and recognition that will define their relationship at
the Doric.

## Professional Role

[[francesco-millo|Millo]] is first mentioned in conversation between [[fabio-boselli|Fabio]] and [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] before
the latter's interview at the Doric. [[fabio-boselli|Fabio]] instructs [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] not to appear
"shabby before [[francesco-millo|Millo]]" (075-003), signaling the director's stature. As director,
[[francesco-millo|Millo]] presides over the restaurant's operations with a blend of strategic
foresight and personal charm.

His philosophy of hospitality is grounded in personal recognition: "that was
what was so nice about [[francesco-millo|Millo]], he never forgot the face of a client" (134-017).
This attention to patrons contributes to the Doric's atmosphere of "well-being
that [[francesco-millo|Millo]] had managed to create" (135-001).

## Commercial Acumen

During the uncertain post-war period, [[francesco-millo|Millo]] demonstrates considerable business
instinct. "[[francesco-millo|Millo]], in his office, sat long into the night scheming, calculating,
foreseeing" (107-002), confronting what he terms "a total collapse of our
system" (107-003). He reassures his Board of Directors "that if all Europe
crumbled, the Doric would still go on" (107-004), a defiant optimism that
characterizes his approach to management.

[[francesco-millo|Millo]] is "also a hunter, as keen on the chase of a truffle as on the chase
of beautiful women" (070-004). He is "a restless man" (070-006), perpetually
in motion, ever seeking new sensations and new conquests. This dual appetite
— gastronomic and erotic — makes him a figure of unabashed earthly vitality,
in contrast to [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s ascetic spiritual hunger.

## The Doric Under Millo

Under [[francesco-millo|Millo]]'s direction, the post-Armistice season at the Doric becomes
unprecedented: "There had never been such a season at the Doric as the season
that followed the Armistice" (134-001). [[francesco-millo|Millo]] is "in the seventh heaven of
delight, and so were most of his waiters, for people were recklessly spending
their money" (134-001). He doubles the restaurant's band, recognizing that
"a supper without dancing was unheard of" (134-002).

## Relationship with Gian-Luca

[[francesco-millo|Millo]] regards his headwaiter [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca Boselli]] with professional approval.
"[[francesco-millo|Millo]], watching his clever headwaiter, would consider him well-nigh perfect"
(135-002). [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]], for his part, aspires to [[francesco-millo|Millo]]'s level of fame:
"I shall one day be famous as [[francesco-millo|Millo]] is famous" (096-018). Yet [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] also
notes [[francesco-millo|Millo]]'s strictness: "[[francesco-millo|Millo]] hates eyes and ears except for his orders"
(096-018), suggesting the director expects absolute professional focus from
his staff.

Their first meeting in the Boselli shop prefigures the relationship to come.
[[francesco-millo|Millo]] "bowed slightly" and said "Piacere" (070-017) — the Italian formality a
mark of respect. When [[francesco-millo|Millo]] "turned and addressed him directly" (070-024),
[[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] is singled out, a moment of recognition that will ultimately lead
him to leave [[the-capo|the Capo]] di Monte for the Doric.

## Character

[[francesco-millo|Millo]] is described as possessing a "wise head" (135-001). His worldview is
summarized by his characteristic murmur: "Le monde est ainsi fait" — the
world is made thus (135-001). This Gallic resignation, combined with his
commercial drive, makes him a figure of worldly competence against which
[[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s spiritual searching is measured.
