## Overview

Geppe is a minor but vividly drawn supporting character in [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca Boselli]]'s childhood chapters in Book I of Radclyffe Hall's *Adam's Breed*. The turbulent son of [[rosa-varese|Rosa]] and a constant presence in the Boselli household, Geppe embodies the noisy, chaotic energy of Italian immigrant family life in early-twentieth-century London. His greedy appetites, loud protests, and unself-conscious "Italian-ness" serve as both a foil and a point of fascination for the young [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]], who at one point wishes "to be as Geppe is—Italian" (027-058). Geppe later dies in the First World War, a fate shared by many in the Italian immigrant community.

## Biography

### Childhood in the Boselli Household

Geppe is introduced as [[rosa-varese|Rosa]]'s son, "a turbulent creature of six years" (026-004), living alongside his siblings [[mario-boselli|Mario]] and [[berta|Berta]] in the cramped quarters of the Boselli family home in [[old-compton-street|Soho]]. From his earliest appearances, he is defined by his insatiable appetites and lack of restraint: "Geppe, as always, was busy sucking" at the dinner table (028-005), and he was notorious for taking food "without asking" (028-007).

His greed was a source of family legend. [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] later recalls a childhood memory of "raspberry jam—I remember because Geppe ate so many" tarts (033-030). At mealtimes, Geppe and his sister [[berta|Berta]] would chant "We're hungry!" in unison, "beginning to jump up and down" (035-009), driving the adults to exasperation.

### School and the Pidocchi Incident

Geppe's school life brought its own disruptions to the household. When "a boy at Geppe's school has got pidocchi" (lice), [[rosa-varese|Rosa]] immediately fears contamination (028-017). Geppe's response is characteristically defiant: "'Scema!' spluttered Geppe. 'I have not got pidocchi'" (028-019), though his protests are undermined when his sister [[berta|Berta]] later announces "Geppe has given me pidocchi!" (032-010). The incident encapsulates Geppe's role in the narrative: a vector of chaos who denies responsibility but whose influence spreads regardless.

### Family Outings

A rare excursion outside Soho reveals Geppe at his most disruptive. On a crowded bus ride, "Geppe sat on top of [[rosa-varese|Rosa]]" (036-009), and when he proves too heavy, [[mario-boselli|Mario]] is instructed: "You had better carry Geppe... I fear he will fall" (036-013). Geppe objects by "beginning to cry" (036-014), and by the journey's end "Geppe's nose required attention" (036-024). These domestic scenes, rendered with Hall's sharp comic observation, build a portrait of a child whose physical needs constantly impinge on the adults around him.

### War and Death

Although Geppe vanishes from the narrative after Book I, the type survey of the novel records that he, like many young Italian immigrants in London, was mobilized during the First World War and killed in combat. His death forms part of the novel's broader treatment of war's destructive impact on the [[italian-diaspora|Italian community]]—a theme that reaches its fullest expression in the loss of characters such as the family friend Geppe (unrelated) mentioned in later chapters.

## Relationships

### Gian-Luca Boselli

The young [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s relationship with Geppe is marked by rivalry and reluctant fascination. [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] "trod slyly on Geppe's toe, and Geppe screamed like a stuck pig" (033-010), revealing both [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s capacity for quiet cruelty and Geppe's theatrical reactivity. Yet [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] also envies Geppe's uncomplicated identity: "I wish to be as Geppe is—Italian" (027-058). For the protagonist, Geppe represents a mode of being—loud, embodied, unconflicted—that [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] himself can never fully inhabit.

### Rosa

[[rosa-varese|Rosa]] is Geppe's mother and primary disciplinarian. She alternates between protective anxiety—"I so carefully warned Geppe to keep himself clean" (035-034)—and physical exasperation, as when "[[rosa-varese|Rosa]]'s large finger entered" Geppe's mouth to extract something he should not have eaten (035-012). Their relationship illustrates the strains of immigrant parenting in overcrowded conditions.

### Mario and Berta

Geppe's elder brother [[mario-boselli|Mario]] is described as "gentler than Geppe, but he looked rather like him" (033-029). [[mario-boselli|Mario]] is often tasked with managing Geppe's behavior on family outings. His sister [[berta|Berta]] is Geppe's partner in mischief—the two function as a chaotic duo, chanting and jumping in unison, until Geppe's actions inevitably provoke a louder response.

## Significance

Geppe's function in *Adam's Breed* extends beyond comic relief. As a character who is unapologetically loud, greedy, and demanding, he embodies the "Italian" vitality that the more assimilated [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] both desires and cannot claim. The [[loneliness-and-alienation|alienation]] that defines [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s adult life is prefigured in his childhood inability to match Geppe's unself-conscious belonging.

Geppe also serves a structural role in the novel's [[concept|thematic framework]] of appetite and restraint. Where [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] internalizes, Geppe externalizes. Where [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] questions his identity, Geppe simply *is*. This contrast between the introspective protagonist and the extroverted foil is a recurring pattern in Hall's character system, and Geppe is its purest early expression.

His death in the war—off-stage, un-narrated—mirrors the novel's treatment of large historical forces: they are felt through absence, through the sudden silencing of a once-deafening voice.
