## Overview

Nerone is the Soho tobacconist whose little shop stands just down the street from the Boselli household. Father of [[rosa-varese]] and father-in-law to [[mario-boselli]], he is a fixture of the Italian immigrant community—a genial, coughing purveyor of "rotten Macedonia" cigarettes, a devoted dominoes player, and a man whose laughter could defuse tension and whose affection for his family was as instinctive as breathing.

## Biography

### The Tobacconist

Nerone "sold tobacco a little way down the street" from the Boselli household, and his shop was a neighborhood landmark [009-002]. His daughter [[rosa-varese|Rosa Varese]] worked there and would later become the center of the Boselli household. The shop was also frequented by a "mongrel that wandered in and out of Nerone's little shop" [010-006], adding to its chaotic charm.

### Dominoes with Fabio

For fifteen years, Nerone and [[fabio-boselli]] played dominoes together, "drinking Amarena in the evenings" [011-024]. Their friendship was marked by affectionate bickering: "When [[fabio-boselli|Fabio]] lost the game—as he usually did—Nerone never failed to jeer" [011-024]. But "when sorrow had come upon them—and to [[fabio-boselli|Fabio]] shame as well—they had ceased to nag each other for the moment" [011-027]. Their dominoes ritual was a stabilizing force in both men's lives, a small but meaningful continuity amid the upheavals of immigrant existence.

### Temperament

Nerone's defining trait was his irrepressible laughter. When [[fabio-boselli|Fabio]] once walked in at an awkward moment, finding Nerone with [[rosa-varese|Rosa]], "it was even more unfortunate that Nerone should have laughed, with something like a chuckle of genuine amusement" [011-010]. His signature exclamation—"*Ma che!*"—punctuated scenes of minor crisis with deflating humor [011-011]. [[teresa-boselli|Teresa]], ever the severe matriarch, attributed his chronic cough to "his rotten Macedonia" cigarettes [011-016].

### The Father's Role

Nerone's paternal affection was a benchmark of normal, healthy family feeling in the novel's moral economy. When [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] contemplated the disturbing possibility that fathers might not love their children, he "thought of Nerone's affection for [[rosa-varese|Rosa]]" as evidence of the natural order [014-009]. [[rosa-varese|Rosa]] had "showed [[mario-boselli|Mario]] to Nerone... a long time before they got married," seeking her father's approval in the customary way [014-009].

## Relationships

### Rosa Varese
Nerone's daughter [[rosa-varese|Rosa]] was the "concrete creature" at the center of his world [009-002]. Their bond exemplified the novel's representation of Italian familial affection as "a primitive instinct like that of eating and drinking" [014-009].

### Fabio Boselli
Nerone's fifteen-year dominoes partnership with [[fabio-boselli|Fabio]] was one of the novel's most durable friendships. Their evenings of Amarena and quiet competition represented a form of male intimacy and mutual support that transcended the novel's more dramatic relationships.

### Gian-Luca Boselli
Nerone watched [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] grow up and, in the boy's later illness, was among the first to voice concern: "*Per Bacco*, he is not! I think he is probably ill, our [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]" [137-005].

## Significance

Nerone embodies the life-force of the Soho [[italian-diaspora|Italian community]]—earthy, irreverent, physically present (with his cough and his "rotten Macedonia"), and instinctively affectionate. His laughter serves as a counterpoint to the novel's prevailing tone of spiritual anguish, and his fifteen-year dominoes ritual with [[fabio-boselli|Fabio]] stands as a small monument to the sustaining power of ordinary friendship. In a novel about [[loneliness-and-alienation|alienation]], Nerone is emphatically *not* alienated—he is rooted, connected, and unselfconsciously alive.
