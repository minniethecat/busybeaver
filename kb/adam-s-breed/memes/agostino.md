## Biography

Agostino served as the storeman at the Doric restaurant in London. According to [[henri|Henri]], the Doric's chef, Agostino was "a disagreeable fellow" whom [[henri|Henri]] was "not sorry" to see leave (175-028). Agostino departed the Doric to return to Como, where he planned to run a hotel (175-028). His departure created a vacancy that [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] would fill — indirectly — by recommending his old friend [[mario-boselli|Mario Varese]].

> Henri laughed and looked very wise. "Once a waiter always a waiter," said Henri, "and once a chef always a chef, *mon ami*—we can never get away from food. By the way," he added, "our storeman leaves too, he is going back to Como to run a hotel. I for one am not sorry, a disagreeable fellow—I never could support Agostino!" (175-028)

[[henri|Henri]] and [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] discussed Agostino briefly before [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] took his leave of the chef (175-029). Though Agostino himself never appears directly in the narrative — he is known only through [[henri|Henri]]'s report — his departure carries considerable structural weight. It is one of several simultaneous departures from the Doric that mark the novel's closing movement: [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] himself is leaving, Agostino is leaving, and the restaurant's staff is in transition.

### The Replacement

Following Agostino's departure, [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] wrote to [[francesco-millo|Millo]] recommending [[mario-boselli|Mario Varese]] as the new storeman. [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] argued that [[mario-boselli|Mario]] "would make an excellent storeroom keeper, for there his lameness would not affect him," and that "he must be as young as Agostino" (181-002). This recommendation is significant: it is one of [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s last acts before leaving the Doric, and it reveals his ongoing loyalty to the Varese family — a loyalty rooted in his childhood bond with [[mario-boselli|Mario]]'s father, who had shown him kindness during his lonely boyhood in the Boselli household.

> "If you could try Mario Varese," he wrote, "I think he would give you satisfaction. He would make an excellent storeroom keeper, for there his lameness would not affect him, and, moreover, he knows a great deal about food, its price, and how to select it. He is honest and sober, and a very hard worker; he has had to work hard all his life, and although he is not as young as he was, he must be as young as Agostino." (181-002)

The letter demonstrates [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s careful, persuasive manner — he lists [[mario-boselli|Mario]]'s virtues methodically, addressing potential objections (his lameness, his age) before they can be raised. It also shows his quiet generosity: even as he prepares to leave the restaurant world behind, he takes the time to secure a position for an old family friend.

## Role at the Doric

Agostino's position as storeman placed him in charge of provisions and supplies — a role of quiet importance in a restaurant of the Doric's calibre. The storeman managed inventory, selected ingredients, and tracked costs, forming a bridge between the kitchen and the front-of-house. His departure, coinciding with [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s own resignation, represented a period of staff turnover at the Doric.

The fact that [[henri|Henri]] so readily expressed his dislike of Agostino — and that the narrative records this dislike without comment — is characteristic of the novel's unsentimental portrayal of workplace relationships. [[the-doric|The Doric]]'s staff are professionals bound by the demands of service, not friends; personal antipathies are noted and set aside.

## Narrative Function

Agostino exemplifies the novel's use of minor off-stage characters to advance the plot and illuminate its major figures. He never appears in a scene; everything we know of him comes through [[henri|Henri]]'s brief report. Yet his departure:

1. **Creates narrative opportunity**: The vacant storeman position gives [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] a chance to repay the Varese family's past kindness.
2. **Reinforces the novel's closing mood**: Agostino's departure is part of a general exodus — [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] leaves the Doric, leaves England, leaves behind the identity he has built.
3. **Highlights [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s character**: Even as he prepares for his own departure, [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] takes responsibility for finding a suitable replacement — a final act of professional conscientiousness.

## Relationships

- **[[[henri|Henri]]](henri.md)**: [[the-doric|The Doric]]'s chef, who held Agostino in open contempt, describing him as "a disagreeable fellow" whom he "never could support" (175-028). [[henri|Henri]]'s frankness about his colleague underscores the Doric's utilitarian workplace culture.
- **[[[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca Boselli]]](gian-luca-boselli.md)**: Though the two men do not interact directly in the narrative, [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] was conscientious enough about the vacancy Agostino created to recommend [[mario-boselli|Mario Varese]] as his replacement (181-002).
- **[[[mario-boselli|Mario Varese]]](mario-varese.md)**: Agostino's eventual successor as storeman at the Doric. The contrast between the two men is implicit: Agostino is disliked and leaving for personal ambition (a hotel in Como), while [[mario-boselli|Mario]] is recommended as honest, sober, and hardworking — a man in need of the position.
