## Overview

Teresa Boselli is the matriarch of the Boselli family, a first-generation Italian immigrant who settles in London's Soho district. Her character embodies the tension between Old World Italian values and the realities of immigrant life in England. As grandmother to the protagonist [[gian-luca-boselli]], Teresa exerts a formative influence on his upbringing after the death of his mother [[olga-boselli|Olga]] in childbirth. Her fierce pragmatism, moral complexity, and unyielding will make her one of the novel's most formidable figures.

## Biography

### Early Life and Immigration

Teresa Boselli emigrated from Lombardy, Italy, to London as part of the Italian diaspora that settled in the Soho district. Her early life in Italy is alluded to through references to the *[[vendemmia-as-symbol|vendemmia]]*—the grape harvest—suggesting agricultural roots in the Lombard countryside [003-019].

### Life in Soho

In London, Teresa established the Boselli household and became a central figure in the Italian immigrant community. She is first seen standing at a window, watching the life of the street below—a posture that conveys both vigilance and a certain detachment from the world she inhabits [003-001]. Her life is marked by sacrifice and hardship, yet she is described as someone whom "Life itself has not beaten" [120-008].

### Later Years and Death

Teresa's later years are defined by her relationship with her grandson [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] and his wife [[maddalena-boselli]]. In a climactic confrontation, she stands before [[maddalena-boselli|Maddalena]] with characteristic resolve [198-001], drawing herself up with the hard-won dignity of a woman who has outlived nearly everyone she loved [198-007]. Her final appearance comes at a coffin-side vigil, where she lingers alone, having been described as one who "scarcely less steadfast" followed in the wake of fate [200-016]. The narrative suggests that the accumulated trials of her life were bent toward breaking her will, yet she endures to the final pages [200-022].

## Relationships

### Gian-Luca Boselli
Teresa is the primary caregiver to her grandson [[gian-luca-boselli]] following the death of his mother [[olga-boselli]] in childbirth. Her influence shapes [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s character and his conflicted sense of identity as a second-generation Italian immigrant.

### Fabio Boselli
Teresa's son [[fabio-boselli]] is a mild-mannered restaurant worker who embodies the quieter side of the immigrant experience. Teresa's relationship with him is marked by maternal concern and the unspoken expectations of an Old World parent.

### Maddalena
Teresa's relationship with [[maddalena-boselli]], [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s wife, is one of the novel's most charged interpersonal dynamics. Their confrontation late in the narrative [198-001] reveals the generational tensions between first-generation immigrant matriarchs and the younger women who marry into the family.

## Significance

Teresa Boselli represents the moral and psychological anchor of the first-generation immigrant experience in the novel. Her character embodies the tension between the Old World's strict moral codes—including the Catholic concept of sin that the narrative explicitly connects to her past [003-019]—and the adaptive pragmatism required to survive in a foreign city. She is simultaneously a figure of strength and a vessel for accumulated grief, and her endurance across the full span of the narrative makes her the novel's most persistent witness to the Boselli family's unfolding tragedy.
