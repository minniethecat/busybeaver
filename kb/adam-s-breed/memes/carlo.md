## Biography

Carlo is a porter at the train station who appears in a single scene near the end of the novel. He is summoned to assist with luggage when [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] arrives at the station, groaning at the physical demands of the task (164-013)(164-014).

In his brief appearance, Carlo examines the address on a luggage label and mutters about a certain room in the station, "ignoring any inconvenience" his words might cause — a small act of bureaucratic indifference that captures the impersonal nature of such encounters (164-015)(164-017). He performs his duty but without enthusiasm or curiosity about the travelers he serves.

## Character

Carlo is a minor functional character whose presence grounds the novel's final scenes in mundane reality. He does not know [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]], has no stake in his story, and treats him as just another traveler with luggage. This anonymity is thematically significant — after an entire novel spent in the intimate, gossip-filled environment of the Soho [[italian-diaspora|Italian community]], Carlo represents the larger, indifferent world beyond it.

## The Station as Threshold

The station scene in which Carlo appears is one of the novel's most symbolically charged passages. Stations are liminal spaces — thresholds between arrival and departure, between one life and another — and Carlo, as its porter, is an unwitting guardian of that threshold. His physical labor of lifting trunks and reading labels (164-013)(164-014) is rendered in the same precise, unglamorous prose that Hall uses for all the novel's depictions of work, whether in restaurant kitchens or on railway platforms.

Carlo's indifference to [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] is not cruelty but simply the natural condition of the modern world: in the station, as in the city, everyone is a stranger. The Soho [[italian-diaspora|Italian community]] that has defined [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s life is built on the opposite principle — everyone knows everyone, every family history is common knowledge, every action is observed and judged. Carlo, by contrast, does not even look at [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s face; he looks at the luggage label (164-015). It is a moment that crystallizes [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s journey from the intimate to the anonymous, from the known to the unknown.

## The Weight of Luggage

Carlo's groan at the weight of the trunk (164-014) is a small but telling detail. Throughout the novel, physical objects carry metaphorical weight — the food that symbolizes heritage and obligation, the furniture that represents domestic stability, and now the luggage that [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] carries into his uncertain future. Carlo, who has no knowledge of what the trunk contains or what it means, experiences only its literal heaviness. The gap between Carlo's perception (a heavy trunk) and the reader's knowledge (the accumulated weight of a life being left behind) is characteristic of Hall's narrative method: meaning resides not in the event itself but in the reader's awareness of everything that has led to it.

## Role in the Narrative

Carlo's brief appearance serves as a narrative marker of transition. The station is a liminal space — a place of arrivals and departures — and Carlo, as its porter, is a guardian of that threshold. His indifference to [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] underscores the protagonist's [[loneliness-and-alienation|isolation]] in the novel's closing movement: the community that once defined him has been left behind, and in its place is only the impersonal efficiency of a station porter who "groaned" at the weight of a trunk.
