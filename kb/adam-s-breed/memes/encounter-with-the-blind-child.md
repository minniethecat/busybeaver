## Summary

While walking through the streets of London during the late war period,
[[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca Boselli]] encounters a blind child — the son of an organ grinder —
begging on the street. The sight of the child's affliction triggers an
overwhelming emotional and spiritual crisis, forcing [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] to confront
the reality of innocent suffering. His response — to flee — becomes a turning
point in his spiritual journey.

## Detailed Account

The encounter begins when [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] sees the child: "And [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]], who cared
not at all for children, must gaze and gaze at this child as though he were
suddenly suffering with him, as though in some curious way he belonged to
those sightless eyes" (151-009). The child's blindness pierces [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s
habitual detachment, awakening an empathetic anguish he cannot control.

The experience overwhelms him: "But [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] had turned and was rushing away;
away and away from the sightless child, away and away from suffering and
affliction, and the great, blind sadness of the world" (151-018). His flight
is not merely physical but existential — an attempt to escape the unbearable
fact of suffering itself, which the child's blind eyes have made inescapably
present to him.

The encounter leaves [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] shattered. The narrative describes "the great,
blind sadness of the world" as "a quiet, persistent, intangible presence"
(151-018). He returns home to [[maddalena-boselli|Maddalena]], who finds him crouched and sobbing:
"She sat there gently stroking his hair, murmuring her pity for the little
blind creature, murmuring her pity for the desolate man who crouched there
sobbing at her knee" (152-002). [[maddalena-boselli|Maddalena]]'s response is a wordless, maternal
compassion — she does not demand explanation, but simply shares in his
anguish.

Later, [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] attempts to articulate what he experienced: "I saw something
horrible today," he might begin, "a poor wretch in khaki grinding an organ—"
(158-002). But words fail him. The encounter resists narrative — it is a
moment of pure, unmediated confrontation with the suffering that the novel's
restaurant world, with its careful hierarchies and rituals, has allowed him
to avoid.

The blind child is connected to a wider image-pattern running through the
novel. The phrase "a little blind boy" recurs when locals confirm they had
seen "an old man selling matches with a little blind boy" (156-011). Later,
in the forest, [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] reflects on a blinded bird: "why the singing bird
had been blinded. He wanted to think out the meaning of blind things"
(181-003). This meditation extends the blind child encounter into the novel's
theological dimension, linking innocent physical blindness to a larger
spiritual blindness afflicting humankind.

## Participants

- **[[gian-luca-boselli]]** — the protagonist, whose habitual emotional
  detachment is shattered by the encounter. His inability to bear the sight
  of the child's suffering reveals the fragility of his spiritual composure.

- **[[maddalena-boselli]]** — [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s wife, who comforts him afterward,
  murmuring "her pity for the little blind creature" and "her pity for the
  desolate man" (152-002). Her compassion contrasts with [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s flight.

## Consequences

The encounter with the blind child is a pivotal moment in [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]]'s
spiritual trajectory. It exposes the limits of his restaurant-world identity
— the headwaiter's composure, the Italian's pride — and reveals beneath it a
raw vulnerability to the fact of suffering. His flight from the child
prefigures his larger flight from the Doric, from London, and ultimately from
the social self he has constructed.

More broadly, the encounter crystallizes one of the novel's central themes:
the inadequacy of all human structures — restaurants, [[catholic-faith|religion]], art, family —
in the face of innocent suffering. [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] can neither help the child nor
bear to look at him. He can only run.
