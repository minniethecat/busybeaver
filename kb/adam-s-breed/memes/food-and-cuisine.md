## Definition

Food in *Adam's Breed* is the primary medium through which the Soho [[italian-diaspora|Italian community]] expresses identity, affection, status, and connection to the homeland. Cooking is described alongside [[catholic-faith|religion]] as one of "the most important things" in the community's life — a pairing that elevates cuisine to the level of the sacred [024-001].

## Manifestation

The novel presents food across multiple registers: as domestic ritual, as professional craft, and as cultural memory. [[rosa-varese|Rosa]]'s kitchen is a site of both nourishment and exclusion — [[gian-luca-boselli|Gian-Luca]] is driven away when he "bothers" her there, marking the kitchen as a domain of adult female authority [041-002]. The smells of the Italian grocery shop — "oil and cans and Chianti and a little of split peas" — constitute a sensory map of the immigrant neighborhood [014-003].

At the professional level, the restaurant kitchen at [[the-doric|The Doric]] operates on an entirely different scale. [[moscatone|Moscatone]] presides over "gigantic gas rings" where cooking "appeared to be all done by gas," an industrial operation that nonetheless produces food whose "fame" spreads through London [053-015][065-011]. The restaurant's success is measured in the reputation of its cooking, and [[moscatone|Moscatone]]'s wages are augmented accordingly.

Communal meals serve as the novel's primary staging ground for domestic drama. [[rosa-varese|Rosa]]'s supper of polenta with Chianti brings the family together but also provides the setting for [[berta|Berta]]'s greed and [[geppe|Geppe]]'s complaints [028-007]. The ritual of eating together — the wiping of mouths, the pouring of wine — is rendered with ethnographic precision [028-031].

## Food as Cultural Memory

The memory of [[lucrezia|Lucrezia]]'s cooking — particularly her risotto — functions as a standard against which all subsequent cooking is measured. The Padrone recalls "what a marvelous risotto" she made, and her death occasions genuine communal grief expressed through the language of food [011-025]. The dishes themselves — risotto, zabaione, polenta, Chianti — constitute a catalog of Italian regional cuisine transported to London, each dish carrying the weight of homeland memory.

[[nerone|Nerone]]'s memories of Amarena and small caged birds, recalled alongside risotto, suggest that food is inseparable from the broader sensory landscape of the lost Italian home [024-008]. To cook Italian food in London is to perform an act of cultural preservation.
