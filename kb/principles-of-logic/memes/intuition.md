# Intuition

In Bradley's logical system, intuition is the act of immediately perceiving a new relation within a constructed whole. It is the second essential element of inference: "the process is a construction and the result an intuition, while the union of both is logical demonstration" (003-001). Intuition thus names the moment of insight that follows the synthetic construction of premises.

## Overview

Bradley embeds intuition within his general theory of inference. Every inference combines two elements: "it is in the first place a process, and in the second place a result. The process is an operation of synthesis; it takes its data and by ideal construction combines them into a whole. The result is the perception of a new relation within that unity" (003-001). Intuition is this result — the direct seeing of a connection that was not visible before the synthetic operation.

This theory of intuition is distinctive because it makes intuition dependent on prior intellectual construction. Unlike romantic or mystical conceptions of intuition as a faculty that bypasses discursive reasoning, Bradley's intuition is the culmination of reasoning: "we first do a certain work on our data; and this work is the construction. We then by inspection discover and select a new relation, and this intuition is the conclusion" (003-004).

## Key Aspects

### Intuition as the Conclusion of Inference

The conclusion of an inference is not a proposition derived by rule but an intuition: "then, leaving out of view those parts of our construction in which we are not interested, we extract the conclusion we desire to assert" (003-004). Bradley explicitly rejects the view that inference proceeds by the mechanical application of syllogistic rules. The final step — seeing that A-C follows from A-B and B-C — is an act of direct intellectual perception.

### Construction and Intuition

Intuition and construction are complementary and inseparable. Bradley illustrates this with a geometric example: "take three pictures on a wall A, B, and C; if I see them all at once as A—B—C there seems so far no inference, for my mere analytic judgment will give me A-C" (003-005). But when the relations are given separately (first A—B, then B—C), mere analysis cannot yield A—C. "I must first put them together as A—B-C, and this is the construction of a synthetic judgment. I then perceive A-C, and this is the" intuition (003-005).

### Intuition and Comparison

Bradley extends the concept of intuition to acts of comparison. When we compare two data sets and discover identity or difference between them, this discovery is itself an intuition following an ideal process. "There is an ideal operation on ABC, DBF, and that operation presents us with a judgment. We did not know that ABC, DBF were alike, now we know that they possess the point B in common, and this intuition depends on the operation" (003-016).

### Intuition in Mathematical Reasoning

Bradley recognizes mathematical demonstration as a paradigm case of intuition following construction. When geometers demonstrate by superposition that one triangle equals another, "the demonstration removes a barrier from our sight, or provides us with artificial vision, but it does not produce the fact from its elements" (003-015). The truth is seen in the constructed whole; this seeing is intuition.

## Historical Context

Bradley's concept of intuition draws on both Kantian and Aristotelian sources. Kant distinguished sensible intuition (the immediate presentation of objects) from intellectual intuition (which Kant denied to human beings). Bradley transforms this: his intuition is intellectual but is always mediated by a prior synthetic construction. This positions Bradley's account closer to Aristotle's *nous* — the intellectual grasp of first principles — while embedding it within a discursive process. The emphasis on "inspection" and "seeing" reflects a broader 19th-century tendency to model logical knowledge on visual perception, shared by figures like Jevons and Venn.
