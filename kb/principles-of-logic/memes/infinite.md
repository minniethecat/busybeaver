The concept of the infinite in Bradley's philosophy has a dual character. There is the "spurious infinite" — the endless serial progression of phenomena in space and time that can never reach completion — and there is the genuine infinite of the absolute whole, in which all finite determinations find their resolution.

## Overview

Bradley devotes significant attention to the problem of infinity, particularly in relation to space, time, and the nature of judgment. He argues that "the infinite process in space and time" (002-038) is a necessary consequence of the relational character of finite thought, but it is also a mark of its incompleteness. The very fact that we can never complete an infinite series reveals that finite thought, when pushed to its limits, points beyond itself.

The notion of the "spurious infinite" — a term Bradley adapts from Hegel — describes the endless iteration that characterizes the attempt to exhaust reality through finite categories. "That chase of the spurious infinite, where fruition, ever instant, is ever beyond" (022-009) captures the restless quality of thought that can never rest in any finite determination.

## Key Aspects

### The Spurious Infinite

Bradley's critique of the spurious infinite targets the idea that reality can be constructed from an endless addition of finite units. Whether in space, time, or logical analysis, the attempt to reach the whole by adding parts yields only "an infinite series" (007-016), which by its nature can never be complete. The spurious infinite is not false but inadequate — it is the form finitude takes when it strives for totality.

### Infinity and Space-Time

Bradley argues that the spatial and temporal series are inherently infinite. We can always divide space further or extend time further, and this "indefinite detail and endless relation" (002-023) shows that space and time are appearances, not ultimate realities. They are the form in which the finite mind represents the infinite richness of the absolute.

### Individual and Infinite

For Bradley, the individual and the infinite are not opposed but "inseparable characters" (006-010). Every finite individual, properly understood, contains a reference to the infinite whole within which alone it has its complete determination. The true infinite is not opposed to the finite but is the whole that includes and transcends all finitude.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of the infinite draws heavily on Hegel's distinction between the "bad" (spurious) infinite and the true infinite. For both philosophers, the true infinite is not an endless series but the self-contained whole that is present in and through its finite moments.
