# Law of Excluded Middle

The Law of Excluded Middle is the logical principle asserting that every possible judgment must be either true or false. In Bradley's analysis, this principle is not an independent axiom but a special case of [[disjunction|disjunctive judgment]], grounded in the nature of assertion and [[negation|denial]] within a disparate sphere.

## Overview

Bradley treats the Law of Excluded Middle alongside Identity and [[contradiction|Contradiction]] in Chapter V of Book I, but he significantly repositions it within his logical system. Rather than accepting it as a self-evident first principle, he argues that it is derivative — a special case of [[disjunctive-judgment|disjunction]] that cannot be considered co-extensive with [[disjunctive-judgment|disjunction]] itself. (005-008)

The principle rests on the idea that any subject, when a relation to any quality is suggested, is determined with respect to that predicate within the area of position and negation. There is no third alternative between affirmation and [[negation|denial]]. (005-007)

Bradley's treatment is notable for demystifying the principle. He argues against those who treat Excluded Middle as a magical formula for conjuring knowledge from the unknown, and he carefully distinguishes it from the broader category of [[disjunction|disjunctive judgment]]. (005-010)

## Key Aspects

### Excluded Middle as a Case of Disjunction

For Bradley, the Excluded Middle is "merely one instance and application" of the disjunctive principle. Like any [[disjunctive-judgment|disjunction]], it involves: (i) a real subject known to be further determined, (ii) a quality that falls within a certain area of disparates, and (iii) the determination of the subject as one single member of that area. (005-008)

The dual and contradictory alternative of "b or not-b" rests on the existence of contrary opposites. Common disparate [[disjunctive-judgment|disjunction]] is the base, and the dual alternative of assertion and [[negation|denial]] develops from that basis — in the same way that contradiction develops from exclusion. (005-008)

### The Transcending Principle

Bradley identifies a point where Excluded Middle goes beyond ordinary [[disjunction|disjunctive judgment]]. It contains a further principle: it asserts a common quality of all possible existence. It claims that every element of the Cosmos possesses a quality which can determine it logically in relation to every other element. (005-008)

This transcendental principle is prior to the actual [[disjunctive-judgment|disjunction]]. It says beforehand that there is a ground of relation, though it does not know what the relation is. The [[disjunctive-judgment|disjunction]] proceeds only when it is further established that the relation falls within a disparate sphere. (005-009)

### Objections and Bradley's Response

Bradley addresses the objection that one must sometimes answer "both" or "neither" rather than a simple yes or no. He argues that such cases always involve ambiguous questions framed from false alternatives: "There may be a middle between continuity and discretion; there can be none between continuous and not-continuous." (005-009)

Against Mill's suggestion that the "Unmeaning" constitutes a third possibility between true and false, Bradley responds that a proposition which has no meaning is no proposition at all — and if it does mean anything, it is either true or else false. (005-010)

### Jevons's Symbolic Argument

Bradley engages with Jevons's symbolic argument that judgments of the form "A = B or b" must be incorrect. While Bradley agrees with the final conclusion that we should write "A = AB or Ab" instead, he finds Jevons's [[inference|reasoning]] unsound and cannot reconcile the conclusion with the process. The issue turns on whether "B or b" represents anything determinate — a sphere of "B or b" is wholly unlimited and thus cannot equal something definite like A. (005-011)

## Historical Context

The Law of Excluded Middle has been a cornerstone of logical theory since Aristotle's formulation in the *Metaphysics*. Bradley's treatment reflects his broader project of grounding logical principles in the nature of judgment rather than treating them as abstract formal axioms. His analysis draws on Hegel's critique of formal logic, and his demotion of Excluded Middle from independent axiom to derivative case of [[disjunctive-judgment|disjunction]] reverses the traditional ordering found in Aristotle and in nineteenth-century logicians such as Mill and Jevons.

Bradley's position also engages with the principle's role in indirect proof and formal [[inference|reasoning]]. In Book III, he returns to Excluded Middle to argue that no "mystical force arises from the proof of a self-contradiction" — a point directed against those who treat indirect proof as having special logical authority. (016-006)
