# Abstraction (Logical)

The process of isolating a universal content from the concrete particulars in which it appears. In Bradley's logical system, abstraction is the operation that produces general [[idea|ideas]], but abstract universals divorced from concrete reality are mere "poor abstractions" — inference must reconnect them to the real.

## Overview

Abstraction stands at the boundary between psychology and logic in Bradley's system. It is the process by which we "abstract from the concrete" (001-005) to obtain universal meanings. Yet Bradley consistently warns against treating abstractions as if they existed independently. "In abstraction from its relations to my psychical event, the idea has no existence" (001-006). The general idea that results is a "mere abstraction" unless embedded in an ideal construction that restores its connection to reality.

Bradley's treatment of abstraction is double-edged. On one hand, abstraction is indispensable — without it there are no universal meanings, and without universals there is no judgment or inference. On the other hand, abstraction left unchecked produces "unreal abstractions" (006-013) that logical theory mistakes for independent entities. The "concrete universal" is Bradley's preferred alternative: a universal that contains its differences within itself rather than excluding them.

## Key Aspects

### Abstraction and the General Idea

For Bradley, "the general idea is a mere abstraction" (002-029) when considered apart from its concrete instances. The sound of a word, once associated with a meaning, "has not been got by further abstraction" (006-003) — it acquires universality through use, not through a psychological process of extracting common features. This is a key departure from empiricist abstractionism.

### The Poverty of Bare Abstractions

Bradley repeatedly criticizes "poor abstractions" and "bare meanings" as insufficient for logical purposes. "'The tree is green:' but such poor abstractions, such mere bare meanings, are not the stuff of which truth is made" (002-036). A judgment built from abstract universals alone [[privation|lack]]s the connection to reality that makes judgment possible. The universal must be "concrete" — qualified by its differences — to function logically.

### Abstraction in Inference

[[inference|Inference]] involves abstraction at every stage: the [[premise|premises]] are abstracted from their original contexts, and the conclusion abstracts from the [[premise|premises]] through the elision of middle terms. Yet Bradley insists that inference is not mere abstraction — it is "an ideal construction" (010-001) that synthesizes content, not merely selects it. The "product of abstraction" (012-008) appears within the ideal construction, "differenced by the context which contains it."

### Concrete Universal vs. Abstract Universal

Bradley's most distinctive contribution is the distinction between abstract and concrete universals. "The merely particular are unreal abstractions. Concrete universal and concrete particulars are both real, and they are not really separable" (006-013). The concrete universal is not a bare general idea but a living synthesis that includes its differences. This Hegelian concept runs through Bradley's entire treatment of inference and reality.

## Historical Context

Bradley's critique of abstraction continues the German Idealist reaction against British empiricism. His distinction between abstract and concrete universals draws on Hegel, but Bradley's emphasis on the "reality" to which all abstraction must ultimately refer is distinctively his own. The debate about the nature of abstract ideas runs from Locke through Berkeley and Hume to Mill; Bradley's contribution is to show that the logical use of universals requires more than psychological association can provide.
