Will, in Bradley's logical system, denotes the volitional aspect of mental facts — that side of psychical activity which involves striving, desire, and conation. Bradley treats the volitional as inseparable from the intellectual in any complete account of judgment and inference.

## Overview

Bradley's treatment of will appears primarily in his discussion of the psychical background of judgment. In the opening chapter, he notes "the implication of the volitional with the intellectual side" (001-001), indicating that mental activity cannot be cleanly divided into separate faculties. The will is not a separate agent standing behind thought but is interwoven with the very process by which ideas are referred to reality.

Volitional phenomena include desires, strivings, and acts of attention. For Bradley, these are not reducible to mere associations of ideas, as the empirical psychology of his day maintained, but are integral to how the mind engages with its objects. An idea "can often prevent or produce volitions, although the idea is not a fact of the same order as the volition" (001-011), showing the complex causal relationship between thought and will.

## Key Aspects

### Will and Judgment

Bradley's theory of judgment does not treat will as external to the judging process. The act of judgment — attributing an ideal content to reality — involves a volitional element in the selection and synthesis of content. The separation of thought from will is, for Bradley, a useful abstraction rather than a description of actual mental life.

### Volitional Phenomena

In his critique of association psychology, Bradley groups volitions alongside perceptions and feelings as the basic classes of psychical facts (013-007). He argues that the associationist account cannot adequately explain volitional phenomena because it reduces them to mere sequences of ideas, ignoring the active, striving character of will. "In this we have of course volitional phenomena as well as intellectual" (013-015).

### Idea and Volition

Bradley distinguishes between an idea as a psychical existence and its content or meaning. The idea, as a psychical event, can interact causally with volitions; as a logical content, it is referred to reality in judgment. This double nature of the idea — at once psychical and logical — is central to understanding how will and thought interrelate (001-016).

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of will engages with the British empiricist tradition, particularly the association psychology of James Mill and Alexander Bain, who attempted to explain volition in terms of the association of ideas. Bradley rejects this reduction, insisting that volitional phenomena have their own character that cannot be dissolved into sequences of associated ideas. His position is consistent with his broader idealist commitment to seeing mental life as a unified whole rather than a mosaic of atomic facts.
