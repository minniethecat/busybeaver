Cause, in Bradley's treatment, is a category of finite thought — a way of connecting events in temporal succession. While causality is indispensable for science and everyday reasoning, Bradley argues that it cannot be an ultimate principle of reality. The causal relation, like all relations, is an appearance that points beyond itself.

## Overview

Bradley mentions causality primarily in the context of his broader critique of relational thought. An "asserted idea causes action" (001-010) describes a psychical connection, but this does not amount to a logical ground. The causal relation is one of the ways in which finite thought organizes experience, but it shares the inadequacy of all relational categories.

Bradley's treatment of cause is less extensive than his analysis of identity, difference, and judgment, but the same principles apply. The causal relation requires terms that are connected, and the connection itself requires further connections, leading to the same regress that characterizes all relational thought.

## Key Aspects

### Cause and Ground

Bradley distinguishes between causal connection and logical ground. That one idea causes another is a psychological fact; that one judgment grounds another is a logical relation. Confusing these two is a form of psychologism that Bradley consistently resists. "One cause of the error seems to lie in" (001-012) treating logical relations as causal ones.

### Causality as Appearance

Causality, for Bradley, belongs to the realm of appearance. It is the form in which finite thought connects events in time, but it does not reveal the inner nature of reality. In the absolute, the causal series is transcended, and events are connected not by external causal links but by their internal participation in the whole.

### Cause and Explanation

Bradley acknowledges the practical indispensability of causal explanation while denying its metaphysical ultimacy. The search for causes is a legitimate scientific activity, but it cannot reach the final truth because every cause itself requires a cause, leading to an infinite regress.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of causality engages with Hume's critique of causal necessity and with Mill's empiricist account of causation as constant conjunction. For Bradley, both views treat causality as an external relation, missing the internal connection that alone would make causal inference rationally intelligible.
