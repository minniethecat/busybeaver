# Singular Judgment

The singular judgment is the form of judgment that predicates something of an individual subject — typically expressed with a proper name or demonstrative reference. Bradley's extensive analysis of singular judgments in Book I Chapter II shows that they cannot serve as the foundation of logical theory, despite their apparent directness and simplicity.

## Overview

Bradley devotes sustained attention to singular judgments because they appear to offer the simplest case of judgment: a direct predication about a particular individual. If any judgment can be purely categorical — making an unconditional assertion about reality — it should be the singular judgment. (002-006)

His analysis, however, demonstrates the opposite. He divides singular judgments into three classes and examines each in turn, concluding that none achieves the direct, unmediated reference to reality that it claims. The singular judgment, far from being the simplest case, reveals the same complexity that Bradley finds in all judgment forms. (002-029)

This analysis is strategically important for Bradley's broader argument. If even singular judgments contain hidden hypothetical and universal elements, then the traditional distinction between singular, particular, and universal judgments cannot mark a fundamental difference in logical form.

## Key Aspects

### The Three Classes of Singular Judgments

Bradley distinguishes three classes of singular judgments. The first class involves judgments whose subject is indicated by a demonstrative — "this is red" or "I am here." These judgments appear to make direct reference to present experience, but Bradley argues that the demonstrative element is inherently unstable: "this" refers to something that, as soon as it is identified, has already become a universal content. (002-011)

The second class comprises judgments whose subject is a proper name — "Caesar crossed the Rubicon." Proper names appear to refer directly to individuals, but Bradley argues that a proper name functions only because it carries a descriptive content. A name with no associated meaning would not identify a subject at all. (002-014)

The third class involves judgments whose subject is a definite description — "the author of Waverley." These explicitly carry descriptive content and are therefore the easiest for Bradley to assimilate to his general theory, since the descriptive element already introduces universality into the subject position. (002-028)

### The Instability of Singular Reference

Bradley's core argument across all three classes is that singular reference is never pure. The demonstrative "this" cannot fix its reference without bringing universal content to bear; the proper name requires associated descriptions to function; and the definite description explicitly invokes universals. In every case, what appeared to be a direct relation to an individual turns out to be mediated by universal content. (002-030)

This has the important consequence that "All A is B" — the universal judgment — is in a certain sense the more fundamental form. The singular judgment "S is P" is an abbreviation for a judgment that already involves universal elements. Bradley does not deny that we make singular judgments; he denies that they have the logical primacy traditionally claimed for them. (002-034)

### Singular Judgment and Reality

Bradley considers the argument that singular judgments are closer to reality than universal or abstract judgments because they refer to concrete individuals. He acknowledges the force of this claim — there is a sense in which "this rose is red" puts us in more direct contact with reality than "all roses are red." (002-035)

However, he argues that this apparent advantage comes at a cost. The singular judgment's claim to direct reference rests on the contingent presence of the subject to perception, and this contingency undermines its logical standing. When the subject is no longer present, the singular judgment loses its basis — and yet we continue to make judgments about past individuals. This shows that the logical force of judgment cannot depend on singular presence. (002-040)

### The Renunciation of the Singular Foundation

Bradley's conclusion is stark: "the only hope for the singular judgment lies in complete renunciation of its claim to a special logical position." The singular judgment must be understood as one form among others, with no privileged relation to reality. The basis of judgment lies not in singular reference but in the systematic interconnection of ideal content within a single reality. (002-041)

This renunciation is part of Bradley's larger project of dismantling the empiricist picture of knowledge, in which singular judgments of experience serve as the foundation for all other knowledge. Bradley reverses the priority: universal judgments, expressing systematic connections of content, are the fundamental form, and singular judgments are derivative abstractions.

## Historical Context

Bradley's analysis of singular judgments engages with a problem that runs from Aristotle's *De Interpretatione* through Mill's *[[system|System]] of Logic*. The empiricist tradition, from Locke to Mill, had treated singular judgments as epistemologically basic — they are the direct reports of experience on which all other knowledge is built. Bradley's argument that singular judgments are logically derivative rather than foundational is thus a direct challenge to empiricist epistemology.

His treatment also engages with contemporary debates about proper names and reference, anticipating issues that would become central to twentieth-century philosophy of language. While Bradley's idealist framework differs sharply from later analytic approaches, his recognition that the logical analysis of singular reference reveals deep philosophical problems has proven enduring.
