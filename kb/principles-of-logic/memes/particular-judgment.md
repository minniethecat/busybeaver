# Particular Judgment

In Bradley's *Principles of Logic*, the particular judgment — the judgment that "Some S is P" — is submitted to a searching critique. Bradley's conclusion is striking: "A mere particular judgment does not really exist, and, if it did exist, would be utterly worthless" (002-042).

The particular judgment, he argues, is nothing but an imperfect hypothetical judgment whose conditions remain unstated.

## Overview

In the traditional logic, particular judgments are distinguished from universal and singular judgments by their quantity — they predicate of only part of the subject's extension.

"The common logic ranks singular and universal judgments together, and opposes the particular to both of these.

A particular judgment is a judgment which fails to take the subject explicitly and avowedly in the whole of its extension" (006-009).

Bradley finds this classification superficial. His analysis in Chapter II and Chapter VI of Book I drives toward the conclusion that the particular judgment, when pressed, reveals itself as an incomplete hypothetical: "The particular judgment, in the end and really, we found to be nothing but a hypothetical in which the conditions remained imperfect" (007-006).

The deeper question Bradley raises is whether any judgment can genuinely be merely particular — whether the form "Some S is P" can be taken as a self-standing categorical assertion about reality.

## Key Aspects

### The Traditional Doctrine

Traditional logic treats the particular judgment as one of four categorical forms (I-proposition: "Some S is P"). Bradley notes that this classification suffices "for the technical use of the syllogism, and it is perhaps in itself not so foolish as it seems" (006-009). But the classification obscures the logical questions: what does "some" actually mean, and what does the judgment actually assert?

### The Reduction to Hypothetical Form

Bradley's central argument is that "Some S is P" never asserts the actual existence of S.

It asserts only that, given S under certain conditions, P follows.

"The assertion that S does actually exist is not contained in the particular judgment, any more than it is in the problematic" (007-006).

Both particular and [[possible-judgment|problematic judgment]]s are "imperfect hypothetical judgments, and both are founded on a basis of fact believed in or supposed" (007-006).

"If a penny is thrown one thousand times, half the number of throws will most probably give head — we have here a purely intensional judgment.

There is nothing contained in it but bare universals: there is nothing but hypothetical junctions of adjectives" (006-009).

The subject is particular "not because it is numerical, but because it is not so, because over and above it has now been taken as a particular fact" (006-009).

### Relative Particular and Universal

In Bradley's own terminology, he introduces a distinction within the particular. "A relative particular judgment is one where the subject is this or that singular or collection. It is the same as the relative universal judgment, but is taken from another side of its nature" (006-012).

The subject excludes all other individuals, and so is particular; but within itself it has diversity, and so is universal. "It possesses attributes other than the predicate, and may be taken within another context. It thus serves as a middle term in [[inference|reasoning]], as is shown in the third of the syllogistic figures" (006-012).

### The Metaphysics of Particularity

Bradley's analysis of the particular judgment connects to his metaphysical views on particularity and universality. He sets out theses: "Nothing that is real is particular," "All that is real is universal," "Nothing that is real is particular," and "Most that is real is particular" — and he holds "the truth of all these propositions" (006-009).

The resolution: "The abstract universal and the abstract particular are what does not exist. The concrete particular and the concrete universal both have reality, and they are different names for the individual" (006-009). The particular, as merely particular, is an abstraction that cannot be real — it is "a metaphysical ens rationis, an abstract universal which can not be real" (006-009).

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of the particular judgment is influenced by the German logicians, especially [[christoph-von-sigwart|Sigwart]], whose analysis of [[possible-judgment|problematic judgment]]s Bradley acknowledges (007-006). The connection between particular and [[possible-judgment|problematic judgment]]s — that "Some S is P" is equivalent to "S may be P" — is drawn from [[christoph-von-sigwart|Sigwart]]'s work.

Bradley's r[[immediate-inference|eduction]] of the particular judgment to the hypothetical form is also a key move in his critique of the syllogism: if the particular premise is not genuinely categorical, then syllogisms employing particular [[premise|premises]] have a different logical character than traditional doctrine assumes.
