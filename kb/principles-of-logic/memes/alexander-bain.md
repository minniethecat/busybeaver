# Alexander Bain

Alexander Bain (1818–1903) was a Scottish philosopher, psychologist, and logician
whose works on the senses, the intellect, and the emotions established him as a
leading figure in British associationist psychology.

In Bradley's *The Principles
of Logic*, [[bain|Bain]] serves as the principal representative of the psychologistic approach
to logic that Bradley seeks to overcome — his theory that belief is readiness to act
is subjected to sustained critique in the opening chapter.

## Life and Works

Alexander Bain was born in Aberdeen, Scotland, and educated at Marischal College.
After working as a journalist, he became Professor of Logic
at the University of Aberdeen in 1860, a position he held for twenty years. His
major works include *The Senses and the Intellect* (1855), *The Emotions and the
Will* (1859), *Mental and Moral Science* (1868), and a two-volume *Logic* (1870).

[[bain|Bain]] was a close associate of [[john-stuart-mill|John Stuart Mill]] and shared Mill's empiricist
commitments, though he differed on important points of detail. Together with Mill,
he represents the British inductive and associationist tradition against which
Bradley's Idealist logic defines itself.

## Key Contributions to Psychology

[[bain|Bain]]'s psychology was grounded in physiological [[association-of-ideas|associationism]] — the view that
mental phenomena can be explained by the association of sensations and [[idea|ideas]],
understood as processes in the nervous system. His *The Senses and the Intellect*
developed a comprehensive account of how sensory experience gives rise to
intellectual powers through the [[association-of-ideas|laws of association]].

[[bain|Bain]] introduced the concept of the "Law of Relativity," which holds that all
knowledge is knowledge of relations and that nothing can be known in isolation.
Bradley references this principle in his discussion of inference:

> …the cardinal mistake of Hegel Prof. Bain's "Law of Relativity," applied to
> the whole field of knowledge, would, if it were true, result in utter
> scepticism.
> — (005-012)

## Theory of Belief

[[bain|Bain]]'s most discussed contribution — and the one that draws the most sustained
attention from Bradley — is his theory that belief is essentially a disposition
to act. On [[bain|Bain]]'s view, to believe a proposition is to be ready to act upon it.
This reduces the logical question of truth and falsity to a psychological question
about behavioral dispositions.

Bradley subjects this theory to detailed criticism in Book I, Chapter I of the
*Principles*. His objections are threefold:

1. The differential (readiness to act) may be absent from beliefs — one can believe
   something without being disposed to act on it
2. The differential may be present without belief — habitual action does not imply
   belief
3. [[belief|Belief]] contains distinctive logical characteristics that the psychological
   account misses entirely

> …the logical mistake which Professor Bain has committed is to argue from the
> fact that belief does influence action, to the conclusion that belief is
> nothing but this inducement to act.
> — (001-012)

Bradley acknowledges the influence of [[bain|Bain]]'s work while rejecting its foundations:

> …in justice to the credit of Professor Bain… the view that in judgment we
> have always a reference to reality is one which we owe in part to him.
> — (001-016)

## Influence on Logical Theory

[[bain|Bain]]'s two-volume *Logic* (1870) was, alongside Mill's *[[system|System]] of Logic*, one of
the major British logical works of the period. Bradley groups [[bain|Bain]] with Mill as
having written "systematic works on Logic" (014-003) that represent the inductive
tradition he seeks to move beyond.

[[bain|Bain]]'s logical theory shares Mill's commitment to induction as the primary form of
inference, treating [[inference|deduction]] (including the syllogism) as derivative. Bradley's
critique of the inductive methods in Book II, Part II engages [[bain|Bain]]'s views along
with Mill's.

Bradley's references to [[bain|Bain]] span the *Principles*:

## Relations to Contemporaries

[[bain|Bain]] worked within the same broad empiricist tradition as [[john-stuart-mill]],
with whom he corresponded extensively. His psychological theories influenced the
development of British psychology through figures like James Sully, whose
*Sensation and Intuition* Bradley cites as engaging [[bain|Bain]]'s doctrines (001-013).

Bradley's critique of [[bain|Bain]] is not dismissive but dialectical — he recognizes
[[bain|Bain]]'s genuine contributions while arguing that the psychological approach to
logic is fundamentally inadequate. In Book III, Bradley acknowledges "the many
services, which Professor Bain has done to our philosophy" (022-012) even as
he maintains his opposition to [[bain|Bain]]'s logical principles.
