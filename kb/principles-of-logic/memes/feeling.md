# Feeling

Feeling, in Bradley's logical psychology, is the immediate, unreflective element of mental life — the raw sensations, emotions, and qualitative states that constitute the starting-point of experience and, crucially, continue to operate within judgment and inference. Bradley's treatment is notable for resisting the sharp separation of feeling from thought that characterizes both empiricist and rationalist traditions.

## Overview

Bradley introduces feeling in the opening chapter of *The Principles of Logic* as part of the texture of mental life that logic must take seriously. He observes that sensations, emotions, and feelings "make my momentary state" and that much of our thinking systematically disregards their role. (001-004) Yet feeling is not merely a primitive given to be overcome by thought; it is an active and persistent factor in the life of judgment.

The relation between feeling and thought is central to Bradley's anti-atomistic psychology. A presentation, accompanied or transformed by feelings, "is as effective practically" as one that is purely intellectual. (001-018) This means that the logical processes of inference and judgment cannot be understood in isolation from the affective and volitional dimensions of mental life.

Bradley's emphasis on feeling reflects his broader idealist commitment to the continuity of experience. The "continuous mass of perception and feeling" is the background against which distinctions are drawn, and to treat these distinctions as absolute separations is to misunderstand the nature of thought. (002-037)

## Key Aspects

### Feeling as Immediate Experience

Bradley treats feeling as what we encounter in immediate experience — "what we encounter in feeling or perception." (002-003) It is the qualitative given that precedes analysis and distinction. Yet this immediacy is never purely passive; feeling already involves an element of attention and selection that points toward the more developed operations of thought.

The immediacy of feeling presents a challenge for logic: if all thought begins from an immediate, unanalyzed whole, how do logical distinctions arise? Bradley's answer is that the distinctions are not imposed from without but emerge through the internal development of the given material itself.

### Feeling and the Life of Judgment

Feeling does not disappear when judgment begins. Bradley argues that "mere urgency of feeling, in the [[privation|absence]] of [[idea|ideas]], might" still influence action and conviction. (001-019) The feelings and emotions associated with [[idea|ideas]] contribute to our holding them for true. (001-011) This is particularly important for understanding belief, which Bradley treats as having an affective dimension that pure intellectualism cannot capture.

In the context of inference, feeling operates as what Bradley calls a "mode of morbid feeling or diseased perception" that can systematically distort [[inference|reasoning]]. (002-024) This is not to say that feeling is always a source of error — rather, it is a permanent factor that logic must reckon with rather than ignore.

### Feeling and Volition

Bradley connects feeling to volitional experience. In his discussion of inference as a practical activity, he notes that the transition from [[premise|premises]] to conclusion often involves a felt urgency that drives the mind forward. (001-016) A presentation accompanied by feeling "produces an action" — a claim that links the logical to the practical in a way that formal logic typically excludes. (001-017)

### Atomism and the Continuity of Feeling

Bradley uses the concept of feeling to critique atomistic psychology. The empiricist attempt to reduce mental life to "atomic and momentary feelings" fails, he argues, because it ignores the continuity that makes any single feeling intelligible. (007-006) The same point applies to desires and feelings: they are not discrete atoms but moments within a continuous process. (013-002, 013-004)

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of feeling engages with the British empiricist tradition (Hume's impressions and [[idea|ideas]]) and with the German idealist tradition (Hegel's critique of immediate sense-certainty). His distinctive contribution is to integrate feeling into the logical process itself, rather than treating it as a pre-logical given or as an obstacle to be eliminated. This position influenced later British idealism and, through criticism, the development of analytic philosophy's own account of the relation between emotion and reason.
