# Ideal Experiment

A mental operation in Bradley's logical system whereby ideal content is manipulated under controlled conditions to test logical relations, discover implications, and procure new judgments without reference to actual fact.

## Overview

In Bradley's *Principles of Logic*, the "ideal experiment" occupies a central methodological role. It is the mechanism by which the mind tests the logical relations among its ideal contents—concepts, judgments, and their interconnections—without appealing to external reality. Bradley characterizes it as "the application of a certain test" (002-032) to ideal content, parallel in some respects to scientific experimentation but conducted entirely within the domain of thought.

The ideal experiment is not merely a metaphor. For Bradley, it is the operative engine of inference itself. When we reason from [[premise|premises]] to a conclusion, we are performing an ideal experiment: we hold certain ideal contents fixed (the [[premise|premises]]), introduce a suggested connection, and observe whether the combination maintains itself or collapses into contradiction. This process is "the movement of an ideal experiment which gives a judgment we had not before" (018-004).

Bradley deploys the ideal experiment to address a fundamental problem: how can inference yield new knowledge? If the conclusion were already contained in the [[premise|premises]], inference would be mere analysis; if it were wholly external, inference would be arbitrary. The ideal experiment bridges this gap by showing how the mind actively synthesizes ideal elements, testing their compatibility and producing genuinely novel results that are nevertheless grounded in the initial data.

The concept recurs throughout the *Principles*, appearing in discussions of judgment (Book I), inference (Book II), and the nature of logical necessity. It is especially prominent in Bradley's critique of formal logic, where he argues that [[syllogism|syllogistic reasoning]] cannot be understood without reference to the ideal experiment that underlies it.

## Key Aspects

### The Ideal Experiment as a Test of Logical Compatibility

Bradley describes the ideal experiment as "the application of a certain test" (002-032). The test consists in bringing two or more ideal contents into relation and observing whether they can coexist without contradiction. If they can, the synthesis is valid; if they cannot, one or the other must be rejected. "We have reached a result by ideal experiment; and of this we can say, Thought does assert it" (018-005).

### Ideal Experiment and Inference

The ideal experiment is "an inference. For it is an ideal experiment which procures a new result" (018-006). Bradley insists that the process of inferring is not a mechanical application of rules but an active mental operation. The mind does not passively register logical connections; it constructs them through the ideal experiment, testing whether a suggested relation "maintains itself in ideal experiment, and abolishes incompatible [[idea|ideas]]" (018-008).

### The Distinction from Real Experiment

Bradley is careful to distinguish the ideal experiment from real-world experimentation. The ideal experiment operates entirely upon ideal content—the "unsubstantial region of ideal experiment" (003-005)—and its results hold regardless of actual fact. This is what gives logic its necessity: "the necessity is as strong where the [[premise|premises]] are known to be false" (007-005). The ideal experiment tracks logical, not causal, relations.

### The Role in Judgment Synthesis

The ideal experiment plays a crucial role in the synthesis that constitutes judgment. When we judge that "S is P," we are not merely associating two [[idea|ideas]]; we are performing an ideal experiment in which the ideal content S is developed, through its internal relations, to yield P. "First A, then A in ideal experiment becoming Aδ, and last the assertion" (019-001). The judgment is the outcome of this ideal process.

### Ideal Experiment and the Major Premise

Bradley makes the striking claim that the ideal experiment not only tests conclusions but can also prove the [[premise|major premise]] itself. "On the contrary our process is the ideal experiment which proves this major" (018-007). The [[premise|major premise]] of a syllogism is not an independently given truth; it is established through the same ideal experimentation that it subsequently governs.

## Historical Context

Bradley's concept of the ideal experiment draws on the German idealist tradition, particularly Hegel's dialectical method, in which conceptual determinations are tested by their ability to maintain themselves through internal contradiction. However, Bradley gives the notion a more explicitly methodological cast. Where Hegel's dialectic is driven by immanent contradiction, Bradley's ideal experiment is a deliberate mental operation—a test the thinker performs upon his own ideal contents.

The concept also engages with Mill's empiricist account of inference and with the emerging psychological literature on thought experiments. Bradley's innovation is to make the ideal experiment constitutive of logical validity itself, rather than a merely heuristic device. This move anticipates aspects of twentieth-century pragmatism and, in a different register, the operationalist philosophy of science.
