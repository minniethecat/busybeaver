# Analytic Judgment

A judgment that works within what is already given in perception or memory, analyzing its content without constructing new connections. In Bradley's logical system, the analytic judgment is a provisional category — "analytic judgments are always tending to become synthetic" (002-028) — and its apparent security is ultimately illusory.

## Overview

Bradley treats the analytic judgment as the simplest form of judgment, the one that appears to demand the least from thought. "The analytic judgment of sense" takes a given fact and analyzes it: "This rose is red" merely makes explicit what perception already contains. "The fact given us is the whole fact" (002-006), and the judgment does no more than articulate a part of that whole.

But Bradley's analysis reveals that analytic judgment, despite its apparent simplicity, is deeply problematic. "In analytic judgments we are equally helpless" (002-016) — the judgment cannot stand on its own because the given itself is not self-sufficient. Every analysis presupposes a synthesis that identifies and stabilizes the elements being analyzed. "The analytic judgment is not per se" (002-038): it depends on a context of synthetic judgments that fix the meaning of its terms and the identity of its subject.

## Key Aspects

### Analysis Within the Given

The defining feature of analytic judgment is that it stays within the boundaries of the already presented. "In analytic judgments the subject is the given fact" (002-012), and the predicate merely selects a feature already present in that fact. The judgment "This wolf is grey" analyzes the presented wolf into subject and attribute, but it does not extend beyond what perception provides. The predicate is already contained in the subject — not logically, as later philosophers would claim, but perceptually.

### The Instability of Analytic Judgment

Bradley's most striking claim is that analytic judgments are inherently unstable. "Analytic judgments are always tending to become synthetic" (002-028). The moment we attend to the elements that analysis distinguishes, we find that their connection is not given but constructed. The "this" that is the subject of analysis is not a simple datum but "an ideal construction" (002-039). As soon as we recognize the constructive element, the judgment ceases to be purely analytic.

### The Relativity of Analytic Judgment

"Let the analytic judgment admit its relativity" (002-039), Bradley urges. The judgment's claim to truth is relative to the context of presentation and to the conceptual apparatus brought by the judging mind. "The analytic judgments of sense are all false" (002-036) if taken as absolute claims about an independent reality. They are true only relative to the context of analysis — and that context is itself a product of synthetic construction.

### Analytic Judgment and Inference

The distinction between analytic and synthetic judgment has consequences for inference. "My mere analytic judgment will give me A-C" (010-003), Bradley notes — if the connection between A and C is already contained in the given, no inference is needed. Real inference requires synthetic judgment to construct a connection not already present. The analytic judgment is thus the limit case where inference is unnecessary because the content is already possessed.

### The Place of Analytic Judgment in the System

Despite its limitations, analytic judgment has a genuine place in Bradley's system. It represents the ideal of a judgment that simply reports what is the case without addition or distortion. While this ideal is ultimately unattainable — every judgment involves some synthesis — it serves as a regulative concept. The effort to make judgments more analytic, to reduce the synthetic element, is the effort to make thought more adequate to reality.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of analytic judgment predates the Kantian revival and the logical positivist appropriation of the analytic-synthetic distinction. For Bradley, the distinction is epistemological and fluid, not logical and fixed. His claim that analytic judgments "are always tending to become synthetic" stands in sharp contrast to the logical empiricist view that analytic truths are true by virtue of meaning alone. Bradley's treatment also engages with the empiricist tradition (Mill, [[alexander-bain|Bain]]) that treated all judgment as ultimately analytic of experience.
