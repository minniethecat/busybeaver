# Formal Reasoning

Formal [[inference|reasoning]] is [[inference|reasoning]] that operates on the bare logical form of propositions, abstracted from their specific material content. Bradley devotes an entire chapter to the critique of this concept, arguing that while formal [[inference|reasoning]] captures an aspect of logical practice, it cannot serve as a complete account of inference.

## Overview

Bradley's chapter on "Formal and [[material-reasoning|Material Reasoning]]" distinguishes two senses in which [[inference|reasoning]] might be called formal. In the first sense, formal [[inference|reasoning]] means using a "bare form" or "skeleton" from which all specific content has been stripped away. In the second sense, it means [[inference|reasoning]] with a "naked form" — a logical structure that operates independently of any particular subject matter. (024-001) (024-002)

Bradley's critique targets both senses. He argues that the first sense — [[inference|reasoning]] with a bare form — is an abstraction that cannot capture the actual movement of thought in inference. The second sense — [[inference|reasoning]] with a naked form — is more defensible but, Bradley contends, still fails to explain what actually makes an inference valid.

The chapter is strategically placed late in the work, after Bradley has already developed his positive account of inference. It serves as a critical retrospective on the formal-logical tradition and a defense of his own idealist alternative.

## Key Aspects

### The Bare Form

Bradley's first target is the idea that valid [[inference|reasoning]] consists in applying a bare logical form to any content whatsoever. On this view, the form "All A is B, all B is C, therefore all A is C" is valid regardless of what A, B, and C stand for. Bradley does not deny that such forms can be abstracted from valid inferences — his objection is that the form alone does not make the inference valid. (024-001)

The problem is that bare forms are empty. They describe the shape of a valid inference but do not explain why that shape is valid. To invoke the bare form as the ground of validity is to mistake a description for an explanation. Bradley compares this to the "clinging illusions" that obscure the real nature of logical connection.

### Formal Reasoning as One Mode Among Others

In its second, more defensible sense, formal [[inference|reasoning]] means [[inference|reasoning]] that attends primarily to formal relations rather than material content. Bradley grants that this is a genuine and useful mode of [[inference|reasoning]] — mathematics is the paradigm case — but insists that it is only one mode among others. (024-003)

The error Bradley diagnoses is not the practice of formal [[inference|reasoning]] but the claim that all valid [[inference|reasoning]] must be formal. This claim, he argues, cannot itself be established by formal [[inference|reasoning]] (on pain of circularity) and is in fact false — much of our actual [[inference|reasoning]], including the [[inference|reasoning]] that establishes logical principles themselves, is not formal in this sense.

### The Material Dimension

Bradley's positive view is that all [[inference|reasoning]] has a material dimension that cannot be eliminated. Even in the most abstract formal [[inference|reasoning]], the symbols and structures employed carry a minimal content — they are about something, even if only about the logical relations themselves. The attempt to reduce inference to pure form always leaves a residue of material content that the formal account cannot explain.

This material dimension is not a defect but the very thing that makes inference possible. [[inference|Inference]] is not the mechanical manipulation of empty forms but the systematic development of ideal content. Form and matter are abstractions from a single logical process, not independent elements that can be separated.

## Historical Context

Bradley's critique of formal [[inference|reasoning]] engages with the dominant tradition in nineteenth-century British logic, represented by Whately, Hamilton, and to some extent Mill. The formal logicians had argued that logic is the science of the form of thought, abstracted from its content — a position that received new impetus from the mathematical logic of Boole and De Morgan.

Bradley's counter-argument draws on idealist resources, particularly Hegel's critique of formal logic, but transposes them into a more analytical idiom. His position influenced the development of British idealism and, through its rejection, helped shape the self-understanding of early analytic philosophy. Russell's early work can be read as an attempt to answer Bradley's challenge by developing a logic that is both formal and capable of capturing [[material-reasoning|material inference]].
