# Possible Judgment

A possible judgment (also called a problematic judgment) is a modal form that asserts a subject-predicate connection as merely possible — "S may be P" — without affirming its actual truth or necessity.

## Overview

In Bradley's logical system, possible judgment occupies the lowest rung of modality. It expresses the bare conceivability of a connection without any commitment to actual fact. Bradley discusses this form in the context of Kant's table of judgments, where "problematic" judgments stand alongside assertoric and apodictic as the three modal categories. (007-003)

The possible judgment raises a fundamental question about the nature of logical truth. When we say "S-P is possible," what do we actually mean? Bradley argues that in the lowest sense of possibility, "it is really wrong to call S-P a judgment at all," because bare possibility [[privation|lack]]s the assertion of reality that characterizes genuine judgment. (007-004) This criticism forms part of his broader challenge to formal logic's classification of judgments by modal status.

Bradley's treatment of possible judgment is connected to his theory of inference. He maintains that the distinction between possible and actual truth collapses under scrutiny — what appears as a mere possibility often conceals an implicit assertion about conditions or data. (002-006) Every possible judgment, he argues, already involves a reference to the conditions under which it would be true.

## Key Aspects

### Possibility as Bare Conceivability

The most minimal sense of possible judgment is that S-P is not self-contradictory. Bradley notes that bare possibility "by itself is nothing" (001-005), a mere abstraction from the concrete conditions of actual judgment. The merely negative criterion — [[privation|absence]] of contradiction — provides no positive ground for asserting the judgment.

### The Passage from Possibility to Actuality

Bradley identifies a critical logical error: arguing from the assertion of possibility to the assertion of actuality. (005-015) A possible judgment does not license an existential conclusion. He insists that we must not move from possibility claims to actuality claims, since possibility is always relative to assumed data, and changing the data may eliminate the possibility altogether. (005-017)

### Possibility and Inference

The possible judgment has a special role in hypothetical [[inference|reasoning]]. Bradley argues that modality is "the passage from judgment to inference" (007-009), transforming what appears to be a quality of the judgment itself into a relation between [[premise|premises]] and conclusion. A possible judgment is implicitly conditional — "S may be P" is equivalent to "if certain conditions hold, S is P."

### Bradley's Critique of Modal Logic

Bradley subjects the traditional modal distinctions to sustained criticism. He argues that logical modality must be limited to logical considerations rather than psychological ones. (007-001) The apodictic modality does not strengthen our assertions; it merely makes explicit what was already implicit in the data. Bradley's conclusion is that modal differences collapse into differences in the scope and explicitness of inference.

## Historical Context

The classification of judgments by modality originates with Kant's table of judgments, where judgments are divided into problematic (possible), assertoric (actual), and apodictic (necessary). Bradley inherits this tripartite scheme but subjects it to a radical critique informed by his idealist metaphysics.

While Kant treated modality as a distinct category of judgment alongside quantity, quality, and relation, Bradley argues that modality is not an independent dimension but a reflection of the judgment's place in an inferential chain. A judgment appears "possible" only when its supporting data are incompletely specified; "necessary" only when its inferential basis is fully explicit. This r[[immediate-inference|eduction]] of modality to inference is characteristic of Bradley's monistic logic.
