# William Hamilton

Sir William Hamilton (1788–1856) was a Scottish metaphysician and logician whose *Lectures on Metaphysics and Logic* and influential edition of Thomas Reid's works provide the source for Bradley's key technical term "[[redintegration|Redintegration]]" in the *Principles of Logic*.

## Overview

William Hamilton enters Bradley's *Principles of Logic* primarily as the source of the term "[[redintegration|Redintegration]]" — Bradley's preferred name for the fundamental law of mental reproduction that he opposes to the empiricist [[association-of-ideas|laws of association]]. "This may be called the law of [[redintegration|Redintegration]]. For we may take this name from Sir W. Hamilton (Reid, p. 897), having found nothing else that we could well take" (013-004). The borrowing is significant: Bradley appropriates a term from the Scottish common-sense tradition while radically reinterpreting its theoretical content.

Hamilton's presence in the *Principles* is mediated largely through [[john-stuart-mill|J.S. Mill]]'s *Examination of Sir William Hamilton's Philosophy* (1865), which Bradley cites extensively in his critique of [[association-of-ideas|associationism]]. Bradley engages with Hamilton both as a source of terminology and as a representative of philosophical positions that, while closer to Bradley's own than Mill's empiricism, still fall short of Bradley's idealist logic.

## Key Ideas

### Redintegration

Hamilton's concept of redintegration — the law that any part of a mental state tends to reinstate the whole — is the foundation upon which Bradley builds his own theory of mental reproduction. Bradley's formulation closely follows Hamilton's: "Any part of a single state of mind tends, if reproduced, to re-instate the remainder; or Any element tends to reproduce those elements with which it has formed one state of mind" (013-004). However, Bradley radicalizes the concept: where Hamilton treated redintegration as a psychological law, Bradley uses it to argue for the logical priority of identity over association.

### Defense of Associationism

Hamilton attempted to defend the [[association-of-ideas|laws of association]] against continental critics. Bradley discusses Hamilton's reply to an objection by the German philosopher Maas, who had argued that similarity — being an objective relation between [[idea|ideas]] — cannot explain the subjective connection of association. "Sir. W. Hamilton (Reid. p. 914) has replied to this argument by a criticism which shows that he did not understand it" (013-014). Bradley sides with Maas against Hamilton, arguing that Hamilton's defense fails to address the fundamental logical problem.

### Logic and the Quantification of the Predicate

Hamilton is known in the history of logic for his doctrine of the quantification of the predicate — the view that the predicate of a proposition should be quantified just as the subject is. While Bradley does not engage directly with this doctrine in the *Principles*, Hamilton's broader logical project of reforming formal logic through quantification provides part of the background against which Bradley develops his own critique of formal logic.

## Bradley's Critique

Bradley's treatment of Hamilton is characterized by selective appropriation rather than systematic refutation. Bradley takes Hamilton's term "[[redintegration|Redintegration]]" while rejecting the psychological framework in which Hamilton deployed it. For Hamilton, redintegration was a law of [[association-of-ideas|mental association]]; for Bradley, it becomes a principle of ideal identity — the thesis that reproduction is governed not by mechanical association but by the identity of content.

Bradley's critique of Hamilton is sharpest on the topic of association. When Hamilton attempts to defend the associationist account against Maas's objection, Bradley dismisses the defense as resting on a misunderstanding: Hamilton "did not understand it" (013-014). Bradley aligns himself with the German critics of [[association-of-ideas|associationism]] — Maas, Wundt, and ultimately Lotze — against the British defenders, including Hamilton.

Bradley also follows Mill in noting Hamilton's reliance on the concept of unconscious mental processes — a notion Bradley regards as theoretically suspect. The reference to [[john-stuart-mill|J.S. Mill]]'s *Hamilton* (013-008, 013-018, 013-025) indicates that Bradley's engagement with Hamilton is triangulated through Mill's critical examination, which Bradley uses both to understand Hamilton's positions and to expose weaknesses in Mill's own empiricist framework.

## Influence

Sir William Hamilton was the leading figure of the Scottish school of common-sense philosophy in the mid-19th century, holding the Chair of Logic and Metaphysics at Edinburgh. His *Lectures on Metaphysics and Logic* (1859–1860) and his annotated edition of Reid's works were standard texts in British philosophy. Hamilton's influence was eclipsed by [[john-stuart-mill|J.S. Mill]]'s devastating *Examination* (1865), which Bradley draws upon. For Bradley, Hamilton represents a transitional figure: closer to the truth than the empiricists in recognizing the holistic character of mental life (through redintegration), yet still trapped within a psychological framework that cannot reach the logical insight Bradley demands.
