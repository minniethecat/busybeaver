Reality, in Bradley's logical system, is the ultimate subject to which all ideas and judgments ultimately refer. It stands in opposition to appearance, and the nature of this opposition is one of the central themes of *The Principles of Logic*.

## Overview

Bradley treats reality not as a mere aggregate of sensible facts but as the ultimate ground to which all judgment refers. An idea, taken by itself, is not reality but a psychical state; judgment is the act of attributing an idea to reality. The distinction between idea and reality is fundamental to understanding truth and falsehood, as truth consists in the proper reference of an ideal content to the real (001-009).

## Key Aspects

### Idea and Reality

Bradley insists that in judgment, "no idea but always reality" is the ultimate referent (001-008). The idea is a psychical existence — a particular mental event — but its meaning or content is what is referred to reality in judgment. The opposition between idea and reality depends upon the distinction between the existence of an idea as a psychical fact and its content as a logical meaning (001-016).

### Appearance vs. Reality

The distinction between appearance and reality runs through Bradley's work. An appearance is not what it seems; it may be "either not real at all, or real only in a sense and to a degree" (001-018). Bradley's critique of mere appearance leads to his view that what appears must in some sense be real, yet the appearance as such is not the final truth about reality (001-019).

### Ultimate Reality

In ultimate reality, what must be said of any idea or judgment is that it contributes to a single, comprehensive whole. Bradley argues that psychical states, taken as isolated facts, cannot be the final truth; "in ultimate reality what must be said of it" is that all judgments participate in a single system (001-001).

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of reality draws on the idealist tradition from Kant and Hegel, but his emphasis on the logical structure of judgment as the means by which we approach reality is distinctive. For Bradley, logic is not merely formal but is the study of how thought relates to the real.
