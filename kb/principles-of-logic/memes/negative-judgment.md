# Negative Judgment

The judgment that denies a predicate of a subject, as in "the tree is not yellow." In Bradley's logic, [[negation|negative judgment]] presents distinctive difficulties that expose the limitations of the subject-predicate form and reveal the true ground of negation in the positive character of the real.

## Overview

Bradley treats [[negation|negative judgment]] as a crucial test case for any theory of judgment. "The [[negation|negative judgment]] presents such difficulties that we must postpone its full discussion" (001-006) — these difficulties are not peripheral but strike at the heart of what judgment is. The central question is: what fact does a [[negation|negative judgment]] assert? "What fact is asserted in [[negation|negative judgment]]s? Has every negation I choose to make a fact corresponding to it?" (002-003).

Bradley's answer is that [[negation|negative judgment]] does not assert a negative fact. Rather, it recognizes that the real already excludes the denied predicate. "In the [[negation|negative judgment]] where 'yellow' is denied, the real basis is not the [[privation|absence]] of yellow but the presence of green" (003-002). The ground of negation is always positive — the real contains something that excludes the denied content.

## Key Aspects

### The Problem of Negative Facts

If judgment always refers an ideal content to reality, what reality does a [[negation|negative judgment]] refer to? Bradley rejects the idea that there are negative facts — "not-yellow" is not a feature of the world. Instead, [[negation|negative judgment]] is grounded in the positive character of the real. "The exclusion by presented fact is the foundation" (003-002); a [[negation|negative judgment]] simply makes this exclusion explicit.

### Varieties of Negative Judgment

Bradley identifies several kinds of [[negation|negative judgment]], each corresponding to a different form of positive exclusion. "The various kinds of [[negation|negative judgment]] follow closely the varieties of affirmative judgment" (003-005). Just as there are categorical, hypothetical, and disjunctive affirmations, there are corresponding forms of negation, each grounded differently in the structure of the real.

### Negative Judgment and the Subject-Predicate Form

The [[negation|negative judgment]] exposes the inadequacy of the traditional subject-predicate-copula analysis. In a [[negation|negative judgment]], the copula cannot be the mere sign of identity — it must express exclusion. This shows that "subject, predicate, and copula are mere superstitions" (001-013) when treated as independent logical constituents. [[judgment|Judgment]] is a single act, not a compound of parts.

### The Limits of Negative Judgment

Bradley notes that we cannot always infer a [[negation|negative judgment]] from the [[privation|absence]] of an affirmative one. "One can not be made use of as the mere negation of the other" (005-009). Negative judgment requires positive knowledge of what the real contains — it is not the default when affirmation fails.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of [[negation|negative judgment]] engages with a problem that runs from Plato's Sophist through Kant's critique of [[negation|negative judgment]] to Hegel's dialectic. The question of how negation can be objective — how a [[negation|negative judgment]] can be true — is a perennial philosophical challenge. Bradley's solution, grounding negation in the positive character of the real, is a distinctive contribution that avoids both the reification of negative facts and the subjectivization of negation.
