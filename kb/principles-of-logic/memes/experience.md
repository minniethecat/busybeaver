# Experience

Experience, in Bradley's logical philosophy, is the cumulative body of past encounters whose results are "fixed in an ideal and universal form" and thereby made available for inference and judgment. (001-020) Bradley's treatment is complex: he simultaneously affirms the indispensability of experience as the material basis of all [[inference|reasoning]] and criticizes the empiricist "school of experience" for failing to account for the ideal construction that experience itself presupposes.

## Overview

Bradley locates experience at the foundation of inference. In his account of the practical syllogism, he argues that the [[premise|major premise]] represents "the results of past experience" organized into a general form. (001-017) The transition from particular experiences to general principles is not itself given in experience but is an achievement of thought — a point that separates Bradley from both crude empiricism and transcendental rationalism.

The relationship between experience and logical form is dialectical. Experience provides the content without which inference would be empty, but the organization of experience into systematic form requires an activity of ideal construction that goes beyond anything merely given. It is because experience is "fixed in an ideal and universal form" that it can serve as a premise in [[inference|reasoning]]. (001-020)

Bradley's engagement with the "school of experience" — the British empiricist tradition represented by Mill and [[alexander-bain|Bain]] — is critical but not dismissive. He acknowledges the importance of experiential data while arguing that empiricism's own commitments undermine its attempt to explain logical necessity.

## Key Aspects

### Experience as the Basis of Inference

Bradley insists that all inference ultimately depends on experience. "It is because the results of experience are fixed in an ideal and universal form that they can serve as [[premise|premises]]." (001-020) This is not a concession to empiricism but a recognition that thought requires a material basis. Even the most abstract logical operations trace back, through a chain of idealizations, to the concrete encounters of experience.

The volitional dimension of experience is particularly important for Bradley. [[inference|Inference]] begins in practical situations where past experience guides present action — "his experience, we will suppose, was of a whole" — and the logical articulation of this practical guidance is a later development. (001-021)

### Critique of the "School of Experience"

Bradley repeatedly criticizes the empiricist "school of experience" for treating experience as a collection of discrete atoms rather than as a continuous and systematically connected whole. The thinker "whose passion for 'experience' is mere prejudice in favour of atomism" misses the synthetic activity that makes experience intelligible. (002-040)

The empiricist "fell into the cardinal mistake" of treating the distinctions that reflection introduces into experience as if they were original constituents of experience itself. (005-012) Bradley's point is that experience, as actually lived, is a continuous whole; the atomistic conception is a philosophical construction, not a deliverance of experience.

### Experience and the Junction of Ideas

The experience we have gained, Bradley notes, may "warn us" against following certain trains of association. (002-004) This is a crucial function: experience disciplines inference by registering discrepancies between expected and actual outcomes. The "school of experience" is right to emphasize this corrective function but wrong to think it exhausts the nature of logical thought.

### Ideal Fixation and the Universality of Experience

The key to Bradley's account is the concept of ideal fixation. Experience does not simply accumulate as a heap of instances; it is transformed into ideal content that can be deployed across contexts. This transformation is what makes experience logically significant, and it is a transformation that empiricism cannot explain in its own terms.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of experience belongs to the post-Kantian debate about the relation between experience and the a priori. Where Kant argued that experience presupposes categories not derived from experience, Bradley argues that the very idea of "experience" as a philosophical category is an ideal construction — one that the empiricist tradition uses without acknowledging. His criticism of Mill and [[alexander-bain|Bain]] on this point was influential in British idealism and contributed to the later analytic reaction against idealism's supposed neglect of experience.
