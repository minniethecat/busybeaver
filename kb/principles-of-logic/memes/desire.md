Desire, in Bradley's logical system, denotes the volitional and affective drive that accompanies mental activity. While desire is a psychical fact present in all thinking, Bradley argues that the logical ideal — the pursuit of truth — cannot be reduced to mere desire or satisfaction.

## Overview

Bradley acknowledges that desire is inseparable from actual thinking. Every judgment, every inference, is accompanied by a volitional element: "Our desire is to take it, so far as we can" (001-001). The mind does not operate in a vacuum of pure intellect; it is propelled by interests, by the felt need to resolve contradictions, and by the pursuit of coherence.

However, Bradley is careful to distinguish the logical from the merely psychological. While thinking is always accompanied by desire, the validity of a judgment does not rest on the strength of the desire that accompanies it. The "desire of the mind for an infinite totality" (022-011) — the drive toward complete systematic coherence — is what ultimately distinguishes genuine thinking from mere association.

## Key Aspects

### Desire and Judgment

For Bradley, judgment contains a volitional dimension. When we judge, we "desire to deny my judgment" (005-012) or to affirm it; the act of judgment is not a passive reception of data but an active taking of a stance. Yet this desire is not arbitrary — it is constrained by the ideal content that judgment refers to reality.

### Desire and the Infinite

Bradley connects desire to the "spurious infinite" — the endless pursuit of satisfaction that can never be complete. The mind "desires to have the idea of uniqueness" (002-027) and seeks a final, complete truth, but finite thinking always falls short. This restless desire points beyond itself to the absolute whole in which alone satisfaction would be complete.

### Desire versus Logic

Bradley insists that logic cannot be reduced to psychology. While "it excludes desire and emotion" (002-031) in its pure form, the logical ideal is not indifferent to the mind's strivings. Rather, desire is sublimated into the will to truth, which is the ethical counterpart of the logical impulse.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of desire engages with the empiricist and associationist traditions that sought to explain all mental life in terms of pleasure, pain, and desire. Against this reduction, Bradley argues that the logical — the reference of ideas to reality — introduces a normativity that desire alone cannot ground.
