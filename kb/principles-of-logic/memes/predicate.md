# Predicate

In Bradley's logical system, the predicate is the ideal content that is affirmed of a subject in judgment. "Judgment is the act of referring an ideal content (recognized as such) to a reality beyond the act" (001-010), and the predicate is precisely this ideal content — the universal meaning that is attributed to reality in judging. Unlike traditional logic, which treats predicate as one term in a subject-copula-predicate structure, Bradley dissolves this framework into a direct relation between ideal content and reality.

## Overview

Bradley's treatment of the predicate is revolutionary. He rejects the traditional analysis of judgment into subject, copula, and predicate as "mere superstitions" (001-013). Instead, "all judgments predicate their ideal content as an attribute of the real which appears in presentation" (002-009). The predicate is not a term standing opposite the subject but is the entire ideal content brought to bear upon reality.

This has profound consequences. First, the predicate always has a universal character — "ideas are universal, and, no matter what it is that we try to say and dimly mean, what we really express and succeed in asserting, is nothing individual" (002-008). Second, because predicates are universal while reality is singular, every judgment involves a gap between what we say and what we mean. The predicate always "sticks for ever in the adjectives; it does not reach the substantive" (002-008).

## Key Aspects

### Predication as Attribution to Reality

The core of Bradley's theory is that all predication is ultimately predication of reality. "The subject in all judgment is ultimately reality" (001-016), and the predicate is the ideal content that we affirm of this reality. Even when a judgment appears to have a finite subject like "this rose," the true subject is the reality that appears as this rose, and the predicate — "is red" — is affirmed of that reality.

### The Universal Nature of Predicates

Every predicate is a universal. Even when we predicate what seems to be a particular quality of a particular thing, the predicate itself is a general meaning. "'That bough is broken,' but so are many others, and we do not say which" (002-008). The brokenness we predicate is a type, not a unique individual. This universal character means that predication always involves an element of abstraction from the concrete uniqueness of reality.

### Predicate and Copula

Bradley argues that the copula — the "is" that supposedly connects subject and predicate — is "a sheer superstition. Judgments can exist without any copula and with but one idea" (002-009). What the copula represents is not a third term but the act of referring ideal content to reality. The judgment is not a coupling of two ideas but a single act in which an idea is identified with reality as one of its attributes.

### Predication in Different Judgment Forms

Bradley analyzes how predication functions across different judgment forms. In analytic judgments of sense, the predicate analyzes what is given in perception. In synthetic judgments, the predicate extends beyond the given through ideal construction. In hypothetical judgments, the predicate is affirmed under a condition. In all cases, the predicate remains the ideal content referred to reality, though the mode of reference varies.

## Historical Context

Bradley's theory of predication engages with a central problem in the history of logic: how can a universal predicate be true of a particular subject? Aristotle's solution involved the categories and the doctrine of secondary substance. Medieval logicians developed the theory of supposition. Kant argued that judgment is the act of subsuming an intuition under a concept. Bradley's innovation is to dissolve the subject-predicate dichotomy into a single act of referring ideal content to reality, thereby making predication a species of identity-in-difference rather than a relation between distinct terms.
