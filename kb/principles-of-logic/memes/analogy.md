# Analogy

A mode of inference that proceeds from one particular case to another on the basis of resemblance — Bradley argues that analogy is not [[inference|reasoning]] from bare particulars but from a shared universal element present in both cases (014-001).

## Overview

Analogy occupies a distinctive place in Bradley's theory of inference. On the surface, analogical reasoning appears to move directly from one particular to another: a child sees a dog wag its tail when pleased, and infers that a cat wagging its tail is also pleased. But Bradley argues that this surface appearance is misleading. What is actually transferred in analogical reasoning is not the whole particular but a relation of qualities — a universal element abstracted from the particular (014-001).

Bradley's analysis of analogy serves a double purpose in his logical system. Negatively, it refutes the empiricist claim that we reason directly from particulars to particulars without the mediation of universals. Positively, it shows that all inference, even the most informal, operates through the apprehension of identity in difference — the recognition that the same universal element is present in diverse instances.

The concept of analogy thus connects Bradley's theory of inference to his broader metaphysical commitments about the relation between universals and particulars, and about the role of abstraction in all cognitive processes.

## Key Aspects

### Analogy as Reasoning from Particulars

Bradley states that [[inference|reasoning]] from a particular to a particular is "obviously an argument from analogy" (014-001). In such [[inference|reasoning]], we do not use the whole of the particular from which we argue. The child who infers the cat's pleasure from the dog's wagging tail does not transfer every attribute of the dog to the cat; she transfers only a specific relation of qualities — the connection between tail-wagging and the emotional state of pleasure.

### True Analogy and Implicit Principle

Bradley draws a crucial distinction between true and false analogy. In true analogy, we employ a principle even though we cannot state it explicitly. The reasoner feels that "it is the same thing" in both cases, operating on a "general impression" rather than an explicit universal (014-001). The premise from which the conclusion directly comes is not the particular but "an universal extract" — what Bradley calls a "general impression."

### False Analogy as Bad Generalization

False analogy, Bradley argues, is fundamentally a failure of generalization. When we object to a false analogy, we do so because it treats a fact as an instance of a rule when no common rule connects the cases (014-001). The fault in a vicious inference by analogy can always be located in a wrong generalization — the reasoner has abstracted the wrong universal element or has applied a valid element to a case where it is not present.

### Resemblance as Identity

Bradley makes the striking claim that [[inference|reasoning]] from resemblance is ultimately [[inference|reasoning]] from identity. When we reason "A, B, and C are a, and therefore D, which resembles them, is a," we are not arguing from the mere fact that A, B, and C are a. We are arguing because in A, B, and C some element is connected with a, and we find that same element in D (014-001). The [[inference|reasoning]] process thus depends on the recognition of a common element — an identity — beneath the surface resemblance.

### Analogy and Abstraction

The operation of analogy necessarily involves abstraction. In arguing from particular cases, we leave out some of the differences between them, and we could not argue if we did not leave them out. When the differences are disregarded, the residue is a universal (014-001). This is the core of Bradley's anti-empiricist argument: the empiricist who claims to reason from bare particulars is unknowingly relying on abstracted universals.

## Critique of Analogical Reasoning in Comparative Psychology

Beyond his logical analysis of analogy, Bradley applies the concept critically to the study of animal intelligence. He warns against interpreting the successes of lower animals "by direct analogy from the highest functions of the human intellect" (023-007), noting that if one applies this principle to the successes, one must also apply it to the failures — and the total consequence would be a "strange compound."

This critique illustrates Bradley's broader methodological point: analogy is a valid form of [[inference|reasoning]] only when the shared element is properly identified and the limits of resemblance are respected. Uncritical analogy, which transfers complex human cognitive capacities wholesale to animals, fails precisely because it neglects the differences that make the universal element inapplicable.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of analogy engages with a long tradition in British empiricism, particularly the claim advanced by [[john-stuart-mill|John Stuart Mill]] and others that we reason from particulars to particulars without the mediation of general principles. Mill had argued that the syllogism in extension is a petitio principii, and that genuine inference proceeds directly from experienced instances to new cases. Bradley accepts Mill's critique of the extensional syllogism but rejects his positive conclusion. Analogy, Bradley argues, is not an alternative to [[syllogism|syllogistic reasoning]] that bypasses universals; it is itself a form of [[inference|reasoning]] through universals, operating on implicit rather than explicit principles.

The analysis also reflects Bradley's engagement with associationist psychology, which explained inference as the association of particular images. Bradley's argument that analogy requires abstraction — the neglect of differences and the isolation of a common element — is directed against the associationist claim that [[inference|reasoning]] can be reduced to the mechanical linkage of particular mental contents.
