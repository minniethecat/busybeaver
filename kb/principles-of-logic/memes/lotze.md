# Hermann Lotze

Rudolf Hermann Lotze (1817–1881) was a German philosopher and logician whose *Logik* (1874) is one of Bradley's most frequently cited authorities in the *Principles of Logic*. Bradley's references to Lotze span from the opening chapter to the final sections, marking Lotze as a thinker whose views Bradley regards as substantially aligned with his own.

## Overview

Lotze occupies a unique position in Bradley's *Principles of Logic*: unlike most figures Bradley discusses, Lotze is cited not as a target of criticism but as an ally. Bradley's characteristic formula "cf. Lotze, *Logik*" or "I must refer to Lotze's admirable chapter" (002-042) signals genuine intellectual debt. The first chapter alone contains three references to Lotze (001-015, 002-036, 002-042), establishing the German logician as a foundational influence on Bradley's conception of judgment and inference.

Bradley's engagement with Lotze is notable for its consistency across the work. While Bradley engages critically with British empiricists and formal logicians, Lotze serves as a continental counterweight — a thinker who has already grasped, from Bradley's perspective, the inseparability of logic from metaphysics and the inadequacy of purely formal approaches.

## Key Ideas

### The Nature of Judgment

Lotze's treatment of judgment as a unitary act that cannot be decomposed into subject and predicate as independent terms is a view Bradley explicitly endorses. In the opening pages of the *Principles*, Bradley writes: "the reader may cf. Lotze, *Logik*, 80–2" (001-015), directing attention to Lotze's discussion of the copula and the unity of the judgment. For both thinkers, "A is B" does not express a relation between two self-subsistent entities but a single ideal content qualifying reality.

### Logic and Metaphysics

Lotze's insistence that logic cannot be separated from metaphysical inquiry is a theme that runs throughout Bradley's references. "I must refer to Lotze's admirable chapter" (002-042) — Bradley's praise is reserved for passages where Lotze demonstrates that purely formal logical analysis inevitably presupposes metaphysical commitments. This alignment is particularly significant in Bradley's rejection of psychologism and his insistence that logical laws are not generalizations from psychological processes.

### Teleology and Mechanism

Lotze's broader philosophical system, which sought to reconcile mechanistic science with teleological understanding, resonates with Bradley's own distinction between the abstract understanding (which deals in fixed relations) and the living movement of thought. Bradley acknowledges Lotze alongside [[sigwart|Sigwart]] as one of the thinkers from whom he has "learnt most" (007-019) on the subject of logical theory.

## Bradley's Engagement

Bradley's treatment of Lotze is predominantly affirmative. In Chapter VII, discussing the theory of inference, Bradley writes: "The writers from whom on this subject I have learnt most are Lotze, *Logik*; [[sigwart|Sigwart]], *Logik*; Wundt, *Logik*" (007-019). This acknowledgment places Lotze first in Bradley's intellectual genealogy for questions of logical method.

The alignment between Bradley and Lotze extends to specific technical discussions. In Chapter XV, Bradley invokes Lotze's authority against [[john-stuart-mill|J.S. Mill]]'s inductive methods: "in their own country, and Professors Lotze and [[sigwart|Sigwart]] in Germany, have..." (015-006), signaling that the critique of Mill is not a British eccentricity but part of a broader European logical tradition.

Even where Bradley advances his own distinctive positions, Lotze remains a reference point. In Chapter XVI, discussing the quantification of judgments, Bradley notes: "now Professor Lotze is dead, has done more" (016-010) — a tribute to Lotze's continuing influence even after his passing. And in Chapter XVIII, Bradley invokes Lotze's authority on a technical point: "Some dogs are mammals (cf. Lotze, *Logik*, §81)" (018-011).

## Influence

Lotze's *Logik* (1874, English translation 1884) was a major work of late 19th-century philosophy, influential on both sides of the Atlantic. His attempt to integrate logical formalism with metaphysical insight shaped the development of British idealism, and his students included Josiah Royce and George Santayana. For Bradley specifically, Lotze provided a model of how logic could be pursued as a genuinely philosophical discipline rather than a merely technical one. Bradley's frequent and respectful citations testify to Lotze's role as a key ally in the project of the *Principles of Logic*.
