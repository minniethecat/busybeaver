# Extension and Intension

In Bradley's *Principles of Logic*, every judgment admits of a twofold reading: in *extension* (denotation) or in *intension* (comprehension). When we attend to the content or meaning of an idea, we read it intensionally; when we attend to the instances or individuals of which the idea is true, we read it extensionally.

Bradley's discussion in Chapter VI of Book I subjects both readings to sustained critique, arguing that neither taken alone can ground logical inference.

## Overview

"If in considering an idea you attend to its content, you have its intension or comprehension. Its extension may be taken in two different senses. It is an instance or instances, ideal or actual" (006-001). This opening definition frames Bradley's inquiry into the quantity of judgments.

Extension refers ultimately to the real but may directly signify "any other more concrete idea which contains the intension" or "any individual of which the meaning is true" (006-001).

Bradley is sharply critical of the doctrine — associated with the Port-Royal logicians and entrenched in textbook logic — that extension and intension vary inversely. He calls this "either false or frivolous" (006-003): increasing the number of instances falling under a term does not diminish its meaning in any intelligible sense.

His deeper concern is that both extensional and intensional readings, when taken as the exclusive interpretation of judgment, misrepresent what a judgment actually asserts.

The attempt to read a judgment purely in extension collapses under its own weight because "if the ostensible predicate has been taken in extension, the proposition has in part been read intensionally; for it has asserted an attribute of the subject" (006-006).

## Key Aspects

### The Extensional Reading

When we read a judgment in extension, we interpret the predicate as referring to a class or collection of individuals. "Dogs are mammals" is taken to mean that dogs are included within the class of mammals. Bradley objects that this inclusion is not what the judgment directly asserts: "the extension is not affirmed as a predicate" (006-007).

What we actually predicate of dogs is a quality — being mammalian — and the class-inclusion interpretation is a derivative abstraction. The extensional reading cannot sustain itself without covertly relying on intensional content: "every judgment can be taken in extension; can not only the predicate, but the subject and the copula also, be read in extension?" (006-007).

The project of a purely extensional logic, Bradley suggests, is chimerical.

### The Intensional Reading

"When read in intension the judgment runs thus, 'Given the subject with its intension, it is an attribute of that subject to possess an attribute which includes the intension of the predicate'" (006-008). On this reading, a judgment asserts a connection of attributes or adjectivals, not a relation of classes.

Bradley finds the intensional reading philosophically more honest because it acknowledges that what we predicate is always qualitative. However, the intensional reading too is an abstraction: "the whole judgment according to intension, we fare no better. It is a fragment torn from its context, and the attempt to make it self-complete must end in failure" (018-010).

### Denotation and Connotation

Bradley addresses the English terminology of "denotation" and "connotation," popularised by Mill. His verdict is blunt: "these phrases have found favour with the English public, and the indiscriminate use of 'connotation' marks one kind of superior person. But they serve no useful purpose in logic. They are unnecessary and objectionable" (006-002).

They add nothing that "extension" and "intension" do not already capture, and their association with Mill's theory of the import of propositions makes them positively misleading.

### Quantity and the Limits of Extension

Bradley's discussion of extension grounds his treatment of [[quantity-of-judgments|judgment quantity]]. "If the subject is taken as a quantity which stands in a certain fractional relation to another quantity, it is absurd to think that, on the strength of these mere qualities, you leave universals and get down to individuals" (006-009). The quantitative treatment of "all,"

"some," and "this" fails because it mistakes abstract distinctions for features of reality. The ultimate subject of every judgment is the individual real, and no manipulation of extension can reach it.

## Historical Context

The distinction between extension and intension has roots in medieval logic (the *suppositio* theory) and was systematised in the Port-Royal *Logic* (1662). Bradley's critique targets the nineteenth-century textbook tradition that transmitted this doctrine uncritically, as well as Mill's empiricist theory of propositional import.

His engagement connects to his broader campaign against the syllogism: the [[premise|major premise]], he argues in Book II, is a fiction generated by taking a judgment purely in extension. Bradley's own position — that every judgment is ultimately "the reference of an ideal content to a reality beyond the act"

(002-039) — cuts across the extension/intension dichotomy, treating both readings as partial abstractions from the concrete act of judging.
