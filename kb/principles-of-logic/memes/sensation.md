Sensation, in Bradley's logical system, is the primitive psychical element — the immediate datum of perception that stands in contrast to the idea. Bradley treats the distinction between sensation and idea as foundational for understanding how judgment operates upon and transcends mere givenness.

## Overview

Bradley discusses sensation primarily in the context of the distinction between sensation and idea. Early in Book I, he notes the need to "distinguish between ideas and sensations" (001-001), a distinction that runs through the entire empiricist tradition but which Bradley reinterprets within his idealist framework. Sensations are not the ultimate atoms of experience, as the empiricists held, but are themselves abstractions from the concrete whole of psychical life.

The effective distinction between sensation and idea is not one of vividness or origin, as the British empiricists maintained. Bradley argues that if an image is "taken in itself as a psychical fact, it is as real as, or not less real than the sensation" (001-019). What distinguishes them is their logical function: sensation is given, while the idea is referred to reality in judgment.

## Key Aspects

### Sensation and the Psychical Series

Sensations belong to the broader class of psychical events, alongside images and reflections. Bradley groups them together: "all psychical events, be they sensations, or images, or reflections" (002-017) share the same fundamental character as mental occurrences. This classification is important because it undermines the empiricist claim that sensations have a privileged epistemic status.

### Sensation and Idea

The relation between sensation and idea is central to Bradley's theory. He rejects the view that an idea is merely a faint copy of a sensation, arguing instead that the difference is logical, not merely psychological. "The association of an idea with a sensation" (001-016) cannot explain how judgment refers ideal content to reality, because both sensation and idea are, as psychical facts, on the same level.

### Sensation in Judgment

For Bradley, sensation alone does not constitute knowledge. The mere "apprehension of simple sensations" (001-017) is not yet judgment, because judgment requires the reference of an ideal content to reality. Sensation provides the psychical material, but judgment transforms it by bringing it under universal logical forms.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of sensation engages critically with the British empiricist tradition from Locke through Mill, which treated sensations as the ultimate building blocks of knowledge. Bradley shares the empiricist starting point — that experience begins with sensation — but rejects the conclusion that complex thought can be reduced to associations of sensory atoms. His position draws on the idealist insight that the givenness of sensation is itself an abstraction from the richer whole of experience.
