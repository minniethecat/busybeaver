# Equational Logic

The algebraic approach to formal logic that represents propositions as equations between terms and logical operations as algebraic manipulations. In Bradley's *Principles of Logic*, equational logic — associated primarily with [[jevons|W.S. Jevons]] — receives sustained critical scrutiny in a dedicated chapter.

## Overview

Bradley devotes Chapter XVI of Book II to equational logic "as it appears in the theory of Jevons" (016-001). His treatment is largely critical: he argues that the equational formalism misrepresents the nature of logical inference by reducing it to mechanical substitution. "It is needless to repeat against the equational logic the objections we have urged against formalism in general" (016-004), Bradley writes, but he goes on to identify specific defects peculiar to the equational approach.

Equational logic treats judgments as equations of the form A = B and inference as the substitution of equals for equals. This algebraic model, pioneered by Boole and developed by Jevons, promised to make logic as rigorous as mathematics. Bradley acknowledges the formal elegance of the system but denies that it captures what inference actually is.

## Key Aspects

### The Equation Model of Judgment

In equational logic, "All A is B" becomes "A = AB" — the subject A is equated with the intersection of A and B. This algebraic translation is ingenious but, Bradley argues, it obscures the actual logical structure of judgment. [[judgment|Judgment]] is not an equation but a reference of an ideal content to reality. Reducing it to an equation loses the very feature — reference to reality — that makes judgment judgment.

### Substitution as Inference

The equational logician's model of inference is substitution: if A = B and B = C, then A = C. Bradley does not deny that substitution is a valid logical operation, but he denies that all inference reduces to it. "The inference which is possible in equational logic or in syllogism can not duplicate the actual movement of thought" (016-009). Genuine inference involves ideal construction, not mere substitution.

### Limits of Algebraic Formalism

Bradley's fundamental objection is that equational logic mistakes the instrument for the subject matter. Logic concerns the necessary connections among ideal contents as they refer to reality; algebra concerns the formal manipulation of symbols according to fixed rules. The two are not the same, and treating logic as algebra yields a system that is formally consistent but logically empty — it shows that conclusions follow from [[premise|premises]] according to stipulated rules, but not why they follow.

### The Role of Identity

Equational logic makes heavy use of the [[law-of-identity|principle of identity]] (A = A), but Bradley argues that it uses identity only formally — as a rule for symbol manipulation — not as the living logical principle that makes inference possible. "The objections we have urged against formalism" apply with special force to the equational version, because its formalism is explicit and systematic, making the gap between logical form and [[ideal-content|logical content]] impossible to ignore.

## Historical Context

Equational logic emerged from the work of George Boole (*The Laws of Thought*, 1854) and was refined by [[jevons|W.S. Jevons]] (*The Principles of Science*, 1874). It represented the leading edge of formal logic in Bradley's day, and his critical engagement with it shows him taking seriously the most sophisticated formalism available while rejecting its foundational assumptions. The subsequent development of mathematical logic by Frege, Peano, and Russell would vindicate some of the formalist ambitions Bradley criticized, but his questions about the relation between logical form and [[ideal-content|logical content]] remain philosophically live.
