# Inference

In Bradley's *Principles of Logic*, inference is the union of two elements: a process of [[ideal-construction|ideal synthesis]] and a result of intuitive perception. "The process is an operation of synthesis; it takes its data and by ideal construction combines them into a whole. The result is the perception of a new relation within that unity" (010-001). This dual character — construction followed by inspection — is Bradley's distinctive account of what reasoning fundamentally is.

## Overview

Bradley presents his general theory of inference in Book II, Part I, Chapter III ("A General Idea of Inference"), after clearing the ground of erroneous views in the preceding chapters. His aim is to identify what is common to all inference, regardless of form — syllogistic, mathematical, or everyday. The answer is that inference is always a mental construction that reveals a previously unseen relation.

"Inference a perception ensuing on a synthesis" (010-001) is Bradley's summary formula. We begin with [[premise|premises]] — judgments asserting relations between terms, such as A–B and B–C. We construct these into a single whole by means of their shared term (B), yielding A–B–C. We then inspect this construction and perceive a new relation — A–C — that was not given in the original [[premise|premises]]. The conclusion is not deduced by rule but seen by an act of intellectual intuition.

## Key Aspects

### Construction and Intuition

Bradley distinguishes two phases of inference: construction (the synthetic work) and intuition (the perceptual result). "We first do a certain work on our data; and this work is the construction. We then by inspection discover and select a new relation, and this intuition is the conclusion" (010-004). Neither phase can be reduced to rules: construction requires the power to see points of union, and inspection requires "a good eye" — "there are no rules which can tell you what to perceive" (010-006).

### The Role of Identity

Inference is possible only because [[premise|premises]] share common terms. "We can not logically join our [[premise|premises]] into a whole unless they offer us points of connection. But if the terms between which the relations subsist are all of them different we are perfectly helpless, for we can not make an arch without a key-stone" (010-004). The identity of B in A–B and B–C is what allows the construction A–B–C. Without shared identity, no synthesis is possible.

### Demonstration as Showing

Bradley broadens the concept of demonstration beyond logic. "[[demonstration|Demonstration]] is merely pointing out or showing; and if the conclusion of an inference is seen and thus may be shown, so also may a nerve or again an omelette" (010-002). What distinguishes [[demonstration|logical demonstration]] is that the preparation is ideal — "the rearrangement which displays the unknown fact is an operation in our heads" (010-003). In contrast, the anatomist's demonstration manipulates physical facts, and the cook's demonstration arranges ingredients. All are demonstrations; only the logical one is inference.

### No Limit to Terms

Bradley challenges the traditional limitation of inference to three terms (the syllogism's subject, predicate, and middle). "Logic sets no limit to the number of [[premise|premises]] which may precede the conclusion, and it is the weakness of our heads which narrows our constructions" (010-006). A geographer reasoning from multiple spatial relations, or a scientist integrating many data points, constructs with as many terms as needed. "A man, who can not use more than three terms in reasoning, is unlikely to do much in any subject" (010-006).

## Historical Context

Bradley's account of inference as construction-plus-intuition develops themes from German Idealism — particularly the idea that synthesis is the fundamental act of thought — while rejecting the formal approach of British logicians like Jevons and the syllogistic tradition. His emphasis on the psychological reality of reasoning (the actual mental operations involved) contrasts with purely formal treatments while avoiding the r[[immediate-inference|eduction]] of logic to psychology that he criticizes in Mill. Bradley's theory influenced subsequent idealist accounts of reasoning, particularly Bosanquet's, and his critique of rule-based inference anticipated later discussions of the limits of formalization.
