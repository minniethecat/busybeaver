# Subject and Attribute

The fundamental categorical relation in Bradley's logical analysis, in which a content (the subject) has another content (the attribute) predicated of it, forming the basic structure of judgment and, Bradley argues, the sole relation capable of supporting valid [[syllogism|syllogistic inference]].

## Overview

The relation of subject and attribute is one of the most pervasive concepts in Bradley's *Principles of Logic*. It defines the elementary form of judgment—"the matter may be arranged as subject and attribute" (001-013)—and, more importantly, it circumscribes the domain within which formal [[syllogism|syllogistic reasoning]] is valid. For Bradley, understanding the nature and limits of this relation is essential to understanding both the power and the inadequacy of traditional logic.

Bradley's treatment of the subject-attribute relation serves a dual purpose. Positively, he explains why [[syllogism|syllogistic inference]] works when it does: the subject-attribute form permits the transitivity of predication that syllogism requires. Negatively, he demonstrates that many judgments—perhaps most judgments of philosophical interest—do not fit this form, and that traditional logic's attempt to force all judgments into the subject-attribute mold is a fundamental error.

The concept is developed most extensively in Bradley's discussion of the validity of syllogistic figures (Chapters IX–XII of Book I) and in his critique of the traditional doctrine of judgment. "Confined to the category of subject and attribute, it fails wherever you pass beyond" (009-003). This limitation becomes the cornerstone of Bradley's argument that formal logic requires supplementation by a more comprehensive theory of judgment and inference.

The subject-attribute relation also connects to Bradley's broader metaphysical concerns. His later *Appearance and Reality* would develop the implications of the claim that not all reality can be captured in subject-predicate form—a thesis already latent in his logical writings.

## Key Aspects

### The Basic Structure

For Bradley, the subject-attribute relation is the form in which "a certain content A is qualified by another content B" (005-003). The subject term designates a reality, and the attribute term designates an ideal content that is affirmed to qualify that reality. "The different from A does not merely appear alongside A; it is joined to it in the relation of subject and attribute" (005-003). This joining is the act of judgment.

### Validity Condition for Syllogism

Bradley makes the strong claim that the subject-attribute relation is "the only one which, if used in syllogism, gives a formally valid conclusion" (011-003). When [[premise|premises]] involve relations other than subject-attribute—spatial relations, temporal relations, quantitative comparisons—the syllogistic form may fail to yield a valid conclusion. This is not a defect of syllogism per se but a limitation of its scope.

### The Limitation of Formal Logic

"Confined to the category of subject and attribute, it fails wherever you pass beyond" (009-003). Bradley catalogues types of judgment that resist r[[immediate-inference|eduction]] to subject-attribute form: existential judgments, impersonal judgments, judgments of comparison, and judgments involving relations. Each of these presents a challenge to the traditional logic that Bradley seeks to expose and address.

### The Role in Negative Inference

The subject-attribute relation is essential even for [[negative-reasoning|negative inference]]. Bradley argues that "if one is in the category of subject and attribute, there is a [[negative-reasoning|negative inference]]" (012-001). But if the [[premise|premises]] involve other relations, even the negative conclusion may be invalid. "Unless one is a relation of subject and attribute, there is no connection at all" (012-002).

### The Synthesis of Subject and Attribute

Bradley does not regard the subject-attribute relation as an unanalyzable primitive. It is itself a synthesis—an act of thought that brings together subject and predicate into a unified judgment. "All the other hypotheses except the synthesis of subject and attribute" fail to ground valid inference (011-007). The synthesis is the work of judgment, not merely a grammatical form.

## Historical Context

The subject-predicate analysis of judgment has dominated Western logic since Aristotle's *De Interpretatione*. Bradley's treatment is both a continuation and a critique of this tradition. He accepts the validity of the subject-attribute form within its proper domain but argues vigorously against its universal application—a theme he shares with other nineteenth-century philosophical logicians, including Lotze and [[christoph-von-sigwart|Sigwart]].

Bradley's position also reflects Kant's distinction between categorical and non-categorical judgments, though Bradley pushes the critique further. Where Kant's table of judgments includes relational forms beyond the categorical (hypothetical and disjunctive), Bradley argues that even these do not exhaust the variety of judgment forms, and that the subject-attribute model fails to capture the unity of judgment itself.
