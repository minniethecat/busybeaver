# Dilemma

A dilemma is a logical argument form that presents two alternatives — each of which leads to an unacceptable or contradictory consequence — forcing a choice that reveals the limits of the [[inference|reasoning]] framework within which it operates. Bradley treats the dilemma not merely as a rhetorical device but as a genuine logical structure that exposes deep features of judgment and inference.

## Overview

Bradley's engagement with the dilemma runs throughout *The Principles of Logic*, appearing in contexts ranging from the analysis of judgment to the theory of inference. He uses the dilemma form not as a stand-alone topic of formal logic but as a diagnostic tool: when a position generates a dilemma, this signals that its basic concepts require re-examination. (001-015)

The dilemma occupies a distinctive place in Bradley's method. Rather than treating it as a fallacy to be avoided or a weapon of dialectic, Bradley repeatedly shows that genuine philosophical problems naturally take the form of dilemmas. The correct response is not to choose one horn or the other but to dissolve the dilemma by revising the assumptions that generate it. (002-020)

This approach is most visible in his treatment of judgment, where the attempt to understand the relation of subject and predicate generates a series of dilemmas that drive the argument toward his idealist conclusions about the nature of logical form.

## Key Aspects

### The Structure of a Dilemma

A dilemma, in logical form, presents an alternative: either A or B. If A leads to an unacceptable consequence, and B leads to an unacceptable consequence, then the original assumption that generated the alternatives must be rejected. Bradley employs this structure repeatedly but typically uses it to question the [[disjunctive-judgment|disjunction]] itself rather than to accept either horn. (007-005)

The formal structure matters because it reveals hidden assumptions. When a dilemma is well-constructed, the two alternatives exhaust the logical possibilities under the given assumptions. The impossibility of accepting either horn therefore demonstrates the falsity of the shared premise.

### The Dilemma in Judgment Theory

Bradley's analysis of judgment generates a fundamental dilemma: either a judgment asserts an identity between subject and predicate (in which case it is tautologous) or it asserts a difference (in which case the connection is inexplicable). Neither horn is acceptable, and Bradley concludes that the subject-predicate form of judgment must be revised. (002-031)

This is a central argumentative move in Bradley's logic. The dilemma is not a rhetorical flourish but the precise logical form of his critique of traditional judgment theory. By showing that both horns lead to untenable positions, he establishes the necessity of his own alternative account.

### Dilemma and Inductive Proof

Bradley deploys a dilemma in his critique of inductive methods. The methods of inductive proof, he argues, face the following choice: either they are genuinely inductive (in which case they [[privation|lack]] logical necessity) or they are actually syllogistic [[inference|deduction]]s (in which case they are not inductive at all). (015-004) (015-005)

This dilemma is central to Bradley's argument that the sharp distinction between induction and [[inference|deduction]] cannot be maintained. The point is not to reject induction but to show that the inductivist's presuppositions cannot sustain the distinction they claim.

Bradley's treatment of induction through the dilemma form is characteristic of his broader strategy of using logical form to expose philosophical confusion.

### Dilemma and the Validity of Inference

In his final chapter on the validity of inference, Bradley returns to the dilemma form to frame the deepest problem of all: if inference moves from [[premise|premises]] to conclusion, either the conclusion is already contained in the [[premise|premises]] (making inference tautologous) or it is not (making inference invalid). (026-018) (026-022)

This is the master dilemma of logical theory, and Bradley's entire work can be read as an attempt to dissolve it. The resolution Bradley offers is that inference is neither a mechanical extraction nor an arbitrary leap, but a systematic development of ideal content within a single reality. The dilemma is dissolved by rejecting the conception of inference that generates it.

## Historical Context

The dilemma has a long history in logic and rhetoric, from the "horned syllogism" of ancient dialectic to its treatment in medieval logic as a distinct argument form. Bradley's use of the dilemma is distinctive in two respects. First, he treats it as a genuine logical form rather than a rhetorical trick. (002-031)

Second, and more importantly, he repeatedly uses the dilemma to dissolve rather than to win arguments — the point is not to trap an opponent but to reveal the inadequacy of the framework that generates the dilemma.

This method has affinities with Hegelian dialectic, where contradictions drive conceptual development. Bradley's innovation is to bring the dilemma form into the service of detailed logical analysis rather than grand historical narrative.
