# Idea (Logical)

In Bradley's *The Principles of Logic*, an idea is defined not as a mental image
or psychological state but as a **symbol** — a sign that stands for a reality other
than itself. This seemingly simple distinction is the cornerstone of Bradley's entire
logical system: judgment is impossible without ideas, but ideas are not ideas until
they are used *as* symbols.

## Overview

Bradley opens the *Principles* by establishing his theory of ideas as the foundation
for all that follows. Against the British empiricist tradition that treated ideas as
mental copies of sensations, Bradley argues that an idea is not defined by its
psychological origin but by its **logical function** — it means something, and that
meaning is what matters for logic.

> …we have not learnt that, wherever we have truth or falsehood, it is the
> signification we use, and not the existence. We never assert the fact in our
> heads, but something else which that fact stands for.
> — (001-002)

This has a crucial consequence: ideas are not private mental events but shareable
meanings. The psychological fact of "having an idea" is irrelevant to logic; what
matters is what the idea signifies. Bradley's anti-psychologism — his rejection of
the view that logic reduces to psychology — begins with this account of ideas.

Bradley develops his theory primarily in Book I, Chapters I–II of the *Principles*,
where the nature of ideas is examined as a necessary preliminary to the theory of
judgment. The discussion extends through the treatment of universal and particular
judgments in later chapters.

## Key Aspects

### Ideas as Symbols, Not Images

Bradley's central claim is that an idea is essentially a symbol:

> A symbol is a fact which stands for something else, but not all facts are
> symbols. Some signify nothing, others stand generally for the kind which
> they represent, while others again go on to remind us of something else.
> But the flower can never itself be what it means.
> — (001-003)

An image in the mind — a visual picture of a rose, say — exists as a psychological
fact. But as a mere fact, it has no meaning; it simply is what it is. An idea, by
contrast, points beyond itself. It is this *symbolic* character, not any sensory
quality, that makes something an idea.

This distinction cuts through a persistent confusion in empiricist philosophy:
treating ideas as if they were faded copies of sensations. Bradley insists that
even if ideas originate in sensory experience, their logical function is entirely
different from that of sensations or images.

### Ideas and Judgment

Bradley argues that judgment proper requires ideas used *as* ideas — that is, with
awareness of their symbolic character:

> …strictly speaking, we can not judge till we use them as ideas. We must have
> become aware that they are not realities, that they are mere ideas, signs of
> an existence other than themselves. Ideas are not ideas until they are symbols,
> and, before we use symbols, we can not judge.
> — (001-001)

This is a strong claim: judgment is impossible for creatures that cannot distinguish
between a sign and what it signifies. A dog chasing a cat has perceptions and may
even have memory-images, but it does not *judge* in Bradley's sense because it does
not use those images as symbols of something beyond themselves.

>The perception as we say "calls up" the ideas, and he runs eagerly forward. His
> experience, we will suppose, was of a white cat or a black retriever… To-day
> images are present again…
> — (001-021)

The dog's behavior is guided by association, not by judgment. [[judgment|Judgment]] begins where
the mind recognizes that its ideas are not the things themselves but representations
of them.

### The Universality of Ideas

A second crucial feature of Bradley's account is that ideas, as symbols, are
inherently **universal** in their meaning:

> If you keep to ideas it is useless to make a reference to the present…
> — (002-016)

An idea means a *kind* of thing, not a particular individual. Even when I think of
"this toothache," the idea I use is not the toothache itself but a content that could
apply to any sufficiently similar toothache. This universality is not a defect but
the essential feature that makes ideas usable in [[inference|reasoning]].

This connects directly to Bradley's theory of judgment: all judgments are at bottom
universal, because the ideas that compose them are universal in their signification.
A singular judgment like "I have a toothache" uses ideas that refer beyond the
present moment:

> The actual toothache is not any other toothache, and the actual I is myself as
> having this very toothache. But the truth I assert has been and will be true of
> all other toothaches of my altering self.
> — (002-007)

### Ideas and Reality

Bradley's theory of ideas as symbols leads to a distinctive account of the relation
between thought and reality. Ideas are not realities themselves, nor do they copy
reality. Instead, they serve as "adjectival" — they qualify reality without being
independent existents:

> …what we see is not self-subsistent but borrowed and adjectival.
> — (002-011)

This has metaphysical implications that Bradley only sketches in the *Principles*
but that become central in his later work *Appearance and Reality*. Ideas give us
truth about reality, but they never capture it fully. Every judgment says too little
or too much:

> …the analytic judgment is not per se. It can not stand by itself. Asserting,
> as it does, of the particular presentation, it must always suppose a further
> content, which falls outside that fraction it affirms.
> — (002-038)

### The Idea in Logical Theory

| Aspect | Bradley's View | Rejected View |
|--------|---------------|---------------|
| Nature of idea | Symbol/sign | Mental image/copy |
| Locus of meaning | Signification (logical) | Existence (psychological) |
| Scope | Universal | Particular |
| Role in judgment | Content asserted of reality | One term related to another |
| Relation to reality | Adjectival (qualifies reality) | Self-subsistent entity |

## Historical Context

Bradley's theory of ideas is directed primarily against the British empiricist
tradition, particularly the associationist psychology of [[bain|Alexander Bain]]
and the inductive logic of [[john-stuart-mill]]. The empiricists had treated ideas
as derived from sensations through association, making logic ultimately a branch
of psychology.

Bradley's insistence that ideas are symbols, not images, is a
declaration of independence for logic from psychology.

The theory also draws on German Idealist sources. [[hegel]]'s treatment of the
concept (*Begriff*) as universal and the distinction between representation
(*Vorstellung*) and thought (*Denken*) are clear influences, though Bradley's
account is more austere and focused on logical function. The German logician
[[sigwart]] is also cited in Bradley's discussion of judgment and ideas.

Bradley's account of ideas as symbols influenced subsequent British Idealism
and, through it, the broader development of anti-psychologistic logic in the
late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries.
