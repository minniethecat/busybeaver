# Predication

The logical act of attributing a predicate to a subject — the fundamental operation of judgment that Bradley analyzes at the outset of *The Principles of Logic*, arguing that predication does not use the mental fact as a mere sign of the reality (001-005).

## Overview

Predication is where Bradley's logical analysis begins. Before any theory of inference or [[inference|reasoning]] can be developed, the nature of predication itself must be understood. What does it mean to attribute a predicate to a subject? Bradley's answer is that predication is not the mechanical application of a concept to an object but a complex act involving the relation between mental content and reality.

Bradley is particularly concerned to refute the view that in predication we use the mental fact as a mere sign or representation of an external reality. Instead, he argues that the relation between predicate and subject is internal to the judgment itself — predication constitutes the unity of the judgment rather than merely reporting a pre-existing connection.

The concept of predication runs through Bradley's entire logical system, from the analysis of categorical judgment to the treatment of hypothetical and disjunctive forms. Understanding predication is essential to understanding Bradley's critique of traditional formal logic.

## Key Aspects

### Predication and Mental Facts

Bradley insists that in predication we do not use the mental fact as a mere intermediary between word and thing (001-005). The predicate is not a sign standing for something else; it is an integral part of the judgment whose relation to the subject constitutes the judgment's content.

### Predication and Denial

Bradley carefully distinguishes predication from [[negation|denial]]. Denial is not the predication of a contradictory (005-010); it is a distinct logical act with its own structure. Confusing [[negation|denial]] with negative predication leads to errors about the nature of [[negation|negative judgment]].

### Categorical Predication

When the predicate falls entirely outside the subject, the predication will be categorical (006-011). Bradley uses this criterion to distinguish categorical from hypothetical judgment: in categorical predication, the predicate is attributed to the subject without condition.

### Predication of Whole and Part

Bradley analyzes the predication of either the whole or part of a given totality (020-001). This analysis is central to his treatment of quantification and the logic of classes, showing how predication relates to the part-whole structure of concepts.

## Historical Context

Bradley's analysis of predication engages with the Aristotelian tradition, where predication is the fundamental logical relation, and with the newer formal logic of Frege and Russell, where predication is analyzed as function-argument structure. Bradley's position is distinctive in its emphasis on the psychological and metaphysical dimensions of predication — dimensions that formal logic deliberately excludes.
