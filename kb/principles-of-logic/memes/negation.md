# Negation

In Bradley's *Principles of Logic*, negation (or negative judgment) is the declared refusal of a real subject to accept an offered ideal content. Unlike affirmation, which directly attributes a quality to reality, negation operates at a higher level of reflection: it requires the idea of an affirmative relation before that relation can be excluded.

## Overview

Bradley devotes Chapter III of Book I to the negative judgment, treating it not as a derivative of affirmation but as a distinct logical form with its own structure and conditions. "Like every other variety, the negative judgment depends on the real which appears in perception. In the end it consists in the declared refusal of that subject to accept an ideal content" (003-001). The suggestion of the real as qualified in a certain way, and the exclusion of that suggestion by its application to actual reality, is the proper essence of the negative judgment.

Bradley's treatment is notable for two central claims. First, negation stands at a higher level of reflection than affirmation: to deny, one must first entertain the suggestion of an affirmative relation. "In negative judgment, this very reference of content to reality must itself be an idea" (003-002). Second, every negation rests on a positive ground — a quality in the subject that is incompatible with the rejected predicate. "Every negation must have a ground, and this ground is positive" (003-007).

## Key Aspects

### The Structure of Negative Judgment

Given a real subject X and an ideal content a–b, affirmation simply attributes a–b to X. But negation requires more: one must first form the idea of X as qualified by a–b — which Bradley writes as x(a–b) — and then X repels this suggested synthesis. "What we deny is not the reference of the idea to actual fact. It is the mere idea of the fact, as so qualified, which negation excludes; it repels the suggested synthesis, not the real judgment" (003-004).

### The Positive Ground

Bradley insists that no negation is possible without a positive basis. "A is not B because A is such that, if it were B, it would cease to be itself" (003-007). The subject maintains itself against the predicate by virtue of a quality x that is disparate or incompatible with B. This ground may be explicit (when we know what quality excludes B) or implicit (when we only know that accepting B would alter the subject's character). "The ground is not merely unstated but is unknown" in some cases (003-007).

### Opposition and Privation

Bradley examines two bases for denial. In *opposition*, the subject repels the predicate because it possesses a positive quality filling the space the predicate would occupy — as blue eyes exclude brown. In *privation*, denial rests on [[privation|absence]]: a body is not red because it is uncoloured. Bradley argues that even privation ultimately involves a positive character, for "the nothing which is nothing can not possibly do anything, or be a reason for aught" (003-009). The empty space where a quality should be is itself a positive determination of the subject.

### Logical Negation as Subjective

A crucial distinction in Bradley's account is that logical negation, in its strict character, is "subjective" — it does not hold good outside our thinking. "The reality repels the suggested alteration; but the suggestion is not any movement of the fact, nor in fact does the given subject maintain itself against the actual attack of a disparate quality. The process takes place in the unsubstantial region of ideal experiment" (003-013). The truth of negation lies not in the exclusion itself but in the positive quality that grounds it, which must be expressed by an affirmative judgment.

### Contradiction and Contrary

Bradley carefully distinguishes contradiction from contrary opposition. "Between acceptance and rejection there is no middle-point, and so contradiction is always dual. There is but one Not-B. But contrary opposition is indefinitely plural" (003-018). [[contradiction|Contradiction]] rests on an undetermined contrary — "A is not B" tells us something excludes B, but not what that something is. "Sokrates may be not sick because he is well, or because there is now no such thing as Sokrates" (003-017). The ground of denial is always some positive quality, but it remains unspecified in the negative judgment itself.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of negation engages both the formal-logical tradition and the dialectical method of Hegel. He rejects the view that negation merely affects the copula, that it can be reduced to affirmation with a negative predicate ("A is Not-B"), and that it presupposes an existing affirmative judgment. At the same time, he acknowledges the dialectical insight that "everything is determined by all negation" (003-014) while insisting that logical negation, as such, does not express real relations — those require affirmative judgments. His realist grounding of negation in the positive qualities of reality aligns him with the British idealist tradition while distinguishing his position from both formal logic and unmodified Hegelian dialectics.
