The concept of the whole is central to Bradley's idealist metaphysics. For Bradley, no finite truth or particular fact is ultimately real in isolation; every judgment, every idea, and every relation participates in and derives its meaning from the comprehensive whole that is ultimate reality.

## Overview

Bradley argues that the ultimate subject of every judgment is the whole of reality. When we judge, we refer an ideal content not to a fragmentary piece of reality but, ultimately, to the entire system of reality. The whole is not merely the sum of its parts but that in which all parts find their complete determination (001-013). Bradley's holism means that "the whole before our mind" cannot be reduced to a single idea or image; it is the comprehensive context within which all particular truths hold (001-007).

## Key Aspects

### The Whole as Ultimate Subject

The logical subject of any judgment is, in the final analysis, reality as a whole. Bradley argues that "the ultimate subject, to which the whole ideal content is referred" is not any particular thing but the entire system of reality (001-013). This has profound consequences: no judgment can be fully true in isolation, for its truth depends on its place within the whole.

### Whole and Parts

Bradley rejects the view that the whole is merely an aggregate of independent parts. The parts of reality are not self-subsistent entities; they are abstracted moments within the whole. When we remove a predicate from the whole, Bradley suggests, "we have removed the whole judgment" — indicating that the separation of part from whole is always artificial (001-015).

### Blending and Unity

Bradley uses the metaphor of blending to describe how the whole is produced: "In this case the whole, produced by blending, comes" into being not as a mechanical sum but as a qualitative unity (001-009). The whole transcends its constituent elements while including them.

## Historical Context

Bradley's holism is deeply influenced by Hegel's notion of the concrete universal and the idealist critique of atomism. For Bradley, as for Hegel, the truth is the whole — and this principle governs both metaphysics and logic.
