# Thisness

The quality of being a particular "this" rather than a universal "what" — a concept Bradley investigates in his analysis of how thought, which deals in universals, can nevertheless refer to individual things (002-017).

## Overview

Thisness presents a fundamental challenge for Bradley's logical theory. Thought operates with universals — general [[idea|ideas]] that apply to many instances. But reality presents us with particulars — individual things that are "this" rather than "that." How can universal [[idea|ideas]] capture the thisness of individuals?

Bradley's answer is subtle. He acknowledges that [[idea|ideas]] will suffice so far as thisness is concerned, but they can never give the full "this" (002-017). There is always a gap between the universal content of thought and the particularity of the thing thought about. This gap is not a defect to be eliminated but a structural feature of the relation between thought and reality.

The concept of thisness runs through Bradley's discussion of judgment, inference, and the nature of reality. It connects his logical analysis to deeper metaphysical questions about individuation, identity, and the relation between universals and particulars.

## Key Aspects

### Thisness and Symbolic Content

Bradley argues that symbolic content must have thisness to function as a representation of reality (002-023). A purely abstract symbol, [[privation|lack]]ing any connection to particularity, cannot refer to the individual things that make up the world.

### The Limits of Thisness

Bradley is careful to note that we only fancy that some contents have more thisness than others (002-026). The degree of thisness is not an intrinsic property of the content but a function of how the content is used in judgment and inference.

### Thisness and Universality

Bradley claims that the aim of logical thought is to purge out thisness — to reconstruct the given as a system of universal relations (002-042). This is a striking claim: logic moves away from particularity toward universality, and this movement is essential to the project of understanding reality.

### Thisness and the Individual

The concept of thisness is central to Bradley's account of how individuals are known. Since thought deals in universals, knowing an individual requires situating it within a network of universal relations; thisness marks the point at which that network connects to concrete existence.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of thisness engages with the medieval concept of haecceity (thisness), particularly as developed by Duns Scotus, who used it to explain individuation. Bradley adapts the concept to his Idealist framework, where the problem is not how individuals are constituted but how universal thought can capture individual reality.
