Relation, in Bradley's philosophy, is the fundamental form of finite thought and the key to his critique of the adequacy of discursive understanding. Every judgment, every inference, involves relations between ideas, terms, or facts. Yet Bradley's most famous argument — the regress of relations — aims to show that relations cannot be ultimately real.

## Overview

Bradley's treatment of relation operates on two levels. On the descriptive level, he acknowledges that all thinking is relational: "the relation is the same, whether I affirm or deny" (001-008). Judgment involves the relation of ideal content to reality; inference involves relations between judgments. Relation is the medium of finite thought.

On the critical level, Bradley argues that relation is a form of appearance, not ultimate reality. A relation requires terms to relate, but the terms themselves are constituted by their relations — leading to an infinite regress. "Its relation to other psychical phenomena" (001-001) is characteristic of every finite thing, and this relational character is precisely what marks it as appearance rather than reality.

## Key Aspects

### Relation in Judgment

Judgment, for Bradley, is essentially relational. The "relation of the ideal content to reality" (001-012) is what distinguishes a judgment from a mere image or association. This relation has no degrees — a judgment either refers to reality or it does not. The relational character of judgment is not a defect but the very form of logical activity.

### Internal and External Relations

Bradley's distinction between internal and external relations is central to his metaphysics. An internal relation is one that enters into the essence of its terms; an external relation does not. Bradley argues that all relations are ultimately internal — every term is what it is through its relations to other terms within the whole.

### The Regress of Relations

Bradley's regress argument contends that the relational form is self-defeating when taken as ultimate. If a relation R connects terms A and B, we need further relations to connect R to A and R to B, and so on indefinitely. This regress shows that the relational structure of thought cannot be the final truth about reality.

## Historical Context

Bradley's critique of relations is directed against the empiricist and positivist traditions that took relations as ultimate facts. His argument influenced Russell's early work and the subsequent development of analytic philosophy, though Russell ultimately rejected Bradley's conclusions.
