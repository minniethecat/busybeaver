In Bradley's theory of judgment, quality refers to the fundamental character of a judgment as either affirmative or negative. It is one of the basic dimensions along which judgments are classified, alongside quantity and modality.

## Overview

Bradley treats the quality of judgment as a central logical category. The affirmative judgment attributes a predicate to a subject, while the negative judgment denies it. But Bradley goes beyond the traditional view: quality is not merely a formal mark but reflects the way in which the ideal content is referred to reality. A judgment's quality shapes how the content is meant — whether it is posited as belonging to reality or excluded from it (001-008).

## Key Aspects

### Affirmative and Negative Quality

The basic distinction in quality is between affirmative and negative judgments. An affirmative judgment takes "a part of the quality of each" term and attributes it to the subject, while a negative judgment denies this attribution (001-008). Bradley emphasizes that negation is not a mere psychological act but a logical operation with its own distinctive structure.

### Quality and Content

Bradley connects the quality of a judgment with its content: "by merging its own quality in a wider meaning," an idea can pass beyond its initial significance (001-003). The quality of a judgment is not an external label but is internal to the meaning expressed.

### Positive Exclusion

Negation, for Bradley, involves what he calls "a positive quality which excludes the fact" (001-011). The negative judgment does not simply lack a predicate; it positively asserts that the subject possesses a quality incompatible with the predicate in question.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of quality draws on the traditional logical doctrine of quality (affirmative/negative) found in Aristotle and the Scholastics, but he reinterprets it within his idealist framework where logic is inseparable from metaphysics.
