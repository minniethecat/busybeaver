## Overview

John Venn (1834–1923) was an English logician and philosopher, primarily remembered for the diagrammatic method that bears his name. In Bradley's *Principles of Logic*, Venn appears as a representative of the symbolic and extensional approach to logic that Bradley systematically opposes. Bradley engages with Venn's *Symbolic Logic* (1881) and *The Logic of Chance* (1866), treating Venn as a capable exponent of the formalist tradition whose doctrines nonetheless rest on what Bradley considers fundamental errors about the nature of judgment and inference.

Bradley's treatment of Venn is notably respectful in tone — he acknowledges Venn's "powers" and takes care to clarify the precise nature of their disagreement. (005-017) This makes Venn a significant figure for understanding the contrast between Bradley's idealist logic and the emerging formal-symbolic tradition in late nineteenth-century British philosophy.

## Key Ideas

### Symbolic Logic and Extension

Venn's *Symbolic Logic* developed a systematic treatment of logical operations in extensional terms, representing classes and their relations through diagrams and algebraic notation. Bradley references Venn's view that logical terms should "be taken in extension," citing *Symbolic Logic* page 395. (006-009) For Bradley, this extensional approach exemplifies the error of treating logical subjects as mere collections of individuals rather than as ideal contents united by qualitative synthesis.

### The Logic of Chance

Venn's work on probability theory in *The Logic of Chance* is noted alongside De Morgan and Jevons as part of the broader development of formal reasoning about uncertainty. (007-019) Bradley acknowledges this tradition but subordinates it to his own account of inference as grounded in ideal necessity rather than statistical regularity.

### The Ideal Form of Judgment

Bradley discusses Venn's notion that judgment is "stated in the ideal form," referencing *Symbolic Logic* page 137. (007-018) While Bradley shares the view that judgment involves ideal content, he diverges from Venn on what this ideality consists in — for Bradley, it is the universal synthesis of content, not merely formal representation.

## Bradley's Critique

Bradley's central objection to Venn concerns the nature of logical subjects. He argues that Venn's symbolic approach treats logical terms as if they could be exhaustively characterized by their extension, neglecting the intensive or qualitative dimension that Bradley takes to be fundamental. "The error I condemn, Mr. Venn, I think, has certainly done," Bradley writes, going on to characterize Venn's doctrine as leading to "seeming absurdity." (005-014) (005-015)

Yet Bradley's critique is not dismissive. He takes pains to "emphasize my difference with Mr. Venn" precisely because he recognizes the sophistication of Venn's position. (005-017) Bradley also notes a technical point about "getting a determinant" that Venn observes in *Symbolic Logic* (pages 323-5). (018-013)
