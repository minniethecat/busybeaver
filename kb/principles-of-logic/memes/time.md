# Time

In Bradley's system, time is treated as a form of appearance rather than a feature of ultimate reality. Logical relations, meanings, and the content of judgment are not events in time but ideal contents that transcend temporal succession.

## Overview

Bradley draws a sharp distinction between psychical events, which occur in time, and logical meanings, which do not. An idea considered as a meaning is "not in any sense an event in time" (001-004). When we grasp a logical truth, we grasp something that holds regardless of when it is thought.

This distinction is central to Bradley's idealism: the real is the timeless, and temporal succession characterizes only the world of appearance. Events that occur in space and time are understood as manifestations of an underlying reality that itself is not temporal (002-003).

## Key Aspects

### Meaning vs. Temporal Event

The meaning of a judgment is not identical with the psychical act of judging. Bradley emphasizes that he is "talking of the meaning, not of the psychical event" (001-004). The truth of "2+2=4" does not depend on when anyone thinks it.

### Time in Judgment

Bradley discusses cases where temporal opposition enters into judgment — for instance, the opposition "in time of A to B" in certain forms of assertion (001-016). However, he treats this temporal element as part of the judged content, not as the form of judgment itself.

### Time and Reality

Ultimate reality, for Bradley, is timeless. The things "that occur in space and time" belong to appearance, while "that once more which really exists" is not in time (002-003). This view reflects his Hegelian inheritance.
