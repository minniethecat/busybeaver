# Elision (Logical)

The logical operation by which part of the content — typically the middle term in a syllogism — is dropped from the conclusion. In Bradley's *Principles of Logic*, elision is a necessary feature of inference, but its misuse or misunderstanding is a source of logical error.

## Overview

Elision appears throughout Bradley's treatment of inference. In [[syllogism|syllogistic reasoning]], the middle term that connects subject and predicate in the [[premise|premises]] is "elided" — dropped from the conclusion. "A is B, A is C, therefore B is C" exhibits the standard pattern: the middle term A, having performed its connective function, is no longer needed in the result (018-012).

Bradley treats elision ambivalently. On one hand, "every inference involves and must involve some elision" (020-001) — it is structurally necessary because the conclusion cannot contain everything the [[premise|premises]] contain. On the other hand, elision can be "arbitrary" (020-003) when it drops content that is logically relevant. Distinguishing necessary from arbitrary elision is a central task of logical theory.

## Key Aspects

### Elision in the Syllogism

The standard syllogism — Barbara, Celarent, etc. — functions through the systematic elision of the middle term. "You abstain in the conclusion from elision of the middle" (018-012): the conclusion is precisely what remains after the middle has been dropped. Bradley notes that this is not a defect but the very point of [[syllogism|syllogistic reasoning]] — the middle term's sole function is to establish a connection that then no longer needs explicit statement.

### Necessary vs. Arbitrary Elision

Bradley distinguishes two cases. In some inferences, "the elision is necessary" (020-003): the dropped content genuinely plays no role in the conclusion. In others, "the elision is arbitrary" — content is dropped that, if retained, would alter or qualify the conclusion. The distinction matters because arbitrary elision produces conclusions that are formally valid but materially misleading.

### Elision and the Method of Difference

Elision is closely connected to what Bradley calls the "method of difference." After analysis distinguishes the components of a complex content, "elimination or elision of part of the content" (018-006) isolates the operative factor. This method, central to experimental science as well as to logic, relies on the correct identification of what can safely be elided.

### Elision and Abstraction

Elision involves abstraction — the dropped content is abstracted from, leaving a sparer result. But Bradley warns that "elision and abstraction will demonstrate anything you please" (026-010) if used without restraint. The danger is not in elision itself but in the failure to recognize that the elided content may be logically relevant to the truth of the conclusion.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of elision engages with the traditional logical doctrine of the *dictum de omni et nullo* — the principle that what is affirmed of a whole is affirmed of its parts. By making the elision of the middle explicit, Bradley shows that the traditional doctrine presupposes an operation it does not name. His analysis also anticipates later discussions of relevance logic, which query whether the middle term genuinely connects the extremes in every formally valid syllogism.
