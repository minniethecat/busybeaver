Imagination, in Bradley's treatment, is the capacity to form and hold before the mind images and representations that are not immediately given in perception. It occupies an intermediate position between bare sensation and logical thought, and Bradley's analysis of its role is central to his account of how judgment transcends the merely psychical.

## Overview

Bradley recognizes that imagination is pervasive in mental life. When we think, we are accompanied by images: "the relation as it exists in my imagination" (001-007) is part of the concrete psychical fact of thinking. Yet imagination alone does not constitute judgment. A mere image, "let it be ever so actual in my imagination, is clearly not what we meant by truth" (006-005).

The crucial distinction for Bradley is between imagination as a psychical fact and judgment as a logical act. Imagination presents an image; judgment refers that image to reality and takes a stance on its truth. "In the former we have the reference to reality; and it is this which distinguishes truth from bare imagination" (002-026).

## Key Aspects

### Imagination and Judgment

Bradley argues that imagination provides the sensory material that judgment works upon, but judgment adds something that imagination lacks: the reference of an ideal content to reality. Without this reference, "there can be no difference between imagination and reality, truth and falsehood" (001-009).

### Imagination and Truth

The distinction between imagination and truth is fundamental to Bradley's epistemology. Dreams, delusions, and poems all involve imagination (002-001), but they do not involve the claim to truth that characterizes judgment. Imagination "is not confined to fact" (002-027) — it can range freely, while judgment is bound by the logical demand for coherence with reality.

### Creative Imagination

Bradley acknowledges the creative power of imagination, particularly in scientific and philosophical thinking. What Jevons called "subjective connection in the imagination" (013-014) may guide inquiry, but it does not guarantee truth. The creations we "owe to the imagination of Professor Bain" (013-015) must ultimately be tested against the logical standard.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of imagination engages with the British empiricist tradition, particularly Hume's account of imagination as the faculty that associates ideas. For Bradley, the empiricists rightly recognized imagination's role but wrongly reduced judgment to imaginative association.
