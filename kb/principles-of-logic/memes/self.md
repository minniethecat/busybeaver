The self, in Bradley's philosophy, is not a simple substance or Cartesian ego but the felt unity of psychical experience that develops through the "evolution of self-consciousness" (001-016). For Bradley, the self is a real but finite appearance — a necessary stage in experience that is ultimately transcended in the absolute.

## Overview

Bradley treats the self as a psychical fact rather than a metaphysical substance. The self is experienced as a centre of feeling, desire, and thought, but this experience does not warrant the inference to a permanent, independent ego. The self "can never itself be what it means" (001-003) — it points beyond itself to the wider reality within which it exists.

The development of self-consciousness is, for Bradley, a relatively "late acquisition of the mind" (001-017), marking a stage in the mind's progress from mere feeling to reflective thought. The self is not given from the start but is constructed through the process of experience and judgment.

## Key Aspects

### Self as Appearance

The self, like all finite things, belongs to the realm of appearance. It is a real fact of experience — "as stubborn a fact as any" (001-001) — but it is not ultimate reality. The self is caught up in the same relational web that characterizes all finite existence, and its apparent independence is an abstraction.

### Self-Consciousness and Judgment

Bradley connects self-consciousness to the act of judgment. When we judge, we distinguish the self from the not-self, the subject from the object. This distinction is necessary for thought but is ultimately transcended in the absolute, where the opposition of self and world is resolved.

### The Finite Self

The finite self is self-destructive in Bradley's sense: "fleeting and self-destructive as is their very nature" (001-005). The self constantly changes, and its identity through time is not the identity of a substance but the continuity of a process. The self is real, but its reality is that of an appearance within the whole.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of the self engages critically with both the Cartesian tradition of the self as thinking substance and the empiricist reduction of the self to a bundle of perceptions. For Bradley, both views are one-sided: the self is neither an independent substance nor a mere collection, but a finite centre of experience that participates in the absolute.
