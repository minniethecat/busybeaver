# W.S. Jevons

William Stanley Jevons (1835–1882) was a British logician and economist whose *Principles of Science* (1874) proposed an equational logic that Bradley subjects to detailed critical scrutiny in the *Principles of Logic*, dedicating an entire chapter to its examination.

## Overview

Jevons is Bradley's most extensively discussed contemporary logician. Bradley engages with Jevons' equational logic — a system that represents logical operations as algebraic equations — across multiple chapters, with Chapter VIII ("Jevons' [[equational-logic|Equational Logic]]") devoted entirely to its critical examination. Bradley's treatment is respectful but ultimately negative: he acknowledges Jevons' ingenuity while arguing that the equational approach fundamentally misrepresents the nature of logical inference.

The frequency of Bradley's references to Jevons — "Vid. Jevons, Principles of Science" (001-014), "Professor Jevons" (004-004), and numerous other citations — testifies to Jevons' importance in the logical landscape of the 1880s. For Bradley, Jevons represents the most sophisticated version of the formal, algebraic approach to logic that he ultimately rejects.

## Key Ideas

### Equational Logic

Jevons proposed treating logical propositions as equations, where the predicate is equated with the subject in a manner analogous to mathematical equality. "The doctrine of equation, or identity of the terms" (001-016) is the core of Jevons' approach. For Jevons, "A is B" becomes "A = B" — a move that Bradley argues collapses the distinction between subject and predicate that is essential to judgment.

### The Substitution of Similars

Jevons' fundamental principle is that "whatever is true of a thing is true of its like." Bradley cites this principle critically: "the principle of substitution of similars" is, in Bradley's view, insufficient to account for the actual operation of inference, which requires a real identity persisting through difference, not mere similarity.

## Bradley's Critique

Bradley's main objection to Jevons' equational logic is that it reduces judgment to a relation between [[idea|ideas]] rather than a reference to reality. "The doctrine of equation, or identity of the terms, has itself grasped a truth" (001-016), Bradley concedes, but the truth it grasps is partial. [[judgment|Judgment]] is not the equation of two [[idea|ideas]] but the qualification of reality by an ideal content.

Against Jevons' specific logical proposals, Bradley raises technical objections. He argues that Jevons' treatment of [[disjunctive-judgment|disjunction]] is flawed: "the subtle argument of Professor Jevons, which I regret to state I am unable to follow" (005-011) — a characteristically Bradleyan dismissal that combines politeness with devastating effect.

Bradley also objects that equational logic cannot account for the varieties of judgment he has identified: [[negation|negative judgment]], [[disjunction|disjunctive judgment]], and hypothetical judgment all resist r[[immediate-inference|eduction]] to equations.

## Influence

Jevons' *Principles of Science* was an influential work in 19th-century logic, representing the algebraic tradition that culminated in Boole's and Venn's systems. While Bradley's critique limited Jevons' influence on the idealist tradition, Jevons' equational approach anticipated aspects of modern formal logic. Bradley's engagement with Jevons represents an important moment in the debate between formal and philosophical approaches to logic.
