# Quantity of Judgments

The quantity of judgments is the classification of judgments according to the extension of their subject term: singular (this S is P), particular (some S is P), and universal (all S is P). Bradley examines this traditional Kantian division to uncover its logical foundations and limitations.

## Overview

In the traditional logical scheme inherited from Kant, the quantity of a judgment determines how much of the subject's extension is involved in the predication. Bradley devotes sustained attention to this classification, particularly to the status of singular judgments, which he treats as a distinct and problematic category. (002-006)

Bradley's analysis of judgment quantity goes beyond mere taxonomy. He uses the quantity distinction to probe deeper questions about the nature of judgment itself: whether all judgments are ultimately universal in form, whether singular judgments have a privileged relation to reality, and whether the traditional classification survives scrutiny. (006-009) His conclusion is characteristically subversive — the distinctions of quantity, like those of modality, are not fundamental features of judgment but artifacts of a particular mode of analysis.

## Key Aspects

### Singular Judgments

Singular judgments form a special class because their subject is an individual. Bradley examines three divisions of singular judgments in detail. (002-014) The first class includes judgments whose subject is a proper name; the second, those with demonstrative subjects; and the third, those with subjects fixed by a description that picks out a unique individual. (002-028) In each case, Bradley questions whether the singular judgment truly differs in logical form from the universal.

The singular judgment faces a fundamental challenge: if its subject is a unique individual, what justifies the attribution of a predicate? Bradley argues that the singular judgment "is nearer the fact" (002-035) than the universal, but it inherits all the metaphysical difficulties of reference to individuals. Ultimately, the "only hope for the singular judgment lies in complete renunciation" (002-041) of the claim to a special logical status — it is, in essence, a universal judgment with a uniqueness condition built into its subject.

### Universal Judgments

The universal judgment — "All A is B" — is for Bradley the paradigm of logical form. He analyzes it not as a mere summary of singular judgments but as expressing a necessary connection between subject-concept and predicate-concept. (002-030) The universal judgment "All A is B" is "an abbreviation" (002-030) for a more complex logical relation.

Bradley challenges the empiricist view that universal judgments are simply collections of singular observations. The universal, he argues, involves an ideal content that goes beyond any enumeration of instances. (002-005) This ideal content is what makes inference possible — without universality, [[inference|reasoning]] would reduce to the mere association of particulars.

### Particular Judgments

The particular judgment — "Some S is P" — occupies an intermediate position in the quantity scheme. Bradley notes that the particular judgment is logically weaker than the universal but stronger than the merely possible. Its logical function is primarily negative: "Some S is P" rules out the universal negative "No S is P" without committing to a full universal affirmative.

### Beyond the Traditional Classification

Bradley's analysis of judgment quantity ultimately points toward the dissolution of the traditional tripartite scheme. He argues that identity in quality and quantity ultimately collapse into each other, as sameness in one respect implies sameness in the other under the monistic logic. (013-006) The categories of quantity are provisional distinctions useful for analysis but not ultimate features of logical reality.

## Historical Context

The classification of judgments by quantity is one of the four headings in Kant's table of judgments (alongside quality, relation, and modality). It was taken over from Aristotelian logic, where the distinction between universal and [[particular-judgment|particular proposition]]s was fundamental to syllogistic theory. Bradley's treatment is informed by both traditions but aims to show that neither provides an adequate account of what quantity actually contributes to judgment.

Bradley's distinctive contribution is to connect the problem of judgment quantity with the metaphysical problem of universals and particulars. Whether a judgment is singular or universal is not merely a grammatical question but reflects deep assumptions about the nature of reality — specifically, whether individuals or universals are logically prior.
