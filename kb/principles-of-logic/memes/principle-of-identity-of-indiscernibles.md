# Principle of Identity of Indiscernibles

The Principle of Identity of Indiscernibles states that if two entities share all their properties, they are identical — no two distinct things can be exactly alike in every respect. Bradley examines this principle in the context of inference and the nature of qualitative identity.

## Overview

The identity of indiscernibles is a principle with roots in Leibniz's metaphysics but with significant implications for logical theory. Bradley discusses it in connection with the problem of how inference can be valid despite the numerical diversity of its instances. If two things are indiscernible in their relevant properties, then what holds of one must hold of the other — and this is the foundation of [[inference|reasoning]] by substitution of identicals.

Bradley's treatment is characteristically nuanced. "The identity of indiscernibles may be true or false" (012-011), he notes, but its truth or falsity is not the primary question. The deeper issue is what it means for two things to share all properties — whether "sharing a property" is itself a coherent notion in Bradley's monistic framework, where all distinctions are ultimately internal to a single reality.

The principle plays a crucial role in Bradley's theory of inference. "Reasoning depends on the identity of indiscernibles" (022-012), because the middle term in a syllogism must be identically the same in both [[premise|premises]] for the inference to be valid. Without the identity of indiscernibles, the middle term could vary imperceptibly between [[premise|premises]], breaking the inferential chain.

## Key Aspects

### Qualitative Identity and Numerical Difference

Bradley's central question about the principle concerns the relationship between qualitative and numerical identity. Two things may share a quality — they are the same in respect of having that quality — without being numerically identical. "Sameness of quality proves nothing as to numerical identity" (002-023), Bradley insists. The indiscernibility of properties does not directly entail identity, because sharing a property is itself a relation that presupposes a wider system in which the sharing occurs.

### Indiscernibles and Space-Time

Bradley considers the case of spatial and temporal indiscernibles. Spaces and times "are indiscernible and yet are not identical" (012-009) — two distinct spatial positions may share all qualitative properties while remaining distinct by virtue of their spatial relations. This poses a direct challenge to the principle: if spatial relations are properties, then the two positions are not indiscernible (they have different spatial relations); if they are not properties, then the principle's domain of application is narrower than its proponents claim.

### Inference and the Middle Term

The principle is essential to [[syllogism|syllogistic inference]]. When we reason "A = B, B = C, therefore A = C," the validity depends on the middle term B being identically the same in both [[premise|premises]]. "Reasoning depends on the identity of indiscernibles; for the middle operation must be identically one." (022-012) If B in the first premise were merely similar to, but not identical with, B in the second premise, the inference would fail. Bradley uses this logical role to illuminate the metaphysical commitments of inference.

### Bradley's Assessment

Bradley's final assessment of the principle is ambivalent. The principle is logically indispensable for inference but metaphysically problematic when interpreted as a claim about the ultimate nature of things. "The identity of indiscernibles may be true or false, but not both" (012-011) — Bradley's point is that the principle cannot be simultaneously a logical rule and a metaphysical thesis about real entities without generating contradictions.

## Historical Context

Leibniz formulated the principle as part of his metaphysics of individual substance, using it to argue against the existence of atoms and to support the identity of his "monads." In Leibniz's hands, the principle was a tool for establishing the uniqueness of each substance. Bradley inherits the principle through the British idealist tradition, where it had been discussed by followers of Hegel.

Bradley's treatment differs from Leibniz's in two crucial respects. First, Bradley does not assume a world of independently existing individuals to which the principle applies; his monism holds that all individuals are ultimately appearances of a single reality. Second, Bradley is more interested in the logical role of the principle — its function in grounding inference — than in its metaphysical implications for substance theory.
