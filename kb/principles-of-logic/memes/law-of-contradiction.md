# Law of Contradiction

In Bradley's *Principles of Logic*, the Law of Contradiction states that contradictory attributes cannot both be true of the same subject at the same time. Unlike the formal-logical version, Bradley grounds this principle not in the structure of thought alone but in "the nature of things" — contradiction exists in reality, not merely in the form of judgment.

## Overview

Bradley treats the Law of Contradiction in Chapter V alongside Identity, [[law-of-excluded-middle|Excluded Middle]], and [[double-negation|Double Negation]]. His central claim is that "in the nature of things contradiction exists. It is the fact that opposites cannot coexist" (005-005). This realist grounding distinguishes his account from purely formal treatments. The law is not a rule we impose on thought; it reflects a genuine incompatibility in the real world.

Bradley is careful to distinguish contradiction from mere difference. "Denial or contradiction is not the same thing as the assertion of disparates" (003-006). Two things can be different without being contradictory. [[contradiction|Contradiction]] proper involves the rejection of an ideal content from a real subject — it is the assertion that a particular predicate cannot qualify a particular reality.

## Key Aspects

### Contradiction in Reality

Bradley's most distinctive claim is that contradiction exists objectively. "It is the fact that opposites cannot coexist" (005-005). This is not a psychological claim about what we can think, nor merely a linguistic rule about what we can say. The incompatibility is in the things themselves. If someone objects that when the opposing things no longer exist, "then where is the contradiction?", Bradley replies that this misses the point — the contradiction exists while the incompatible attributes are both present (005-006).

### Contradiction and Exclusion

Bradley traces the development of contradiction from the more basic notion of exclusion. "The basis in the way in which contradiction is developed from exclusion" (005-007) is key to understanding its logical force. Exclusion is a real relation between qualities; contradiction is the logical expression of this real exclusion.

### Mere Contradiction Is Not Assertion

Bradley emphasizes a subtle but important point: "the mere contradiction does not affirm this or any particular contrary assertion" (005-014). To contradict "A is red" is not automatically to assert that A is blue or green or any specific alternative. [[contradiction|Contradiction]] negates without positively determining what is true in place of the negated content.

## Historical Context

Bradley's realist treatment of contradiction contrasts with the psychologistic approaches of his contemporaries. Where Mill and the associationists treated logical laws as empirical generalizations about mental operations, Bradley insists on their objective, metaphysical grounding. His view develops themes from Hegel's Science of Logic — particularly the idea that contradiction is not a mark of error but a feature of reality itself — while adapting them to the British philosophical idiom and his own distinctive metaphysical realism.
