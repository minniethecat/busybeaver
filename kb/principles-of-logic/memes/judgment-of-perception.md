# Judgment of Perception

The most elementary form of judgment, arising directly from perceptual encounter — yet even at this level, Bradley argues, judgment involves universality and inferential structure (002-041).

## Overview

Bradley treats the judgment of perception as the lowest round of the logical ladder — the point at which thought makes contact with immediate experience. But his analysis reveals that even this primitive form of judgment is not a mere recording of sensory data. Every judgment of perception is in a sense universal, bringing the particular under a general form (002-041).

This claim is central to Bradley's critique of empiricism. If even perceptual judgment involves universality, then the empiricist hope of building knowledge from purely particular sense-data is misguided. Thought is always already at work in perception, organizing and universalizing.

## Key Aspects

### Perception and Universality

Bradley's key insight is that every judgment of perception is in a sense universal (002-041). When we judge "this is red," we are not merely registering a sensory quality; we are applying the universal concept "red" to a particular instance. This application is the work of thought, not of passive sensation.

### Categorical and Hypothetical Structure

Bradley notes that a categorical judgment of perception already contains a hypothetical element — it means that under certain conditions, a certain predication holds (013-002). This reveals the inferential structure latent in even the simplest judgments.

## Historical Context

Bradley's analysis of perceptual judgment engages with the empiricist tradition, particularly Hume's treatment of impressions and [[idea|ideas]], and with Kant's distinction between intuitions and concepts. Bradley's position is that perception and conception are not separate faculties but intertwined aspects of a single cognitive process.
