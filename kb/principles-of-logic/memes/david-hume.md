# David Hume

David Hume (1711–1776) was a Scottish philosopher, historian, and essayist whose radical empiricism — particularly his account of belief, his theory of association, and his treatment of identity — constitutes the primary philosophical target underlying much of Bradley's *Principles of Logic*.

## Overview

David Hume stands behind the British empiricist tradition as its most rigorous and uncompromising exponent, and his presence pervades Bradley's *Principles of Logic* even where he is not named. Bradley's engagement with Hume is both explicit and structural: Hume provides the metaphysical [[premise|premises]] — psychological atomism, the separation of distinct existences, the r[[immediate-inference|eduction]] of belief to vivacity — against which Bradley's entire logical project is directed.

Bradley references Hume at critical junctures throughout the work. In the opening chapter on judgment, Bradley notes with approval that "it has been often remarked that, on Hume's theory of belief, there can be no difference between imagination and reality, truth and falsehood" (001-009). This acknowledgment of a well-known objection to Hume's theory sets the stage for Bradley's own alternative account of judgment. Later, Bradley traces the fundamental error of the empiricist school to Hume's principle that "all our distinct perceptions are distinct existences, and the mind never perceives any real connection among distinct existences" (013-003).

## Key Ideas

### Psychological Atomism

Hume's commitment to the separateness of mental atoms is, for Bradley, the original sin of British empiricism. "The philosophy of [[experience|Experience]] is psychological Atomism," Bradley writes, quoting Hume's dictum that "all our distinct perceptions are distinct existences" (013-003). From this starting point — that there is nothing which mental atoms possess in common — Bradley argues that the entire empiricist project of explaining mental life through association is doomed from the start: relations cannot be constructed from unrelated elements.

### Theory of Belief

Hume's account of belief as a "lively idea associated with a present impression" is subjected to Bradley's sustained criticism. Bradley notes that the liveliness criterion fails on phenomenological grounds: "The liveliness of the idea not only is not judgment, but it is not always even a condition. The doctrine that an idea judged true must be stronger than one not so judged, will not bear confrontation with the actual phenomena" (001-009). Bradley illustrates this with the example of vivid images of the dead, which can be "distressing, from their strength and particularity and actual localization" yet are not judged real by an educated person.

### Identity and Difference

Hume's bold speculations on personal identity — and his subsequent suppression of them — are cited by Bradley as evidence of the empiricist tradition's inability to confront its own implications. "Since Hume's bold speculations on the subject of identity were suppressed by himself, the English school has repeated a lesson by rote and flaunted a blind ancestral prejudice" (012-010). Bradley argues that the [[law-of-identity|principle of identity]] in difference — central to his own logic — is precisely what Hume's atomism cannot accommodate.

### Analysis and Abstraction

Hume is also identified as the source of a "cardinal principle of error and delusion" in empiricist methodology: the assumption that mental distinctions correspond to separable existences. Bradley quotes Hume (002-036) as the origin of this "most ruinous superstition to suppose that analysis is no alteration, and that, whenever we distinguish, we have at once to do with divisible existence." For Bradley, the true nature of analysis — that it abstracts elements from a whole that never exist independently — is obscured by the Humean tradition.

## Bradley's Critique

Bradley's critique of Hume operates at multiple levels. At the most fundamental level, Bradley rejects Hume's metaphysical atomism: the thesis that mental life consists of distinct, separable units. Bradley argues that this atomism makes relations impossible, since relations between atoms would themselves have to be further atoms, leading to an infinite regress. "If relations are facts that exist between facts, then what comes between the relations and the other facts?" (013-003).

At the psychological level, Bradley rejects Hume's [[association-of-ideas|associationism]] as a theory that cannot explain the actual phenomena of mental reproduction. The laws of contiguity and similarity, Bradley argues, presuppose the very connections they claim to produce.

At the logical level, Bradley's entire theory of judgment is constructed against Hume's r[[immediate-inference|eduction]] of judgment to association-plus-vivacity. For Bradley, judgment is not a psychological event but a logical act — the reference of an ideal content to reality. Hume's failure to recognize this distinction is, Bradley suggests, the root of empiricism's inability to account for truth and falsity.

## Influence

David Hume's *Treatise of Human Nature* (1739–1740) and *Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding* (1748) established the framework for British empiricism that dominated 19th-century philosophy. Hume's influence on Bradley is primarily negative: Hume is the thinker Bradley must overcome to establish his own idealist logic. Yet Bradley's engagement with Hume is also productive — it is through the critique of Humean atomism that Bradley develops his own doctrines of identity in difference, the holistic nature of mental content, and the logical (rather than psychological) character of judgment and inference. Hume thus serves as both Bradley's principal antagonist and, paradoxically, one of the thinkers who most shapes the structure of Bradley's argument.
