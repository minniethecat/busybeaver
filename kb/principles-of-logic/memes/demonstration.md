# Demonstration

The logical operation by which a conclusion is shown to follow necessarily from given [[premise|premises]]. In Bradley's *Principles of Logic*, demonstration unites induction (the gathering of data) with [[inference|deduction]] (the ideal construction that combines them), forming the complete logical process.

## Overview

Bradley defines demonstration in Book II, Chapter II: "the union of both [induction and [[inference|deduction]]] is logical demonstration" (010-001). Demonstration is not merely the formal arrangement of a syllogism; it is the act of pointing out the necessity of a conclusion by showing the ideal connections among the data. "Demonstration is merely pointing out or showing that a thing is so" (010-002).

The chapter on demonstration is strategically placed after Bradley's treatment of inference. His aim is to show that formal logic, when properly understood, is demonstrative through and through. The syllogism is not a mechanical manipulation of terms but the exhibition of a necessary connection, grounded in the identity that persists through difference.

## Key Aspects

### Demonstration vs. Mere Inference

Not every inference amounts to a demonstration. Bradley distinguishes between casual inference, where we simply conclude, and demonstrative inference, where we show why the conclusion must follow. "In either case we employ demonstration, but only in the latter do we understand the ground of the necessity" (010-004). Demonstration makes the inferential ground explicit.

### The Inductive Component

Demonstration requires data. Bradley insists that "demonstration removes a barrier from our sight" (018-005) — it does not create new content but reveals connections already implicit in the [[premise|premises]]. The inductive gathering of data supplies the material; demonstration supplies the form that exhibits necessity. Without adequate data, demonstration is hollow; without demonstration, data remain an unconnected aggregate.

### The Elision of the Middle

In syllogistic demonstration, the conclusion is reached through the [[elision|elision of the middle]] term. "A is B, A is C, therefore B is C" — the middle term A, having served its connecting function, is dropped from the conclusion. This elision is not arbitrary but necessary: the middle term's work is done once the connection between B and C has been exhibited.

### Demonstration and Necessity

Bradley is careful about the scope of demonstrative necessity. [[inference|Inference]]s "so far as they amount to demonstration, have been found to depend upon the [[law-of-identity|principle of identity]]" (026-013). A genuine demonstration shows that the conclusion is already contained in the [[premise|premises]] through the identity of the connecting term. Where identity is only partial, demonstration is correspondingly limited.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of demonstration engages with the Aristotelian tradition of *apodeixis* (demonstrative proof) while transforming it through idealist logic. Where Aristotle emphasized the formal structure of the syllogism, Bradley emphasizes the underlying ideal construction that makes the formal structure possible. His account also responds to [[john-stuart-mill|J.S. Mill]]'s r[[immediate-inference|eduction]] of all inference to induction — Bradley insists that induction without the ideal construction of demonstration achieves no logical necessity.
