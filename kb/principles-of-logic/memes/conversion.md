# Conversion

Conversion is the traditional logical operation of interchanging the subject and predicate terms of a categorical proposition while preserving truth. In Bradley's treatment, conversion serves as a test case for the claim that formal transformation rules can capture the actual movement of logical thought.

## Overview

Conversion belongs to the class of "immediate inferences" in traditional logic — inferences that proceed from a single premise to a conclusion without a middle term. The operation is simple: from "All A is B," conversion yields (in the traditional scheme) "Some B is A." Bradley examines conversion not as an exercise in formal logic but as a revealing case of what actually happens when thought moves from one judgment to another. (017-001)

His analysis connects conversion to the broader problem of inference. If conversion is a genuine inference — if it produces a conclusion that was not already present in the premise — then the simplest logical transformation already exhibits the features that make inference philosophically problematic. If it is not a genuine inference — if the conclusion is already contained in the premise — then traditional logic has misdescribed what it is doing. (018-009)

This dilemma exemplifies Bradley's method of using the simplest logical forms to expose the deepest philosophical problems.

## Key Aspects

### Conversion of Affirmative Propositions

The traditional rule for converting affirmative categorical propositions distinguishes between universal affirmatives ("All A is B," which converts to "Some B is A") and particular affirmatives ("Some A is B," which converts to "Some B is A"). Bradley examines whether these transformations are genuinely rational or merely verbal. (018-011)

His objection is that the traditional rules assume what they claim to demonstrate. The conversion of "All A is B" to "Some B is A" is valid only if we already understand what "all" and "some" contribute to the proposition's logical force. The formal rule records this understanding but does not constitute it. The conversion is rational — Bradley does not deny that — but its rationality cannot be explained by the formal rule alone.

### Conversion of Negative Propositions

The conversion of negative propositions raises additional difficulties. The universal negative ("No A is B") converts simply to "No B is A," and Bradley notes that this case appears to involve a genuine symmetry that the affirmative case [[privation|lack]]s. (018-012)

However, even this apparently straightforward case conceals complexity. The symmetry of "No A is B" and "No B is A" depends on the [[negation|negative judgment]]'s distinctive logical character — a character that Bradley has already analyzed at length in his chapter on [[negation|negative judgment]]. The simplicity of negative conversion is purchased at the cost of the [[negation|negative judgment]]'s own problematic nature.

### Conversion as Immediate Inference

Bradley situates conversion within the broader category of immediate inference — inferences that do not require a middle term. Some immediate inferences appear to be mere tautologies, repackaging the same content in different words. Others, including conversion, appear to yield genuinely new knowledge. (017-001)

The tension between these two descriptions — conversion as tautology and conversion as inference — is Bradley's real target. If conversion is tautology, then traditional logic has been presenting verbal transformations as logical inferences. If it is genuine inference, then even the simplest logical operation involves the systematic development of content that Bradley identifies as the essence of all [[inference|reasoning]]. Bradley inclines toward the second view: conversion is a limiting case of inference, and its analysis reveals features that are present in all inference, however complex.

## Historical Context

Conversion has been part of the logical curriculum since Aristotle's *Prior Analytics*, where it is treated among the basic operations on propositions. In medieval logic, conversion, obversion, and contraposition were collectively known as the "immediate inferences" and were taught as preliminary to the theory of the syllogism.

Bradley's treatment is innovative in using conversion as a philosophical problem rather than a technical rule. His question is not "what are the rules of conversion?" but "what makes conversion possible as a movement of thought?" This shift — from formal description to philosophical explanation — is characteristic of his idealist approach to logic. The question anticipates, in a different idiom, later debates about whether logical rules are constitutive of [[inference|reasoning]] or merely descriptive of it.
