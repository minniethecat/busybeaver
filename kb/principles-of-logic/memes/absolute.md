The absolute, in Bradley's logical system, is ultimate reality considered as a complete and self-consistent whole. It is not a separate entity standing apart from appearances but is the reality in which all appearances, properly understood, are resolved. The concept of the absolute is the metaphysical culmination of Bradley's idealism.

## Overview

Bradley's absolute is introduced through the logical analysis of judgment. Every judgment refers an ideal content to reality, and reality, when considered in its entirety, is the absolute. The absolute is not one thing among others but is the whole within which all things find their place. Bradley's absolute is not a theological concept but a logical-metaphysical one: it is what reality must be if judgment is to be possible at all.

The absolute stands in contrast to the relative and the finite. Bradley distinguishes between relative and absolute uniqueness: a series is "relative or absolute" depending on whether its character is determined by relations to other series or is self-contained (002-027). Absolute uniqueness occurs "when the series is taken as self-complete" (002-028). This logical distinction maps onto the metaphysical distinction between finite appearances, which exist only in relation to other things, and the absolute, which is self-subsistent.

## Key Aspects

### Absolute and Relative

Bradley contrasts the absolute with the relative throughout his logic. External facts "are not absolutely fixed" (002-018); rather, they are what they are only in relation to a wider context. The truth of a statement may be "absolutely independent of the existence" of particular facts (002-035), meaning that logical truth does not depend on contingent psychical events. The movement from relative to absolute is the movement of thought toward completeness.

### Absolute Uniqueness

The concept of absolute uniqueness plays a key role in Bradley's theory of inference and identity. He argues that "bare numerical diversity does not carry with it an absolute uniqueness" (002-016) — the mere fact that two things are numerically distinct does not make each absolutely unique, because what a thing is always involves relations to other things. Absolute uniqueness would require self-completeness without external relations.

### The Absolute as Whole

For Bradley, the absolute is the whole of reality. No finite fact can be made absolute: "itself, it can never be made absolute. Its existence refers itself beyond" (002-039). Every finite thing points beyond itself to a larger context, and this chain of reference terminates only in the absolute whole. The absolute is thus not an item within experience but the totality within which all experience occurs.

## Historical Context

Bradley's concept of the absolute develops the idealist tradition from Hegel, for whom the absolute is the final synthesis of all finite determinations. Bradley departs from Hegel in giving the concept a more logical than historical-dialectical grounding: his argument for the absolute proceeds from the analysis of judgment and inference rather than from a philosophy of history. The concept also serves as Bradley's response to the empiricist and positivist rejection of metaphysics — the absolute is not a speculative postulate but is implied by the very structure of judgment.
