# Premise

In Bradley's logic, a judgment that serves as the starting point for inference, whose truth and logical force are not given but are themselves established through the ideal experiment that they in turn govern.

## Overview

The concept of a premise appears straightforward in traditional logic: premises are the given judgments from which a conclusion follows. But Bradley's analysis reveals deep complexities in this seemingly simple notion. In the *Principles of Logic*, premises are not treated as inert starting points; they are active elements in the inferential process, and their own logical status is as much in question as the conclusions drawn from them.

Bradley's discussion of premises ranges across fundamental issues in epistemology and the philosophy of logic. What makes a premise a premise? Are premises known independently of conclusions, or is their status as premises dependent on the inferential role they play? Can ultimate premises be proven, or are they simply accepted? Bradley's answers to these questions are characteristically dialectical: premises are both given and made, both starting points and results.

The concept is explored in connection with the ideal experiment. The premises of an inference set the initial conditions for the ideal experiment; they are the fixed points relative to which suggested connections are tested. Yet Bradley insists that even premises are subject to revision through the same process. "On the contrary our process is the ideal experiment which proves this major" (018-007). The major premise is not an unquestionable given; it is established through the very inferential process it governs.

Bradley also considers the problem of ultimate premises—the "ultimate premises of every proof can not be proved" (007-003). If every premise required a prior proof, we would face an infinite regress. Bradley's resolution involves the ideal experiment: ultimate premises are those that maintain themselves through repeated ideal experimentation, not those that rest on some external foundation.

## Key Aspects

### Premises as Starting Points

At one level, premises are simply the judgments from which inference begins. "If we start from premises" we may arrive at a conclusion (007-001). Bradley acknowledges this ordinary sense of the term: in a syllogism, the two premises are the given judgments whose combination yields the conclusion. The conclusion "appears from the union of the premises" (006-003).

### The Proof of Premises

Bradley challenges the assumption that premises are simply given. The major premise of a syllogism, for instance, is not an independent truth but a judgment whose validity is established through the ideal experiment. "On the contrary our process is the ideal experiment which proves this major" (018-007). This means that premises and conclusions are not fixed roles; the same judgment can function as premise in one context and as conclusion in another.

### The Problem of Ultimate Premises

Bradley confronts the regress problem directly. "The ultimate premises of every proof can not be proved" (007-003). If proof always requires premises, and premises always require proof, no proof could ever begin. Bradley's solution appeals to the ideal experiment and the coherence theory of truth: ultimate premises are those that maintain themselves across the widest range of ideal experiments, not those that are self-evident or foundationally given.

### Premises Known to Be False

Bradley makes the striking observation that inference is valid even when premises are known to be false. "The necessity is as strong where the premises are known to be false" (007-005). The logical connection between premises and conclusion does not depend on the truth of the premises. This underlines the formal character of inference: what matters is the ideal connection, not the correspondence with reality.

### Denying the Premise

In a critical exchange with empiricist logic, Bradley discusses the possibility of directly denying a premise rather than the conclusion. "In the next place we directly deny the premise" (007-008). This move—rejecting a premise rather than accepting a problematic conclusion—is a legitimate logical response, and Bradley analyzes its conditions and limits. The [[negation|denial]] of a premise, like its assertion, must be tested through the ideal experiment.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of premises engages with a long tradition of reflection on the foundations of deductive [[inference|reasoning]]. Aristotle's doctrine that demonstrative science requires indemonstrable first principles (*Posterior Analytics*) sets the terms for subsequent debate. Bradley inherits this problem but rejects the Aristotelian solution of self-evident axioms, offering instead a coherence-based account grounded in the ideal experiment.

The concept also connects to nineteenth-century debates about the nature of logic. Mill's empiricism treated the premises of syllogism as summaries of particular experiences; Bradley's idealism treats them as ideal constructions whose validity is internal to the system of thought. This contrast encapsulates the broader disagreement between empiricist and idealist philosophies of logic.
