# Validity

Validity, in Bradley's logical system, is the property by which an inference rightly claims to move from [[premise|premises]] to conclusion. Bradley's treatment is distinctive: he rejects the idea that validity can be reduced to formal rules and instead treats it as a question about the nature of the logical connection itself.

## Overview

Bradley addresses the problem of validity most intensively in his final chapter, "The Validity of [[inference|Inference]]," where the central question is not which inferences are valid but what validity itself consists in. He resists the formal-logical answer that validity is a matter of following rules, arguing that rules themselves require justification — and this justification cannot itself be rule-governed without regress. (026-003)

His treatment connects validity to the systematic character of thought as a whole. An inference is valid not because it conforms to an external standard but because it expresses a genuine connection of content within the single system of reality that judgment and inference together construct. This is a deeply idealist conception, but Bradley defends it through detailed engagement with alternative views.

The question of validity pervades the entire work, appearing whenever Bradley examines whether a particular logical operation — abstraction, analysis, [[syllogism|syllogistic reasoning]] — can justify its own procedures. (002-006)

## Key Aspects

### Validity as a Problem, Not a Criterion

Bradley opens his chapter on validity by framing it as a dilemma: if the conclusion is already contained in the [[premise|premises]], inference is valid but tautological; if the conclusion is not contained, inference is invalid. This dilemma shows that validity cannot be understood in terms of containment or novelty alone. (026-003)

The standard formal-logical answer — that validity is a matter of logical form, guaranteed by rules of inference — does not satisfy Bradley. He points out that the rules themselves require justification, and that justification cannot be supplied by more rules without infinite regress. The question of validity is therefore a substantive philosophical problem, not a technical one. (011-005)

### Validity and the Syllogism

Bradley examines traditional syllogistic logic as a case study in the problem of validity. The syllogistic maxims (dictum de omni, etc.) were supposed to guarantee the validity of [[syllogism|syllogistic inference]], but Bradley argues that they either beg the question or fail to capture what actually makes inferences valid. (012-007)

His extended critique of the syllogistic figures is not an attack on [[syllogism|syllogistic reasoning]] itself but on the claim that its validity can be explained by formal rules alone. The real ground of validity, for Bradley, lies in the systematic connection of content, not in conformity to formal schemata. (012-009)

### The Principle of Validity

Bradley identifies the principle that tests inferences as also the principle that constructs them: "the principle by which we test the validity of inferences is also the principle which has made them." This is a central insight of his idealist logic — the criterion of validity is internal to the process of [[inference|reasoning]], not externally imposed. (013-017)

This means that validity is ultimately a matter of systematic coherence. An inference is valid when it contributes to the construction of a single, coherent system of judgments that together express the nature of reality. The test is not formal but substantive: does the inference genuinely advance our understanding, or does it merely rearrange what we already know?

### Validity in Dialectic and Mathematics

Bradley distinguishes his approach to validity from two alternatives: the dialectical method (which he associates with Hegel) and the mathematical method. Dialectic, he argues, cannot supply a test of validity because it presupposes the very connections it claims to demonstrate. Mathematics, by contrast, achieves perfect validity within its own domain but at the cost of abstraction from concrete reality. (005-006)

Neither approach answers the question of validity for judgments about the real world. Bradley's own solution is to embed validity in the total system of knowledge, where each inference is justified by its contribution to the coherence of the whole rather than by conformity to a formal template.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of validity engages with a tradition stretching from Aristotle's *Prior Analytics* to the nineteenth-century debates between formal logicians (Whately, Hamilton) and idealist logicians (Hegel, Lotze). His distinctive contribution is to refuse the formal/material distinction as a solution to the problem of validity. Where formal logicians restrict validity to form and material logicians appeal to content, Bradley argues that the distinction itself is unstable — logical form is always the form of content, and content is always shaped by logical form.

This position influenced later British idealists and, through criticism, contributed to the development of modern logical theory. Russell and Moore's rejection of Bradleyan idealism was in part a rejection of his conception of validity in favor of the formal, truth-functional account that became standard in twentieth-century logic.
