# James Mill

James Mill (1773–1836) was a Scottish philosopher, historian, and political theorist whose associationist psychology — as analyzed by his son [[john-stuart-mill|J.S. Mill]] — provides the primary target for Bradley's extended critique of the theory of association of [[idea|ideas]] in the *Principles of Logic*.

## Overview

James Mill appears in Bradley's *Principles of Logic* not through his own writings directly but through [[john-stuart-mill|J.S. Mill]]'s detailed commentary on his father's work. Bradley devotes substantial attention to the associationist account of mental reproduction, quoting [[john-stuart-mill|J.S. Mill]]'s analysis of James Mill at length: "J. S. Mill, on James Mill, I. 112, 113" (013-010). The elder Mill's [[association-of-ideas|associationism]] represents, for Bradley, the most developed form of a theory that attempts to explain all mental operations — including logical inference — through the mechanical combination of simple elements.

Bradley's engagement with James Mill is mediated through a critical lens: he uses [[john-stuart-mill|J.S. Mill]]'s own careful analysis of his father's theory to expose what Bradley sees as fatal contradictions in the associationist framework. The issue is not merely psychological but logical: Bradley argues that the theory of association cannot account for the unity of judgment or the reality of relations without presupposing what it claims to explain.

## Key Ideas

### Association of Ideas

James Mill's *Analysis of the Phenomena of the Human Mind* (1829) developed an exhaustive associationist psychology, attempting to derive all mental phenomena from sensory elements combined through the [[association-of-ideas|laws of association]]. Bradley, via [[john-stuart-mill|J.S. Mill]], presents this as the canonical statement of the theory: "There is, therefore, a suggestion by resemblance — a calling up of the idea of a past sensation by a present sensation like it — which not only does not depend on association by contiguity, but is itself the foundation which association by contiguity requires for its support" (013-010).

### Contiguity and Similarity

The core of James Mill's theory concerns the two fundamental [[association-of-ideas|laws of association]]: contiguity and similarity. Bradley examines [[john-stuart-mill|J.S. Mill]]'s nuanced account of their interdependence, quoting: "Any objects, whether physical or mental, are related, or are in a relation, to one another, in virtue of any complex state of consciousness into which they both enter" (013-013). Bradley argues that this account makes the relation of similarity dependent on the presence of both terms to consciousness, undermining the claim that similarity can serve as an independent mechanism of association.

## Bradley's Critique

Bradley's criticism of the Millian [[association-of-ideas|associationism]] unfolds across Chapter XIII as part of his broader argument against empiricist accounts of mental life. His central objection is that [[association-of-ideas|associationism]] cannot explain how [[idea|ideas]] are called up without presupposing the very relation it claims to produce. Bradley argues: "Is it not clear that two mental elements are not like, unless I have them before me at once or in immediate succession? But, if so, what meaning can we attach to the calling up of an idea by similarity?" (013-013).

Bradley further charges that the associationist account introduces theoretical fictions — such as unconscious [[idea|ideas]] and the "Laws of Obliviscence" — to salvage a fundamentally inadequate model. He quotes [[john-stuart-mill|J.S. Mill]]'s own candid description of the problem: "Do the lost [[idea|ideas]] pass through the mind without consciousness? Do they pass consciously through the mind and are they then instantly forgotten? Or do they never come into the mind at all, being, as it were, overleaped and pressed out by the rush of the subsequent [[idea|ideas]]?" (013-016). For Bradley, the very need to pose such questions reveals the bankruptcy of the theory.

Against James Mill's [[association-of-ideas|associationism]], Bradley advances his own theory of [[redintegration]], arguing that reproduction is not a mechanical process of association but a redintegrative movement within a holistic mental content.

## Influence

James Mill's associationist psychology, systematized in his *Analysis* (1829), profoundly shaped 19th-century British empiricism. His influence reached Bradley primarily through [[john-stuart-mill|J.S. Mill]]'s critical reworking of his father's doctrines. While Bradley's critique in the *Principles of Logic* is largely negative, James Mill's formulation of the problems of mental reproduction and similarity provided the essential foil against which Bradley developed his own account of [[idea|ideal]] content and [[inference|logical inference]].
