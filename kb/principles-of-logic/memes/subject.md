The concept of the subject is central to Bradley's theory of judgment. While traditional logic treats the subject as one term in a subject-predicate relation, Bradley argues that "the subject in all judgment is ultimately reality" (001-016). Every finite subject is a provisional abstraction from the one true subject: the whole of reality.

## Overview

Bradley's analysis of the subject begins with a critique of the traditional view. The ordinary grammatical subject — "the traditional subject, predicate, and copula are mere" appearances (001-013) — does not capture the logical structure of judgment. When we say "the wolf is eating the lamb," the real subject is not the word "wolf" but the complex reality that the judgment refers to.

The subject of a judgment is "referred away to another real subject" (001-003) in an endless chain, until we reach the ultimate subject that is reality itself. This means that no judgment is fully self-contained; every assertion about a finite subject implicitly refers to the whole within which that subject exists.

## Key Aspects

### Subject and Predicate

Bradley argues that the subject-predicate distinction is not ultimate. "If the subject and predicate are equated" (001-014), the judgment collapses into identity; if they are merely conjoined, the unity of judgment is lost. The subject-predicate form is a useful abstraction but does not capture the living unity of judgment.

### The Ultimate Subject

The ultimate subject of all judgment is reality as a whole. "The subject in all judgment is ultimately" the one reality (001-016), and every finite judgment is true only to the extent that it participates in this ultimate reference. This doctrine is the logical expression of Bradley's metaphysical holism.

### Subject and Copula

Bradley notes that "we make no mention of the subject and copula" (001-007) in many actual judgments, suggesting that the grammatical analysis of judgment into subject, predicate, and copula is artificial. The real work of judgment is done by the reference of ideal content to reality, not by the syntactic form.

## Historical Context

Bradley's doctrine of the ultimate subject draws on Hegel's critique of the subject-predicate form in the Preface to the Phenomenology. For both philosophers, the propositional form suggests a fixed subject and a contingent predicate, whereas speculative truth requires the mutual determination of subject and predicate.
