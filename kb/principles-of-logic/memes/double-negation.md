# Double Negation

The logical principle that "duplex negatio affirmat" — the [[negation|denial]] of a [[negation|denial]] is equivalent to affirmation. In Bradley's analysis, this is not because two negations formally cancel each other out, but because the second [[negation|denial]] requires a positive ground that can only be the original predicate itself.

## Overview

Bradley treats double negation in Chapter V of Book I, alongside the principles of Identity, [[contradiction|Contradiction]], and [[law-of-excluded-middle|Excluded Middle]]. His treatment is distinctive: he rejects the formalist interpretation that negation simply reverses itself when applied twice, and instead grounds double negation in the requirement that every [[negation|denial]] must rest on a positive ground. "In all [[negation|denial]] we must have the assertion of a positive ground; and the positive ground of the second [[negation|denial]] can be nothing but the predicate denied by the first" (005-012).

The principle has practical significance: to deny the judgment "A is not B" is not merely to reject a formal negation but to assert positively that A is B. Bradley warns, however, that "it is so difficult to work with double denial" (005-014) that the principle is easily misapplied. The ambiguity of negation — which we have seen in the constant uncertainty about the positive ground of any [[negation|denial]] — makes double negation a precarious logical tool.

## Key Aspects

### Not Merely Formal Cancellation

Bradley explicitly rejects the simple view that two negations cancel out. "If mere not-A is simply zero, then not-not-A is, if possible, less" (005-012). Pure negation, taken as bare [[privation|absence]], yields nothing — and applying negation to nothing does not magically produce something. The real reason double negation yields affirmation is not formal but substantive: the second [[negation|denial]] requires a positive ground.

### The Positive Ground of the Second Denial

The core of Bradley's analysis is that every [[negation|denial]] presupposes a positive quality that excludes the denied predicate. To deny "A is not b," I must possess some quality in A that excludes not-b. But what quality excludes not-b? Only b itself. "The only certainty that b is not absent is got by showing that b is present" (005-013). Any other quality — call it z — might equally exclude b as not-b. Only the actual presence of b guarantees the falsity of "A is not b."

### The Ambiguity Problem

Bradley emphasizes that double negation is practically treacherous. Suppose we deny "it rained" on the ground that it snowed. We might then deny "it snowed" on the ground that it was fine. "The result of our double negation of 'it rained' might be either 'it snowed,' or again 'it was fine'" (005-013). Double negation alone does not uniquely determine its result — only the positive presence of the original predicate does. "You get rid of this [ambiguity] only by the judgment 'it did rain'" (005-013).

### The Disjunctive Precondition

Bradley notes that double negation would work unambiguously only under a prior [[disjunction|disjunctive judgment]] that exhaustively limits the possibilities. "You could only do that if the number of possibilities with respect to A had already been limited by a [[disjunction|disjunctive judgment]]. And this is not here the case" (005-013). Without an exhaustive [[disjunctive-judgment|disjunction]], the ground of [[negation|denial]] remains indefinite, and double negation floats free of its intended target.

### Refutation by Refutation Is Insufficient

A practical consequence of Bradley's analysis is that refuting an opponent's argument is not the same as establishing one's own position. "If we wish to deny the original judgment, we can not do that by refuting our antagonists. We must show ourselves that reality is knowable" (005-013). To deny "reality is not knowable" requires proving that reality is knowable — not merely exposing flaws in the argument for unknowability.

## Historical Context

The principle of double negation has a long history in logic, from Aristotle's *De Interpretatione* through the Stoics to medieval logic. In modern formal logic, double negation [[elision|elimination]] is a standard rule of inference. Bradley's treatment is notable for resisting formalization: he insists that double negation is not a formal axiom but a substantive logical truth grounded in the nature of [[negation|denial]] and the positive character of reality. His analysis influenced subsequent idealist treatments and stands as a critique of purely syntactic approaches to logical principles.
