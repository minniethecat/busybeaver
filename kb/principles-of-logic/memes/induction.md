# Induction

Induction is the logical process of inferring general conclusions from particular instances. Bradley subjects this traditional mode of [[inference|reasoning]] to critical scrutiny, examining its claims to provide proof and its relationship to the experimental methods.

## Overview

Bradley's treatment of induction appears across multiple chapters of *The Principles of Logic*, primarily in connection with his discussion of inference and the so-called Methods of Induction. He does not treat induction as a self-sufficient logical method but rather situates it within his broader theory of inference. (015-001)

A central concern for Bradley is whether induction constitutes genuine proof. He observes that "counting by itself is not the induction, and is not by itself a general inference," emphasizing that the logical force of induction depends on principles beyond mere enumeration. (015-001)

Bradley engages critically with Mill's treatment of the inductive methods, arguing that they face a fundamental dilemma. The methods either assume the very causal connections they purport to discover, or they cannot deliver the certainty they promise. (015-004)

## Key Aspects

### Induction and Complete Enumeration

Bradley distinguishes induction from mere counting or complete enumeration. Complete induction — inferring a universal from an exhaustive survey of all instances — "shares the fortunes of the syllogism" in that it depends on the principle that what holds of each member holds of the whole. This is not an inductive principle but a logical one. (009-002)

The logical work of induction, Bradley argues, is not in the enumeration itself but in the inference that connects the observed instances to unobserved ones. This inferential step requires a principle that goes beyond what experience alone can provide.

### The Dilemma of Inductive Methods

Bradley presents a sharp dilemma for Mill's Methods of Induction. If the methods presuppose knowledge of causal laws, then they cannot serve as a means of discovering those laws. If instead they operate without such presuppositions, they cannot establish causal connections with logical necessity. Either way, the methods fail to deliver what they promise. (015-004)

This critique is part of Bradley's broader rejection of empiricist logic. He argues that "induction is not a mere mode of investigation" that can be applied mechanically, but rather depends on theoretical principles that empiricism cannot account for.

### Induction and Proof

Bradley questions whether induction, understood in its ordinary sense, offers genuine proof. He distinguishes between the psychological process of forming general beliefs from experience and the logical justification of those beliefs. The latter, he maintains, requires a rational ground that experience alone cannot supply. (022-003)

In Bradley's system, induction ultimately depends on the same logical principles that govern all inference — identity, contradiction, and the nature of systematic connection. It is not an independent or fundamental mode of [[inference|reasoning]] but a particular application of more basic logical forms.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of induction must be understood against the background of nineteenth-century debates about scientific method. Mill's *[[system|System]] of Logic* (1843) had presented the inductive methods as the foundation of all genuine knowledge, including mathematics and logic. Bradley, writing from an idealist perspective influenced by Hegel, sought to reverse this priority — arguing that induction itself depends on logical principles that are not derived from experience.

His position aligns with Whewell's earlier critique of Mill but goes further in grounding the critique in a systematic theory of judgment and inference. The question of induction's logical status remained central to philosophy of science through the twentieth century, from the logical positivists to Popper and beyond.
