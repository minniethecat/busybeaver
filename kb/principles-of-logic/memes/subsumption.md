# Subsumption

The logical operation of placing a particular case under a universal rule — a traditional model of [[inference|reasoning]] that Bradley subjects to critical scrutiny, arguing that not all [[inference|reasoning]] takes this form (006-004).

## Overview

Subsumption is the paradigmatic operation of traditional formal logic: a particular instance is brought under a general rule, and the conclusion follows from the combination of the two. Bradley examines this model carefully and finds it wanting as a complete account of [[inference|reasoning]].

His critique is not that subsumption never occurs — it clearly does in certain contexts. Rather, Bradley argues that subsumption fails to capture the full range of inferential activity. Many forms of [[inference|reasoning]] do not involve the application of a pre-existing universal to a particular case; instead, the universal and the particular emerge together in the process of [[inference|reasoning]] itself.

Bradley's treatment of subsumption is closely tied to his broader critique of formal logic. If [[inference|reasoning]] were always subsumption, logic would be a mechanical procedure of rule-application. But [[inference|reasoning]] is more varied, more context-sensitive, and more creative than this model allows.

## Key Aspects

### The Limits of Subsumption

Bradley opens his analysis with the provocative claim that if [[inference|reasoning]] were always subsumption — the placing of a stone on a lower tier under one on a higher — then logic would be simple (006-004). But [[inference|reasoning]] is not always subsumption, and the attempt to reduce all inference to this single form distorts our understanding of thought.

### Subsumption in Formal Logic

Bradley questions whether we actually use a function of subsumption in our [[inference|reasoning]] (024-004). The formal logician claims that particular [[inference|reasoning]]s are proved by subsumption under general conditions, but Bradley asks whether this accurately describes what we do when we reason.

### Subsumption and Action

Bradley extends his critique to practical [[inference|reasoning]], arguing against the idea that action is subsumption under a form of activity (024-006). Just as theoretical [[inference|reasoning]] cannot be reduced to subsumption, practical [[inference|reasoning]] — the [[inference|reasoning]] that guides action — resists this model.

### Subsumption and the Reasoning Process

The concept of subsumption is central to Bradley's broader investigation of the [[inference|reasoning]] process. It serves as the foil against which he develops his own, more nuanced account of how inference operates — an account that acknowledges the diversity of [[inference|reasoning]] without reducing it to a single formal pattern.

## Historical Context

Bradley's critique of subsumption engages with the Aristotelian syllogistic tradition, where subsumption, the application of a middle term, is the fundamental logical operation. His argument that subsumption is insufficient as a general model of [[inference|reasoning]] reflects the broader Idealist reaction against formalism in logic — a reaction that would eventually contribute to the development of more flexible logical systems in the twentieth century.
