Pleasure, in Bradley's philosophy, is treated primarily as a psychical fact — the felt satisfaction or agreeable sensation that accompanies certain mental states. Bradley's main concern is not to deny the reality of pleasure but to resist its elevation to a logical or ethical principle.

## Overview

Bradley mentions pleasure in several contexts, always with the aim of showing its limited philosophical significance. In the logical writings, he notes the parallelism between logical operations and psychological states: "assertion and denial, or pain and pleasure, are incompatible" (005-003). The logical law of non-contradiction has a psychological counterpart in the incompatibility of pleasure and pain, but this does not mean logic is grounded in psychology.

The hedonistic reduction of all motivation to pleasure is, for Bradley, an instance of the fallacy of taking a partial truth for the whole. While pleasure accompanies many mental states, "vague feelings of pleasure or a dim sense of pain" (019-005) do not constitute knowledge, nor do they provide the standard by which judgments are evaluated.

## Key Aspects

### Pleasure and Logic

Bradley's primary treatment of pleasure in the logical context is negative: he denies that logical principles can be derived from the pursuit of pleasure or the avoidance of pain. The logical "must" is not a psychological compulsion but a normative demand. To say that a conclusion follows from premises is not to report a feeling of pleasure at the inference.

### Pleasure and the Will

Pleasure is connected to desire and will in Bradley's psychology. The "full sensation and pleasure of possession" (023-003) represents the satisfied state of the will, but Bradley cautions against mistaking this psychological satisfaction for logical truth. The will seeks satisfaction, but the intellect seeks truth, and these two pursuits, while related, are not identical.

### Pleasure as Appearance

Pleasure, like all psychical phenomena, belongs to the realm of appearance rather than ultimate reality. It is a real fact of experience, but it does not provide the key to metaphysical truth. The "manifestation of pleasure and pain" (023-002) is part of the finite self's experience, which is transcended in the absolute.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of pleasure is directed against the hedonistic utilitarianism of Mill and the associationist psychology of Bain, both of which sought to ground ethics and logic in the pursuit of pleasure. For Bradley, this is a category mistake: value and validity cannot be reduced to psychological facts.
