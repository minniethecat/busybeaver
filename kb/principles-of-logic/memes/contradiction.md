# Contradiction

The logical relation in which two assertions cannot both be true — a concept that Bradley treats not merely as a formal principle but as a dynamic feature of thought itself (001-015).

## Overview

Contradiction occupies a central place in Bradley's logical system. It is more than a formal rule that prohibits asserting both A and not-A; for Bradley, contradiction reveals something fundamental about the nature of thought and reality. The fact that contradiction exists in the nature of things — that opposite qualities cannot occupy the same ground — is for him a basic truth about the structure of experience (005-005).

Bradley distinguishes contradiction from mere [[negation|denial]]. Denial is the rejection of an idea on the basis of positive knowledge, whereas contradiction is the logical relation that makes [[negation|denial]] possible (003-006). This distinction is essential to his broader argument that logical principles are not arbitrary conventions but reflect the actual structure of thought and its objects.

The concept runs through Bradley's entire work, from his analysis of judgment to his treatment of inference and the nature of reality. His willingness to confront contradiction directly — to see it not as a defect to be eliminated but as a feature of thought to be understood — marks a distinctive philosophical stance.

## Key Aspects

### Contradiction and the Law of Thought

Bradley acknowledges that courage is needed to confront the last contradiction — the point at which thought must either resolve its internal tensions or acknowledge a limit (001-015). The law of contradiction, which states that A cannot be truly the same as not-A, is an expression of the nature of thought itself.

### Contradiction in Presentation

Temporal succession provides a vivid example of contradiction in immediate experience. The past and future cannot coexist in the same moment; the idea that they could is a sheer self-contradiction (002-024). Bradley uses this to illustrate that contradiction is not merely a logical construct but a feature of the world as presented.

### Contradiction and Exclusion

Bradley develops contradiction from the more fundamental notion of exclusion (005-007). Two qualities exclude one another — they cannot occupy the same logical space — and this exclusion is the ground from which formal contradiction arises.

### Contradiction and Positive Assertion

Mere contradiction does not affirm any particular positive claim (005-014). Denying an assertion is not equivalent to asserting its contradictory; the logical force of contradiction is that it rules out alternatives without positively specifying which alternative holds.

### Contradiction and Error

For Bradley, contradiction operates on the strength of positive knowledge (003-007). One can never be removed from contradiction without positive grounds; contradiction is the rejection of an idea that requires something positive to reject from.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of contradiction draws on the Aristotelian tradition, where the law of non-contradiction is a fundamental principle of being, while engaging with Hegelian dialectics, where contradiction is the motor of conceptual development. Bradley steers a middle course: contradiction is real and fundamental, but it is a structural feature of thought rather than a generative principle.
