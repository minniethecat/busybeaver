In Bradley's logical theory, existence is one of the two fundamental aspects of every judgment, distinguished from content. Every judgment simultaneously asserts something about the existence of its subject and about the qualities or content attributed to that subject.

## Overview

Bradley's analysis of judgment distinguishes between two inseparable aspects: existence and content. When we judge "A is B," we do not merely assert a relation between concepts; we also implicitly affirm that A exists in some sense. Bradley argues that we "never assert the fact in" isolation from existence, for the judgment's meaning always involves a reference to the being of its subject (001-002). The whole in which subject and predicate are united involves both the existence of the meaning and the existence of the thing. (001-007)

This dual-aspect theory is foundational to his entire logic. The distinction between fact and idea, existence and meaning, is unconsciously presupposed in every act of judgment. (001-021)

## Key Aspects

### Existence and Content

The distinction between existence and content runs through Bradley's entire theory of judgment. "Confined to the two aspects of existence and content," the judgment cannot be reduced to either aspect alone (001-004). The content of a judgment — what is said about the subject — must be distinguished from the existential import of the judgment — the fact that the subject is taken to exist. A meaning can be used apart from its own existence, and in abstraction from its relations to the psychical fact. (001-006)

### Existential Judgments

Bradley gives special attention to existential judgments, where "existence is directly asserted or denied" (001-008). In such judgments, the distinction between existence and content becomes particularly evident, because the predicate itself concerns the mode of being of the subject rather than any qualifying attribute. Judgment does not, however, always assert actual existence — as in the case of "a four-cornered circle," whose existence is not asserted. (002-001)

### Self-Existence and Individuality

Bradley connects existence with "individuality" and "self-existence" (001-003). For a thing to exist, in the full sense, is for it to possess a kind of independent being — though Bradley's idealism ultimately qualifies this independence by showing how all finite existences depend upon the absolute whole. The question of how a thing acts and maintains itself in existence is connected to the broader metaphysical question of what it means to be real. (002-003)

### Existence and Real Change

For Bradley, judgment is not merely a psychical event but involves the production of a change in real existence. (001-010) This link between judgment and existence shows that logical operations are not disconnected from reality — each judgment modifies our understanding of what exists and how it is qualified.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of existence draws on the logical tradition from Aristotle through Kant, but his insistence that existence is inseparable from the judgment's content reflects his idealist conviction that being and thought are ultimately one.
