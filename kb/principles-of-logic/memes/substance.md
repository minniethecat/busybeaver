# Substance

In Bradley's logical system, a substance is a self-existent individual that serves as the ultimate subject of predication. Unlike adjectives or attributes, which are "parasites" cut loose from their concrete setting, substance is what underlies and supports predication. Bradley treats substance critically: the mere grammatical substantives of ordinary language are typically not true substances but provisional abstractions.

## Overview

Bradley's treatment of substance emerges from his analysis of judgment. In the categorical-hypothetical distinction, he notes that "universal judgments were merely hypothetical, because they stated, not individual substantives, but connections of adjectives" (002-007). The singular judgment, by contrast, aims at an individual subject — a substance — that is "self-existent and individual" (002-010). This distinction between the adjectival and the substantive runs through Bradley's entire logical theory.

Bradley argues that "the subject which appears in the series of time, and to which we attribute our ideas as predicates, must itself be real. And, if real, it must not be purely adjectival" (002-010). The momentary appearance or phenomenon cannot serve as substance because it is not truly individual — it is caught in a web of relations that undermine its self-existence. True substance, for Bradley, is ultimately reality as a whole.

## Key Aspects

### Substance and Adjective

The fundamental distinction in Bradley's logic is between substance and adjective. "Adjectives unsupported float in the air: their junction with reality is supposed and not asserted" (002-008). Every judgment predicates an ideal content (adjective) of a real subject (substance). But this distinction is not absolute — what appears as substance in one judgment may function as adjective in a broader context, leading toward Bradley's holistic conclusion that only the whole of reality is truly substantial.

### Substance in Metaphysical Judgments

Bradley identifies a class of judgments "which have to do with a reality which is never a sensible event in time" (002-007), citing "The soul is a substance" as an example. These metaphysical judgments, while beyond sense perception, "certainly must have a place" in logic. This reveals Bradley's recognition that the concept of substance transcends empirical verification while remaining logically indispensable.

### The Critique of Individual Substance

Bradley's analysis progressively undermines the notion of finite individual substances. He argues that "the particular phenomenon, the momentary appearance, is not individual, and is so not the subject which we use in judgment" (002-010). Every finite thing is conditioned by its relations to other things, and thus cannot claim the self-existence that true substance requires. This leads toward the monistic conclusion that only the absolute whole qualifies as genuine substance.

### Substance and Subject in Judgment

The grammatical subject of a proposition is not necessarily the logical substance. Bradley observes that "the traditional subject, predicate, and copula are mere superstitions" (001-013), and that in judgment, "it is present reality which is the actual subject, and the genuine substantive of the ideal content" (002-009). The true substance of judgment is always the reality that appears in perception, not the linguistic form that refers to it.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of substance engages with a long philosophical tradition from Aristotle through Spinoza to Hegel. Aristotle defined substance as the primary category — that which exists in itself and not in another. Spinoza radicalized this into a single substance monism. Bradley, while indebted to Hegel's critique of finite substance, develops his own distinctive position through the analysis of judgment rather than through metaphysical speculation. His conclusion that "the adjective sticks forever in the adjectives; it does not reach the substantive" (002-008) reflects a Kantian humility about the limits of discursive thought, combined with a Hegelian insistence that these limits point toward a more comprehensive unity.
