# Law of Identity

In Bradley's *Principles of Logic*, the Law of Identity is not a barren tautology but the principle that every judgment affirms a real identity that persists through and despite differences. It is the first of the three traditional laws of thought, treated in Chapter V alongside [[contradiction|Contradiction]], [[law-of-excluded-middle|Excluded Middle]], and [[double-negation|Double Negation]].

## Overview

Bradley opens his treatment of the Law of Identity with a striking claim: "It does not even assert identity. For identity without difference is nothing" (005-001). This sets the tone for his entire approach — the law is not the empty "A is A" of formal logic, but a substantive claim about how judgment operates. In every genuine judgment, Bradley argues, "we assert the identity of subject and predicate" (005-002), but this identity is never the "mere abstract identity without any difference" that critics of traditional logic rightly reject (005-003).

The principle must not be a tautology. A judgment that merely repeats the subject adds nothing to knowledge. Bradley insists that the identity asserted in judgment is always an identity that holds across and through real differences — "the identity which persists under differences" (001-017). The predicate differs from the subject in content, yet the judgment declares them identical in reference to reality.

## Key Aspects

### Against Tautology

Bradley is emphatic that the Law of Identity must not be reduced to "A is A." A tautology "does not even assert identity" in any meaningful sense (005-001). The whole purpose of judgment is to extend knowledge by connecting a predicate to a subject that is not obviously contained within it. If the law were merely formal, it would be useless for actual [[inference|reasoning]].

### Identity Through Difference

The core of Bradley's account is that identity always involves difference. "Every judgment affirms either the identity which persists under differences, or else the difference which is predicated despite an identity" (001-017). This is not a paradox but a description of how judgment actually works: when we say "gold is yellow," we identify the subject (gold) with the predicate (yellow) while recognizing they are not the same concept.

### Real, Not Merely Ideal, Identity

The identity asserted in judgment is not a relation between [[idea|ideas]] in the mind but a claim about reality. Bradley's realism about identity sets him apart from associationist psychology. The identity holds between "disparate moments of a single identity" (005-005) in the real world, not merely in the thinker's mental states.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of the laws of thought reflects his engagement with German Idealism and his departure from formal logic. Where Kant treated the laws of thought as transcendental conditions of possible experience, and where Mill reduced them to empirical generalizations, Bradley seeks a middle path: the laws describe the actual structure of judgment as a reference to reality. His insistence that identity always involves difference echoes Hegel's dialectical logic, though Bradley explicitly distances himself from Hegel's "main principle" in the Preface.
