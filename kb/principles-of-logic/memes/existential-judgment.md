# Existential Judgment

The existential judgment — a judgment that asserts or denies existence ("A exists," "There is no B") — receives sustained attention in Bradley's *Principles of Logic*. His analysis challenges the ordinary view that existential judgments form a distinct logical class.

Instead, Bradley argues that every genuine judgment is ultimately existential in character, because every judgment refers an ideal content to reality, and this reference to the real is the core of existential assertion.

## Overview

"We may finally observe that existential judgments do not lend themselves easily to the ordinary scheme of logic" (001-013). Bradley opens his discussion by noting that existential judgments resist classification as categorical, hypothetical, or disjunctive in any straightforward way.

The judgment "God exists," for example, does not predicate a quality of a subject in the manner of "Socrates is wise," nor is it conditional in form.

The central difficulty, for Bradley, is that existence is not a predicate like other predicates. "The subject of an unexpressed existential judgment" (002-012) — when we say "Wolf!" rather than "There is a wolf" — is not an idea but the present reality itself. The genuine subject of any existential judgment is "the real which appears in presentation"

(002-008), and the judgment's content is that this real is characterised in such-and-such a way. "It is present reality which is the actual subject, and the genuine substantive of the ideal content" (002-008).

## Key Aspects

### Existence Is Not a Predicate

Bradley anticipates a line of argument later associated with Kant and Frege: existence is not a real predicate. "The genuine subject is the content of the idea plus our psychological state of mind" (003-005). When we say "A exists," we are not adding a new quality to our idea of A; we are asserting that the idea of A is realised in present experience.

The existential judgment thus straddles the boundary between thought and reality in a way that purely predicative judgments do not.

### Existential and Categorical

Bradley's most distinctive claim is that existential judgments are categorical — indeed, more fundamentally categorical than ordinary predicative judgments. "We have here a negative existential judgment, for the nature of which we must refer to the following chapter" (002-028).

The claim that there is no such thing as X is, on Bradley's analysis, an assertion about reality as a whole: the real system has no place for the suggested determination. "In existential judgment, as we saw before (Chap. II. § 24), the subject is the ultimate reality" (003-005).

Because the subject is reality itself, and because the assertion is unconditional, the existential judgment is categorical in the deepest sense.

### Singular and Universal

Bradley also addresses the question of whether existential judgments are singular or universal. "We cannot say there is a class of existential judgments, for all singular judgments are existential" (002-029). Every singular judgment — "This rose is red" — implicitly asserts the existence of its subject.

Conversely, what appears to be a universal judgment ("All men are mortal") is existential only in a derivative sense, because it is really a hypothetical judgment about the connection of attributes. The true existential judgment is always tied to the "this" of present experience.

### Negative Existential Judgments

The negative existential judgment — "There is no ghost" — presents special difficulties. Bradley treats it as a form of negation grounded in privation: reality as experienced offers no place for the suggested content. "The truth of the negative may be seen in the end to lie in the affirmation of a positive quality" (003-003).

To say "There is no ghost" is not merely to deny a predicate but to affirm that reality has a positive character incompatible with the suggested determination. The negative existential is, in the end, a positive judgment about the character of the real.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of existential judgments engages with a long philosophical tradition stretching from Anselm's ontological argument through Kant's critique of it. His insistence that existence is not a predicate but a relation of thought to reality reflects idealist commitments that distinguish his position from both empiricist and Kantian accounts.

The analysis also connects to his metaphysics: because the ultimate subject of every judgment is reality itself, every judgment is at bottom existential — it is always an assertion, however mediated, about what is real.
