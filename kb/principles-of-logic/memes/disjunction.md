# Disjunction

In Bradley's *Principles of Logic*, the disjunctive judgment ("A is b or c") is neither purely categorical nor purely hypothetical. It asserts a categorical fact — that A possesses a common quality x — and upon this basis erects hypothetical supposals about which specific determination of x applies. This union of categorical ground with hypothetical alternatives is Bradley's distinctive account of [[disjunctive-judgment|disjunction]].

## Overview

Bradley treats the disjunctive judgment in Chapter IV of Book I, arguing that it has been neglected by most logicians who treat it as "a simple application of the hypothetical" and "a mere appendage" (004-001). His central claim is that every [[disjunctive-judgment|disjunction]] rests on a categorical basis: a common quality x that is shared by the alternatives and is unconditionally asserted of the subject.

"The assertion in 'A is b or c' is not that A is b or c. What then do we affirm? We say in the first place that A exists. In the next place we certainly give it some quality" (004-004). This quality is neither b nor c exclusively, but something common to both — the sphere within which the [[disjunctive-judgment|disjunction]] is affirmed. For example, in "white or b[[privation|lack]]," the categorically asserted quality is "coloured, and coloured so as to exclude other hues" (004-005).

## Key Aspects

### The Categorical Basis

Every [[disjunctive-judgment|disjunction]] asserts a common quality x unconditionally of the subject. "'A is x' is categorically true" (004-005). For "man, woman, and child," x is "human being." For "in England or America," x is "somewhere not elsewhere." For "alive or dead," x is "organized being." Even "existence and non-existence have so much in common that, in any sense in which we can use them, they imply some kind of contact with my mind" (004-005). There is no exception: "in every [[disjunctive-judgment|disjunction]] and as the ground of it, there must be the assertion of a common quality, the sphere within which the [[disjunctive-judgment|disjunction]] is affirmed" (004-005).

### The Hypothetical Superstructure

On the categorical basis x, Bradley erects hypotheses. Since b and c are disparate (incompatible) and A is particularized within the region they define, A must be one of them. "If A is not b, it must be c; and it must be b, if it is not c. This supposal completes the 'either–or'" (004-006). The disjunctive judgment is thus "the union of hypotheticals on a categoric basis" (004-006).

### Alternatives Are Always Exclusive

Bradley insists, against Jevons and others, that "or" is rigidly disjunctive. "I confess I should despair of human language, if such distinctions as separate 'and' from 'or' could be broken down" (004-011). When we say "A is b or c," we strictly exclude "A is both b and c." Apparent counterexamples — such as "wreath or anadem" — involve alternatives applied to names, not to things: "you may call it by either name you please" but "not by both of them at once" (004-011). Ordinary speech may be slovenly, but "inaccuracy remains inaccuracy" (004-007).

### The Danger of False Alternatives

Bradley warns that "about the commonest mistake in metaphysics is the setting up of false alternatives" (004-007). If we exclude a compatible combination or include an incompatible one, we reach false conclusions. His example — calling a man "fool or rogue" and then concluding he must be a rogue — illustrates the practical danger: "unfortunately the man has been a fool as well, and was not in any way to be relied on" (004-007).

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of [[disjunctive-judgment|disjunction]] engages directly with Jevons' *Principles of Science*, which had challenged the exclusive character of alternatives. Bradley's defence of exclusive [[disjunctive-judgment|disjunction]] and his analysis of the categorical basis of "either–or" represent a significant contribution to the logical theory of his time. His distinction between apparent and ultimate subject in disjunctive judgments — "A is either b or c" may really predicate something of the nature of things, not of A directly — reflects his broader idealist commitment to the primacy of reality over appearance in logical analysis.
