# Identity

In Bradley's logic, identity is the relation of sameness that makes judgment possible — every assertion of subject and predicate posits an identity that holds across their difference in meaning.

## Overview

Identity is not, for Bradley, a mere tautology. Judgment does not simply repeat the subject in the predicate; it asserts a real identity that persists under difference (001-017). When we say "sugar is sweet," we identify two distinct contents under a common reality. This identity-through-difference is the fundamental structure of all judgment.

Bradley critiques the doctrine of simple equation or identity of terms, arguing that it fails to account for how judgment actually works — we do not merely equate two identical terms but identify a real connection (001-016). Any reality that could be known would be an identity which appears and remains the same throughout its diverse manifestations. (002-003)

## Key Aspects

### Identity in Judgment

The judgment affirms an identity that transcends the mere terms. The subject and predicate differ in meaning yet are asserted to be identical in reference to reality (001-016). This is not a contradiction but the essential form of logical assertion. Every judgment, Bradley insists, affirms either the identity which persists under difference, or the diversity which is truly the same. (001-017)

### Real Identity Through Change

Bradley argues that reality involves a real identity persisting throughout change and diversity (002-027). This identity is not a static sameness but an active unity that appears in and through its various manifestations. A something the same — a real identity which appears in all, but which transcends any single appearance — is the ground of judgment and inference. (002-028)

### Identity of Quality

One mode of identity Bradley discusses is identity through shared quality — when two things share the same attribute, an inference through identity of quality connects them to the real that appears in that quality (002-025). This underlies analogical and inductive reasoning. A point of identity is required to fasten the ideal content onto reality, and without it judgment would lack a ground for its assertion. (002-026)

### Identity of Content

Bradley also discusses identity of content, which is essential for the preservation of meaning across different contexts. A judgment preserves, or should preserve, its identity of content throughout the process of inference. (002-031) An identity of content in different contexts is what makes the formation of universal judgments possible, for without sameness of meaning across instances, generalization would be groundless. (013-007)
