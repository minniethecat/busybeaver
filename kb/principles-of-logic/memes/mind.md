Mind, in Bradley's philosophy, is the locus of psychical events — the "state of my mind" (001-001) that includes sensations, images, desires, and thoughts. While all logical activity occurs within the mind, Bradley carefully distinguishes the psychological from the logical: the fact that a judgment occurs in a mind does not determine its truth.

## Overview

Bradley's treatment of mind is inseparable from his critique of psychologism. The mind is real — "as stubborn a fact as any" (001-001) — and its contents are the material upon which logical operations work. But the principles of logic are not psychological laws. The "whole before our mind" (001-007) in judgment is not a mental image but an ideal content that refers beyond the mind to reality.

The mind, for Bradley, is not a substance but a process — a centre of experience that develops through the interaction of feeling, thought, and will. "The flower can never itself be what it means" (001-003): mental contents are signs that point beyond themselves, and this referential character is the essence of thought.

## Key Aspects

### Mind and Judgment

When we judge, we have ideas in our minds, but the judgment is not about those mental ideas. The "ideal content which our minds had fixed as the general meaning" (001-006) is referred to reality, not to the mind itself. This is the difference between a psychological event and a logical act.

### The Development of Mind

Bradley traces the development of the mind from mere feeling through imagination to reflective thought. The mind's ability to form judgments — to refer ideas to reality — is a "relatively late acquisition" that "marks a period in its upward progress" (001-017). This developmental view connects logic to psychology without reducing the former to the latter.

### Mind and the Absolute

The finite mind is an appearance within the absolute. Its contents are real as psychical facts, but the mind itself is not an independent substance. The opposition between mind and world, subject and object, is transcended in the absolute, where thought and reality are one.

## Historical Context

Bradley's philosophy of mind engages with the British empiricist tradition (Locke, Hume, Mill) and with German idealism (Kant, Hegel). Against empiricism, he insists that the mind is not a passive receiver of impressions; against Kant, he denies the fixed distinction between the mental and the extra-mental.
