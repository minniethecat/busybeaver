# Synthesis

The mental act in Bradley's logic by which distinct ideal elements are united into a single judgment—the positive moment of thought that constitutes judgment as more than the mere association of [[idea|ideas]] and that grounds the possibility of truth.

## Overview

Synthesis is one of the most fundamental operations in Bradley's logical theory. It is the act by which the mind brings together distinct ideal contents into a unitary judgment. Bradley introduces the concept in opposition to the empiricist view that judgment is merely the association or separation of [[idea|ideas]]: "If judgment is the synthesis of two [[idea|ideas]], then truth consists in the agreement of this synthesis with reality" (002-003). The inadequacy of this view becomes the launching point for his own positive account.

Bradley's treatment of synthesis develops through a dialectical progression. He begins by rejecting the simple associationist model—judgment is not "the synthesis of ideas, but the reference of ideal content to reality" (002-011). Yet synthesis is not thereby eliminated from his account. It reappears at a higher level as the act by which the mind constructs the ideal content that is then referred to reality. "The synthesis of the ideal elements is predicated of the real" (002-012).

The concept of synthesis plays a crucial role in Bradley's account of inference as well. When we infer, we are not merely analyzing given [[premise|premises]]; we are synthesizing them with new ideal content to produce the conclusion. This synthetic character of inference is what makes it genuinely productive of new knowledge. "We have a junction or synthesis of ideas in the mind, and this synthesis either is or is not a fact" (002-002).

Synthesis also connects to Bradley's broader idealist metaphysics. If thought is fundamentally synthetic, then the world as known—the only world we have—is a synthesis of the given and the ideal. This theme, only implicit in the *Principles*, becomes central in *Appearance and Reality*.

## Key Aspects

### Synthesis vs. Association

Bradley's primary critical target is the associationist account of judgment. The empiricist tradition, from Hume through Mill, treated judgment as the association of [[idea|ideas]] in the mind. Bradley argues that this conflates a psychological event with a logical act. "[[judgment|Judgment]] is not the synthesis of ideas, but the reference of ideal content to a reality beyond the act" (002-011). Synthesis alone—the mere putting together of [[idea|ideas]]—does not constitute judgment, because judgment makes a claim about reality that synthesis as such does not.

### Synthesis and the Subject-Attribute Relation

The synthesis most characteristic of judgment is the synthesis of subject and attribute. Bradley argues that "all the other hypotheses except the synthesis of subject and attribute" fail to explain valid inference (011-007). The synthesis of subject and attribute is not merely grammatical; it is a logical operation that creates the unity in which predication becomes possible.

### Synthesis in Inference

[[inference|Inference]] is not purely analytic. "First A, then A in ideal experiment becoming Aδ, and last the assertion" (019-001). The ideal experiment—Bradley's account of the inferential process—involves synthesizing the initial datum with further ideal content. The conclusion is not already contained in the [[premise|premises]]; it is produced through a synthetic act that expands the ideal content while maintaining continuity of identity.

### The Limits of Synthesis

Bradley recognizes that synthesis has limits. Not every combination of [[idea|ideas]] constitutes a valid synthesis. "The necessity for two [[idea|ideas]] does not by any means consist in their synthesis" (002-007). A valid synthesis must be grounded in the internal relations of the contents involved. Arbitrary combination produces no judgment, merely fantasy. This constraint is what distinguishes logical synthesis from mere psychological association.

### Analysis as a Form of Synthesis

In a characteristic dialectical move, Bradley suggests that even analysis involves synthesis. "When your [[ideal-construction|ideal synthesis]] is a mere analysis of this given fact" (002-029), the distinction between the two collapses. To analyze a complex is to synthesize its elements in thought—to hold them together while distinguishing them. This insight anticipates phenomenological and gestalt approaches to mental acts.

## Historical Context

Bradley's concept of synthesis is deeply indebted to Kant and Hegel. Kant's "synthetic unity of apperception" is the transcendental condition of judgment; Hegel's dialectic is driven by the synthesis of opposites. Bradley inherits from both the conviction that thought is essentially synthetic, not merely associative or analytical.

However, Bradley's synthesis differs from Hegel's in important respects. For Hegel, synthesis (or sublation) resolves contradictions by incorporating opposed moments into a higher unity. For Bradley, synthesis in the logical sense is more modest: it is the act of bringing contents together in judgment, without claiming that every such synthesis resolves a contradiction. This difference reflects Bradley's more cautious epistemology and his resistance to the more extravagant claims of Hegelian metaphysics.
