# Privation

Privation (*steresis*) is, in Bradley's *Principles of Logic*, one of the fundamental modes of negation. It is distinguished from mere [[denial|logical denial]] and from formal opposition: privation is the exclusion of a predicate grounded in a positive quality of the subject. "The truth of the negative may be seen in the end to lie in the affirmation of a positive quality" (003-003).

When we say "This tree is not yellow," we are not merely withholding a predicate; we are affirming that the tree has a positive character — its greenness — which excludes yellowness.

## Overview

Bradley draws the concept of privation from the Aristotelian tradition, where *steresis* denotes the absence of a form that a subject might be expected to have. But he gives it a distinctively idealist reinterpretation. "The distinctions of privation and opposition ([[christoph-von-sigwart|Sigwart]], *Logic*, § 23) are of the highest importance" (003-003).

For Bradley, privation is the key to understanding how negation can be true of reality: it is not that reality contains negative facts alongside positive ones, but that reality's positive character itself excludes certain determinations.

The concept is introduced in Chapter III ("The [[negative-judgment|Negative Judgment]]") and reappears throughout the work. "Privation can rest on another basis. The content of the subject may contain no space which could possibly be qualified by the suggested predicate" (003-004).

In such cases, the predication is rejected not because the subject possesses an incompatible quality but because the subject's nature provides no foothold for the suggested determination.

## Key Aspects

### Privation as Positive Exclusion

Bradley insists that genuine negation always rests on a positive ground. "The place left empty by this mere privation is occupied by a positive attribute" (007-007). When we deny a predicate of a subject, we do so because the subject *is* something — it has a determinate character — and that determinate character leaves no room for the denied predicate.

[[negation|Negation]] is thus parasitic on affirmation: [[negation|denial]] is always the [[negation|denial]] of a suggested synthesis, and what makes the [[negation|denial]] true is the affirmative character of the real.

### Privation and Opposition

Bradley distinguishes privation from formal opposition (contradiction and contrariety). In formal opposition, two predicates are logically incompatible by definition — "round" and "square" cannot both characterise the same surface. Privation is broader: "the universal abstraction, ostensibly unqualified, is determined by my mental repulsion of qualities" (003-005).

The ground of exclusion need not be a formal relation between predicates; it can be the concrete character of the subject as experienced.

### Privation and the Concrete Individual

The ultimate ground of privation, for Bradley, is the concrete individual. "The concrete particular and the concrete universal both have reality" (006-010). Because reality is individual through and through, every genuine exclusion is rooted in the determinate nature of some real individual. Abstract negation — "A is not not-A" — is derivative from the concrete exclusions that experience reveals.

Formal logic mistakes these derivative formulae for the ground of negation, when in truth they merely codify patterns of exclusion that originate in our encounter with reality.

### Privation in Inference

Bradley explores the role of privation in inference, particularly negative [[inference|reasoning]] (Book II, Part I, Chapter IV). "The general nature of negative [[inference|reasoning]] does not vitally differ from positive [[inference|reasoning]]" (012-001).

Just as positive inference moves from a concrete connection of attributes to a new case, [[negative-reasoning|negative inference]] moves from a concrete exclusion — a privation — to the [[negation|denial]] of a suggested connection. The syllogistic treatment of negative [[premise|premises]] as formal negations obscures the privative ground from which they derive their force.

## Historical Context

The concept of privation originates with Aristotle's analysis of change and negation in the *Physics* and *Metaphysics*. Bradley inherits the term through the German logical tradition, particularly [[christoph-von-sigwart|Sigwart]]'s *Logic*, which he cites extensively.

Bradley's contribution is to integrate privation into an idealist theory of negation: negation is not a primitive logical operation but a mode of judgment grounded in the positive character of reality. This positions him against both formal logicians (who treat negation as a syncategorematic operator) and empiricists (who treat negative facts as primitive constituents of reality).
