# Negative Reasoning

In Bradley's *Principles of Logic*, negative [[inference|reasoning]] is the form of inference in which at least one premise denies. Bradley's treatment in Book II, Chapter V is notable for its departure from traditional syllogistic doctrine: he argues that negative [[inference|reasoning]] does not differ in nature from positive [[inference|reasoning]], being governed by the same principle of ideal construction.

"The general nature of negative [[inference|reasoning]] does not vitally differ from that of positive" (012-001). Both involve the combination of relations around an identical point and the subsequent inspection of the resulting whole for a new relation.

## Overview

Bradley's analysis of negative [[inference|reasoning]] is part of his larger project of providing a unified theory of inference grounded in the construction of an ideal whole.

In positive [[inference|reasoning]], the [[premise|premises]] present relations that share an identical element, and on the basis of that identity we construct a whole within which a new relation becomes visible.

Negative [[inference|reasoning]] follows the same pattern, with the difference that "one part of the figure, which ideally we construct, consists of a repulsion; and the fresh connection we draw from that construction is a connection by exclusion" (012-001).

This approach leads Bradley to challenge two traditional syllogistic rules: the rule forbidding two negative [[premise|premises]], and the rule requiring a negative conclusion when one premise is negative.

He finds both rules to have "a solid foundation" (012-003) when properly interpreted, but he also argues that they are overstated and that genuine inferences exist which the traditional rules fail to accommodate.

## Key Aspects

### The Principle of Negative Inference

Bradley's central claim is that negative [[inference|reasoning]] operates by the same constructive principle as positive [[inference|reasoning]]. Given [[premise|premises]] A-B and B-C where one relation is one of [[negation|denial]], "we combine the relations into an individual whole. We then by inspection find a new relation within that whole" (012-001).

The difference between affirmative and negative inference lies not in the nature of the [[inference|reasoning]] but in the character of the relations combined and the result obtained. "The conclusion may connect two terms directly, as in A-B-C .'. A-C, or it may connect them indirectly" (012-001).

### The Rule Against Two Negative Premises

The traditional logic forbids inference from two negative [[premise|premises]]. Bradley accepts this rule in its strict interpretation: "From two bare [[negation|denial]]s there can come no conclusion, because there can not be any construction" (012-003). The reason is that either there is no common centre, or the terms whose relations of [[negation|denial]] are given have no determinate position relative to each other.

"Thus even if two [[negation|denial]]s have a common subject, we can not go from those [[negation|denial]]s to a further relation. And we are stopped elsewhere by another obstacle, for we have not got the common centre required for a construction" (012-003). Bradley's analysis thus provides a principled justification for a traditional rule, while showing that its apparent exceptions are not genuine counterexamples.

### Beyond Bare Denial

Where Bradley departs from tradition is in arguing that negative [[premise|premises]] need not be interpreted as bare [[negation|denial]]s.

If we decline to elide the middle term, we may obtain conclusions that are not mere [[negation|denial]]s.

"The syllogistic practice is to elide the middle; but if we do not choose to perform this elision, who on the one hand can order us to do so?

And on the other hand who can deny that the result which we obtain is a real inference?" (012-007).

Bradley gives the example: "A takes precedence of B, B is not younger than C, therefore A takes precedence of a person (B) not younger than himself. There is here no direct conclusion A-C, and there is again no inference within one category, and at the same time one premise seems to be used as mere [[negation|denial]]" (012-007).

### The General Formula

Bradley's final formulation for negative [[inference|reasoning]], "if we confine ourselves to the side of bare [[negation|denial]]," is: "If B repels a content C, and is in relation with a third term A, then A and C will either be related directly by way of [[negation|denial]] or else will be elements in a whole A-B-C, of which at least one member will be confined to the genus of subject and attribute" (012-007).

This formula preserves the traditional restrictions while acknowledging the wider range of valid negative inferences that the syllogism, narrowly interpreted, excludes.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of negative [[inference|reasoning]] engages a long tradition of logical analysis of negation, from Aristotle's *Prior Analytics* through the medieval logicians to the nineteenth-century formal logicians.

The syllogistic rules governing negative [[premise|premises]] were among the most settled parts of the logical curriculum, and Bradley's willingness to question them reflects his broader scepticism about the adequacy of syllogistic formalism.

His approach is influenced by the German logicians, particularly Lotze and [[christoph-von-sigwart|Sigwart]], who had also questioned the rigidity of traditional syllogistic doctrine. Bradley's constructive theory — the view that inference is always the construction of an ideal whole within which new relations become visible — provides a framework in which positive and negative inference are species of a single genus.
