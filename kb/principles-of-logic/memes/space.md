Space plays a significant role in Bradley's analysis of judgment and individuation in the *Principles of Logic*. Bradley treats space, along with time, as a principle of individuation — a way in which particulars are distinguished from one another — but he argues that spatial determinations cannot provide the genuine individuality that logic and metaphysics demand.

## Space as a Principle of Individuation

In ordinary thought, we distinguish one thing from another by its position in space and time. The series of events that occur in space and time provide a framework for identifying what is individual. (002-003) A particular phenomenon is located at a specific place in space; this spatial location seems to mark it off from all other phenomena.

However, Bradley argues that space and time are not merely given features of reality but are themselves relational determinations that must be accounted for. The notion that space and time are "principles of individuation" cannot be accepted without critical examination. (002-016) A mere quality of appearance in space or time cannot give singularity to a thing, for spatial position is itself a universal determination. (002-017)

## The Relativity of Spatial Determinations

Bradley emphasizes the relational character of spatial determinations. The nature of space excludes the connection of square and circle, for spatial determinations involve mutual exclusion as well as relation. (002-001) What appears in space is always qualified by its spatial relations to other things, and these relations point beyond the thing itself.

Space and time beyond the here and now are not simply given in perception. (002-011) Our knowledge of distant spaces is synthetic — it extends beyond what is immediately given in perception, and this extension requires justification. (002-021) The spatial determinations that seem to mark off individual things are themselves dependent on a system of relations that transcends any particular spatial location.

## Space and the Limits of Individuation

The fundamental problem with space as a principle of individuation is that spatial position cannot confer true self-existence. A thing located in space is located by its relations to other spatial things, and these relations are external to it. The outside of a thing in space makes it vulnerable to the inroads of unreality. (002-022)

For Bradley, genuine individuality requires self-existence and self-containedness — qualities that no spatially determined particular can possess. Space is a form of externality, and what is spatially determined is determined by what lies outside it. True individuality, being self-contained, cannot depend on spatial externality.

## Place in Bradley's System

Space is not dismissed by Bradley as illusory; rather, it is recognized as a necessary form of appearance that must be transcended in the movement toward the Absolute. Spatial determinations are real as appearances, but they are not ultimately real. They are absorbed into the concrete whole, where the externality of space is overcome in the internality of systematic connection.
