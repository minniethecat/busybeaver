# Association of Ideas

In Bradley's *Principles of Logic*, the "association of [[idea|ideas]]" is a phrase that stands for a familiar psychological fact — that one mental content can call up another — but it has also been elevated by the empiricist school into a comprehensive theory of mind.

Bradley rejects the theory while accepting the fact, arguing in Book II, Part II that association requires an underlying *identity of content* and cannot be explained by external conjunction alone.

## Overview

Bradley opens his discussion by distinguishing two senses of "association." As a name for a well-known psychological fact, it is "nothing but a title" (013-002). But as understood by the Philosophy of [[experience|Experience]], association has become "the battle-cry of a school, and a metaphysical doctrine and theory of things" (013-002). In this latter sense Bradley denies its existence.

His central objection is that association theories posit mental atoms — "particular feelings and particular [[idea|ideas]], in either case repellent units" (013-003) — that have "absolutely no internal bond of connection." Without any ground common to the different units, there is "no real basis for their union" (013-003). Universality and identity are derided as fictions by the associationists.

Against this, Bradley argues that all reproduction depends on an ideal identity across different mental occurrences: "I maintain that all association is between universals" (013-006). What explains the reproduction of [[idea|ideas]] is not past contiguity but a present identity of content.

## Key Aspects

### Association as Chance-Conjunction

Bradley analyses the very word "association" to uncover its philosophical commitments. "The word Association, I suppose, implies properly some kind of voluntary union. That signification of course disappears, but it leaves a shade of meaning behind. For things are not associated by their own necessity, and by virtue of some internal connection" (013-004).

Association thus comes to mean "chance-conjunction" (013-004) — a union caused by circumstances external to the elements themselves. While such conjunctions do occur in mental life, Bradley denies they can explain the systematic coherence of thought.

### The Critique of Contiguity

The Law of Association by Contiguity is subjected to a close logical analysis. Bradley asks: what is contiguous, and what is associated? "The impressions are not associated; I presume that is obvious. They are conjoined in presentation, just like anything else we perceive together is conjoined. It is the [[idea|ideas]] which are associated" (013-010).

This creates a fatal problem: "Association and contiguity fall hopelessly asunder; and hence let our law be never so real, it can not be the Law of Association by Contiguity" (013-010). If impressions were once contiguous, and [[idea|ideas]] now tend to excite one another because they resemble those impressions, then what is associated was not contiguous, and what was contiguous is not associated.

### Redintegration and Ideal Identity

Against associationism, Bradley sets his theory of redintegration — reproduction through partial identity of content. "[[redintegration|Redintegration]] is not anything else. For it never re-instates the particular fact. It can not" (013-005). What is reproduced is never the original concrete experience but an ideal content abstracted from it.

The mechanism that drives reproduction is not external association but internal identity: an element present in current experience shares content with a past experience, and this shared content — which is a universal — serves as the basis for reinstatement. "An identity of content in different contexts is and must be an universal, whether we are dealing with perceptions or feelings or volitions" (013-007).

### The Logical Stakes

Bradley's interest in association is not primarily psychological but logical. If associationism were true — if all mental life consisted of externally conjoined particulars — then inference itself would be impossible. Logical [[inference|reasoning]] requires identity across [[premise|premises]] and conclusion; it requires precisely the kind of internal connection that associationism denies.

The chapter on association thus serves Bradley's broader argument about the nature of judgment and inference, preparing the ground for his theory that all [[inference|reasoning]] depends on ideal identities within a concrete universal.

## Historical Context

The associationist psychology Bradley attacks traces back to Hobbes and Locke, was systematised by David Hartley, and reached its most rigorous form in [[james-mill|James Mill]]'s *Analysis of the Phenomena of the Human Mind* (1829). [[john-stuart-mill|John Stuart Mill]]'s edition of his father's work, with extensive commentary by [[alexander-bain|Alexander Bain]] and others, kept the tradition alive into Bradley's time.

Bradley's critique shares ground with idealist criticisms of empiricist psychology — particularly the Hegelian point that particulars cannot stand alone but require universals. However Bradley does not appeal to Hegelian metaphysics directly; his argument proceeds by internal critique of the associationist position, showing that its own examples presuppose the identity it denies.
