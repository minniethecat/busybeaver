# Necessary Judgment

A necessary judgment (also called an apodictic judgment) is a modal form asserting that a connection holds by logical necessity — "S must be P." In Bradley's analysis, what appears as a special modal quality of judgment is in reality an inference whose grounds have been made fully explicit.

## Overview

The necessary judgment occupies the highest position in Kant's modal scheme, above the possible and the assertoric. It is the form in which we claim not merely that something is the case, but that it must be the case. Bradley devotes careful attention to the nature of this necessity, arguing that it is fundamentally inferential rather than a sui generis modal property. (007-003)

Bradley's central claim is that "a necessary truth is really an inference." (007-019) What we call a necessary judgment is simply a conclusion that follows from [[premise|premises]], and the sense of necessity arises from our grasp of the logical connection between data and conclusion. This r[[immediate-inference|eduction]] of necessity to inference is a key move in Bradley's monistic logic: necessity is not a mysterious metaphysical glue but the transparency of logical derivation.

The phrase "S-P is a necessary truth" means, for Bradley, "S-P follows from some ground which is taken as real." (007-005) The necessity lies not in the judgment itself but in the relation of ground to consequent. A judgment appears necessary when we can exhibit the full chain of [[inference|reasoning]] that supports it.

## Key Aspects

### Necessity as Inference

Bradley's most radical claim about necessary judgment is that it collapses into inference. "In logic we find that a necessary truth is really an inference." (007-019) This means that the necessity of a judgment is never an intrinsic property of the proposition considered in isolation; it is always relative to a body of data and a chain of [[inference|reasoning]]. When that chain is fully explicit, the judgment appears as necessary; when it is implicit, the same judgment may appear merely assertoric.

### The Relation to Data

A necessary judgment is always relative to assumed data. Bradley emphasizes that the same judgment can be necessary relative to one set of data and merely possible relative to another. (009-001) This relativization of necessity undermines the traditional view that modal distinctions mark absolute differences in the kind of truth a judgment possesses. "The necessary truth need be no more particular than its data" (009-001) — necessity is inherited from the [[premise|premises]], not a special quality superadded to the conclusion.

### Apodictic Modality and Assertion Strength

Bradley challenges the common assumption that apodictic modality strengthens an assertion. "It is not true that apodeiktic modality strengthens our assertions." (007-003) Rather, the appearance of strength comes from making the inferential basis explicit, not from adding a modal operator. The necessary judgment says nothing more than the assertoric judgment with its full grounds laid bare.

### Necessity and the Unity of Judgment

The necessary judgment reveals the ultimate unity of all judgment in Bradley's system. Since every judgment implicitly involves an inference, and every inference, when made fully explicit, yields a necessary connection, the distinction between necessary and non-necessary judgments is one of degree rather than kind. All judgment tends toward necessity as its grounds are progressively articulated.

## Historical Context

The apodictic judgment is the third of Kant's modal categories in the table of judgments. Kant understood apodictic judgments as those "accompanied with the consciousness of necessity," distinguishing them from assertoric judgments (accompanied with the consciousness of actuality) and [[possible-judgment|problematic judgment]]s (accompanied with the consciousness of mere possibility).

Bradley's treatment departs from Kant in a crucial respect. Where Kant saw modality as a function of the subject's attitude toward the judgment, Bradley sees it as a function of the judgment's logical infrastructure — specifically, the degree to which its inferential basis has been articulated. This is part of Bradley's broader project of replacing Kant's transcendental psychology with a logic grounded in the nature of judgment itself.
