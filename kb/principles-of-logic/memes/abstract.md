The concept of the abstract is central to Bradley's logic and metaphysics. For Bradley, abstraction is not merely a psychological operation but a logical defect that arises when an element is separated from the concrete whole in which it has its being. Bradley uses the distinction between abstract and concrete to critique both traditional logic and empiricist metaphysics.

## Abstract Universals and Abstract Particulars

Bradley argues that both abstract universals and abstract particulars are unreal when taken as self-subsistent entities. An abstract universal is a general concept considered in isolation from its instances. The judgment "A is B" expresses an abstract universal: it does not assert the real existence of any subject, but only a hypothetical connection between predicates. (002-030) All abstract universal judgments are, for Bradley, hypothetical in character. (002-034)

The abstract particular is the converse error. It is the bare "this" — a particular considered in abstraction from all its qualities and relations. Bradley holds that the merely particular, no less than the abstract universal, is an unreal abstraction. (006-013) The concrete universal, which unites particularity and universality, is Bradley's alternative to both.

## Abstraction as a Logical Operation

In Bradley's account, abstraction is the act of attending to one aspect of a concrete whole while ignoring others. We are able to abstract an idea of presence from a given presentation, but in doing so we lose the thing itself. (002-019) The general idea produced by abstraction is, considered in itself, a mere abstraction — it does not exist as such in reality. (002-029)

The process of abstraction and generalization is what produces universal concepts, but these concepts gain their meaning only through their connection to the concrete system of reality. (006-002) Bradley insists that abstractions, taken as independent facts, are delusive. We seek rest in abstractions from the concrete, but these are mere possibilities, not actual realities. (001-005)

## Criticism of Abstract Thinking

Bradley's critique of abstraction extends to both common sense and philosophical systems. Common sense treats abstractions such as "this tree is green" as straightforward truths, but for Bradley such poor abstractions are mere bare meanings that fail to capture reality. (002-036) The abstract hypothetical judgment does not assert factual existence; it merely states a conditional relationship. (002-040)

In metaphysics, the error of abstraction is even more consequential. Bradley contends that the soul is more of an abstraction than any particular psychical phenomenon, and that abstractions produced by philosophical analysis do not exist as real entities. (006-004) Ideals that are down-right abstractions have no independent existence. (007-018)

## The Concrete Universal as Resolution

Bradley's positive doctrine is the concrete universal — a universal that contains its particulars within itself rather than standing apart from them in abstraction. The bare particular and the abstract universal are distinctions within a more fundamental concrete reality. (013-017) True universals are not abstracted from particulars but are the concrete ground that makes particulars what they are.

Abstraction, for Bradley, is not an error to be eliminated but a necessary moment in thought that must be transcended. The goal of logic is not to rest in abstractions but to recover the concrete whole from which abstraction has separated us. (002-042)
