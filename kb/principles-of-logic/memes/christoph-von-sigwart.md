# Christoph von Sigwart

Christoph von Sigwart (1830–1904) was a German philosopher and logician whose
two-volume *Logik* (1873–1878) stands as one of the most comprehensive works of
logical theory in the nineteenth century.

In Bradley's *The Principles of Logic*,
[[sigwart|Sigwart]] is the most frequently cited Continental authority — his analyses of
judgment, negation, universal [[idea|ideas]], and disjunctive [[inference|reasoning]] provide essential
reference points for Bradley's own positions.

## Life and Works

Christoph von Sigwart was born in Tübingen, Germany, into a family with a strong
philosophical tradition — his father, Heinrich Christoph Wilhelm von [[sigwart|Sigwart]],
was also a philosopher. The younger [[sigwart|Sigwart]] studied theology and philosophy at
Tübingen and later became Professor of Philosophy there, holding the position
from 1865 until his death in 1904.

His magnum opus, *Logik* (Volume I: 1873, Volume II: 1878), went through multiple
editions and was translated into English in 1895. It represents a major attempt
to reform logical theory by grounding it in the analysis of judgment as a mental
act while avoiding the r[[immediate-inference|eduction]] of logic to psychology — a project with clear
parallels to Bradley's own.

## Theory of Judgment

[[sigwart|Sigwart]]'s theory of judgment is one of the most important reference points for
Bradley's own account. [[sigwart|Sigwart]] held that judgment is fundamentally a mental act
in which a subject accepts or rejects an ideal content — a view Bradley engages
with at length:

> Compare on this whole subject Sigwart, Logik, I. 119 and foil.
> — (003-002)

While Bradley shares [[sigwart|Sigwart]]'s emphasis on judgment as the central logical act
and the importance of the distinction between subject and predicate, he differs
from [[sigwart|Sigwart]] on the ultimate nature of the subject — for Bradley it is always
reality itself, not a mental representation.

### Negation and Opposition

[[sigwart|Sigwart]]'s analysis of negation and opposition is one of his most influential
contributions. He distinguished between different senses of negation and argued
that [[negation|logical negation]] always presupposes a positive ground:

> …"privation" and "opposition" (Sigwart, 128 foil) do not alter the essential
> character of negation.
> — (003-003)

Bradley draws on this analysis in his own treatment of [[negation|negative judgment]] and
negative [[inference|reasoning]] (Book II, Chapter V), though he argues that [[sigwart|Sigwart]] does not
go far enough in recognizing the positive element in all negation.

### Universal Ideas

[[sigwart|Sigwart]]'s treatment of universal [[idea|ideas]] is another key reference for Bradley.
[[sigwart|Sigwart]] argued that the universal idea, when kept in abstraction, cannot serve
as the subject of a genuine judgment:

> The universal idea (cf. Sigwart, 130), if you keep it in abstraction from
> all relation to the individual, is nothing that can be judged about at all.
> — (003-004)

This aligns closely with Bradley's own view that abstract universals are unreal
and that all judgment is ultimately about the concrete individual — reality itself.

## Disjunctive Judgment

[[sigwart|Sigwart]]'s analysis of [[disjunction|disjunctive judgment]] is especially important for Bradley.
[[sigwart|Sigwart]] called attention to the distinctive logical features of disjunctive
[[inference|reasoning]]:

> …consequence Sigwart has called attention to these facts, and has urged
> their importance for logical theory.
> — (004-005)

Bradley builds on [[sigwart|Sigwart]]'s observations in his own treatment of disjunctive
[[inference|reasoning]], developing a more systematic account of how [[disjunction|disjunctive judgment]]s
function in inference.

## Possibility and Probability

[[sigwart|Sigwart]]'s analysis of possibility judgments is cited by Bradley in his discussion
of modal [[inference|reasoning]]:

> …the positive result laid down by Sigwart (182, 227), and which our
> discussion has confirmed, is that possibility is always relative to our
> knowledge.
> — (007-006)

This view — that possibility is epistemic rather than ontological — is one that
Bradley largely accepts, though he integrates it into a broader idealist framework.

## Influence on Bradley

[[sigwart|Sigwart]]'s influence on Bradley's *Principles* can be traced across multiple
dimensions:

**[[judgment|Judgment]] theory**: [[sigwart|Sigwart]]'s account of judgment as the acceptance or rejection of an ideal content provides the foundation for Bradley's own theory.

**[[negation|Negation]]**: [[sigwart|Sigwart]]'s insight that negation presupposes a positive ground is extended in Bradley's analysis of negative [[inference|reasoning]].

**Universal [[idea|ideas]]**: [[sigwart|Sigwart]]'s argument that abstract universals cannot serve as subjects of judgment is integrated into Bradley's theory of the concrete universal.

**[[disjunction|Disjunction]]**: [[sigwart|Sigwart]]'s identification of the distinctive logical features of [[disjunctive-judgment|disjunction]] forms the basis for Bradley's chapter on [[disjunction|disjunctive judgment]].

**Modality**: [[sigwart|Sigwart]]'s view that possibility is always relative to our knowledge is adopted and developed in Bradley's modal analysis.

Bradley acknowledges his debt to [[sigwart|Sigwart]] explicitly:

> …the writers from whom on this subject I have learnt most are Lotze, Logik;
> Sigwart, Logik; Wundt, Logik; Jevons, Principles of Science.
> — (007-019)

## Relations to Contemporaries

[[sigwart|Sigwart]] stood at the intersection of several philosophical traditions. His *Logik*
engaged both the German Idealist tradition (particularly through his Tübingen
predecessors) and the newer psychological approaches to logic.

He was a contemporary
of [[lotze|Hermann Lotze]], whose logical work Bradley also draws on extensively,
and his approach influenced subsequent German logical theory including the
phenomenological tradition.

In the English-speaking world, [[sigwart|Sigwart]]'s influence came largely through Bradley's
extensive citations. Bradley groups [[sigwart|Sigwart]] with Lotze as representing the German
approach to logic that, in his view, surpassed the British inductive tradition of
Mill and [[alexander-bain|Bain]] in philosophical depth:

> …in England, Professors Lotze and Sigwart in Germany, have taken a view
> which approaches more nearly to the truth.
> — (015-006)
