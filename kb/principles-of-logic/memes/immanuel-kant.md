# Immanuel Kant

Immanuel Kant (1724–1804) was a German philosopher whose critical philosophy provided essential terminology and conceptual distinctions that Bradley both draws upon and criticizes throughout the *Principles of Logic*.

## Overview

Immanuel Kant's presence in Bradley's *Principles of Logic* is terminological and structural rather than exegetical. Bradley does not offer a detailed engagement with Kant's *Critique of Pure Reason*; instead, Kant's vocabulary — the analytic/synthetic distinction, the "condition of the rule," the thing-in-itself — provides a recurring reference point that Bradley uses to position his own logical doctrine relative to both the empiricist tradition he rejects and the German idealism he partly embraces.

Bradley's references to Kant span the *Principles* from the early treatment of judgment through the discussion of syllogism. The pattern is consistent: Bradley acknowledges Kant's advances over empiricism while arguing that Kant's framework remains incomplete — trapped, in Bradley's view, within a formalism that cannot reach the living movement of actual thought.

## Key Ideas

### Analytic and Synthetic Judgments

Kant's distinction between analytic and synthetic judgments is the Kantian doctrine Bradley engages with most extensively. Bradley's treatment is characteristically revisionary: he argues that the distinction is fluid rather than fixed, depending on the state of knowledge of the judging subject. "A judgment is not fixed as 'synthetic' or 'analytic': its character varies with the knowledge possessed by various persons, and at different times" (006-009). Bradley draws the radical conclusion that "a synthetic judgment, so soon as it is made, is at once analytic" — a position that effectively dissolves Kant's distinction into a dynamic process of conceptual growth.

Bradley further insists that "the analytic and synthetic judgments must not for one moment be confounded with Kant's" (002-006), signaling that while he borrows Kant's terminology, he transforms its meaning. For Bradley, "every possible judgment is both analytic and synthetic" — analytic insofar as it unpacks the content of a subject, synthetic insofar as it asserts a connection not previously contained in that content.

### The Principle of Syllogism

Kant's formulation of the principle of syllogism — *nota notae est nota rei ipsius* (the mark of a mark is a mark of the thing itself) — is examined in Bradley's discussion of inference. "What stands under the condition of a rule," Kant wrote, "stands under the rule." Bradley acknowledges the validity of this principle: "It is evident that this principle of [[inference|reasoning]] is valid" (009-003). However, Bradley argues that it cannot cover the whole field of [[inference|reasoning]]: "confined to the category of subject and attribute, it fails wherever you pass beyond." Relations of priority, equality, and other non-attributive connections resist Kant's formulation.

### The Thing-in-Itself

Bradley's single direct reference to Kant's thing-in-itself occurs in a polemical context, dismissing it as a "wretched ghost" that empiricists invoke while failing to recognize that their own position leads to the same contradictions they attribute to idealism (005-005). This passing reference reveals Bradley's broadly Hegelian assessment of Kant: the critical philosophy correctly identified the inadequacies of empiricism but retained a residual dualism (phenomena/noumena) that a more thoroughgoing idealism must overcome.

## Bradley's Engagement

Bradley's relationship to Kant is complex. He is clearly indebted to Kant's demonstration that formal logic cannot be separated from epistemological inquiry — that the conditions of judgment and inference are inseparable from the nature of thought itself. Yet Bradley consistently positions himself beyond Kant, suggesting that Kant's insights are better expressed within Bradley's own idealist framework.

The key point of divergence concerns the nature of synthesis. For Kant, synthesis is an act of the understanding that combines a sensible manifold under categories. For Bradley, synthesis is internal to the content of judgment itself — not an act performed upon content but the very structure of ideal content as such. When Bradley writes that "every judgment is essentially synthetical" (005-002), he means something different from Kant: synthesis is not the application of categories to intuitions but the assertion of identity within difference that constitutes judgment as such.

Bradley's most pointed criticism of Kant concerns the formalism of the critical philosophy. Kant's principle of syllogism, Bradley argues, works only for subject-predicate judgments; it cannot handle relational inferences. The "unfortunate division" of analytic and synthetic judgments is "a distinction which he seems to have inherited" rather than argued for (006-009). The real question, Bradley suggests, is not whether a judgment is analytic or synthetic but "What kind of synthesis does each judgment contain, and what in each synthesis is the principle of unity?"

## Influence

Kant's critical philosophy transformed European philosophy and made German idealism possible. For Bradley and the British idealists, Kant was the thinker who had demonstrated the bankruptcy of empiricism while leaving unfinished the task of developing a fully idealist logic. Bradley's *Principles of Logic* can be read as an attempt to complete this Kantian project: to develop a logic that is neither formal (like Aristotle's) nor psychological (like Mill's) but genuinely philosophical — a logic grounded in the nature of thought itself. Bradley's debts to Kant are perhaps deeper than his explicit references suggest: the *Principles*' central doctrine that judgment is the reference of an ideal content to reality is a radicalization of Kant's insight that judgment is never merely the analysis of concepts.
