# J.S. Mill

John Stuart Mill (1806–1873), British empiricist philosopher and logician, is Bradley's most frequent critical target in the *Principles of Logic*. Mill's *[[system|System]] of Logic* (1843) represents the empiricist, associationist approach to logic that Bradley's entire work is designed to refute.

## Overview

Bradley engages Mill across nearly every major topic in the *Principles of Logic*: the nature of judgment, the copula, the laws of thought, modality, and the theory of inference. Mill stands for the view that logical laws are empirical generalizations derived from experience, that judgment is the association of [[idea|ideas]], and that the copula merely signifies coexistence of attributes. Bradley's systematic rejection of each of these positions structures much of his argument.

Bradley's references to Mill range from technical criticism — "J.S. Mill in his Logic (I. 4, § 3) approves the statement that the copula is the mere sign of predication" (002-030) — to broader philosophical disagreement about the foundations of logic itself. Bradley treats Mill as the most serious representative of the errors he seeks to correct.

## Key Ideas

### Associationism

Mill's logic rests on the associationist psychology inherited from his father [[james-mill|James Mill]] and from David Hartley. On this view, logical connections between [[idea|ideas]] are explained by psychological [[association-of-ideas|laws of association]] — contiguity, similarity, and frequency. Bradley devotes an entire chapter (Book II, Part II, Chapter I) to refuting the association theory, arguing that it cannot explain how [[idea|ideas]] come to have meaning or how judgment becomes possible.

### The Copula

Mill treats the copula ("is") as a mere sign of predication, not as expressing identity between subject and predicate. Bradley objects that this misses the essential function of judgment: "In every judgment we assert the identity of subject and predicate" (001-017). The copula expresses a real relation, not merely a psychological habit of association.

### Laws of Thought as Empirical Generalizations

For Mill, the laws of identity, contradiction, and excluded middle are not a priori truths but empirical generalizations — the most general inductions from experience. Bradley's entire treatment of the laws of thought in Chapter V is directed against this view. The laws are grounded "in the nature of things" (005-005), not in psychological habit.

## Bradley's Critique

Bradley's critique of Mill operates at multiple levels. At the most fundamental level, he argues that Mill's empiricism cannot account for the universality that judgment requires. "The belief in universal [[idea|ideas]] does not involve the conviction that abstractions exist, even as facts in my head" (001-007). Universals are not psychological entities but logical meanings, and no amount of association can transform particular impressions into universal [[idea|ideas]].

At a more technical level, Bradley argues that Mill's treatment of specific logical topics — the modality of judgments, the nature of inference, the status of hypothetical judgments — is inconsistent or incomplete. Bradley frequently cites Mill to illustrate an error before presenting his own correction.

Bradley also objects to Mill's "Law of Relativity," which holds that all knowledge is of relations between phenomena. For Bradley, this doctrine is self-refuting: if all knowledge is relative, then the claim that "all knowledge is relative" must itself be relative — and thus not universally true.

## Influence

Mill's *[[system|System]] of Logic* was the dominant English-language logic text of the 19th century, and Bradley's decision to structure much of his argument as a critique of Mill reflects Mill's centrality to the logical debates of the period. Bradley's anti-psychologism — his insistence that logic is about meanings, not mental events — anticipated the critique of psychologism that Frege and Husserl would develop more systematically in the following decades.
