# Immediate Inference

In the traditional logical curriculum, immediate inferences are contrasted with mediate inferences (syllogisms): they draw a conclusion from a single premise without the intervention of a middle term. Bradley's treatment of immediate inference in Book III of *The Principles of Logic* is characteristically sceptical.

He examines whether immediate inferences are genuine inferences at all, and concludes that "none of them can strictly be called 'immediate,' for none gives a conclusion without an operation" (018-014). Where they are valid, they fall under the same constructive principles as mediate [[inference|reasoning]].

## Overview

Bradley approaches immediate inference with the question that governs his entire theory of [[inference|reasoning]]: does the process involve the construction of an ideal whole within which a new relation becomes visible? If so, it is a genuine inference; if not, it is a merely verbal transformation.

"Some of them appear to be little else. To argue from 'A is B' to 'Some B is A' gives rise to suspicion, and that suspicion is deepened if we infer that B is equal to A because A is equal to B" (018-009). In such cases, Bradley asks: "what new conclusion comes from the process?" (018-009).

His answer is nuanced. Some immediate inferences are mere tautologies — "rearrangements of words without alteration of [[idea|ideas]]" (018-009). Others do yield genuine conclusions, but only because they involve an implicit operation of construction, which is to say they are not truly immediate.

## Key Aspects

### The Problem of Conversion

[[conversion|Conversion]] — the inference from "A is B" to "Some B is A" — is the paradigm case Bradley examines. His analysis turns on whether the judgment is taken categorically or problematically.

"The truth is that, if you keep to categorical affirmatives, your conversion or opposition is not ratiocinative, but is simply grammatical. The one conversion which is real inference is a modal conversion, a [[possible-judgment|problematic judgment]]" (018-011). When we convert a categorical judgment, we merely reorder its terms without adding any content; the result is not an inference but a restatement.

Genuine conversion, Bradley argues, occurs only when we move from a [[possible-judgment|problematic judgment]] to a categorical one, or vice versa — because then the modal character itself constitutes a new element in the construction.

### The Question of Tautology

Bradley raises the fundamental challenge: "If some immediate inferences seem to be tautologies, yet others are more stubborn. They appear to get to a fresh result, and they certainly do not seem to move in accord with our formula" (017-001).

The formula in question is Bradley's constructive theory of inference — the view that all [[inference|reasoning]] involves the synthesis of given elements around an identical centre, followed by the analysis of the resulting whole. Immediate inferences seem to bypass this machinery, and Bradley's task is to show that the appearance of immediacy is deceptive.

### The Operation Requirement

Bradley's settled view is that no inference is immediate in the strict sense because every inference requires an intellectual operation. "None of them can strictly be called 'immediate,' for none gives a conclusion without an operation" (018-014). The operation may be minimal — the repositioning of terms, the shift from one grammatical form to another — but it is an operation nonetheless.

This conclusion aligns with Bradley's broader thesis that [[inference|reasoning]] is not formal manipulation of symbols but the constructive activity of thought upon ideal contents. If inference were merely formal, conversion might be genuine; but because inference is constructive, what appears to be immediate is either trivial or covertly complex.

### The Place in Bradley's System

Bradley's treatment of immediate inference serves a strategic purpose in his argument. By showing that even the simplest logical operations require the constructive activity of thought, he undermines the formalist programme that would reduce logic to the manipulation of symbols according to rules.

The immediate inferences, traditionally treated as preliminaries to the syllogism, become in Bradley's hands evidence that no inference is ever truly formal — that "the rational is individual and individuality is truth" (022-007), and that logical validity depends on content, not on shape.

## Historical Context

The doctrine of immediate inference received its classical formulation in the medieval logic of the *Summulae Logicales* and was transmitted through the nineteenth-century textbook tradition. [[conversion|Conversion]], obversion, contraposition, and the square of opposition were standard topics in any logic curriculum.

Bradley's treatment is influenced by the German logicians' critiques of formal logic — particularly Lotze's view that logical operations are always operations upon content, not upon mere forms.

His conclusion that immediate inferences require an operation anticipates later debates in the philosophy of logic about the nature of logical consequence and the distinction between formal and material validity.
