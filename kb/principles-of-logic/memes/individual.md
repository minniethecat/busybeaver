The concept of the individual is a recurring theme throughout Bradley's *Principles of Logic*. Bradley examines the nature of individuality in logic, judgment, and metaphysics, ultimately concluding that no finite particular can be truly individual — only the Absolute, as the all-inclusive concrete whole, satisfies the demand for genuine individuality.

## Individuality in Judgment

Bradley holds that every judgment is ultimately about reality as a whole, not about isolated individuals. A fact is individual, while an idea is universal. (002-002) The judgment attempts to unite the individual character of fact with the universality of ideas, but this union is never fully achieved at the level of ordinary judgment.

The claim that a judgment refers to an individual subject proves, on analysis, to be untenable. No judgment is ever truly about the individual as such, for the individual that enters into judgment is always qualified by universals. (002-016) When we say "this rose is red," the subject "this rose" is not the rich individual of perception but an abstraction selected for the purposes of the judgment.

## The Individual and the Particular

Bradley distinguishes carefully between the individual and the merely particular. The real, if it is to be self-existent, must be individual. (002-003) But the particular phenomenon, considered in isolation, cannot be truly individual because it depends on relations to other phenomena and to the whole that encompasses them. (002-008)

A symbol, when used as such, foregoes its individuality and self-existence. (001-003) The individual thing, taken as a hard unique particular, is an abstraction — its individuality (if it has such) is conferred by its place in a system, not by its mere particularity. (001-006)

## Individual Identity

Bradley questions whether individual identity can be established through qualitative sameness. Qualitative sameness is not sufficient for individual identity, for two things may be qualitatively identical yet numerically distinct. (001-014) Individual identity requires more than shared qualities; it requires a unique position within the whole of reality.

## The Concrete Individual

Bradley's conclusion is that true individuality belongs only to the whole. The concrete universal is individual precisely because it contains its differences within itself, as a single self-differentiating system. Finite individuals are "individual" only in a relative and provisional sense — they are moments within the one true individual, the Absolute.

The real is what is individual, and it is the task of metaphysics to determine what this individual reality is. (002-003) Bradley's answer is that only the all-inclusive system of reality satisfies the demand for genuine individuality, for only it is truly self-existent and self-contained.
