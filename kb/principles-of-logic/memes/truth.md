Truth and falsehood are, for Bradley, the defining characteristics of judgment. Wherever we have truth or falsehood, it is the signification of an idea that is in question. (001-002) The distinction between truth and falsehood separates judgment from mere psychical association, and understanding this distinction is essential to Bradley's entire logical project.

## Truth as the Characteristic of Judgment

Bradley opens the *Principles of Logic* by locating truth and falsehood at the very center of judgment. In judgment, an ideal content is referred to a reality beyond the act of judging, and the truth of the judgment consists in the adequacy of this reference. By the truth of a judgment we mean that its suggestion is accepted as qualifying the real subject. (001-006)

Truth does not lie in the production of a psychical state. (001-010) It is not a feeling of conviction or a subjective event in the mind of the judger. Truth and falsehood, with their difference, are real phenomena that belong to the logical order, not merely to the psychological order. (001-012)

## Truth and the Structure of Judgment

Bradley argues that truth cannot be understood in isolation from the structure of judgment. The judgment is the location of truth and falsehood. It is impossible to have truth where judgment is impossible, for truth and falsehood arise only where an ideal content is referred to a reality that accepts or rejects it. (001-017)

The contrast of idea and reality is the ground of truth and falsehood. (001-018) When an idea is applied to reality, the result is either truth or falsehood. An idea that does not refer beyond itself — that remains a mere psychical image — is neither true nor false. It becomes true or false only in the act of judgment, where it is predicated of something real.

## The Limits of Truth

Bradley places important limits on the scope of truth. No single truth can state fact completely, for every judgment abstracts from the full concreteness of reality. (002-005) Truth exists in a system of judgments, and no isolated truth is self-sufficient. Truth has its being in the whole of knowledge, not in any single proposition.

This leads to Bradley's doctrine that truth is ultimately a matter of degree. A judgment is more or less true depending on how adequately it represents the whole of reality. Complete truth would require the complete system of all judgments — which is to say, the Absolute itself.

## Truth and Reality

For Bradley, truth is not something that can be separated from reality and set alongside it. Truth does not constitute reality; rather, truth is the ideal expression of reality. (002-003) No event or context can make truth falsehood, for truth is an eternal relation of ideal content to reality. (005-002)
