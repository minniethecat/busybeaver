# Syllogism

The syllogism is the classical form of deductive inference in which a conclusion
follows necessarily from two given [[premise|premises]].

In Bradley's *The Principles of Logic*,
the syllogism serves as the central test case for his broader theory of inference —
he neither accepts it uncritically nor rejects it wholesale, but instead uses its
strengths and limitations to illuminate what inference fundamentally is.

## Overview

Bradley places the syllogism at the heart of his investigation into the nature of
logical [[inference|reasoning]].

Rather than treating it as a completed doctrine to be either
defended or discarded, he subjects it to sustained critical examination across
multiple dimensions: its formal structure, its capacity to handle negative [[inference|reasoning]],
its relationship to induction, and its claim to represent the universal type of all
inference.

The syllogism matters to Bradley not merely as a historical artifact of Aristotelian
logic but as a living question: can all valid [[inference|reasoning]] be reduced to syllogistic
form, or does the attempt to do so reveal something essential about the nature of
inference itself? His answer is characteristically nuanced — the syllogism captures
something genuine about inference, but it cannot exhaust it.

Bradley's treatment of the syllogism spans the entirety of the *Principles*, from
an early passing reference in Book I through sustained analysis in Book II's
discussion of inference, culminating in Book III's consideration of formal and
material [[inference|reasoning]].

## Key Aspects

### The Traditional Doctrine

Bradley begins his examination by acknowledging the syllogism's historical standing. The traditional doctrine holds that all valid [[inference|reasoning]] conforms to the syllogistic
form: [[premise|major premise]], [[premise|minor premise]], and conclusion, connected through a middle term. Bradley notes that this doctrine has been both fiercely defended and fiercely attacked.

Even in his earliest reference to the syllogism, Bradley signals his methodological
orientation:

> …a headache may coexist with a syllogism; but such a coexistence is not a
> connection.
> — (001-008)

Mere co-occurrence of elements does not constitute logical connection. The syllogism
must be judged by whether its elements are genuinely connected, not merely placed
side by side.

### Bradley's Critical Examination

Bradley's examination of the syllogism (Book II, Part I) proceeds through several
stages. He first acknowledges the syllogism's genuine logical force while also
identifying its limitations:

> …in the syllogism itself, if we decline to elide the differences, and if we
> refuse to narrow ourselves to one single category in which the syllogism is
> shut up. A denial as such, we have seen, has a positive side…
> — (012-007)

The syllogism cannot, in Bradley's view, capture every form of valid [[inference|reasoning]].
Some inferences resist r[[immediate-inference|eduction]] to syllogistic form, and attempting to force them
into that mold obscures rather than clarifies the logical relations involved. The
question becomes not whether the syllogism is right or wrong, but what its
limitations reveal about inference itself.

### Negative Reasoning and the Syllogism

A significant test for the syllogism is its capacity to handle negative [[inference|reasoning]]
(Book II, Chapter V). Bradley argues that [[negative-reasoning|negative inference]] presents a genuine
challenge to the traditional syllogistic framework because negation does not fit
neatly into the subject-predicate-copula structure:

> …the two categories which have appeared in the premises. Unless A—B B—C are
> within the same genus, or unless one is a relation of subject and attribute,
> there is no connection of the differences…
> — (012-002)

The resolution Bradley offers is that negation has a positive aspect — every [[negation|denial]]
rests on some positive ground — and it is this positive ground that provides the
connecting tissue required for inference. This insight is central to his broader
theory: inference always requires identity across difference.

### Induction versus Syllogism

Bradley engages extensively with the question of whether induction is fundamentally
distinct from syllogism or merely another form of it (Book II, Part II). He takes a
clear position on the limits of both:

> …if we say that everything is induction which is not syllogism, then certainly
> these inferences must be induction.
> — (015-004)

But this is no concession to inductivists. Bradley argues that the vaunted Methods
of [[induction|Induction]] (Mill's Canons) fail to deliver what they promise — they cannot
generalize beyond the given instances and are not truly "inductive" in the sense
required:

> We have seen that the Methods are not "inductive," since they will not generalize
> beyond the given instance. They fail again of being "inductive," since they can
> not be applied to simple facts.
> — (015-008)

### The Equational Logic Challenge

Bradley devotes an entire chapter (Book II, Chapter IV) to examining Jevons'
[[equational-logic|Equational Logic]] as an alternative to the syllogism. He acknowledges its
achievements but argues it ultimately fails to supplant the syllogism:

> …it is simply the syllogism turned upside down, and it is no less exposed to
> those objections which we have urged against the syllogism. If a logic will not
> deal with certain facts, it is not sufficient to turn these round; for they
> remain obstinately what they were.
> — (016-004)

This assessment is characteristic of Bradley's method: each proposed alternative to
the syllogism turns out to involve the very principles it claims to replace.

### Formal and Material Reasoning

In Book III, Bradley returns to the syllogism in the context of the distinction
between formal and material [[inference|reasoning]]. Here he offers his most balanced assessment:

> It is not merely that the syllogism has broken down, and that it can not be
> accepted as a true account of reasoning. But there is another side to the
> question.
> — (024-003)

He concludes that while the syllogism does not supply "a general type of the
[[inference|reasoning]] act," it may yet serve as "a valid formula for a certain kind of
derivation" (024-006). The syllogism is not false; it is partial.

## Bradley's Assessment

**Syllogism as universal type**: Rejected — cannot cover all valid inference.

**Syllogism as valid formula**: Accepted — genuine for certain derivations.

**Relation to induction**: Neither form subsumes the other; both have limits.

**Equational logic**: Does not escape the syllogistic principles it claims to replace.

**Negative [[inference|reasoning]]**: Can be accommodated if negation's positive side is recognized.

**Formal vs material**: Syllogism is formal; [[material-reasoning|material inference]] goes beyond it.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of the syllogism belongs to a tradition of critical engagement
with Aristotelian logic that runs through [[john-stuart-mill]], [[jevons]], and into
the late nineteenth-century logical reform movement.

Mill had argued that the
syllogism commits a *petitio principii* — the conclusion is already contained in
the [[premise|premises]] — while Jevons sought to replace syllogistic logic with an equational
system.

Bradley positions himself between these extremes: he accepts Mill's
criticism that the syllogism alone cannot account for all [[inference|reasoning]], but he rejects
Jevons' claim that an equational system can do better.

The German logical tradition also informs Bradley's analysis. [[sigwart]]'s *Logik*
and Lotze's logical works are cited throughout Bradley's discussion, and the
influence of Hegel's dialectical treatment of logical forms is evident even when
Bradley differs from Hegel on specific points.
