# Cause and Effect

The relation of causal connection that Bradley treats as a synthesis — a judgment-based construction rather than a mere product of empirical association — belonging to the fundamental categories through which experience is organized (011-004).

## Overview

Bradley addresses cause and effect as a central example of synthetic judgment — the mind's active contribution to the organization of experience. He rejects the empiricist view that causal beliefs result merely from the repetition of conjunctions; instead, he argues that the causal relation involves a judgment that goes beyond what is given in sense experience alone (025-002).

The concept appears at a crucial juncture in Bradley's logical system: the point where logic meets metaphysics. Cause and effect is not merely a logical relation like entailment nor merely an empirical one like constant conjunction; it is a synthesis that the mind imposes on experience, reflecting the active, constructive character of judgment.

Bradley's treatment is distinctive in its emphasis on the role of judgment in constituting causal relations, anticipating later critiques of Humean empiricism while remaining grounded in the analysis of actual inferential practice.

## Key Aspects

### Cause and Effect as Synthesis

Bradley asks whether the synthesis of cause and effect falls entirely under the heading of judgment (011-004). His answer is that it does: causal connection is not a passive registration of regularities but an active synthesis performed by the judging mind.

### Belief and Causation

The belief in cause and effect is not adequately explained by mere repetition of conjunctions (025-002). Bradley argues that repetition alone cannot generate the idea of necessary connection; the judgment of causality involves an intellectual contribution that goes beyond associative learning.

### The Scope of Causal Judgment

Bradley observes that our judgments as to cause and effect never exhaust the whole material (025-006). Causal judgments are always selective: they pick out certain factors as causally relevant while ignoring others. This selectivity reflects the purposive character of judgment rather than a purely passive reception of data.

### Cause and Effect in Reasoning

Bradley draws parallels between causal processes and [[inference|reasoning]] processes (019-001), noting that both involve ordered sequences. However, the nature of the order differs: causal order is governed by natural necessity, while logical order is governed by inferential relations.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of causation engages with Hume's celebrated critique of necessary connection and Mill's analysis of causation as invariable sequence. Bradley's position is closer to Kant's: causation is a category through which the mind organizes experience, not a mere habit of association. His emphasis on the synthetic character of causal judgment places him in the Idealist tradition.
