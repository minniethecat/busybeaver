# Herbert Spencer

Herbert Spencer (1820–1903) was an English philosopher, biologist, and sociologist whose evolutionary philosophy and psychological theory of inference are repeatedly referenced — though never systematically examined — in Bradley's *Principles of Logic*.

## Overview

Herbert Spencer appears in Bradley's *Principles of Logic* as a figure whose prominence demands acknowledgment even where Bradley declines full engagement. Bradley's references span from the early chapters on judgment to the final discussions of cause and effect, yet Bradley consistently defers or declines a systematic treatment of Spencer's views on inference. "I intend to be guilty of what some readers may think an unpardonable omission," Bradley writes, "Mr. Spencer, in his *Psychology*, has developed a view of the nature of inference, which despite its ingenuity, despite its perception of some of those truths which the syllogism has forgotten, I am obliged to consider fundamentally mistaken" (014-002).

Bradley's decision to pass over Spencer's theory is itself a significant critical judgment. He justifies the omission by noting that neither [[john-stuart-mill|J.S. Mill]] nor [[alexander-bain|Alexander Bain]] — both of whom admired Spencer — had examined Spencer's theory of [[inference|reasoning]]: "Both must have been aware that Mr. Spencer has more than once published what appears to be a novel theory of [[inference|reasoning]]. And yet neither (so far as I know) has examined the most peculiar and salient assertions of that theory" (014-003). Bradley thus positions his own silence as following established precedent within British logic.

## Key Ideas

### Theory of Inference

Spencer developed an evolutionary account of inference in his *Principles of Psychology* (1855), arguing that [[inference|reasoning]] is a continuation of the adaptive processes observable in organic life. Bradley, while declining detailed criticism, characterizes Spencer's theory as "so arbitrary and so forced, so far away in the end from the real facts, that I can not believe a discussion of it here would tend to throw any light on the problems of logic" (014-002).

### The Axiom of Reasoning

Spencer proposed that all [[inference|reasoning]] rests on a fundamental axiom: "Things related to the same are related to each other." Bradley notes that this principle "has succeeded in gaining the authority of Wundt" (009-004). Bradley himself is skeptical of this and similar attempts to reduce inference to a single formal principle, arguing that such axioms fail to capture the synthetic movement of actual [[inference|reasoning]].

### Evolutionary Epistemology

Spencer's broader project of explaining logical necessity through inherited psychological associations is part of what Bradley rejects in the empiricist tradition. Bradley's own view — that logical relations are ideal, not psychological — stands in direct opposition to Spencer's attempt to derive logical laws from biological adaptation.

## Bradley's Engagement

Bradley's engagement with Spencer is marked by a studied ambivalence. On the one hand, Bradley acknowledges Spencer's "position in English philosophy" as that of "an established master in the subject" (014-003). He concedes that Spencer's theory shows "perception of some of those truths which the syllogism has forgotten" (014-002). On the other hand, Bradley considers the theory fundamentally mistaken and declines to give it the systematic refutation he devotes to [[john-stuart-mill|J.S. Mill]], Jevons, and the associationists.

In later chapters, Bradley's references to Spencer shift from deferral to pointed criticism. He cites "an achievement of Mr. Spencer, by a proof a priori" as an example of methodological error in [[inference|reasoning]] about cause and effect (025-006), and identifies "a somewhat similar fallacy in Mr. Spencer's Psychology, vol. II. p. 430" where Spencer argues that shorter arguments are more trustworthy than longer ones — a claim Bradley dismisses as resting on an unjustified assumption (026-015).

Early in the work, Bradley also makes a rhetorical point through Spencer: faced with the Hegelian critique of the law of contradiction, Bradley writes "I had rather Mr. Spencer, or some other great authority — whoever may feel himself able to bear them, or unable to understand them — should take them on himself" (005-005), humorously suggesting that Spencer's empiricist commitments make him a better target for Hegelian polemics than Bradley himself.

## Influence

Herbert Spencer was one of the most widely read and influential English-language philosophers of the 19th century, his synthetic philosophy attempting to unify biology, psychology, sociology, and ethics under evolutionary principles. His *Principles of Psychology* (1855) and *Principles of Sociology* (1876–1896) enjoyed enormous popularity. For Bradley, Spencer represented the dominant evolutionary naturalism against which British idealism defined itself. Bradley's decision to pass over rather than refute Spencer may reflect a judgment that Spencer's evolutionary logic was too far removed from Bradley's own idealist framework to be worth detailed engagement — a different strategy from his extensive treatment of Mill, whose logic Bradley saw as a more serious intellectual challenge.
