## Overview

Johann Friedrich Herbart (1776–1841) was a German philosopher, psychologist, and educational theorist who developed a distinctive position in post-Kantian philosophy. In Bradley's *Principles of Logic*, Herbart receives sustained critical attention in Book I, Chapter II ("The Doctrine of Judgment"), where Bradley takes Herbart's treatment of the copula and existential import as a starting point for developing his own account of judgment.

Bradley engages with Herbart not as a figure to be dismissed but as a serious interlocutor whose views must be carefully examined and refuted. "The length and the difficulty of the well-known discussion aroused by Herbart" is acknowledged at the outset, signaling that Herbart's position represents a genuine theoretical challenge. (002-001)

## Key Ideas

### The Copula and Existence

Herbart's central claim, as Bradley presents it, is that the copula in judgment does not assert existence. Bradley characterizes this as the view that the logical form "S is P" means something other than "S exists." Bradley finds Herbart "not so easy" to refute on this point and devotes considerable analysis to unpacking the implications. (002-002)

### Judgment as Ideal Content

Bradley traces a line of influence from Herbart through subsequent logicians, noting that "Ueberweg (Logik, § 56), following Herbart (I. 106), translates the judgment" in a particular way. (002-041) This indicates Herbart's broader influence on the German logical tradition that Bradley is engaging with.

### The Surprising View

Bradley describes "the somewhat surprising view which Herbart has advocated" and directs readers to a later section for detailed treatment. (002-029) The characterization of Herbart's view as "surprising" suggests Bradley finds it counterintuitive yet worth sustained philosophical scrutiny.

## Bradley's Critique

Bradley's engagement with Herbart is dialectical rather than dismissive. He acknowledges that Herbart identifies a genuine problem about the logical form of judgment — whether the copula expresses existence, predication, or something else. However, Bradley argues that Herbart's solution fails to account for the way judgment unifies ideal content with reality.

The reference to Wolff alongside Herbart at (002-002) positions Herbart within a tradition of German logical thought that Bradley is working both with and against. Bradley's ultimate aim is to show that Herbart's analysis, while sophisticated, rests on an inadequate metaphysics of the subject-predicate relation.
