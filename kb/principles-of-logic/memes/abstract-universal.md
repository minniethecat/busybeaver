# Abstract Universal

The abstract universal is a central critical concept in Bradley's *Principles of Logic*. It denotes a judgment whose subject is a universal taken in abstraction from the concrete individual — "A is B," understood as a connection of mere adjectivals without reference to any particular subject.

Bradley argues that the abstract universal is "unreal" because it treats one side or aspect of the whole as if it were an independent existent, whereas in reality it is "nothing but an adjective, an internal distinction within a concrete individual" (006-009).

## Overview

Bradley introduces the abstract universal in the course of his analysis of judgment forms. "The abstract universal, 'A is B,' means no more than a hypothetical judgment. We have, in fact, the application of an ideal content under a supposal" (002-030).

The claim is that what appears to be a categorical assertion — "All triangles are equiangular" — is in truth hypothetical: it asserts that *if* something has the qualities of a triangle, *then* it has the qualities of equiangularity. No assertion is made about any actual existent.

This analysis flows from Bradley's theory of the ultimate subject. Every genuine judgment must ultimately refer to reality — to "the individual which is present in perception" (002-008). The abstract universal, by severing the adjectival content from any individual subject to which it could be attributed, fails to make contact with reality.

"The abstract universal judgment a-b does not ascribe either a or b or their connection to real existence" (002-041). It floats free of the real and so cannot be true in the full sense.

## Key Aspects

### The Abstract Universal as Hypothetical

Bradley's central claim is that "all abstract universals are hypothetical" (002-033). When we say "Mammals are warm-blooded," we do not assert anything about this or that mammal, nor even about the class of mammals taken as a real collection. We assert only a connection of attributes: mammal-ness implies warm-bloodedness.

"In 'Equilateral triangles are equiangular' all I affirm is that with one set of qualities you will have the other set, but I make no assertion about where and when" (002-030). This is the form of a scientific law, and "a law is nothing but a hypothetical judgment" (002-035).

### Unreality of the Abstract Universal

"The abstract universal and abstract particular are both unreal, because neither are names for the individual. They take the two aspects or characters of the whole, and, turning them into independent existences, then assert their reality. But one side of a whole can not stand by itself except in our heads" (006-010). This passage crystallises Bradley's metaphysical critique.

The abstract universal is a product of selective attention — we isolate one character of the concrete individual and treat it as a self-subsistent entity. The resulting judgment can be useful for scientific purposes, but it cannot be true in the strict sense because truth requires reference to the individual real.

### The Concrete Universal

Bradley contrasts the abstract universal with the concrete universal — the individual taken in its full qualitative richness. "The concrete particular and the concrete universal both have reality, and they are the same thing" (006-010). The concrete universal is not a mere abstraction but "the ultimate reality" which "is also absolute" (006-011).

This Hegelian notion — that the real individual is at once particular and universal — underpins Bradley's entire logical theory. The abstract universal is the concrete universal seen one-sidedly, stripped of its thisness.

### Relation to Inference

The abstract universal's hypothetical character has direct consequences for inference. "The proposition 'C is D' is a legitimate inference from the proposition 'A is B'" (002-031) — but only because both are hypotheticals that rest on the same ideal experiment.

Bradley's rejection of the syllogism in Book II turns on this point: the [[premise|major premise]] of a syllogism is an abstract universal, and as such it cannot supply the categorical ground that genuine inference requires.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of the abstract universal draws on Hegel's critique of *Verstand* (abstract understanding) and his doctrine of the concrete universal. Where Hegel developed these [[idea|ideas]] in the service of a positive metaphysical system, Bradley uses them primarily as critical tools to dismantle the presuppositions of formal logic.

His analysis also engages with Mill's empiricist account of the import of propositions — Bradley agrees with Mill that [[universal-judgment|universal proposition]]s are not about classes of individuals, but disagrees profoundly about what they *are* about. For Bradley, the abstract universal is not about sensations or their constant conjunctions but about the ideal content that thought brings to reality.
