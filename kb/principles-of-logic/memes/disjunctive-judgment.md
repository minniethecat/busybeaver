# Disjunctive Judgment

A judgment of the form "A is b or c," where the predicate consists of alternatives between which the judgment itself does not decide. In Bradley's logical system, the [[disjunction|disjunctive judgment]] is a hybrid form — it combines the categorical assertion of a subject with a hypothetical element in the predicate, and its coherence depends on the ground of an exhaustive disjunction in the real.

## Overview

Bradley devotes Book I Chapter IV of the *Principles of Logic* entirely to the [[disjunction|disjunctive judgment]], treating it as one of the fundamental varieties of judgment alongside the categorical and hypothetical forms. His central concern is to show that the [[disjunction|disjunctive judgment]] cannot be reduced to a purely categorical form, nor to a purely hypothetical one. "The [[disjunction|disjunctive judgment]] is not wholly categoric" (004-003) — it asserts categorically that the real is qualified by one of the alternatives, but does not categorically assert which one.

The difficulty of the [[disjunction|disjunctive judgment]] lies in its peculiar mode of reference to reality. "A is b or c" makes a claim about reality, but the claim is indeterminate between b and c. Bradley resolves this by showing that the [[disjunction|disjunctive judgment]] rests on a prior exhaustive division of a whole into mutually exclusive and jointly exhaustive parts. The disjunction is "not got by adding together categorical judgments" (004-006) — rather, it expresses a single judgment about a whole that is internally articulated into alternatives.

## Key Aspects

### The Hybrid Nature of Disjunctive Judgment

Bradley characterizes the [[disjunction|disjunctive judgment]] as containing both a categorical and a hypothetical element. "A is b or c" asserts categorically that A is qualified by the disjunction as a whole, but the internal relation between b and c — that if not b then c — is hypothetical. "The [[disjunction|disjunctive judgment]] is not wholly categoric. Being a compound it has a hypothetical side" (004-003). The judgment "A is b or c" is true or false as a whole; it does not decompose into separate categorical judgments about b and c.

### The Ground in Exhaustive Disjunction

The coherence of a [[disjunction|disjunctive judgment]] depends on the alternatives being genuinely exhaustive. Bradley argues that disjunction "does not rest on [[law-of-excluded-middle|Excluded Middle]]" (004-005) as a mere formal principle, but on a real division of the subject matter. The alternatives must together exhaust the possibilities for the subject. As Bradley notes, the judgment operates by "holding within an exhaustive disjunction" (005-015) — the real itself supplies the ground that the alternatives cover all possibilities, not a merely formal logical law.

### Disjunction and the Principle of Excluded Middle

Bradley treats the relation between [[disjunction|disjunctive judgment]] and the law of excluded middle with care. The principle of excluded middle "supplies the principle of disjunction" (005-007) insofar as it guarantees that any subject must be qualified by one of two contradictory predicates. However, Bradley goes further: "an affirmative existential disjunction" (005-016) is required as a foundation — the real must positively contain the alternatives, not merely be determined by a formal law. The principle is necessary but not sufficient.

### The Priority of the Whole

A distinctive feature of Bradley's analysis is the claim that the disjunctive whole is prior to its parts. The alternatives b and c are not independent predicates subsequently combined; they are the internal articulation of a whole that is given first. "It is merely one instance and one application of the general axiom" (005-007), Bradley writes, that the whole determines the parts. This reflects his broader idealist metaphysics: the real is a unified whole, and [[disjunction|disjunctive judgment]] is one way of representing its internal differentiation.

### Disjunctive Reasoning

Beyond its role in judgment, Bradley explores disjunctive [[inference|reasoning]] in Book II. He examines the classical form — "A is either b or c; it is not b; therefore it is c" — and argues that this mode of [[inference|reasoning]] contains "the soul of disjunctive [[inference|reasoning]]" (018-008) but also awakens philosophical scruples. The inference depends on the alternatives being genuinely exhaustive. "The earliest form of disjunctive [[inference|reasoning]] dispenses with such a basis" (018-007), Bradley observes, but mature logic demands that the ground of the disjunction be established in the real rather than assumed formally.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of [[disjunction|disjunctive judgment]] engages with a well-established topic in traditional logic. The Aristotelian tradition recognized disjunctive propositions but tended to treat them as reducible to combinations of hypotheticals. The Stoics developed a more sophisticated treatment of disjunction within their propositional logic. Bradley's contribution is to show that the [[disjunction|disjunctive judgment]] has an irreducible categorical core — it asserts something about the real as a whole, not merely about relations between propositions. His emphasis on exhaustive disjunction as a ground in the real reflects his broader idealist commitment to the priority of the whole over its parts.
