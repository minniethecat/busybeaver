# Universal

In Bradley's logic, a universal is an ideal content that transcends any particular instance — it is meaning detached from the specific psychical events that carry it, and it forms the essential subject matter of all judgment and inference.

## Overview

Bradley distinguishes sharply between the particular psychical event (an image, a sensation, a spoken word) and the universal meaning it conveys. When I think "mammal," I do not think of any specific mammal but of the universal content that applies to all mammals (001-006). This capacity to use universals is what distinguishes logical thought from mere association.

The universal is "an ideal content which is universal, and which assuredly is not the particular existence that floats before my mind" (001-008). It is meaning abstracted from its psychical vehicle.

## Key Aspects

### Universal vs. Particular

Bradley insists that the universal is not a collection of particulars. It is a fundamental error to take the universal in the form of a collection (001-016). The universal is one meaning that applies to many instances, not the aggregate of those instances themselves.

### Universals in Judgment

All judgment operates with universals — the subject term and predicate term are both universal contents. Even when we judge about a particular, we do so through universal meanings (001-014). The logical form of judgment is inherently universal.

### The Origin of Universals

Bradley traces the origin of universal meaning to the earliest stages of mental life. Even in "the first beginnings of soul-life universals are used" (001-020). The animal that responds to a general type of stimulus is already employing a primitive universal.
