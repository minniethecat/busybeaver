# Modality (Logical)

In Bradley's logical theory, modality affects not the form but the content of judgment. The traditional modal categories — assertoric, apodeictic (necessary), and problematic (possible) — are distinctions within what is asserted, not differences in the mode of assertion itself.

## Overview

Bradley devotes Chapter VIII of Book I ("The Modality of [[judgment|Judgment]]s") to a sustained critique of the traditional doctrine of modality. His central claim is that "modality does not exist in fact" — that is, there is no distinct form of modal judgment separate from the content of what is judged. (007-001)

The traditional view, derived from Kant, treated modality as one of the four fundamental dimensions of judgment (alongside quantity, quality, and relation). Bradley challenges this classification, arguing that the necessary and the possible are both species of hypothetical judgment, while the assertoric is simply judgment without modal qualification. (007-003)

Bradley's treatment of modality connects his theory of judgment to broader metaphysical questions about necessity, possibility, and the nature of reality. The chapter serves as a bridge between the discussion of judgment forms in Book I and the theory of inference in Book II.

## Key Aspects

### Logical Modality Defined

For Bradley, logical modality must be limited to that which affects the content of judgment. It is not a separate dimension added to judgment but a characterization of the relation between subject and predicate within the judgment itself. (007-001)

The three modal categories are interpreted as follows: the assertoric judgment simply asserts its content; the necessary judgment asserts its content under a condition (making it hypothetical); and the possible judgment asserts its content as compatible with certain conditions. The key insight is that necessity and possibility are not distinct judgment *forms* but distinct ways in which content can be asserted.

### Modality Does Not Exist in Fact

Bradley's provocative claim that "modality does not exist in fact" means that there is no real distinction between modal and non-modal judgments at the level of logical form. What appears as a modal difference is always a difference in the content being asserted. (007-004)

He demonstrates this for necessity: when we say "S must be P," we are asserting a hypothetical — "given certain conditions, S is P." The necessity lies in the connection between condition and consequent, not in a special mode of judgment. Similarly, possibility statements are assertions about compatibility rather than a distinct judgment form. (007-003)

### The Basis of Necessity and Possibility

Despite denying that modality exists as a distinct logical form, Bradley acknowledges that there must be a real basis for necessity and possibility. The basis of necessity lies in systematic connection — when elements are connected within a system, the connection can be expressed as a necessary hypothetical. The basis of possibility lies in compatibility with known conditions. (007-004)

However, Bradley insists that "conditions, as such, are not facts" — they are abstractions from the concrete reality of systematic connection. This means that modal distinctions, while they have a real basis, do not correspond to ultimate metaphysical divisions in reality.

### The Problematic and Particular Judgments

Bradley argues that the problematic (possible) judgment is identical to the particular judgment. Both assert compatibility without full determination. This identification is part of his broader project of reducing the traditional table of judgment forms to a smaller set of fundamental logical relations. (007-005)

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of modality reflects the influence of Hegel's critique of Kant's table of judgments. Hegel had argued that Kant's twelve categories were derived empirically from the logical textbooks of his time rather than deduced from the nature of thought itself. Bradley extends this critique to modality, showing that the traditional modal distinctions collapse into the hypothetical form of judgment.

The chapter also engages with Herbart's logical theory and with [[christoph-von-sigwart|Sigwart]]'s *Logik*, reflecting Bradley's engagement with German logical scholarship. His r[[immediate-inference|eduction]] of modality to hypothetical judgment anticipates later developments in modal logic, where necessity and possibility are treated as operators on propositions rather than as distinct judgment types.
