# Synthetic Judgment

A judgment that goes beyond what is immediately given in perception or memory by constructing a connection between elements that are not presented together. In Bradley's logical system, the distinction between analytic and synthetic judgment is fluid — what matters is not a fixed classification but the act of synthesis by which judgment transcends the given.

## Overview

Bradley introduces the analytic-synthetic distinction early in Book I of the *Principles of Logic*, using it to organize the varieties of judgment. Analytic judgments merely "analyse the given" (002-014), working within what is already presented; synthetic judgments "transcend the given" (002-015) by constructing connections beyond what perception or memory alone provide. But Bradley immediately complicates this distinction: "every judgment is essentially synthetical" (005-002), because even the simplest judgment involves an act of construction that goes beyond the passively received data.

The synthetic character of judgment is fundamental to Bradley's epistemology. If judgment merely reported what is given, it could never extend knowledge. "We transcend the given through synthetic judgments" (002-024), and all inference, all [[inference|reasoning]], and all science depend on this capacity. Bradley's treatment of synthetic judgment thus lays the groundwork for his theory of inference in Book II.

## Key Aspects

### Transcending the Given

The defining feature of synthetic judgment is that it "extends the given" (002-006). While an analytic judgment can be verified by inspecting the content already possessed, a synthetic judgment constructs a connection between elements that are not already united in perception. "In synthetic judgments the ultimate subject is still the real, but the real is not given as containing the predicate" (002-015). The judgment supplies the synthesis that perception does not.

### The Fluidity of the Distinction

Bradley refuses to treat "analytic" and "synthetic" as fixed labels for types of judgment. "A judgment is not fixed as 'synthetic' or 'analytic': its character depends on the state of our knowledge" (006-009). What appears synthetic to one observer may be analytic to another who already possesses the relevant connection. The distinction is epistemological — it concerns what the judging mind contributes — rather than metaphysical.

### Synthetic Construction of the Real

Bradley emphasizes that synthetic judgment is not arbitrary construction. It operates under the constraint of identity: "Every judgment is essentially synthetical" (005-002), but the synthesis must be grounded in the real. The "axiom of Identity" governs the synthetic process, ensuring that the constructed connection is not a mere subjective addition but a genuine qualification of reality. The synthesis "constructs synthetically the phenomena" (013-003) by tracing real connections.

### Synthetic Judgment and Inference

The link between synthetic judgment and inference is direct. In Book II, Bradley describes how "we reconstruct our course by a synthetic process" (018-002) in inference — the inferential movement from [[premise|premises]] to conclusion is itself a synthetic act. "Without synthetic judgments which assume an identity" (019-001), inference would be impossible. The whole edifice of logical [[inference|reasoning]] rests on the capacity of judgment to synthesize.

### The Analytic-Synthetic Continuum

Bradley treats analytic and synthetic not as two exclusive classes but as poles of a continuum. "The analytic judgment becomes synthetic" (002-027) when we extend it beyond the immediately given. Even a seemingly pure analytic judgment — "this rose is red" — involves spatial and temporal synthesis if the qualities are not given together in a single act of perception. The continuum reflects the fact that all judgment involves both analysis of the given and synthesis beyond it.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of the analytic-synthetic distinction predates but resonates with later philosophical developments. Kant had made the distinction central to his critical philosophy, treating synthetic a priori judgments as the foundation of mathematics and metaphysics. Bradley acknowledges the Kantian background but gives the distinction a distinctively idealist inflection: for Bradley, the synthetic character of judgment reflects the nature of reality as a unified whole that thought progressively articulates. His fluid, epistemological treatment of the distinction contrasts with the rigid, metaphysical treatment it would later receive in logical positivism.
