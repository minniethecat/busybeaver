Consciousness occupies an important but often implicit role in Bradley's *Principles of Logic*. While Bradley's primary concern is with the logical structure of judgment and inference, he frequently appeals to the nature of consciousness to explain how logical operations are possible and why they take the form they do.

## Consciousness and Judgment

For Bradley, judgment is an act of consciousness. The consciousness that what my act joins is joined in the real world is essential to the nature of judgment. (001-009) Judgment is not a blind mechanism but a conscious act in which an ideal content is referred to reality.

Bradley emphasizes that judgment must be brought to consciousness — it is not enough that the elements of judgment are merely associated in the mind. The logical connection must be explicitly recognized. (001-004) This insistence on conscious awareness distinguishes logical judgment from mere psychical association.

## Self-Consciousness and the Opposition of Idea and Reality

Bradley traces the origin of judgment to the evolution of self-consciousness. In the evolution of self-consciousness, the opposition of idea and reality first emerges, and this opposition is the ground of judgment. (001-016) Before self-consciousness, there is no distinction between what is thought and what is real, and therefore no possibility of truth or falsehood.

Self-consciousness introduces a reflective distance between the thinking subject and the object of thought. This distance makes it possible to ask whether an idea corresponds to reality — which is precisely the question that judgment answers.

## Consciousness in Inference and Association

Bradley discusses consciousness in connection with the psychology of inference and the association of ideas. A complex state of consciousness enters into the connections that constitute inference. (013-013) One idea or perception tends to call into consciousness another like itself, and this mechanism underlies the psychological process of reasoning. (013-014)

However, Bradley is careful to distinguish the psychological mechanism of association from the logical validity of inference. The mere fact that one idea calls another into consciousness does not establish a logical connection between them. Consciousness provides the material for inference, but logic provides the norm.

## The Limits of Consciousness

Bradley recognizes that much of mental life occurs outside of explicit consciousness. Aspects of one feeling may remain below the threshold of conscious awareness. (023-002) Not every mental process is represented in consciousness, and logical operations may depend on connections that are felt rather than explicitly thought. (023-003)

The question of self-consciousness in animals — whether dogs have self-consciousness or the power of abstraction — illustrates the graded nature of conscious awareness. (023-007) Consciousness is not an all-or-nothing property but admits of degrees.

## Consciousness and the Absolute

In Bradley's metaphysics, consciousness is ultimately transcended in the Absolute. Finite consciousness involves a subject-object distinction that cannot be ultimate. The Absolute is not a consciousness standing over against an object, but a unity in which the distinction between consciousness and its object is overcome.
