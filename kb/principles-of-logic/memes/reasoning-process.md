# Reasoning Process

The general nature of inferential thought — the activity by which the mind moves from [[premise|premises]] to conclusions, whose structure Bradley subjects to critical examination throughout *The Principles of Logic* (017-002).

## Overview

Bradley devotes sustained attention to the [[inference|reasoning]] process, treating it not as a settled matter but as a phenomenon whose general nature has yet to be ascertained (018-014). He is skeptical of accounts that oversimplify [[inference|reasoning]], rejecting the view that [[inference|reasoning]] is always the subsumption of a particular under a universal rule. For Bradley, [[inference|reasoning]] is more varied and context-dependent than formal logic typically acknowledges.

The [[inference|reasoning]] process occupies a central place in Bradley's system because it connects judgment, the basic unit of logical analysis, with inference, the extension of knowledge beyond what is immediately given. Understanding how [[inference|reasoning]] actually operates — as opposed to how it is conventionally described — is essential to his broader project of grounding logic in the actual practice of thought.

## Key Aspects

### Reasoning and Fiction

Bradley sharply criticizes what he calls the "fictitious account" of the [[inference|reasoning]] process — the idea that [[inference|reasoning]] can be reduced to a mechanical procedure of applying general rules to particular cases (013-018). This fiction altogether misrepresents how [[inference|reasoning]] actually works.

### The General Nature of Reasoning

A key question for Bradley is whether the [[inference|reasoning]] process has a single general nature that can be specified in advance (017-002). He treats this as an open question requiring investigation, not a presupposition to be assumed.

### Reasoning and Subsumption

Bradley rejects the view that [[inference|reasoning]] is always subsumption — the placing of a particular under a universal (006-004). While subsumption may characterize some forms of [[inference|reasoning]], it fails to capture the full range of inferential activity.

### Causal and Reasoning Processes

Bradley draws attention to the parallels and differences between causal processes and [[inference|reasoning]] processes (019-001). Both involve ordered sequences, but the nature of the order differs: causal sequences are governed by natural necessity, while [[inference|reasoning]] sequences are governed by logical relations.

### Validity of Reasoning

The question of [[inference|reasoning]]'s validity — whether our inferential processes genuinely lead to truth — is distinguished from the question of their soundness (026-003). Bradley is concerned with the real validity of [[inference|reasoning]] as a guide to knowledge.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of the [[inference|reasoning]] process engages with both the Aristotelian syllogistic tradition, which treats [[inference|reasoning]] as the application of universal rules, and Mill's empiricist account, which emphasizes inductive generalization. Bradley rejects both as incomplete, arguing that neither captures the full complexity of how [[inference|reasoning]] actually operates.
