Phenomenon, in Bradley's logical system, denotes an appearance or psychical event as it is given in experience — the sensible or mental datum that stands in need of reference to reality. Bradley's use of the term engages critically with both the empiricist appeal to phenomena as the basis of knowledge and the Kantian distinction between phenomena and noumena.

## Overview

Bradley discusses phenomena primarily in Book I, where he examines the nature of judgment and the distinction between appearance and reality. A phenomenon, taken by itself "as an actual phenomenon" (001-002), does not yet constitute knowledge; it is only through judgment that a phenomenon is referred to something beyond itself. The phenomenal realm is what appears, but what appears must be judged as real or unreal through the logical act of judgment.

The concept of the "phenomenal series" is central to Bradley's analysis. He asks whether we can "confine ourselves to judgments about the phenomenal series" (002-034), and his answer is that we cannot. The attempt to restrict knowledge to phenomena — as the empiricist and positivist traditions attempted — is self-defeating, because the very notion of a "mere phenomenon" presupposes a reality against which it is measured.

## Key Aspects

### Phenomenon as Appearance

For Bradley, a phenomenon is not opposed to reality in a simple way. The "particular phenomenon, the momentary appearance" (002-008) is real as a psychical fact, but its meaning — what it reveals about reality — is not exhausted by its existence as a mental event. A phenomenon "that is past or future" (002-024) cannot be reduced to present perception, yet we speak of it meaningfully, indicating that thought transcends the phenomenal given.

### The Phenomenal Series

Bradley investigates whether judgment can be confined to the phenomenal series — the interconnected flow of appearances in time and space. He argues that "every phenomenon will exist in more times or spaces than one" (002-003), meaning that phenomena are not isolated atoms but are bound together in a continuous series. However, the phenomenal series is not self-sufficient; it points beyond itself to a reality that is not merely phenomenal.

### Beyond the Phenomenal

Bradley's critique of phenomenalism is that it cannot account for the universality and necessity that judgment requires. If knowledge were confined to phenomena, then "there is no phenomenon but what I here and now perceive" (002-025), which would make science and common sense alike impossible. The logical structure of judgment — with its reference to a reality beyond the given — shows that the phenomenal is not the final word.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of phenomenon draws on the Kantian distinction between phenomena (appearances) and noumena (things-in-themselves), but he rejects Kant's dualism. For Bradley, the idea that there is an unknowable reality behind phenomena is incoherent; reality is not behind appearances but is that which appearances, properly judged, reveal. His position also critiques the empiricist claim, associated with Mill, that all knowledge reduces to relations among phenomena.
