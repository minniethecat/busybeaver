## Overview

Christian Wolff (1679–1754) was a German rationalist philosopher and mathematician who systematized Leibnizian philosophy and exerted enormous influence on eighteenth-century German thought. In Bradley's *Principles of Logic*, Wolff appears as a representative of the pre-Kantian logical tradition whose formulations Bradley draws upon and critiques at several key junctures.

Wolff is referenced across four different chapters of Bradley's work (II, VII, XIII, XIX), indicating that his logical doctrines touch on multiple aspects of Bradley's project — from the nature of judgment to the laws of association and the principles of reasoning. ## Key Ideas

### Hypothetical Judgments

Bradley notes that Wolff formulated a principle concerning hypothetical judgments, writing that "the two hypothetical judgments Wolff has expressed the principle." (007-010) This places Wolff in the tradition of logicians who attempted to systematize the forms of judgment beyond simple categorical assertions.

### Principles of Association

In the context of discussing the laws of association, Bradley references "the formula we found laid down by Wolff and others." (013-004) Bradley critiques this Wolffian formula, arguing that the ground of association cannot be reduced to a simple mechanical principle.

### Reproduction and Reasoning

Bradley engages with Wolff's account of logical reproduction, criticizing "the formula we found laid down by Wolff, on the ground that reproduction..." (019-005) This connects Wolff's logical doctrines to Bradley's broader concerns about the nature of inference and the movement of thought.

## Bradley's Critique

Bradley places Wolff within a tradition of logical formalism that he subjects to sustained criticism. At (002-002), Bradley references Wolff alongside Herbart in the context of the debate about the copula and existential import, noting that a particular authority "refers here to Wolff, by whom, in this point he has..." — indicating Wolff's role in shaping the terms of this foundational logical debate.

Bradley's scattered references to Wolff collectively suggest a figure whose formulations were influential enough to require engagement, but whose underlying assumptions Bradley ultimately rejects as part of the broader critique of formal logic that runs through *The Principles of Logic*.
