# Difference

In Bradley's logical system, difference is the fundamental relation of non-identity that makes judgment possible — without the distinction between subject and predicate, between truth and falsehood, no assertion could be made.

## Overview

Difference functions in Bradley's system as both a logical and metaphysical category. On the logical side, the very act of judgment presupposes a difference between what is asserted and what is denied (001-012). On the metaphysical side, difference stands in tension with identity: reality is understood as an identity that persists through and under differences (001-017).

Bradley treats difference not as an absolute separation but as a relation that implies an underlying unity. Two things can only differ against a common background — pure difference without any identity would be unintelligible. The marks, relations, and differences that constitute particular contents only have meaning within a system where identity and difference are mutually implicated. (001-020)

## Key Aspects

### Difference in Judgment

The logical operation of judgment depends on the difference between truth and falsehood. When we judge, we distinguish a content from its denial, and this distinction is the ground of assertion itself (001-012). Without the capacity to mark difference, neither affirmation nor negation would be possible. The difference of the situation, as it actually exists, is what makes the truth of a judgment a real matter rather than an empty formality. (001-018)

### Identity and Difference

Bradley holds that identity persists under difference — reality is not a mere collection of discrete items but a continuous whole in which differences are internal modifications (001-017). The concept of a thing includes both what makes it the same and what makes it different from others. Despite their difference in meaning, subject and predicate are united in the act of judgment. (001-016)

### Difference as Differentia

In the context of ideal content, Bradley analyzes how a concept's differentia — the specific differences that mark it off from other contents — constitutes its logical character (001-008). These differences are what allow classification and inference. The differentia is found in what differences the content, as asserted, from the same content not so asserted. (001-008)

### Numerical and Qualitative Difference

Bradley distinguishes between numerical difference — where two things are distinct simply by being two — and qualitative difference — where they differ in their characteristics. When there is no difference whatever between two concepts, we can pass from one to the other without altering the content of judgment, which shows that logical difference is tied to content, not to mere numerical distinctness. (001-014)
