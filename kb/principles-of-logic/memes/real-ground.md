# Real Ground

The actual basis in reality from which necessary truth follows — for Bradley, all necessity affirms a real ground, and this concept bridges the gap between logical form and metaphysical substance (007-005).

## Overview

The concept of real ground is central to Bradley's account of necessity and inference. When a conclusion follows necessarily from [[premise|premises]], what makes this following possible is not merely the formal relation between propositions but a real ground — something in the nature of things that supports the inferential movement.

Bradley introduces the concept in his analysis of necessary truth, arguing that every necessary truth points beyond itself to the ground from which it follows. This ground is "real" in the sense that it belongs to the structure of reality, not merely to the conventions of logical notation.

The concept appears at several crucial points in Bradley's system: in the analysis of inference (where real ground explains why some inferences are valid), in the discussion of induction (where the Method of Agreement requires a real ground), and in the treatment of reality (where real ground connects thought to what exists).

## Key Aspects

### Real Ground and Necessity

Bradley's core claim is that all necessity affirms a real ground, explicit or implicit (007-005). This means that logical necessity is not a free-floating formal property; it is anchored in the nature of the subject matter. Without a real ground, there is no genuine necessity.

### Real Ground in Induction

Bradley identifies the real ground of the Method of Agreement as a key example of how real ground operates in scientific [[inference|reasoning]] (015-006). The Method of Agreement — a cornerstone of Mill's inductive logic — works only because there is a real connection between the phenomena under investigation.

### Real Ground and Existence

In his later discussion, Bradley argues that the real ground we possess for the existence of something must turn explicit for [[inference|reasoning]] to be complete (026-010). This connects the logical concept of real ground to the metaphysical question of what grounds existence itself.

## Historical Context

Bradley's concept of real ground draws on the Leibnizian principle of sufficient reason — the idea that everything must have a reason or ground — while adapting it to the context of logical analysis. His treatment also engages with Kant's distinction between logical ground (ratio cognoscendi) and real ground (ratio essendi), though Bradley's version is integrated into his own Idealist framework.
