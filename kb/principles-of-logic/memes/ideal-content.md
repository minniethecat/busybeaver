# Ideal Content

The meaning or signification that an idea carries — what the idea "means" as distinct from its existence as a psychical event. In Bradley's logic, ideal content is the material of judgment: every judgment asserts an ideal content of reality, and the entire structure of inference depends on the relations among ideal contents.

## Overview

Bradley introduces ideal content as the logical dimension of an idea, distinguished from its psychological existence. "The idea in judgment is the act which refers an ideal content (recognized as such) to a reality beyond the act" (001-006). An idea exists as a psychical event in my mind, but what it means — its content — is not a psychical event. This distinction between existence and content is foundational for Bradley's theory of judgment.

The crucial feature of ideal content is that it is "also fact" (001-019). The meaning of an idea is not a subjective creation but something objective that can be referred to reality. "The ideal content which is also fact" means that meanings belong to the world, not merely to the mind. This objectivity of ideal content is what makes judgment and inference possible — if meanings were merely subjective, no assertion could be true or false.

## Key Aspects

### Ideal Content as the Material of Judgment

For Bradley, "judgment is the act which refers an ideal content (recognized as such) to a reality beyond the act" (001-006). The ideal content is what the judgment is about — it is the "adjective" that judgment ascribes to the real. Without ideal content, there is nothing to judge.

### Ideal Content and Reality

The relation of ideal content to reality admits of degrees. Bradley notes that "the relation of the ideal content to reality must be their or no judgment at all" (001-012). This means that ideal content must genuinely qualify reality — not merely be associated with it psychologically. The failure of an ideal content to qualify the real is what constitutes falsehood.

### Ideal Content Distinguished from Psychical Fact

An idea has both psychical existence (as an event in consciousness) and ideal content (what it means). Bradley insists these must not be confused: "the ideal content, the adjective divorced, is more than a psychical fact" (001-016). The content survives the passing of the psychical event and can enter into logical relations with other contents.

### The Genuine Substantive

Bradley argues that "the genuine substantive of the ideal content" (002-008) is reality itself. Ideal contents are not free-floating entities but determinations of the real. This thesis shapes Bradley's entire metaphysics of logic — meanings are not a separate realm but qualifications of the single reality that judgment always presupposes.

## Historical Context

Bradley's concept of ideal content develops themes from German Idealism, particularly Hegel's notion of objective thought. However, Bradley gives the concept a distinctive epistemological turn by grounding it in the act of judgment rather than in a metaphysical system. The distinction between psychical existence and ideal content also engages with Fregean and Husserlian anti-psychologism, though Bradley's idiom and metaphysical commitments differ markedly from both.
