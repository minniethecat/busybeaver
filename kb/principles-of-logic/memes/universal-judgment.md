# Universal Judgment

The judgment that asserts a predicate universally of a subject, as in "all men are mortal." In Bradley's analysis, such judgments are not about real existence but are hypothetical in character — they assert a relation between ideal contents, not a fact about what actually exists.

## Overview

Bradley's treatment of universal judgment is central to his critique of formal logic. He argues that "every [[abstract-universal|abstract universal judgment]] is but hypothetical" (002-034). When we say "all A is B," we do not assert that any A actually exists — we assert only that if something is A, then it is B. The universal judgment is thus a relation between ideal contents, not a claim about reality.

This analysis has far-reaching consequences. It means that universal judgments cannot serve as [[premise|premises]] in syllogism in the way traditional logic assumes — the conclusion of a syllogism with universal [[premise|premises]] inherits their hypothetical character. "Such universal judgments are all hypothetical, and will be found to depend on an ideal construction" (002-031).

## Key Aspects

### Universal and Particular Judgments

Bradley contrasts universal judgments with particular judgments. "The [[abstract-universal|abstract universal judgment]] a-b does not ascribe either a or b to real existence" (002-041). A particular judgment, by contrast, does assert real existence — it says that some A actually is B. This distinction is fundamental: universal judgments are about conceptual relations; particular judgments are about what exists.

### The Hypothetical Character of Universal Judgments

The hypothetical nature of universal judgments follows from abstraction. When we abstract from concrete particulars to form the concept "man," we lose the connection to real existence. The resulting judgment "all men are mortal" asserts only that mortality accompanies humanity in thought — it does not assert that any man actually exists. This is why "every [[abstract-universal|abstract universal judgment]] was but hypothetical" (002-034).

### Universal Judgment and the Common Type

Bradley identifies a "common type of universal judgment" (002-029) that has misled logicians. This type appears to be categorical — "A is B" — but on analysis reveals itself as hypothetical. The point is that no genuinely categorical universal judgment is possible, because universality requires abstraction from concrete existence.

### The Scope of Universal Judgment

Bradley questions whether we have "any universal judgments left us" (002-030) once we recognize that [[abstract-universal|abstract universal judgment]]s are hypothetical. His answer is that genuine universal judgments exist, but they are not abstract — they depend on the concrete universal, which contains its differences within itself rather than abstracting from them.

## Historical Context

Bradley's thesis that universal judgments are hypothetical echoes themes in Leibniz and Kant, but his argument is more radical than either. Where Kant saw universal judgments as synthetic a priori, Bradley sees them as ideal constructions that do not directly assert reality. This position influenced later British Idealists and, through them, the development of alternative logics in the early twentieth century.
