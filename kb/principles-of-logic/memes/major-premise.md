# Major Premise

In Bradley's *Principles of Logic*, the [[premise|major premise]] of the syllogism is the principal target of his critique of formal inference. Far from being the secure foundation of deductive [[inference|reasoning]], the [[premise|major premise]] is "a delusion" (009-002), "a chimera" whose apparent necessity conceals a profound confusion about the nature of judgment and inference.

Bradley devotes Chapter II of Book II to "the removal of certain mistaken [[idea|ideas]]. And the first to go must be the [[premise|major premise]]" (009-001).

## Overview

Bradley's attack on the [[premise|major premise]] is the centrepiece of his broader rejection of the syllogism as a model of inference. The standard syllogism — "All men are mortal; Socrates is a man; therefore Socrates is mortal" — presents the [[premise|major premise]] as an independent universal truth from which, together with the [[premise|minor premise]], the conclusion follows.

Bradley denies that the [[premise|major premise]] has any such independent status. What we call the [[premise|major premise]] is really "a superstition" (012-011): it is a formula we construct *after* the fact of inference, abstracting from the concrete connections of attributes that actually drive our [[inference|reasoning]].

"The [[premise|major premise]], we have seen, is a delusion, begotten by an old metaphysical blunder, nourished by a senseless choice of examples, fostered by the stupid consent of logicians, and protected by the impotence of younger rivals. Really dead long since, it has enjoyed a sham security" (009-002). This characteristically vivid dismissal sets the tone for Bradley's critique.

## Key Aspects

### The Major Premise as Afterthought

Bradley argues that in actual [[inference|reasoning]] we never start from a [[premise|major premise]]. We begin with a concrete connection of attributes discovered in experience, and we extend that connection to new cases. The [[premise|major premise]] is formulated only retrospectively, as a way of codifying what we have already done.

"Throw the [[premise|major premise]] overboard, and call anything an inference that really advances from known to unknown" (009-004). The syllogistic form disguises the genuine movement of thought by presenting the conclusion as already contained in the [[premise|premises]].

### The Manufacture of Major Premises

If we insist on the syllogistic form, Bradley warns, we shall find ourselves "manufacturing countless [[premise|major premise]]s" (011-005) for every inference we make. Each specific inference will require its own tailor-made universal, and "the second alternative is impossible" (011-007) — we cannot pre-enumerate all the universals that our [[inference|reasoning]] might require.

The [[premise|major premise]] is thus not a genuine logical resource but a post-hoc rationalisation.

### The Superstition of the Major Premise

Bradley's most striking formulation comes in the context of his analysis of negative [[inference|reasoning]]: "though the [[premise|major premise]] is a superstition, one premise is necessary" (012-011). This nuanced verdict is important. Bradley is not denying that inference requires a ground — an ideal connection that licenses the movement from one judgment to another.

What he denies is that this ground takes the form of a universal categorical proposition standing above and before the particular inference. The real ground of inference is always concrete and contextual: "it proceeds from and rests upon a distinguished connection of attributes" (009-003).

### Individual and Universal

The deeper issue, for Bradley, is the relation between individual and universal. The syllogism presents the individual (Socrates) as subsumed under a universal (mortality), but this subsumption presupposes precisely what it claims to prove — that the connection of attributes holds for this individual.

"If you say that each individual has been known to die, the conclusion here does not rest on enumeration complete or otherwise; it proceeds from and rests upon a distinguished connection of attributes" (009-003). The movement of inference is always from the concrete to the concrete, mediated by ideal content; the [[premise|major premise]] is an abstraction from this movement that we mistake for its ground.

## Historical Context

Bradley's critique of the [[premise|major premise]] belongs to a nineteenth-century reaction against Aristotelian syllogistic that also includes Mill's *[[system|System]] of Logic* (1843). Where Mill sought to replace formal logic with inductive methodology, Bradley seeks to reground logic in a theory of judgment and inference as movements of ideal content.

His critique is more radical than Mill's: it is not just that the syllogism is circular (the petitio principii charge) but that its very form — universal premise, particular premise, conclusion — misrepresents the nature of thought.

Bradley's idealist logic, with its insistence on the primacy of the concrete individual and the derivative character of abstractions, draws on Hegel's critique of formal logic but gives it a distinctively epistemological cast.
