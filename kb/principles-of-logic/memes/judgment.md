# Judgment

The act of referring an ideal content (recognized as such) to a reality beyond the act. In F.H. Bradley's *Principles of Logic*, judgment is the foundational logical operation that unites thought with reality, distinct from mere association or practical belief.

## Overview

Judgment occupies the centre of Bradley's logical system. Book I of the *Principles of Logic* is devoted entirely to its nature, forms, and varieties. Bradley defines judgment proper as "the act which refers an ideal content (recognized as such) to a reality beyond the act" (001-010). This definition separates judgment from both the psychological association of [[idea|ideas]] and the merely practical attitude of belief.

For Bradley, judgment requires [[idea|ideas]] understood as logical meanings — universal contents abstracted from their particular psychical occurrences. "Without [[idea|ideas]] no judgment" (001-005), he insists, but these [[idea|ideas]] are not mental images; they are universal meanings used as symbols. The [[idea|logical idea]] "is the universal meaning; it is not ever the occasional imagery" (001-009).

Bradley's treatment of judgment is polemical: he systematically refutes competing theories before presenting his own. He rejects the view that judgment is the association of [[idea|ideas]] (001-013), the equation of subject and predicate (001-016), or a mere practical attitude (001-010). Each error, he argues, contains a partial truth that his own account preserves while avoiding the error.

## Key Aspects

### Judgment and Ideas

Bradley distinguishes between the psycho[[idea|logical idea]] (a particular mental event) and the [[idea|logical idea]] (a universal meaning). "For logical purposes [[idea|ideas]] are symbols, and they are nothing but symbols" (001-004). Every sign has three sides: existence, content, and signification. In judgment, it is the signification that matters — the idea is used to qualify reality, not as a fact in itself.

### The Reference to Reality

What distinguishes judgment from mere ideation is the reference to a reality beyond the act. When I judge "The sea-serpent exists," I "qualify the real world by the adjective of the sea-serpent" and recognize that "apart from our act, it is so qualified" (001-010). Judgment thus involves a claim about how things are, independent of the judging mind.

### Judgment Is Not Association

Bradley devotes considerable space to refuting the associationist account. "You first assume that in judgment we have a relation between two [[idea|ideas]], and then go on to show that by the relation we mean a connection of the corresponding facts" (001-014). But [[idea|ideas]] as particular psychical events cannot be true or false — only their meaning can. The association of mental phenomena "is mere reality, which is, but does not stand for anything" (001-014).

### Universal and Singular Judgments

Every judgment, Bradley argues, involves a universal element. Even singular judgments ("This is a quadruped") apply a universal predicate to a particular subject. "Every judgment affirms either the identity which persists under differences, or else the difference which is predicated despite an identity" (001-017). This universality is what makes judgment possible at all.

## Historical Context

Bradley's theory of judgment develops themes from German Idealism, particularly Hegel, while departing from Hegel's main principle (as Bradley himself notes in the Preface). His emphasis on the "reality beyond the act" situates him within the British idealist tradition, opposing the empiricist psychology of Mill and the [[association-of-ideas|associationism]] of the British school. Bradley's account influenced later idealist treatments of judgment by Bosanquet and others, and his critique of psychologism in logic anticipated themes later developed by Frege and Husserl.
