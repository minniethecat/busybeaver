# Assertoric Judgment

An assertoric judgment is a modal form that asserts a subject-predicate connection as actually obtaining — "S is P" — claiming truth without claiming necessity. In Bradley's analysis, the assertoric judgment reveals itself as the fundamental form of judgment, from which the other modal forms derive by the addition or subtraction of inferential explicitness.

## Overview

The assertoric judgment occupies the middle position in Kant's modal scheme between the merely possible and the necessary. It is the judgment that simply asserts — making a truth claim without modal qualification. Bradley's treatment of the assertoric judgment is central to his critique of modal logic because he argues that all judgments are fundamentally assertoric in character. (007-001)

Bradley makes the striking claim that apodictic modality does not actually strengthen an assertion; rather, "its strength comes from a concealed assertorical." (007-003) The necessary judgment borrows its force from an unstated assertoric component. This inversion of the traditional hierarchy — where necessity was the highest mode — is characteristic of Bradley's approach: the plain assertion of fact is logically prior to modal qualification.

The relationship between assertoric and hypothetical judgment is a recurring theme. Bradley notes that "if, while we keep its assertorical character, we try to read the judgment as hypothetical, we encounter a contradiction." (018-010) The assertoric character of judgment resists r[[immediate-inference|eduction]] to conditional form, pointing to the irreducibly actual character of logical assertion.

## Key Aspects

### Assertoric as the Primary Form

Bradley treats the assertoric judgment as logically primary. The other modal forms — problematic and apodictic — are derivative, obtained by weakening or strengthening the assertoric basis. "All these are simple assertorical statements about my condition," (007-001) Bradley observes, suggesting that even modally qualified judgments reduce to assertoric claims about the state of our knowledge.

### The Concealed Assertoric

A distinctive claim in Bradley's analysis is that apparent modal strength conceals an assertoric core. When we say "S must be P," we are actually making two assertoric claims: that S is P, and that this follows from accepted [[premise|premises]]. The "must" adds nothing to the truth of S-P; it merely reports on the logical situation. (007-003) This demystification of necessity is a key result of Bradley's analysis.

### Assertoric and Hypothetical

Bradley examines the relationship between assertoric and hypothetical judgment in detail. An assertoric judgment is not equivalent to a hypothetical whose condition is fulfilled — the assertoric has a directness that the hypothetical [[privation|lack]]s. "If we keep its assertorical character, we try to read the judgment as hypothetical" (018-010) and find that they are different logical acts altogether.

### Assertoric Conclusion and Inference

When an inference concludes, its conclusion is assertoric in form — it asserts that something is the case. Bradley notes that a conclusion may not be assertoric "but is problematic" (018-011) when the [[premise|premises]] themselves are uncertain. The modality of the conclusion reflects the modality of the [[premise|premises]] and the strength of the inferential connection, not an independent modal operator.

## Historical Context

The assertoric judgment, as the middle term in Kant's modal triad, has traditionally received less philosophical attention than the problematic and apodictic, perhaps because it appears to be the unmarked, default case. Bradley's innovation is to reverse this priority, treating the assertoric as fundamental and the other modes as derivative.

This reversal aligns with Bradley's broader idealist commitment to the primacy of actual judgment over formal schematism. In his view, logic begins not with a table of categories but with the concrete act of assertion — the living judgment that something is the case. The modal distinctions are subsequent abstractions from this primary logical act.
