# Hypothetical Judgment

The hypothetical judgment is the form of judgment that asserts a consequential relation: "if something is, then something else is." For Bradley, this form is not merely one type of judgment among others but the fundamental logical form that underlies all genuine assertion and inference.

## Overview

Bradley's analysis of hypothetical judgment occupies a pivotal position in Book I of *The Principles of Logic*. Where traditional logic treated the categorical proposition as fundamental and the hypothetical as derivative, Bradley reverses this priority. The hypothetical judgment, he argues, reveals the true nature of logical connection more adequately than the categorical form. (002-004)

A hypothetical judgment does not assert the existence of its components. When we say "if A is B, then C is D," we do not assert that A is B nor that C is D. What we assert is the connection between them — a relation of consequence that holds regardless of whether the antecedent is true. This feature makes the hypothetical form uniquely suited to express logical laws and scientific generalizations. (002-035)

Bradley's treatment of hypothetical judgment connects his logical theory to his metaphysics of the Absolute. The hypothetical form reflects the nature of reality as a system of interconnected elements, where each element is what it is only through its relations to others.

## Key Aspects

### The Nature of Hypothetical Assertion

The crucial feature of hypothetical judgment, for Bradley, is that it asserts a connection without asserting the existence of the connected terms. "The fact that is affirmed as existing is not the antecedent or the consequent, but their connection." (002-032) This distinguishes the hypothetical from the categorical, which appears to assert the existence of its subject.

Bradley emphasizes that hypothetical judgments pervade ordinary thought and speech. Every scientific law, every practical maxim, and every conditional statement expresses a hypothetical judgment. The form is not a specialized logical device but the most common and fundamental way in which thought connects its contents.

### Hypothetical and Categorical

Bradley argues against the traditional view that hypothetical judgments can be reduced to categorical ones. The r[[immediate-inference|eduction]] fails because the hypothetical connection — the relation of consequence — cannot be expressed as a simple predication. When we say "if it rains, the ground will be wet," we are not predicating wetness of rain but asserting a relation between two states of affairs. (002-030)

However, Bradley also argues that categorical judgments, when analyzed, reveal hidden hypothetical structure. The connection between subject and predicate in "S is P" depends on conditions and grounds that the categorical form suppresses. In this sense, all judgments are ultimately hypothetical in their logical structure. (004-002)

### Hypothetical Judgment and Inference

Bradley identifies a deep connection between hypothetical judgment and inference. A law, he says, "is nothing but a hypothetical judgment." Scientific and logical laws assert consequential relations that hold across instances, and this is precisely what the hypothetical form expresses. (002-035)

This connection is crucial for Bradley's theory of inference in Book II. [[inference|Inference]] proceeds by establishing hypothetical connections and then, when the conditions are satisfied, drawing the consequence. The hypothetical judgment provides the logical form within which inference operates. Without hypothetical connections, there could be no inference — only the direct assertion of categorical facts.

### The Hypothetical Basis of Necessity

Bradley shows that necessity is fundamentally hypothetical. When we say "S must be P," we are asserting a hypothetical: given the nature of S and the conditions that obtain, S is P. The necessity lies in the connection, not in the terms connected. This insight allows Bradley to reduce modal distinctions to the hypothetical form, unifying his treatment of judgment. (007-003)

### Varieties of Hypothetical Judgment

Bradley acknowledges that hypothetical judgments come in many degrees and varieties. The connection may be logical (as in deductive inference), causal (as in scientific laws), or practical (as in conditional advice). The common element is the assertion of a consequential relation that does not depend on the actual existence of the terms. (007-004)

He also distinguishes between hypothetical judgments where the connection is known with certainty and those where it is merely probable. In all cases, however, the logical form is the same — what varies is the strength and nature of the connection asserted.

## Historical Context

Bradley's elevation of the hypothetical judgment over the categorical reflects the influence of Hegel's logic, which treats judgment as the positing of a relation rather than the attribution of a predicate to a subject. It also engages with the British empiricist tradition, which had struggled to account for the logical status of laws and general statements.

Bradley's position anticipates aspects of twentieth-century logic. The modern propositional calculus treats the conditional ("if... then...") as a fundamental logical connective, and the analysis of scientific laws as conditional statements became standard in philosophy of science. Bradley's deeper claim — that all judgment is ultimately hypothetical — goes beyond these technical developments and reflects his idealist metaphysics of internal relations.
