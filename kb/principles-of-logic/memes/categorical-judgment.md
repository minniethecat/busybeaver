# Categorical Judgment

The categorical judgment is the form of judgment that makes a direct, unconditional assertion about reality — typically of the form "S is P." Bradley's analysis of this apparently simple form reveals deep difficulties, ultimately showing that no judgment can be purely categorical.

## Overview

Bradley's examination of the categorical judgment occupies a central place in his theory of judgment. He begins with the seemingly straightforward idea that a categorical judgment asserts a direct connection between subject and predicate, but his analysis progressively reveals the hidden complexity within this form. (002-002)

The core difficulty Bradley identifies is that a purely categorical judgment would require an immediate, unmediated connection between subject and predicate. But every judgment, he argues, involves a process of ideal separation and synthesis that cannot be captured by the simple categorical form. The categorical judgment in its "first crude form" proves unstable under analysis. (002-042)

Bradley's treatment of categorical judgment connects directly to his accounts of hypothetical and [[disjunction|disjunctive judgment]] — the three judgment forms that structure Book I of *The Principles of Logic*.

## Key Aspects

### The Categorical Form

A categorical judgment, in its simplest expression, asserts that a subject possesses a predicate: "S is P." Bradley notes that this form appears to make a real assertion in which the predicate is directly attributed to the subject without any condition or qualification. This is what distinguishes it from the hypothetical form ("if S, then P"). (002-002)

However, Bradley immediately problematizes this distinction. He argues that every categorical judgment contains an implicit hypothetical element because the connection between subject and predicate always depends on conditions that are not stated in the judgment itself. The apparent directness of categorical assertion is an illusion.

### The Search for Pure Categorical Judgments

Bradley conducts what amounts to a systematic search for genuinely categorical judgments and finds that they are elusive. Analytic judgments (where the predicate is contained in the subject) are not genuine judgments at all — they are tautologies. Synthetic judgments, on the other hand, involve a connection that requires ground, and this ground introduces a hypothetical element. (002-006)

The difficulty is most acute for judgments of perception. When I judge "this rose is red," the judgment appears categorical — a direct report of experience. But Bradley argues that even here, the judgment involves an ideal construction that goes beyond the immediately given. The connection of subject and predicate is not simply found but made. (002-008)

### Categorical and Hypothetical

Bradley's mature position is that categorical and hypothetical judgments are not fundamentally distinct forms. A hypothetical judgment can express the same content as a categorical one, and the categorical form, when analyzed, reveals hidden hypothetical structure. This is not to say that categorical judgments are false or invalid, but that their logical form is more complex than it initially appears. (004-002)

The practical consequence is that we cannot use the categorical form as a foundation for logic. We cannot, for instance, treat categorical propositions as the basic units of logical analysis (as Aristotelian logic does) because the categorical form itself requires analysis into more fundamental logical relations.

### Categorical Judgments as Abstractions

Bradley's ultimate assessment is that particular categorical judgments "may all be reduced to abstractions" from a more fundamental logical process. They are valid as practical expressions but cannot serve as the ultimate units of logical theory. The true basis of judgment lies in the systematic connection of ideal content within a single reality, which is more adequately expressed by the hypothetical and disjunctive forms. (006-013)

## Historical Context

Bradley's critique of the categorical judgment engages with the Aristotelian tradition that had dominated logic for two millennia. In Aristotelian logic, the categorical proposition (A, E, I, O) is the fundamental unit of analysis, and the syllogism — constructed from categorical propositions — is the paradigm of logical inference.

Bradley, following Hegel's lead, challenges this priority. His argument that categorical judgments contain hidden hypothetical structure is part of a broader idealist project of showing that logical form is not given but constituted through the activity of thought. This position influenced later British idealists and, through them, contributed to the development of modern logical theory, which largely abandoned the categorical/hypothetical distinction in favor of the unified treatment of propositions.
