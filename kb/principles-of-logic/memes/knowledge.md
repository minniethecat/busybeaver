# Knowledge

The fundamental cognitive state that makes logic possible — without knowledge of truth and falsehood, no logical judgment can arise (001-001).

## Overview

Bradley opens *The Principles of Logic* by situating knowledge as the necessary precondition for all logical inquiry. [[judgment|Judgment]], inference, and [[inference|reasoning]] all presuppose a subject capable of distinguishing truth from falsehood. The concept of knowledge is therefore not itself a product of logic but its foundation: logic studies the forms and principles by which knowledge advances from immediate presentation to systematic understanding.

Bradley distinguishes between mere ignorance and fallacious knowledge — ignorance alone does not render knowledge erroneous unless it is mistaken for positive insight (001-006). This distinction is critical because it frames the entire logical enterprise as concerned not with the mere [[privation|absence]] of knowledge but with the quality and justification of what is known.

Knowledge in Bradley's sense is always relational: it involves a knower, an object known, and a judgment that connects them. The question that drives his logical analysis is how this relational structure can yield knowledge that is both true, faithful to reality, and coherent, internally consistent.

## Key Aspects

### Knowledge and Judgment

All judgment presupposes knowledge as its raw material. Bradley argues that we cannot judge without first having some acquaintance with the subject matter — judgment transforms primitive knowledge into articulated, propositional form. The objects of knowledge become objects of judgment when truth and falsehood are attributed to them (001-018).

### Knowledge and Appearance

Bradley devotes considerable attention to the relationship between knowledge and appearance. What appears to us in immediate experience is not yet knowledge in the logical sense; knowledge requires the discrimination of reality from appearance, a process that leads from naive encounter to systematic understanding (001-019).

### Knowledge and Imagery

Early knowledge makes extensive use of images, raising the question of whether images function as universals or particulars in the cognitive process (001-020). Bradley treats this as a live philosophical question, not a settled matter, reflecting his engagement with empiricist theories of cognition.

### Intuitive Knowledge

Bradley acknowledges a form of knowledge that operates at the level of presentation or intuition — what we encounter in felt experience before conceptual articulation (002-003). This intuitive knowledge is the raw material that logical reflection subsequently analyzes and systematizes.

### Knowledge and Error

Error, for Bradley, is never a bare defect of knowledge (003-005). It is a positive psychological state — the mind actively affirms something mistaken, rather than simply [[privation|lack]]ing correct information. Denial and contradiction operate on the strength of positive knowledge, not its mere [[privation|absence]] (003-007).

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of knowledge draws on the British empiricist tradition, particularly Mill's analysis of inductive knowledge, while incorporating Idealist concerns about the relation between thought and reality. His emphasis on the psychological dimension of knowledge — that error is a positive state, not a mere [[privation|absence]] — distinguishes his approach from both purely formal logic and naive empiricism.
