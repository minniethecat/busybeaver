# Necessary Truth

A truth that results from some ground and is ultimately identified with inference — for Bradley, necessary truth is not a distinguished class of propositions but the fundamental character of all logical movement (007-003).

## Overview

Bradley's treatment of necessary truth is one of the most distinctive elements of his logical theory. He argues that necessary truth is not a special kind of truth alongside contingent truth; rather, necessity is the mark of all genuine inference. A proposition counts as a necessary truth when it follows from some ground — explicit or implicit — and this "following from" is precisely what inference consists in.

The concept appears primarily in Bradley's discussion of the nature of inference (Book II, Chapter I), where he develops the claim that all necessity affirms a real ground. This ground may be explicit, as in formal [[inference|deduction]], or implicit, as in the ordinary judgments that structure everyday [[inference|reasoning]] (007-005).

Bradley pushes the analysis further, arguing that in logic, a necessary truth is really an inference (007-019). This identification of necessary truth with inference represents a significant departure from traditional treatments that treat necessity as a modal property of propositions.

## Key Aspects

### Necessary Truth as Resulting from Ground

Bradley defines necessary truth functionally: a truth is necessary when it results from a ground. The key formula is that "S-P is a necessary truth" means "S-P follows from some ground" (007-005). This shifts the focus from the intrinsic character of the proposition to its inferential relations.

### Necessary Truth and Inference

The most radical claim in Bradley's analysis is that necessary truth is really inference (007-019). This means that necessity is not a property that some propositions have and others [[privation|lack]]; it is the character of the movement from [[premise|premises]] to conclusion. Any genuine inference yields necessary truth.

### Scope of Necessary Truth

Bradley argues that necessary truth need be no more particular than the ground from which it follows (009-001). The necessity of a conclusion is relative to the [[premise|premises]] that support it; it does not require absolute or unconditional necessity.

### Necessary Truth and Universality

Wherever we have a necessary truth, the connection must hold universally (018-001). Bradley ties necessity to universality: a necessary connection is one that holds without exception within its domain.

### Necessary Truth and the Reasoning Process

Necessary truth enters the field of [[inference|reasoning]] when the ground becomes explicit (018-008). The movement from implicit ground to explicit inference is central to Bradley's account of how [[inference|reasoning]] extends knowledge.

## Historical Context

Bradley's analysis of necessary truth engages with Kant's treatment of necessity as a category of the understanding and with Hegel's identification of necessity with the rational structure of reality. Bradley's distinctive contribution is the functional analysis of necessity in terms of inferential grounding, which anticipates later developments in proof-theoretic semantics.
