# Ideal Construction

The logical act whereby given data are combined into a connected whole through the application of the [[law-of-identity|principle of identity]]. In Bradley's *Principles of Logic*, inference itself is defined as ideal construction — every inference "consists in an ideal construction" (008-001) that results in the perception of a new connection.

## Overview

Ideal construction is the central concept of Bradley's theory of inference. Book II opens with the declaration that inference "takes its data and by ideal construction combines them into a whole" (010-001). The data of inference are ideal contents, and the construction is the act of synthesizing them under the [[law-of-identity|principle of identity]].

"[[inference|Inference]] is always an ideal construction resulting in the perception of a necessary connection" (012-008). The construction yields a result that was not explicitly present in the data taken separately.

The concept appears early, in Book I's discussion of judgment. The given in perception is "supplemented and modified by an ideal construction" (001-017) that represents the results of ideal experiment. [[judgment|Judgment]] itself involves an ideal construction that extends beyond what is immediately given.

## Key Aspects

### Identity as the Ground of Construction

Every ideal construction depends on identity. "An inference always depends, like every other ideal construction, on identity" (002-023). The construction connects two contents through a third that shares identity with both. Without identity, the synthesis would be arbitrary; with identity, it is necessary.

### Construction vs. Discovery

Bradley distinguishes ideal construction from psychological invention. The construction does not create new content ex nihilo; it reveals what is already implied in the data when they are brought together under identity. The object of inference "exists for us only as ideal constructions" (002-025), connected by inference to reality.

### The Ideal Experiment

The concept of ideal construction is closely tied to Bradley's notion of "ideal experiment." Just as a physical experiment manipulates things to reveal their properties, an ideal experiment manipulates meanings to reveal their logical connections. "If you place it in an ideal construction, it developes S-P as a consequence" (007-005).

### Construction and the Syllogism

The syllogism is the paradigmatic case of ideal construction. "The syllogistic practice is the exhibition of an ideal construction" (012-007). The [[premise|premises]] provide the data; the middle term provides the identity; and the conclusion is the constructed whole that unites subject and predicate through the middle.

## Historical Context

Bradley's concept of ideal construction synthesizes Kantian and Hegelian themes. From Kant comes the idea that synthesis is a spontaneous act of mind; from Hegel comes the idea that the synthesis reveals objective connections. Bradley's distinctive contribution is to ground the synthesis in the logical [[law-of-identity|principle of identity]], making logic autonomous from both psychology and metaphysics.
