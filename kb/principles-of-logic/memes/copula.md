# Copula (Logical)

The grammatical or logical link between subject and predicate in traditional judgment theory, typically expressed by "is" or "is not." In Bradley's radical critique, the copula is not a genuine logical constituent but "a sheer superstition" (002-007) — an artifact of grammar projected onto logic.

## Overview

Bradley's attack on the copula is one of the most distinctive features of his logic. He argues that the traditional analysis of judgment into subject, predicate, and copula is "the doctrine most prevalent and most pernicious" (001-007). The copula is supposed to join subject and predicate, but Bradley shows that no such joining is actually performed in judgment — judgment is a unitary act of referring an ideal content to reality, not a compound of parts.

The error arises from confusing grammar with logic. "The necessity for the copula is a sheer superstition" (002-007). Because our language typically expresses judgments in subject-predicate sentences with a linking verb, we imagine that judgment itself must contain these three elements. Bradley insists that "subject, predicate, and copula are mere superstitions" (001-013) — the logical act is simple and indivisible.

## Key Aspects

### The Grammatical Origin of the Copula

Bradley traces the copula to the grammatical form of Indo-European languages. "[[judgment|Judgment]], in our language, must have a subject and predicate" (002-011) — but this is a linguistic requirement, not a logical one. Other languages and other forms of expression show that judgment can occur without any copula at all.

### Judgment Without Copula

Bradley argues that many genuine judgments [[privation|lack]] a copula entirely. When we say "Fire!" or "Rain," we make a judgment without any grammatical copula. "I may judge without using any subject or copula" (002-012). The copula is not essential to judgment; it is merely a feature of certain forms of expression.

### The Copula and the Unity of Judgment

The deepest problem with the copula is that it suggests judgment is a compound — that subject and predicate are independent terms that need to be joined. Bradley's alternative is that judgment is a single act. "They have seen that in judgment the unity is not produced by the copula" (001-015) — the unity is prior to any analysis into parts. The copula obscures this unity by pretending to create it.

### The Copula as Enquiry

Bradley provocatively suggests that "the whole inquiry into the copula" is "merely the same misunderstanding" (002-002) repeated. The very question "what does the copula mean?" presupposes that the copula is a real logical element — a presupposition Bradley rejects.

## Historical Context

The analysis of judgment into subject, predicate, and copula has ancient roots in Aristotle's De Interpretatione and was systematized by medieval logicians. Kant's critique of the copula in the Critique of Pure Reason opened the way for Bradley's more radical rejection. Bradley's position influenced Russell's early rejection of subject-predicate logic, though Russell took the critique in a very different direction. In contemporary philosophy, Bradley's argument anticipates some themes in Wittgenstein's Tractatus.
