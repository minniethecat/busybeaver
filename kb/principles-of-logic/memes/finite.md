The finite, in Bradley's metaphysics, is the realm of determinate, limited, and partial being. Every finite thing, judgment, or truth is what it is only through its relations to what it is not, and this relational character reveals the incompleteness — and ultimate ideality — of the finite.

## Overview

Bradley's treatment of the finite is inseparable from his doctrine of the absolute. The finite is not false but incomplete. "Iron = some partial qualitative identity, definite or indefinite" (001-014) — every finite judgment isolates a partial identity within the whole, and this isolation is both necessary for thought and ultimately unstable.

The finitude of thought consists in its inability to grasp the whole in a single act. Every concept, every judgment, involves "indefinite detail and endless relation" (002-023). This is not a defect to be remedied but the very nature of finite thinking. The recognition of finitude is the beginning of philosophy, for it drives the mind beyond the finite toward the absolute.

## Key Aspects

### Finite and Infinite

The finite and the infinite are correlative in Bradley's system. The "indefinite universal, the vague felt type" (001-020) is the form in which finite thought approaches reality, but reality itself is not finite. The finite is "definite perhaps, but still certainly not complete" (001-021); its very definiteness is its limitation.

### The Indefinite in Judgment

Bradley analyzes the role of indefiniteness in judgment, noting that many judgments involve "more than an indefinite reference" (002-013) even when they appear simple. The words "this," "now," and "mine" are "more abstract and wholly indefinite" (002-016) than they seem, for they point to a reality that exceeds any finite specification.

### Finitude and Truth

No finite truth is wholly true. Every judgment, because it is finite, omits and abstracts. Yet this does not mean finite judgments are worthless. They are partially true, and their partial truth is sufficient for practical and scientific purposes. Philosophy, however, must recognize their incompleteness and strive toward the whole.

## Historical Context

Bradley's doctrine of finitude is deeply indebted to Hegel's Logic, where the finite is shown to pass over into the infinite through its own dialectic. For both thinkers, the finite is not a self-subsistent realm but a moment within the self-determination of the absolute.
