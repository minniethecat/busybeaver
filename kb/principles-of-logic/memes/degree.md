Degree, in Bradley's logical system, refers primarily to the gradation of truth — the notion that truth is not a simple binary of true or false but admits of degrees. This concept is central to Bradley's theory of modality and his broader idealist metaphysics, where reality itself is understood as appearing in varying degrees of completeness.

## Overview

Bradley introduces the concept of degree in his discussion of modality in judgment. He observes that logical assertion "has no degrees" in the strict sense — "the relation of the ideal content to reality is purely logical, and as such has no degrees" (001-012) — yet he also recognizes that judgments vary in their completeness and certainty. The resolution of this apparent tension lies in the nature of the ideal content itself: what is modified is not the act of assertion but the content that is asserted.

The degrees of truth operate across a spectrum. In the hypothetical judgment, "between these extremes come many degrees" (007-004), from the apodictic certainty of necessary truth down to the merely problematic. Bradley traces how truth "sinks by slow degrees" (007-003) from the highest sense, where the ideal content fully corresponds to reality, to the lowest, where the connection is merely suggested.

## Key Aspects

### Modality and Degree

Bradley's treatment of logical modality distinguishes three modes — necessary, actual, and possible — and locates degree primarily in the content of the judgment rather than in the act of judging. The modal judgment asserts not a simple S-P relation but a "transformed and conditioned S-P" whose degree of truth depends on the modal qualification. The apodictic judgment asserts the highest degree, the problematic the lowest.

### Relations of Degree

In his theory of inference, Bradley treats degree as one of the fundamental relations alongside identity, space, and time (011-003). Terms may "stand in a relation of degree with two or more other terms" (011-004), and the inference from one term to another often depends on tracing relations of degree. This logical use of degree connects to the broader metaphysical thesis that reality itself admits of degree.

### Degrees and Reality

The metaphysical significance of degree emerges in Bradley's idealism. If truth admits of degrees, then so does reality, since truth is the correspondence of ideal content to reality. What is less true is, in some sense, less real — it participates in reality "to a degree" (001-016). This position underwrites Bradley's critique of the absolute distinction between appearance and reality.

## Historical Context

Bradley's doctrine of degrees of truth draws on Hegel's dialectical logic, where each stage of thought contains a partial truth that is preserved and transcended in higher stages. Unlike Hegel, however, Bradley grounds the concept in the logical analysis of modal judgment rather than in a historical-dialectical framework. The concept also serves as a critique of the empiricist tendency to treat truth as an all-or-nothing correspondence between proposition and fact.
