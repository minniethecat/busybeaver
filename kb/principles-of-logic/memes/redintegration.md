# Redintegration

The process in Bradley's logical psychology whereby a part of an associated complex reinstates the whole of that complex in ideal form, serving as the basic mechanism of mental reproduction and, in its developed form, of logical inference.

## Overview

Redintegration occupies a foundational position in Bradley's account of mental operations. Introduced early in the *Principles of Logic*, it is described as "the redintegration of ideal elements with sensuous presentation" (001-016), linking the given in perception with the mind's stock of ideal content. For Bradley, redintegration is not a peripheral psychological curiosity but the fundamental engine of all mental reproduction—the process by which the past is made available to present thought.

Bradley devotes sustained attention to redintegration in Chapter XIII of Book I, where he defends it against associationist alternatives. The "law of redintegration is a very different thing" (013-004) from mere association by contiguity, he argues. Whereas [[association-of-ideas|associationism]] posits an external, mechanical linkage between [[idea|ideas]], redintegration is grounded in identity of content: a present element calls up a past complex because it is recognized as *the same* element that belonged to that complex.

The concept has direct logical significance. Bradley argues that inference itself operates through a form of ideal redintegration. "[[inference|Inference]], in short, implies a process of ideal redintegration" (018-005). When we infer, we start with a given ideal content, and through redintegration, further elements of the associated system are reinstated, yielding the conclusion. The transition from psychological redintegration to logical inference is one of the central developmental narratives in the *Principles*.

Bradley's treatment of redintegration also serves his broader anti-empiricist agenda. By grounding reproduction in identity of content rather than external association, he undercuts the Humean picture of the mind as a theater of disconnected impressions. Mental life is intrinsically synthetic, and redintegration is the most primitive expression of this synthesis.

## Key Aspects

### Redintegration vs. Association

Bradley sharply distinguishes redintegration from the empiricist law of association. "The law of redintegration is, a very different thing from the so-called law of contiguity" (013-004). Contiguity explains nothing, according to Bradley, because it merely restates the fact that two things occurred together without explaining why one should recall the other. Redintegration by identity, by contrast, provides a rational ground: the present element *is* the past element, and the whole complex to which it belonged is therefore implicated.

### The Role of Identity

Identity of content is the key to Bradley's account. "The unviolated facts support redintegration by identity" (013-007). A present perception P reinstates the complex P-Q-R not because P was contiguous with Q and R in past experience, but because the present P is recognized as numerically identical with the P that figured in that past complex. This appeal to identity elevates redintegration from a merely psychological mechanism to a logical principle.

### Redintegration and Inference

Bradley argues that inference is a developed form of redintegration. "[[inference|Inference]], in short, implies a process of ideal redintegration" (018-005). In inference, we start with a judgment AB, and the ideal content B, through its internal relations, redintegrates further content that was associated with it. The conclusion is the reinstated whole. Bradley is careful to note that logical redintegration involves ideal content rather than sensuous presentations, and that it operates under normative constraints that mere psychological redintegration [[privation|lack]]s.

### Redintegration in Perception

Bradley extends redintegration to the analysis of perception itself. The "law of partial perception" (013-022)—another name for redintegration—explains how we perceive wholes when only parts are sensuously given. A fragment of a familiar face redintegrates the whole, and we perceive the person, not merely the fragment. This perceptual application bridges the gap between sensory input and meaningful experience.

### The Limits of Redintegration

Bradley acknowledges that not all mental reproduction is redintegration. Some processes involve "ideal construction" rather than reinstatement of a pre-existing whole (019-005). Redintegration proper restores a previously experienced complex; ideal construction builds something new from elements that were never before combined. The distinction is important for Bradley's theory of inference, which involves both redintegrative and constructive elements.

## Historical Context

The term "redintegration" has a history in associationist psychology, where it was used by [[james-mill|James Mill]] and others to describe the reinstatement of a total idea from a partial cue. Bradley appropriates the term but transforms its theoretical basis. Where the associationists appealed to contiguity and frequency, Bradley appeals to identity—a move that reflects his Hegelian inheritance and his commitment to a rationalist psychology.

Bradley's critique of [[association-of-ideas|associationism]] via redintegration parallels similar arguments in the German psychological literature (Lotze, [[christoph-von-sigwart|Sigwart]]) and anticipates gestalt psychology's emphasis on wholes and structured perception. His insistence that redintegration operates by identity rather than contiguity also aligns with his broader metaphysical doctrine that all relations are internal—that the nature of any term involves its relations to other terms.
