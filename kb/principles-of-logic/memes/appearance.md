Appearance, in Bradley's philosophical system, is that which presents itself to experience without being ultimately real. The distinction between appearance and reality is one of the foundational themes of Bradley's idealism and runs throughout *The Principles of Logic*.

## Overview

Bradley insists that appearances cannot be dismissed as mere illusions. An appearance is real in some sense — it genuinely appears — but it is not the final truth about reality. As he writes, "an appearance is not what it seemed" (001-018). The task of philosophy is to understand how appearances relate to reality, not to deny them. The varying "appearances of the image" may differ without undermining the reality that appears through them (001-006).

## Key Aspects

### Appearance is Not Illusion

Bradley's treatment of appearance is nuanced: what appears must in some sense be real, yet the appearance as such is not the complete truth. An appearance may be "either not real at all, or real only in a sense" (001-019). This avoids both naive realism (which takes appearances at face value) and extreme skepticism (which dismisses appearances entirely).

### Appearance and Time

Bradley connects the concept of appearance with temporal experience: "all appearance must occupy some time or space" (002-003). The temporal and spatial character of appearance marks it as belonging to the realm of finite experience rather than ultimate reality. However, Bradley does not consider this spatial-temporal character to be a disqualification of appearance; rather, it defines the conditions under which appearance occurs.

### Momentary Appearance

Bradley questions whether reality can be "identical with the merely momentary appearance" (002-008). The fleeting, transient character of appearances cannot be the final truth; reality must be more than the succession of momentary presentations. The momentary appearance is not the same as reality itself, and yet synthetic judgments about reality are possible precisely because appearances are the manifestation of a deeper reality. (002-015)

### Appearance as Manifestation

Bradley sees appearances as the way in which reality manifests itself to finite consciousness. The appearances through which the light falls are the appearance of a reality which forever transcends any single manifestation. (002-011) A reality that appears in and through changing appearances while remaining the same is the ground of all judgment about the world. (002-014)

## Historical Context

Bradley's appearance/reality distinction is a hallmark of British Idealism. It draws on Kant's phenomenal/noumenal distinction but radicalizes it within an idealist framework where ultimate reality is a single, coherent whole. The mere quality of appearance in space or time cannot give singularity to a thing, for appearance always points beyond itself to the reality that grounds it. (002-017)
