# Belief

Belief, in Bradley's logical psychology, is the intellectual attitude of holding a content to be true. His treatment is notable for distinguishing intellectual belief or conviction from practical belief while insisting that the two are never fully separable. Belief is central to Bradley's account of judgment — judgment, he argues, implies belief — and to his theory of inference, where the strength of belief provides the motive force that carries the mind from [[premise|premises]] to conclusion.

## Overview

Bradley addresses belief most directly in his opening chapter, where he distinguishes intellectual belief from practical belief. "Intellectual belief or conviction is the general attitude of holding-true," while practical belief involves a disposition to act. (001-012) The two are distinct but not independent: practical belief may "operate in spite of disbelief" at the intellectual level, and intellectual conviction may fail to issue in corresponding action. (001-011)

This complexity matters for logic because judgment, as Bradley understands it, involves belief. When we judge that something is the case, we hold it to be true — and this holding-true has both intellectual and practical dimensions. A logic that ignores the role of belief cannot explain why we care about the conclusions of our inferences.

Bradley's analysis of belief is also critical of empiricist accounts. Against Hume's theory, he notes that "on Hume's theory of belief, there can be no difference between belief and vivid association" — a r[[immediate-inference|eduction]] that fails to capture the normative character of belief. (001-009) Belief does not merely associate [[idea|ideas]]; it commits the mind to their truth.

## Key Aspects

### Intellectual vs. Practical Belief

Bradley's distinction between intellectual and practical belief is central to his account. Intellectual belief is the attitude of holding a proposition to be true on the basis of evidence; practical belief is the disposition to act on that proposition. (001-012) The two can come apart: one may intellectually believe that a course of action is dangerous while practically acting as if it were safe.

This distinction complicates the empiricist equation of belief with vividness or liveliness of [[idea|ideas]]. Hume's theory fails, Bradley argues, because it cannot distinguish the intellectual content of belief from the practical force that accompanies it. The judgment "this is true" is not simply a vivid idea; it is a normative commitment.

### Belief and Judgment

[[judgment|Judgment]], for Bradley, implies belief. "[[judgment|Judgment]], as we know, implies belief" — this is a fundamental point that distinguishes judgment from mere association or imagination. (003-002) When we judge that S is P, we do not merely entertain the combination of S and P; we commit ourselves to its truth.

This connection between judgment and belief means that the logic of judgment is also, implicitly, a logic of belief. The norms governing correct judgment — coherence, systematicity, evidential support — are simultaneously norms governing rational belief.

### The Grounds of Belief

Bradley devotes considerable attention to the question of what grounds belief. "The proportion of our grounds for belief" determines, or ought to determine, the degree of conviction we feel. (007-012) But the grounds themselves are not simple — they include direct experience, inference from other beliefs, and the systematic coherence of a belief with the body of accepted knowledge.

The comparison of grounds is itself a logical activity. "A comparison of our grounds for belief" is required whenever we face conflicting grounds, and this comparison is what Bradley calls the weighing of evidence. (007-013) It is not a mechanical procedure but an exercise of judgment that itself presupposes systematic standards.

### Belief, Probability, and Conflicting Grounds

Bradley's interest in belief leads him to an early treatment of probability and conflicting evidence. He recognizes that we often have "conflicting grounds for belief and expectation" — grounds that pull in different directions and cannot be resolved by simple [[inference|deduction]]. (007-015) The rational resolution of such conflicts requires a judgment about the relative weight of grounds, a judgment for which there is no algorithm.

This is closely connected to his analysis of the gambler's fallacy. The gambler's "system" rests on the "belief that independent events are somehow connected" — a belief that is demonstrably false but psychologically powerful. (007-017) The logical task is to distinguish well-grounded belief from mere psychological compulsion.

## Historical Context

Bradley's treatment of belief engages with Hume's associationist account, Mill's inductive logic, and the broader nineteenth-century debate about the relation between belief, evidence, and knowledge. His insistence on the normative dimension of belief — that belief involves a commitment to truth, not merely a psychological state — anticipates later developments in epistemology, including the debate between evidentialism and pragmatism. The distinction between intellectual and practical belief influenced subsequent British philosophy, particularly the ethics of belief debate.
