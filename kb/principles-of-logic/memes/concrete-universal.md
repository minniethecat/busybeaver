# Concrete Universal

In Bradley's *Principles of Logic*, the concrete universal is the individual reality considered as the identity of its internal differences. It stands opposed both to the abstract universal (a mere attribute treated as if independently real) and to the abstract particular (an atom that excludes all internal diversity).

"The concrete particular and the concrete universal both have reality, and they are different names for the individual" (006-009). This doctrine grounds Bradley's entire theory of judgment, inference, and the nature of logical truth.

## Overview

Bradley introduces the concrete universal as the resolution of a set of apparently contradictory theses about reality: that nothing real is universal, that everything real is universal, that nothing real is particular, and that most reality is particular.

He holds all four to be true, with the apparent conflict resolved once we distinguish abstract from concrete senses of "universal" and "particular."

The abstract universal is "nothing whatever but an adjective. It is an epithet divorced, a shadow which apart from its body is nothing, and can not exist" (006-010). The abstract particular, equally, is an atom that "excludes all difference. It is itself and nothing beyond itself. And that self is simple" (006-009). Neither exists in reality.

The concrete universal, by contrast, is real because it is identical with the individual: "What is real is the individual; and this individual, though one and the same, has internal differences. You may hence regard it in two opposite ways. So far as it is one against other individuals, it is particular. So far as it is the same throughout its diversity, it is universal" (006-009).

## Key Aspects

### The Abstract Universal and Particular

Bradley argues the abstract universal and abstract particular are products of the same fundamental mistake — "distinctions within a whole, hardened into units that stand by themselves" (006-010).

The abstract triangle, determined by "that division and consequent exclusion which gave it its origin, has become particular.

And the particular itself, because produced by mental separation, is really no more than an adjective divorced, or abstract universal" (006-010).

In Bradley's analysis, the two abstractions flip into each other: "The universal, the more we emphasize its character, divides itself the more from the whole. We make its being depend on exclusion, and it turns in our hand into its logical contrary. The particular again, excluding others, and being so far as it merely excludes, is its own negative relation to other particulars" (006-010).

### The Individual as Identity of Differences

The concrete universal is not a compromise between universal and particular but the recognition that the individual is their identity. "The individual is both a concrete particular and a concrete universal; and, as names of the whole from different points of view, these both are names of real existence" (006-009).

Bradley gives the example: "Thus a man is particular by virtue of his limiting and exclusive relations to other phenomena. He is universal because he is one throughout all his different attributes. You may call him particular, or again universal, because, being individual, he actually is both, and you wish to emphasize one aspect or side of his individuality" (006-009).

### The Concrete Universal in Reasoning

The doctrine of the concrete universal has direct consequences for Bradley's theory of inference. In Book III, he returns to the concept to explain how analysis and synthesis function together in [[inference|reasoning]]: "In the concrete universal, which has guided our steps, and which has appeared as the identity of analysis and synthesis, we have returned to truth and made our peace with reality" (022-007).

Reasoning, on this view, is neither purely analytic (drawing out what was already contained) nor purely synthetic (adding new content by external conjunction). It is the process by which a concrete universal exhibits its internal articulation — differentiation that is also unification.

### Logic and Reality

The concrete universal also grounds Bradley's claim that the ultimate subject of every judgment is the absolute individual.

"What is real is individual and what is real individual, for logic too the rational is individual and individuality is truth" (022-007).

The goal of thought is not to manipulate abstractions but to achieve "a completion, single and self-contained, where difference and identity are two aspects of one process in a self-same substance" (022-007).

## Historical Context

The concrete universal is a concept with deep roots in German Idealism, particularly Hegel's logic, where the "concrete universal" designates the concept that contains its own differentiation within itself rather than abstracting from it. Bradley's use of the term shows his Hegelian inheritance while adapting it to a British logical context.

Bradley's treatment also engages with the empiricist tradition's handling of universals. Against Mill's view that universals are merely names for classes of particulars, and against the associationist r[[immediate-inference|eduction]] of identity to resemblance, Bradley insists that universality is constitutive of the real itself — not a mental addition to a world of atomic particulars.
