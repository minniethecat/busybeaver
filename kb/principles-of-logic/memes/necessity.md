# Necessity

Necessity, in Bradley's logical system, is the character of a connection that cannot be denied without contradiction given certain grounds. Bradley's treatment is distinctive in arguing that all logical necessity is fundamentally hypothetical: a conclusion is necessary not in itself but relative to the [[premise|premises]] or grounds from which it follows. This move shifts the philosophical problem of necessity from ontology to the structure of [[inference|reasoning]].

## Overview

Bradley introduces the problem of necessity by distinguishing two senses of the term. "A thing is necessary if it is indispensable for some purpose" — this is hypothetical necessity, the kind that governs logical inference. (007-001) The alternative would be categorical necessity — the idea that something is necessary in and of itself, independently of any conditions. Bradley argues that logical necessity is always of the first kind: "So far as its necessity goes, it is hypothetical." (007-002)

This position has radical consequences. If necessity is always relative to grounds, then the question "Is this judgment necessary?" is incomplete — we must always ask "Necessary relative to what grounds?" The search for absolute, unconditioned necessity in logic is a mistake. (007-003)

The relation between necessity and impossibility is equally important for Bradley. "Impossibility and necessity are correlative [[idea|ideas]]" — what is necessary is that whose negation is impossible, and what is impossible is that whose affirmation contradicts what is necessary. (007-006) These correlations define the modal structure of logical thought.

## Key Aspects

### Hypothetical vs. Categorical Necessity

Bradley's central claim is that logical necessity is hypothetical. A judgment is necessary "if it is indispensable for some purpose" — the purpose being the systematic coherence of thought. (007-001) The judgment "This body must fall" is necessary only on the hypothesis that the law of gravitation holds; it is not necessary in any absolute sense.

This does not make necessity subjective or arbitrary. The grounds on which necessity rests are objective — they are the systematic connections of content that constitute knowledge. But the necessity itself is always conditional on those grounds, and to forget this conditionality is to fall into dogmatism. (007-005)

### Necessity and the Major Premise

Bradley uses the concept of necessity to critique the traditional doctrine that all inference requires a [[premise|major premise]]. "It is a mistake to insist on the necessity of a [[premise|major premise]]," he argues, because the logical force of an inference does not always depend on an explicitly formulated universal statement. (009-003) Logical necessity "does not always come from the [[premise|major premise]]" — it can arise from the concrete connection of subject and predicate in the particular case. (009-001)

This is a crucial point for Bradley's rejection of formal logic: if necessity can arise without an explicit [[premise|major premise]], then the syllogistic form does not capture the full range of necessary inference.

### Necessity and Universality

Bradley follows the traditional doctrine that "connects universality with necessity," and he affirms that "that doctrine is true." (007-019) A genuinely necessary connection holds universally — if A necessitates B, then whenever A obtains, B must obtain. But Bradley complicates this picture by arguing that universality is itself an ideal construction, not a simple generalization from instances.

The connection between necessity and universality means that necessity claims are always vulnerable to counterexample. A single genuine exception is enough to show that the supposed necessity was not truly universal and therefore not genuinely necessary.

### Necessity in Association vs. Inference

Bradley distinguishes the necessity proper to logical inference from the mere regularity of psychological association. Ideas "are not associated by their own necessity, and by virtue of some internal connection" — association is contingent and external, while inference claims an internal, necessary connection of content. (013-002) This distinction is fundamental to Bradley's project of distinguishing logic from empirical psychology.

## Historical Context

Bradley's account of necessity engages with a long philosophical tradition from Aristotle's distinction between absolute and hypothetical necessity to Hume's skeptical treatment of necessary connection and Kant's account of synthetic a priori necessity. Bradley's distinctive contribution is to treat all logical necessity as hypothetical while preserving its objectivity — a position that anticipates aspects of twentieth-century holism about meaning and confirmation. His view was criticized by Russell, who sought to restore a more robust conception of logical necessity through the theory of logical form.
