# G.W.F. Hegel

Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel (1770–1831), German idealist philosopher, is the single most important philosophical influence on F.H. Bradley's *Principles of Logic*. Bradley acknowledges this debt while carefully marking the limits of his agreement.

## Overview

In the Preface to *Principles of Logic*, Bradley addresses the question of his "Hegelianism" directly. "For Hegel himself, assuredly I think him a great philosopher; but I never could have called myself an Hegelian, partly because I can not say that I have mastered his system, and partly because I could not accept what seems his main principle, or at least part of that principle." This ambivalent relationship — deep respect combined with principled disagreement — shapes much of Bradley's logical method.

Bradley draws on Hegel's dialectical approach to logical categories while rejecting what he sees as system-building for its own sake. "We want no system-making or systems home-grown or imported," he insists. Yet Hegelian themes pervade the work: the treatment of judgment as a reference to reality, the insistence that identity always involves difference, and the dialectical development of logical forms.

## Key Ideas

### Judgment and Reality

Bradley follows Hegel in treating judgment not as a merely subjective operation but as a claim about reality itself. Hegel's doctrine that the copula expresses the identity of subject and predicate in a higher unity is echoed in Bradley's claim that every judgment "affirms either the identity which persists under differences, or else the difference which is predicated despite an identity" (001-017).

### Dialectical Development

Bradley's method of treating each logical form by first presenting it, then exposing its internal contradictions, and finally resolving those contradictions in a higher form, is recognizably Hegelian. The movement from categorical to hypothetical judgment, and from affirmation to negation, follows a dialectical pattern.

### The Limits of System

Despite these Hegelian elements, Bradley explicitly rejects the ambition of a complete logical system. Where Hegel claimed to derive the entire structure of thought from a single principle, Bradley confesses that "on all questions, if you push me far enough, at present I end in doubts and perplexities."

## Bradley's Critique

Bradley's main disagreement with Hegel concerns the "main principle" — likely the doctrine of absolute idealism or the claim that the real is rational. Bradley distances himself from this: "As Hegel tells us, it sins against the very form of judgment" (005-001), referencing Hegel's critique of tautologous identity statements. Bradley uses Hegel's own insight against the Hegelian system: if identity without difference is empty, then a system that claims to be complete must either include difference (and thus be incomplete) or exclude it (and thus be empty).

Bradley also explicitly warns against "the cardinal mistake of Hegel" (005-012), suggesting that Hegel errs in treating logical determinations as real determinations without qualification.

## Influence

Hegel's influence on British idealism, mediated largely through Bradley's work, was substantial. Bradley's *Principles of Logic* became a central text of the British idealist movement, and his Hegelian-inflected treatment of judgment and inference influenced subsequent idealists including Bernard Bosanquet. The "Hegelian School" that Bradley dismisses in his Preface as existing "only in our reviews" was nevertheless a real intellectual current in late 19th-century British philosophy.
