# Particular

In Bradley's logical system, the particular is the concrete, sensuous instance — a specific image, sound, or psychical event — that carries a universal meaning but is not identical with it. The distinction between particular and universal is foundational to his theory of judgment.

## Overview

Bradley distinguishes the particular as the "clothing" of thought — the specific sensuous particulars in which a meaning is embedded. When we think "dog," we may have a particular image, but the meaning "dog" is not that image; it is the universal content that applies equally to all dogs (001-005). The particular exists in time and space; the universal does not.

The particular has "traits of its own" beyond the universal meaning it conveys (001-004). These traits — the specific shade of a color, the exact pitch of a sound — are irrelevant to the logical content but are the necessary vehicle through which meaning enters consciousness.

## Key Aspects

### Particular vs. Universal

Every act of thought involves both particular and universal. The particular image or word is the psychical vehicle; the universal is the logical content. As Bradley puts it, the idea "is clothed with particulars and qualified by characters which it shares with the real world" (001-006).

### The Role of Particulars in Judgment

When we make a judgment, we use particular images or symbols, but what we assert is a universal content. "That stated fraction is an universal adjective which might be applied to endless numbers" (001-014). The particular is the means, not the object, of judgment.

### Particularity and Localization

Particulars are characterized by their localization in time and space — "their strength and particularity and actual localization in the series of my history" (001-009). This localization is what makes them psychical events rather than logical meanings.
