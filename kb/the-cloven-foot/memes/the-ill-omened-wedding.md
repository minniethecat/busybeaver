# The Ill-Omened Wedding

The wedding of [[john-treverton|John Treverton]] and Laura Malcolm took place at Hazlehurst Parish Church on the last day of the year — "nature's dullest, dreariest interval between the richness of autumn and the fresh young beauty of spring" (012-002). The marriage fulfilled the condition of [[the-reading-of-the-will|Jasper Treverton's will]], but the day ended in catastrophe: after the wedding breakfast, John Treverton abandoned his bride, leaving behind only a letter confessing that the marriage was legally invalid and that he must leave her, perhaps forever.

## Summary

On a "dull grey sky" morning with "drip, drip, drip" of Scotch mist, Laura Malcolm and John Treverton were married by Mr. Clare the vicar at Hazlehurst Church (012-005, 012-015). The ceremony was quiet — only Celia Clare as bridesmaid, Mr. Sampson giving the bride away, and a few family friends. The wedding breakfast was "quietly cheerful," the bridegroom "deadly pale" (012-015). But after the guests departed, Laura found her husband in the study "in an attitude of deepest dejection, his forehead resting on his folded arms, his face hidden" (012-029), sobbing "such as but seldom come from the agonised heart of a strong man" (012-029). When she returned an hour later as he had asked, he was gone — leaving only a letter confessing that he had "committed a crime in order to bind your life with mine" (012-054) and that "an old tie... forbids me as a man of honour to be more to you than I now am" (012-055). This wedding was later superseded by a second, secret ceremony at [[the-church-near-camelot|Camelot Church]] six months later, where the marriage was made legally valid.

## Context

### Preceding Events

The year leading up to the wedding had been one of careful courtship. After [[the-reading-of-the-will|the reading of Jasper Treverton's will]], John Treverton had retreated to London, returning periodically to Hazlehurst to court Laura. Despite his coldness as a lover — "how cold a lover he had been, how seldom he had courted her confidence, how little he had told of his own life" (012-042) — Laura had fallen deeply in love with him. She had "given her love and trust without reservation" to a man she barely knew. The engagement was announced in Chapter 10, and the preparations for a wedding without a trousseau occupied Chapter 11, where Celia Clare lamented the absence of a proper wedding gown.

Significantly, during this courtship period, John Treverton was living a double life. Unknown to Laura and everyone at Hazlehurst, he was also Jack Chicot — married to La Chicot, living in bohemian Cibber Street, and unable to legally marry anyone else. His courtship of Laura was conducted under the shadow of this impossibility, making the wedding a deliberate act of deception.

## Detailed Account

The day began in gloom. Celia Clare, looking out of Laura's dressing-room window, exclaimed "What a miserable morning!" at the sight of "the sodden lawn and the glistening yew-tree hedge" and the "dismal perspective of leafless apple-trees" (012-003). Her next remark proved prophetic: "Well, really, Laura, don't think me unkind for saying so, but I do call this an ill-omened wedding" (012-005).

The two young women took an early breakfast together, "a meal at which neither could eat," both "in a state of nervous agitation" (012-004). Laura's quiet confidence was striking: "What have I to fear? We love each other dearly. How can we fail to be happy?" (012-008). She wore a grey silk travelling dress and a grey Gainsborough hat with a drooping ostrich plume — no white wedding gown, no orange blossoms (012-012).

The drive to church was along "the muddy road, between bare ranks of dark and dripping trees, and under as dull and colourless a sky as ever roofed in Hazlehurst" (012-014). The old parish church, with its "queer corners and darksome side-aisles" and "faded crimson cushions and draperies — a church which the restorer's hand has never improved — looked its darkest and gloomiest today" (012-014).

> John Treverton, and Mr. Sampson, who was to give the bride away, were the last to arrive. The bridegroom was deadly pale, and the smile with which he met his bride, though full of fondest love, was wanting in gladness. (012-015)

The ceremony itself was brief and somber. Mr. Clare "read the service deliberately and well, the pale bridegroom spoke out manfully when his time came; nor did Laura's low voice falter when she pronounced the words that sealed her fate" (012-015).

The wedding breakfast followed — "quietly cheerful" with game pie, truffled turkey, and Jasper Treverton's old wines. Celia's "lively tongue chimed in at every opportunity" (012-016). But the departure was delayed: "The bride and bridegroom were not to start on their travels till after dark" (012-017), leaving an uncomfortable interval.

After the guests departed, Laura went looking for her husband and found him in the study, sobbing. When she asked what was wrong, he answered "hoarsely, putting her arm away as he spoke": "Leave me, Laura. If you pity me, leave me to fight my battle alone" (012-032). He asked her to return in an hour, promising to explain.

> "Leave you, and in such grief as this! No, John, I have a right to share your sorrow. I will not go till you have confided in me. Trust me, love, trust me. Whom can you trust, if not your wife?" (012-033)

When she returned an hour later, the study was empty. A letter lay on the table. In it, John Treverton confessed the truth: "I have committed a crime in order to bind your life with mine, somehow, in the rash hope that some day that bond may be made legal and complete" (012-054). He revealed that "there is an old tie which forbids me as a man of honour to be more to you than I now am" (012-055). He signed himself "Your husband in name; your defender and champion, if need were, before all the world; your adoring slave, in secret and in absence, to the day of my death" (012-055).

Laura's response was extraordinary. She "stood for some minutes pale as marble, with the letter in her hand, and then she lifted the senseless paper to her lips, and kissed it passionately" (012-058). Her first thought was not anger but relief: "He loves me. Thank God for that. I can bear anything now I am sure of that" (012-059).

Unable to face the questions and cross-examinations of Hazlehurst society, Laura fled that same night — going on her honeymoon alone, taking only her maid Mary. She told the butler that "Mr. Treverton will meet us at the station" (012-083), concealing the truth of her abandonment.

## Consequences

The wedding's catastrophic ending shapes the entire middle section of the novel. Laura Malcolm becomes "Mrs. Treverton" in name only, living at Hazlehurst Manor as a married woman without a husband, enduring six months of Celia's curiosity and the village's whispered speculation. She tells no one the truth, maintaining the fiction that John Treverton is abroad on business.

Six months later, she secretly travels to Plymouth — and from there to the remote Cornish town of Camelot — where a second wedding ceremony takes place at a lonely old church, with only her maid Mary and the parish clerk as witnesses. This second wedding, described in Chapter 20, is the novel's resolution of the first wedding's illegality. After [[the-murder-of-la-chicot|La Chicot's murder]], John Treverton's "old tie" had been severed by death, and he was free to marry. The Camelot ceremony — conducted in secrecy by "the most accommodating old parson, who quite entered into my views" (020-048) — made legal what the Hazlehurst wedding had merely pretended.

## Significance

### Plot Function

The ill-omened wedding is the event that transforms John Treverton from a morally ambiguous suitor into a figure of active deception. His decision to marry Laura despite knowing the marriage was invalid — to "commit a crime in order to bind your life with mine" — is the most consequential moral choice he makes in the novel, and it is the secret that the murder investigation threatens to expose. The wedding also establishes Laura's character: her decision to kiss the letter, to believe in her husband's love despite his betrayal, and to protect his secret at the cost of her own reputation, defines her as the novel's moral anchor.

### Thematic Resonance

The wedding's "ill-omened" quality — emphasized by the dreary weather, the absence of a wedding gown, the bridegroom's pallor, and Celia's prophetic remark — participates in the sensation novel's use of atmospheric foreshadowing. The "dull grey sky" and "Scotch mist" become objective correlatives for the moral obscurity of the marriage itself. The wedding's doubleness — one ceremony at Hazlehurst, another at Camelot — echoes the novel's larger preoccupation with duplicates and counterfeits, from the diamond necklace that proves to be paste to Desrolles's multiple identities.

The event also illuminates the novel's treatment of [[marriage-market|marriage as a legal and economic institution]]. John Treverton marries Laura to secure the estate, even though he cannot legally do so; Laura marries him because, as she tells Celia, "We love each other dearly" (012-008). The gap between his instrumental motive and her romantic faith is the tragic irony at the heart of the wedding day.
