# The Trial of John Treverton

John Treverton was arrested on suspicion of murdering La Chicot and taken to the House of Detention in Clerkenwell, while his wife Laura and lawyer Thomas Sampson fought to prove his innocence — a process that would ultimately lead to his complete exoneration.

## Summary

After being arrested at Treverton Manor by a detective from Scotland Yard, John Treverton was transported to London and committed to the House of Detention at Clerkenwell. Laura accompanied him, steadfast in her belief in his innocence. Thomas Sampson engaged the prominent criminal solicitor Mr. Leopold and began investigating the circumstances of the murder at Cibber Street. [[PN039-001]][[PN039-031]]

Laura bore the ordeal with what the narrator calls "noble heroism," neither weeping nor trembling, but standing by her husband's side. She declared she was "almost glad" he should face the charge, "better to be put upon your trial, and prove yourself innocent, as I know you can, than to live all your life under the shadow of a groundless suspicion." [[PN039-003]][[PN039-004]]

## Context

### Preceding Events

The accusation arose from the murder of La Chicot at the Cibber Street lodging-house. John Treverton, who had been living a double life as Jack Chicot, was present in the house at the time of the murder. His evasiveness at the inquest, combined with circumstantial evidence, led Scotland Yard to arrest him months later. [[PN039-029]][[PN039-030]]

## Detailed Account

The arrest came suddenly. John Treverton had been preparing to go hunting when a man from Scotland Yard arrived. He was closeted with his wife in the book-room, while the detective waited. [[PN039-002]]

Laura met the crisis with "noble heroism." She was "pale and steadfast, ready to sustain and comfort him, rather than to add to his burden with the weight of her own grief." [[PN039-003]]

> "I am not afraid, John," she said. "I am almost glad that you should face this hideous charge. Better to be put upon your trial, and prove yourself innocent, as I know you can, than to live all your life under the shadow of a groundless suspicion." [[PN039-004]]

Treverton was taken to the House of Detention at Clerkenwell. Laura, her maid Mary, and the detective accompanied him on the train. At five o'clock that winter afternoon, the doors of the House of Detention closed upon John Treverton. [[PN039-031]]

Laura took rooms at the Midland Hotel, where Mary marveled at the splendour, mistaking it for Buckingham Palace. But for Laura, the evening was "sad and weary." She paced the strange sitting-room, alone and friendless in London, for the first time fully realizing the catastrophe. [[PN039-037]][[PN039-049]]

> "My husband suspected of such a crime," she said to herself. "My husband, whose inmost thoughts are known to me; a man incapable of cruelty to the meanest thing that crawls." [[PN039-051]]

Thomas Sampson arrived late that evening, having already engaged Mr. Leopold, a prominent criminal solicitor. He and Leopold had visited Cibber Street and "picked up all the evidence we could." Laura learned that Desrolles — her father, under his alias — had been in the house at the time of the murder. [[PN039-063]][[PN039-064]]

## Consequences

The trial process led ultimately to John Treverton's exoneration. The intervention of witnesses, including the testimony of those who could speak to the true circumstances of the murder, proved his innocence. The ordeal, however, left deep scars and exposed the double life John had been leading as Jack Chicot.

## Significance

### Plot Function

The trial serves as the novel's climactic crisis. It forces John Treverton to confront the consequences of his deception and brings the Cibber Street murder into the full light of legal scrutiny. Laura's steadfast loyalty during this period cements her role as the moral centre of the novel.

### Thematic Resonance

The trial dramatises the theme of hidden identity and the corrosive effects of concealment. John Treverton's alias — Jack Chicot — becomes the instrument of his near-destruction. His reflection that "it would have been so much easier to face this accusation as Jack Chicot than as John Treverton" underscores how the very device meant to protect him has become his greatest liability.
