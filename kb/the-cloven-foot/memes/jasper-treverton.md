# Jasper Treverton

Jasper Treverton is the absent center of *The Cloven Foot* — a man who dies in the opening chapters but whose will, wealth, and eccentric decisions drive every subsequent event in the novel. His deathbed scene, his testamentary dispositions, and the mystery surrounding his motivations form the foundation upon which the entire plot is built.

## Role in the Novel

Jasper Treverton is introduced in the first chapter as a dying man of "eccentric temper" ([001-043]) who has quarrelled with his only living relative, his nephew [[John-Treverton]]. Despite this estrangement, he summons John to his deathbed — setting in motion the chain of events that will define the novel. His death and the reading of his will occupy Chapter 2, and the consequences of his testamentary choices reverberate through every subsequent chapter.

The will's central provision — that [[john-treverton|John Treverton]] must marry [[Laura-Malcolm]], the adopted daughter, within one year of Jasper's death or forfeit the [[inheritance]] — creates the romantic plot, the [[identity]] mystery, and the legal tensions that structure the narrative. Jasper Treverton is thus the novel's prime mover, a character who acts from beyond the grave.

## Biography

### Background

Jasper Treverton was a man of considerable wealth and property, possessing an estate that included [[the-village|Hazlewood]] Manor and substantial liquid assets. He had "warmly esteemed the Vicar" ([005-029]), suggesting connections to the local gentry and church establishment. His only living relative was his nephew [[john-treverton|John Treverton]], with whom he had quarrelled — the nature and cause of this estrangement being one of the novel's early mysteries. "John, was the only relative Jasper Treverton had in the world; but that ho——" ([001-004]) — the broken sentence suggests a qualifying condition that complicates the simple fact of kinship.

### The Deathbed

Jasper Treverton's final days are marked by failing health and a deteriorating mental state. His [[the-servants|valet]], "a grave-looking, grey-haired man" ([001-090]), attends him. [[Laura-Malcolm]] is at his bedside, "looking very wan and pale" ([001-102]). The terms of his will — prepared in advance and read by Mr. [[thomas-sampson|Thomas Sampson]] immediately after his death — reveal a mind capable of elaborate legal manipulation, even as his physical body failed. "Jasper Treverton lingered nearly a week after" ([002-002]) the opening scenes, dying with "his hand in [[laura-malcolm|Laura Malcolm]]'s, and his face turned toward——" ([002-003]).

### The Will

The will is Jasper Treverton's most consequential act. Read "in the presence of [[mr-clare|Mr. Clare]], the vicar, [[laura-malcolm|Laura Malcolm]], and the upper [[the-servants|servants]] of the household" ([002-005]), it contains a startling provision: [[john-treverton|John Treverton]] must marry "his dearly-beloved adopted daughter, [[laura-malcolm|Laura Malcolm]], within one year of his decease" ([002-006]). The [[inheritance]] is revealed to constitute less than "a third of Jasper Treverton's possessions" ([003-002]), adding a further layer of mystery — where is the rest of the [[inheritance|fortune]]?

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

Jasper Treverton's motivations are a central enigma. Why did he disinherit his only blood relative in favor of an adopted daughter? Why impose the [[marriage-market|marriage condition]]? The novel offers no simple answer, but the possibilities — love for Laura, distrust of John, a desire to control events from beyond the grave — all contribute to the sense of a complex, calculating mind. His "eccentric temper" ([001-043]) suggests a man who defied conventional expectations, and his will is the ultimate expression of that defiance.

### Narrative Function

Jasper Treverton serves a function common in Victorian fiction: the dead patriarch whose testamentary wishes create the conditions for the plot. Like Old Mr. Harmon in *Our Mutual Friend* or Peter Featherstone in *Middlemarch*, Jasper Treverton's will is a mechanism for exploring questions of [[inheritance]], obligation, and the relationship between wealth and love. But Braddon adds a characteristically sensational twist: [[inheritance|the will]] does not simply distribute property — it creates a mystery about [[identity]] itself.

## Key Passages

> "John, was the only relative Jasper Treverton had in the world; but that ho——" ([001-004])

The interrupted sentence hints at a complication that the novel will spend many chapters unraveling.

> "Jasper Treverton's eccentric temper" ([001-043])

The defining characterization — "eccentric" suggesting the unpredictable, the unconventional, [[inheritance|the will]]ful.

> "his dearly-beloved adopted daughter, Laura Malcolm, within one year of his decease" ([002-006])

The will's central provision, rendered in the formal language of testamentary law, which sets a clock ticking on the entire plot.

> "He was angry with fate and Jasper Treverton for the will which had made Laura's wealth a barrier between them" ([028-003])

A retrospective judgment from late in the novel, linking Jasper Treverton's legal act to the cosmic force of [[fate]] itself.

## Literary Significance

Jasper Treverton is a variation on a familiar Victorian type — the wealthy, eccentric patriarch — but Braddon's treatment is distinctive in two ways. First, she kills him off almost immediately, making him a posthumous presence whose influence is felt entirely through his legal instrument ([[inheritance|the will]]) and his emotional legacy (the attachments and resentments he cultivated in life). Second, she makes his will not merely a plot device but a philosophical statement about the nature of [[identity]]: by conditioning [[inheritance]] on marriage, Jasper Treverton ties the legal question of "who inherits" to the existential question of "who are you, really?"
