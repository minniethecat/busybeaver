# Edward Clare

Son of the Vicar of Hazlehurst, aspiring poet, and [[john-treverton|John Treverton]]'s bitter rival -- Edward Clare is the suitor who loses [[laura-malcolm|Laura Malcolm]] to the novel's hero and whose [[jealousy]] drives him to become the novel's chief antagonist, relentlessly investigating Treverton's secret past in a quest for vengeance masquerading as chivalrous concern.

## Role in the Novel

Edward Clare functions as the primary human antagonist of the novel's second half, the "village Iago" whose personal [[jealousy]] fuels the plot's central conflict. First mentioned in Chapter 3, he enters the story as an unseen rival -- the vicar's son known to be in love with [[laura-malcolm|Laura Malcolm]] -- before appearing in person in Chapter 5. His role evolves from disappointed suitor to jealous observer, and finally to active investigator who seeks to expose [[john-treverton|John Treverton]]'s [[identity-deception|hidden identity]] as [[jack-chicot|Jack Chicot]], thereby annulling Laura's marriage and stripping Treverton of the Hazlehurst estate. [PN=005-025] [PN=022-001]

Braddon positions Edward as the dark double of [[john-treverton|John Treverton]]: both men seek the Hazlehurst [[inheritance|fortune]] through marriage to Laura, but where Treverton ultimately proves worthy through genuine love, Edward's affection is shown to be hollow, his true object being [[inheritance|the estate]] rather than the woman. His investigation chapters (15, 22, 28) form the detective thread of the sensation plot, transforming private [[jealousy]] into public accusation. The narrator explicitly frames his hatred as a perversion of the civilised man who can "take all they can get from a man, and go on detesting him cordially to the end." [PN=028-003]

## Biography

### Background

Edward Clare is the only son of [[mr-clare|Mr. Clare]], the Vicar of Hazlehurst, and the brother of [[celia-clare|Celia Clare]], [[laura-malcolm|Laura Malcolm]]'s closest friend. He was educated at Oxford with a view to entering the Church, but failed to get his degree after three years and discovered a "conscientious repugnance" to holy orders, citing his admiration for Ernest Renan -- the French rationalist who wrote a sceptical Life of Jesus -- as grounds. [PN=005-026] [PN=005-027]

After leaving the University, Edward returned to the Vicarage and led an indolent life: reading desultorily, writing occasional verses, sketching, fishing, shooting, and "dawdling away life in the pleasantest manner." His father considered him a disappointment, "a thorn in his side," lamenting that "his cleverness allowed him to leave the University without a degree, and his artistic faculties will never help him to a living." Edward's self-assessment was far more generous -- he believed himself a poet of rare gifts waiting only for a proper occasion to make his mark. [PN=005-025] [PN=005-028]

Physically, Braddon describes him as "of the middle height, slim almost to fragility, yet with a compactness of form which indicated activity and possibly strength" -- a face "very handsome in the estimation of some people, but which assuredly erred on the side of effeminacy," suited to "the velvet and brocade of one of the French Henry's minions." This effeminate handsomeness contrasts sharply with [[john-treverton|John Treverton]]'s virile charm. [PN=007-138]

### Key Actions

- **Cultivating [[laura-malcolm|Laura Malcolm]] (pre-novel).** For three years before the novel opens, Edward was a constant visitor at the [[hazlehurst-manor|Manor House]], cultivating Laura's friendship while hesitating to declare himself. His caution was strategic: he believed [[jasper-treverton|Jasper Treverton]] would leave Laura a [[inheritance|fortune]], so marrying her would secure both a beautiful wife and wealth. He held back from a proposal only because rejection would mean banishment from her society. [PN=005-029] [PN=005-051]

- **Resenting the heir (Chapters 5--7).** When [[john-treverton|John Treverton]] arrives as the designated heir who must marry Laura, Edward's [[jealousy|envy]] flares. He speculates darkly that Treverton must have "some entanglement -- some degrading connection, perhaps" that restrains him from courting Laura promptly, and declares his "instinctive idea that he is a scoundrel." [PN=005-035] [PN=005-043]

- **Departing for [[london|London]] (Chapter 11).** After Laura's engagement to Treverton, Edward leaves Hazlehurst for [[london|London]], telling [[celia-clare|Celia]] he will take lodgings near the British Museum and "work hard at literature." Laura feels "a sense of relief in his absence." [PN=011-039] [PN=011-041]

- **Discovering the likeness (Chapter 15).** In a key turning point, Edward encounters [[jack-chicot|Jack Chicot]], the caricaturist, at a magazine office in [[london|London]] and is struck by his "wondrous likeness of [[john-treverton|John Treverton]]." Learning that Chicot is the husband of the dancer [[la-chicot|La Chicot]], Edward begins piecing together the secret of Treverton's double [[identity]]. He interviews [[desrolles|Desrolles]], a shabby-genteel acquaintance of Chicot, and attends [[la-chicot|La Chicot]]'s performance to observe her world. [PN=015-038] [PN=015-079]

- **The dinner-party confrontation (Chapter 22).** Having returned to Hazlehurst, Edward attends the Trevertons' dinner party and provocatively steers conversation to the Chicot [[murder-concept|murder]], insinuating that the husband must be the killer. When Laura catches "a look of malice" on his face directed at her husband -- "only in the face of Judas in an old Italian picture had she ever beheld such craft and such venom" -- she recoils with a "sense of absolute fear." [PN=022-058] [PN=022-059]

- **Engineering a plot (Chapters 22--28).** Edward becomes a regular visitor at the [[hazlehurst-manor|Manor House]], enjoying Treverton's hospitality while secretly plotting his ruin. He presses the question of photography to obtain an image that could identify Treverton as Chicot, then travels to [[london|London]] on the pretext of seeing a theatre manager, but in reality to enlist the aid of [[george-gerard|George Gerard]] -- the surgeon who attended [[la-chicot|La Chicot]] and who can personally identify her husband. [PN=028-006] [PN=028-106]

- **Bringing Gerard to Hazlehurst (Chapter 28).** By recounting the story of [[jasper-treverton|Jasper Treverton]]'s will to Gerard, Edward persuades the surgeon to travel to Devonshire and identify [[john-treverton|John Treverton]] as [[jack-chicot|Jack Chicot]]. His stated motive is to "rescue the woman I once fondly loved" from a bigamous marriage, but his deeper drive is hatred, [[jealousy|envy]], and the need to destroy the man who won what Edward wanted. [PN=028-106]

### Fate / Resolution

Edward Clare's investigation ultimately fails in its malicious objective. Though he successfully brings Gerard to Hazlehurst, the [[the-discovery|revelation]] of [[john-treverton|John Treverton]]'s true [[identity]] does not destroy him as Edward hoped. Instead, Treverton's legal marriage to Laura is validated when it is discovered that his first "marriage" to [[la-chicot|La Chicot]] was never legal -- she was already the wife of Jean Kergariou. Edward's plot collapses, and he fades from the narrative, his hatred having accomplished nothing but anxiety for the Trevertons and humiliation for himself. [PN=038-003]

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

Edward Clare is driven by [[jealousy|envy]] and wounded vanity more than by love. The narrator makes this explicit: "He had wanted Laura and [[hazlehurst-manor|Hazlehurst Manor]]: not Laura with her modest income of two hundred and fifty pounds a year." When he imagines himself in [[john-treverton|John Treverton]]'s position, his fantasies are of "chambers in the Albany, furnished on the purest aesthetic principles," a yacht, and hunters -- not of Laura's companionship. [PN=028-003] [PN=015-022]

His pursuit of Treverton's secret is self-deception elevated to an art form. He tells himself -- and Gerard -- that he acts from "chivalrous ardour in the defence of the woman he had loved" and "a generous desire to serve her." The narrator immediately undercuts this: "this hatred wore such a noble aspect in the man's own mind." In truth, he cannot bear that another man has the [[inheritance|fortune]] and the wife he failed to secure. [PN=028-003]

Edward also represents the psychology of the failed artist. His literary ambitions have come to nothing; he has "found the gates of literature so beset with aspirants, many of them as richly endowed as himself, that the idea of pushing his way across the threshold seemed almost hopeless." His creative sterility feeds his destructive impulses -- if he cannot create, he will destroy. [PN=022-002]

### Role in the Mystery

Edward Clare is the catalyst who transforms [[john-treverton|John Treverton]]'s secret from a private burden into a public crisis. Without Edward's investigation, the [[identity]] of [[jack-chicot|Jack Chicot]] and the question of Treverton's possible guilt in [[la-chicot|La Chicot]]'s [[murder-concept|murder]] would remain hidden from the Hazlehurst community and from the reader.

His method is [[class-and-status|class]]ic amateur detection: he notices a physical resemblance, follows leads, interviews witnesses ([[desrolles|Desrolles]], [[mrs-evitt|Mrs. Evitt]]), and recruits an expert identifier (Gerard). But his investigation is fatally tainted by his motive -- he seeks not truth but destruction, and his selective reading of the evidence (insisting Chicot must be the [[murder-concept|murder]]er, dismissing alternative explanations) reflects his bias. In this sense, Edward functions as a distorted mirror of sensation fiction's detective figure: same methods, opposite moral compass.

>! His scheme ultimately backfires: by forcing the exposure of Treverton's past, he inadvertently leads to the discovery that Treverton's marriage to La Chicot was never legal, thereby securing rather than destroying the Hazlehurst inheritance.

## Portrayal in the Text

> "Edward Clare was everybody's favourite, and nobody's enemy but his own. That was what the village said of him. He was good-looking, clever, agreeable, but he had no ballast. He was a feather to be blown by every puff of wind." [PN=005-025]

> "The ways of civilisation are not as the ways of the desert. There are men and women whose animosity is not to be appeased by kindness -- who will take all they can get from a man, and go on detesting him cordially to the end. Edward Clare, the sleek, white-handed poet, possessed this constancy in hatred." [PN=028-003]

> "What a loss to society," sneered Edward Clare. "I think you are the most ridiculous girl in the world, Celia, to interest yourself in people who are as far off your groove as if they were the inhabitants of the moon." [PN=007-155]

> "A fellow of average talent. Imagine one of Mr. Swinburne's most facile plagiarists hearing himself called a fellow of average talent." [PN=022-005]

## Significance

Edward Clare embodies several thematic functions. As a rival suitor, he provides the romantic tension that tests Laura's choice and Treverton's worth. As an antagonist, he drives the mystery plot forward, converting private secrets into public jeopardy. As a character study, he illustrates Braddon's interest in the psychology of [[jealousy|envy]]: the man who believes himself superior and is enraged by the success of those he considers beneath him.

He also serves as a critique of the self-deluding artist figure. Edward's poetic pretensions -- he "would not yoke his noble mind to the newspaper plough" and refuses to write children's books or prose -- are exposed as excuses for indolence. His real talent is not for poetry but for malice, and his most sustained creative act is the destruction of another man's happiness. In sensation fiction terms, he is the [[class-and-status|gentleman]] villain: respectably [[identity|disguise]]d, socially embedded, and more dangerous for his proximity to the domestic sphere.

The contrast between Edward and [[john-treverton|John Treverton]] structures the novel's moral universe: the man who pretends love for gain versus the man who discovers love unexpectedly; the man who cannot work versus the man who has struggled for his living; the man who takes hospitality as a prelude to betrayal versus the man who earns his place through genuine affection.
