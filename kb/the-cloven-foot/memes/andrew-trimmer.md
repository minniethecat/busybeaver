# Andrew Trimmer

Andrew Trimmer is the elderly butler at [[hazlehurst-manor|Hazlehurst Manor]], a loyal family retainer who has served [[jasper-treverton|Jasper Treverton]] for more than thirty years. Along with his wife, who serves as cook and housekeeper, Trimmer represents the long-established domestic order of the [[hazlehurst-manor|Manor House]] — a world of faithful service, settled routine, and proprietary interest in the family's [[inheritance|fortune]]s that is unsettled by the novel's disruptions of [[inheritance]] and [[identity]].

## Role in the Novel

Trimmer appears at key domestic moments in the Hazlehurst chapters, functioning as a barometer of the household's mood and a voice of servant-[[class-and-status|class]] common sense. His observations on the evolving relationship between [[john-treverton|John Treverton]] and [[laura-malcolm|Laura Malcolm]] provide a ground-level perspective on [[inheritance|the will]]'s consequences, while his anxieties about his own future — contingent as it is on [[inheritance|the estate]] remaining in private hands — dramatise the wider social stakes of the [[inheritance]] plot.

## Biography

### Background

Andrew Trimmer has been butler at [[hazlehurst-manor|Hazlehurst Manor]] for over thirty years, having entered service under [[jasper-treverton|Jasper Treverton]]. He lifted [[laura-malcolm|Laura Malcolm]] out of the carriage when his master first brought her to the Manor as a delicate child [009-053]. His wife, [[mrs-trimmer|Mrs. Trimmer]], has been cook and housekeeper for nearly as long. Together they form the core of the old servant establishment, deeply identified with "the family" and its [[inheritance|fortune]]s.

### Key Actions

- Shows [[john-treverton|John Treverton]] to his room on the night of his arrival at [[hazlehurst-manor|Hazlehurst Manor]], appearing with a candle at his master's summons [001-099].
- Receives a small annuity in [[jasper-treverton|Jasper Treverton]]'s will alongside sums left to the other [[the-servants|servants]] [002-006].
- Serves [[john-treverton|John Treverton]] and [[laura-malcolm|Laura Malcolm]] at luncheon during their betrothal period, observing that "they looked just for all the world like man and wife" [009-053].
- Expresses relief that the couple seem fond of one another, hoping "before long it'll be that" — meaning marriage — because the alternative would be catastrophic for the [[the-servants|household staff]] [009-054].
- Voices his anxieties plainly: "if they don't marry, and [[inheritance|the estate]] goes to build a norsepital, what's to become of you and me?" [009-056].
- Defends his loyalty against the charge of being a "moneygrubber," maintaining that "a good 'ome ain't to be trifled with" and that he knows "when I'm well off" [009-056].
- On the morning of the wedding, offers a solemn interpretation of [[john-treverton|John Treverton]]'s nervous demeanour, opining that "perhaps a wedding ain't altogether the comfortablest day in a man's life" [011-064].
- Welcomes Laura Treverton back to the [[hazlehurst-manor|Manor House]] after her husband's return, promising to make everything "spick and span" [037-007].

### Fate / Resolution

Trimmer's [[fate]] is bound up with the survival of the Hazlehurst estate as a private dwelling. The novel leaves him in service at the [[hazlehurst-manor|Manor House]], his position preserved by the successful (if troubled) marriage of John and Laura Treverton. His anxieties about the "norsepital" — the hospital that would have been built had the marriage not taken place — are never realised.

## Character Analysis

### Role in the Mystery

Trimmer has no direct role in the novel's criminal mystery, but his position as a long-serving family retainer gives him knowledge of the household's history that could prove significant. His thirty years of service span [[jasper-treverton|Jasper Treverton]]'s entire tenure at the Manor, including the period of the mysterious oath that shapes [[inheritance|the will]]'s conditions.

## Portrayal in the Text

> "They looked just for all the world like man and wife," said Trimmer, when he went back to his pantry, "and I hope before long it'll be that. They'll make a fine couple, and I'm sure they're fond o' one another already." [009-054]

> "Well, if I was young woman I'd marry a'most anybody rather than I'd lose such a 'ome as Hazlehurst Manor," answered Trimmer. "I ain't a moneygrubber, but a good 'ome ain't to be trifled with." [009-056]

## Significance

Andrew Trimmer belongs to the long tradition of loyal family [[the-servants|servants]] in Victorian fiction — characters who serve as confidants, commentators, and guardians of domestic order. His practical anxieties about wages and security remind the reader that the melodramatic machinery of wills and [[inheritance]] has material consequences for people far below the Trevertons' social station. His dropped aspirates (which the narrator notes he only lets slip "now and then when he was impressive") mark his [[class-and-status|class]] position while also distinguishing him from comic-butler stereotypes: Trimmer is a man of genuine dignity and shrewd observation.
