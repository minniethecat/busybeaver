# The Hunt for Desrolles

The climactic pursuit and death of [[desrolles|Desrolles]] in Paris constitutes the final movement of *The Cloven Foot*'s mystery plot. Tracked by an English detective from London, cornered in his squalid fifth-storey room in the Students' Quarter, and killed in a desperate struggle — hurled through a rotting lath-and-plaster wall to his death on the pavement below — Desrolles's demise provides both a spectacular set-piece and a morally conclusive end for the novel's villain.

## Summary

After blackmailing the Trevertons at Hazlehurst Manor and receiving a promise of a thousand pounds a year (026-131 to 026-132), Desrolles fled to Paris to enjoy his newfound wealth. He took up residence in his old haunt in the Students' Quarter, drank absinthe, gambled, and planned an escape to South America under the alias "Colonel Mowbray," booking passage on the steamship *Reine Blanche* bound for Valparaiso (045-034 to 045-036). But he was unaware that the resumption of the Treverton murder inquiry had led to a reward being advertised for his whereabouts (040-071), and that an English detective had been tracking him. On the night of his intended departure, the detective confronted him in his room. During the ensuing struggle, Desrolles drew a knife and fought with animal desperation. The detective hurled him against the wall — and "the frail lath and plaster partition split asunder, the rotten wood crumbled," sending Desrolles "backwards into empty air" (045-062). He died on the pavement below, his body "so battered and disfigured by that awful fall as to be hardly recognisable" (045-063).

## Context

### Preceding Events

The chain of events leading to Desrolles's death began with the resumption of the inquiry into [[the-murder-of-la-chicot|La Chicot's murder]]. After John Treverton's arrest and his first appearance at the Bow Street Police-court, the investigation widened. The Trevertons — John and Laura — had been paying Desrolles a handsome annuity to keep silent about his knowledge of Jack Chicot's true identity and the circumstances of the murder. But as the legal inquiry intensified, a reward of ten pounds was advertised for information leading to Desrolles's whereabouts (040-071). Meanwhile, [[Mrs. Evitt]]'s later testimony — revealing the tuft of iron-grey hair found in La Chicot's dead hand matching Desrolles's hair, and the discovery of his bloodstained chintz dressing-gown (041-109 to 041-123) — had turned suspicion decisively toward the second-floor lodger.

Desrolles, learning of the renewed inquiry from an English newspaper in Paris, felt the ground shifting beneath him. The report of John Treverton's arrest made him consider "the prudence of going further afield" (045-030). He settled on South America as his destination and booked passage.

## Detailed Account

Desrolles had entered Paris "with ninety-five pounds in his pocket, and a promise of a thousand a year" (045-004), intending it as only the first stage of a grand European tour. But Paris proved irresistible: "She stretched out her many arms to detain and hold him, like an octopus" (045-003). He took a room in his old lodging-house in the Students' Quarter, on the fifth storey — "an infernal way up," he complained, but he was "a staunch Conservative" who liked "old quarters" (045-021).

His life quickly degenerated. He had come "with the intention of curing himself of brandy" but "cured himself of brandy by taking to absinthe" (045-004). He spent his money "with royal recklessness, believing himself secure against all future evils" (045-025). He gambled, drank, and found new companions whose "tongues were as foul as his own, and who looked at life in this world and the next from the same standpoint" (045-024).

The turning point came "when one morning chance flung an English newspaper in his way, and he read the report of John Treverton's first appearance at the Bow Street Police-court" (045-025). The news disturbed him deeply: "There was no knowing what might not happen. An investigation of this kind once commenced, might extend its ramifications in the widest directions" (045-028). He resolved to flee to South America and booked passage on the *Reine Blanche*, buying an outfit of new clothes and a portmanteau for the voyage.

At the shipping office, drunk on absinthe, he almost gave himself away. When the clerk asked his name, "he answered without thinking — Desrolles; but in the next moment a ray of light flashed through the darkness of his clouded brain, and he corrected himself" to "Colonel Mowbray, citizen, United States" (045-037 to 045-038).

Desrolles did not know that his new Parisian friend was "a distinguished member of the Parisian swell-mob" constantly under police surveillance, nor that "he himself had been watched and followed by an English detective ever since he left the Quai des Grands Augustins" (045-046). The detective "knew all about Mr. Desrolles' intended voyage in the *Reine Blanche*" (045-046).

The confrontation came after midnight. Desrolles returned to his lodging "not too steady of foot" (045-047). He managed to unlock his door and light a candle, but "when a man, who had crept softly into the room behind him, laid a strong hand upon his shoulder, he collapsed" (045-048).

> "You," answered the intruder in English. "I arrest you on suspicion of being concerned in the murder of La Chicot. You know all about it. You were examined at the inquest. Anything you say now will be used as evidence against you. You had better come quietly with me." (045-049)

Desrolles's reaction was feral — "a wild beast — a beast of a low type, not your kingly lion or your lordly tiger — at bay and knowing escape impossible" (045-053). He drew a knife — "a murderous one" — and the two men closed "hand against hand, limb against limb, Desrolles wrestling with his foe as only rage and despair can wrestle" (045-055).

> He had been a famous bruiser in days of old. Tonight he had the unnatural strength given the overtasked sinews by a mind on the edge of madness. He fought like a madman: he fought like a tiger. There was not a muscle — not a sinew — that was not strained to its utmost in that savage conflict. (045-056)

The detective, who had lied about having backup — "The French policeman who had planned to meet him at that house at midnight had not yet come" (045-057) — found himself fighting for his life. Desrolles drove him toward the open doorway; one more thrust would send him "backwards down the steep staircase — certain death to the intruder" (045-060).

But the detective "gathered himself together, and with a sudden impetus, flinging all his weight against Desrolles, he drove his foe before him across the narrow room, hurled him with all his might against the wall" (045-061). What happened next was catastrophic and utterly unforeseen:

> But as that tall figure fell with terrific force against the gaudy-papered wall, there was a sudden crashing sound, at which the detective recoiled with a cry of horror. The frail lath and plaster partition split asunder, the rotten wood crumbled and scattered itself in a cloud of dust, half that side of the room dropped into ruin, as if the house had been a house of cards, and, with one hoarse shriek, Desrolles rolled backwards into empty air. (045-062)

Desrolles fell from the fifth storey. "In falling he had struck against the timbers that shored up the rotten old house, and life had been beaten out of him before he touched the stones below" (045-063). The narrator's epitaph is terse and final: "It was a bad end of a bad man. There was nobody to be sorry for him, except the detective, who had lost the chance of a handsome reward" (045-063).

## Consequences

Desrolles's death resolves the novel's central mystery in the most definitive way possible. The murderer is dead — not through the orderly processes of law (trial, conviction, execution) but through a spectacular accident that carries its own poetic justice. The rotten wall that crumbles under his weight literalizes his own moral decay: the man and the building collapse together, both structurally unsound.

For John Treverton, the death means exoneration. With Desrolles dead and the evidence of his guilt — the iron-grey hair, the bloodstained dressing-gown — now public, the cloud of suspicion that has hung over Treverton since [[the-murder-of-la-chicot|La Chicot's murder]] is finally lifted. The Parisian newspapers reported the event as "Fall of part of a house in the Boulevard Louis Capet. Horrible death of one of the inmates" (045-064), while the English newspapers followed with "the account of the pursuit and arrest of Desrolles, his desperate resistance, and awful death" (045-065).

The death also frees the Trevertons financially. The annuity they had been paying to Desrolles — extracted through blackmail — is no longer a burden. The secret he carried dies with him, and John Treverton is at last able to live openly as the master of Hazlehurst Manor.

## Significance

### Plot Function

The hunt for Desrolles is the novel's climactic action sequence, providing the violent resolution that the legal proceedings (the inquest, the police-court appearances, the adjourned inquiries) could not deliver. Braddon shifts the scene from English courtrooms and drawing-rooms to the Parisian demi-monde, giving the final chapters a different register — more kinetic, more lurid, more Continental. The death by falling, with its symbolic overtones of structural collapse and deserved ruin, satisfies the sensation novel's requirement for spectacular justice.

### Thematic Resonance

Desrolles's death is the novel's most concentrated expression of its architectural metaphor. The house that shelters him — "spared from demolition by accident only," its "doom... fixed" (045-006), its next-door neighbour "razed to the ground" (045-007) — mirrors the man himself: a decaying remnant of an earlier era, propped up by temporary shoring, destined for destruction. When the wall gives way, it is not mere accident but a kind of moral physics: the rottenness of the man and the rottenness of his environment collapse into each other.

The death also realizes the novel's themes of exposure and unmasking. Desrolles, the man of multiple aliases — Desrolles, Mansfield, Malcolm, Desmond, Mowbray — dies under his true name, arrested as "Desrolles" for the murder of La Chicot. His final alias ("Colonel Mowbray") and his planned escape to Valparaiso are thwarted at the last moment. The man who hid among the witnesses at [[the-inquest|the inquest]], who stood "decently grave and sympathetic" at La Chicot's funeral (018-043), who blackmailed the Trevertons under the guise of a grieving father — is finally exposed and destroyed in the most public way possible, his body lying on a Parisian boulevard for all to see.
