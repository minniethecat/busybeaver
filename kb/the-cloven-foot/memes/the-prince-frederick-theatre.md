# The Prince Frederick Theatre

The Royal Prince Frederick Theatre and Music Hall is the London variety theatre where [[La-Chicot]] performs as *premiere danseuse* after leaving Paris. Owned and managed by the enterprising Mr. Smolendo, it is a flashy, popular house catering to a broad metropolitan audience — and it is here that La Chicot's career reaches its apex and begins its fatal decline.

## Location

The Prince Frederick Theatre is situated in "a dingy street" in the neighbourhood of Leicester Square, "near which, as everyone knows, the Prince Frederick is situated" (004-024). This places it in close proximity to Cibber Street, where the Chicots take lodgings on their arrival in London. The theatre is thus embedded in the same densely populated, dramatic, and transient quarter of central London that Braddon renders as the novel's urban underworld. The exact street is not named, but the theatre is consistently linked to the Leicester Square district and the "close and smoky region" (008-081) that surrounds it.

## Description

The Prince Frederick is not a high-art establishment but a popular music hall and variety theatre. Braddon introduces it through the perspective of Mr. Smolendo, its proprietor:

> "Mr. Smolendo, the enterprising proprietor of the Prince Frederick Theatre, who had come to Paris on purpose to engage La Chicot" (004-018)

The theatre's aesthetic is sensational rather than refined. Its posters — "flaming posters" with "gigantic yellow capitals on a black ground" — advertise La Chicot's name across London (004-063, 008-014):

> "Her name was on the walls in capitals three feet high, her bold bright face smiled on the foot passenger at every turn in the road" (008-014)

The theatre's interior is equipped with elaborate stage machinery, including "a telescopic iron apparatus" used to lift La Chicot through an artificial "coral cave" — a contraption that catastrophically collapses during a performance, causing her near-fatal injury (008-023). The stage apparatus is described as rising "by an arrangement of weights and pullies, through a brilliant and airy fabric representing a coral cave, all pink and silver stalactites, reflected in a sea of silver foil" (008-023). The theatre's scene-shifters are later reported to have been drunk (008-015, 008-023).

The audience at the Prince Frederick is described as favouring extremes: they "liked extremes, and applauded the audacity which would have been hissed at a more dignified house" (008-021). La Chicot's dancing, "more and more like a Thracian Maenad" (004-011), suits this audience perfectly.

## Significance in the Novel

### Plot Function

The Prince Frederick is where La Chicot's London career unfolds and where several crucial plot threads converge. Mr. Smolendo engages her in Paris and brings her to London, promising she will "take the town by storm" (004-020). Her success is immediate: "the theatre was crowded nightly, and La Chicot — the new dancer — was the rage" (004-063).

Among the frequenters of the Prince Frederick is an anonymous admirer — later revealed as [[joseph-lemuel]] — who watches her "night after night" and whose obsessive interest leads to the gifts of champagne and jewellery that provoke jealousy in Jack Chicot (004-064, 004-065). The theatre thus becomes the indirect catalyst for the tensions that culminate in violence at Cibber Street.

The stage accident at the Prince Frederick is a major turning point: the telescopic iron apparatus collapses, causing La Chicot a compound leg fracture and suspected brain injury that leave her hovering between life and death for months (008-021 to 008-023). This accident, caused by a drunken scene-shifter, marks the beginning of her physical decline and foreshadows her violent death.

### Thematic Role

The Prince Frederick Theatre embodies the sensation aesthetic in architectural form — all flaming posters, mechanical spectacle, and audacious performance. It is a temple to the culture of display and excess that Braddon's novel both exploits and critiques. The theatre's "extremes" (008-021) mirror La Chicot's own trajectory: rising from provincial obscurity to celebrity, then falling into drunkenness, injury, and violent death. The malfunctioning stage machinery — a spectacle that literally collapses — symbolizes the instability of the glamorous surface that defines her life.

The theatre also functions as a site of class transgression and urban anonymity. "All the ranks of life mingled in the stalls at the Prince Frederick" (006-008), making it a space where the wealthy Joseph Lemuel can observe La Chicot without revealing his identity, and where Jack Chicot's gentlemanly bearing goes unremarked.

### Key Areas

- **The stage**: Equipped with elaborate scenic effects, including the "coral cave" ascending apparatus; the site of La Chicot's performances and her near-fatal accident.
- **The pit**: Where Edward Clare later goes "to see Mademoiselle Chicot" as part of his investigation into John Treverton's past (015-080).
- **The dressing room**: Where La Chicot drinks a bottle of champagne — and later brandy — before each performance (006-005).
- **The stalls**: Where Joseph Lemuel watches La Chicot night after night, invisibly shaping her fate (004-065).
