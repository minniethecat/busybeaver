# Greed

The hunger for wealth that pervades *The Cloven Foot* from its opening pages — John Treverton's "almost savage longing" for his cousin's possessions (001-065) to Desrolles's negotiation of a "handsome annuity" as the price of his silence (045-002) — and the sensation novel's unflinching depiction of how financial desire corrupts every human relationship it touches.

## Definition

In *The Cloven Foot*, greed is not a simple character flaw but a structural force that shapes the novel's entire plot. Every central character's motivations are defined in relation to money: John Treverton's inheritance hopes, Desrolles's blackmail scheme, La Chicot's rapacious spending, and even Laura Malcolm's marital consent are all entangled with financial calculation. Jasper Treverton, the dying recluse who controls the fortune, explicitly names the dynamic when he tells his heir: "My death will make you a rich man" (001-076). Braddon presents greed not as an aberration within Victorian society but as its unacknowledged operating principle — the suppressed truth beneath the surface of respectability, courtship, and familial duty.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **John Treverton's Avaricious Longing (Ch.1)** : Before learning the terms of the will, John surveys Treverton Manor and is seized by an "almost savage longing" for his cousin's wealth. He meditates on what it would mean to inherit "only half of his cousin's possessions" and escape his "wretched hand to mouth existence" (001-065). This passage establishes greed as John's initial response to Hazlehurst — a raw, pre-moral hunger that the will's conditional terms will later complicate but never erase.
- **Jasper Treverton's Will as Financial Coercion (Ch.2)** : The will conditions John's inheritance on his marriage to Laura Malcolm within one year (002-006), transforming a love-match into a financial transaction. John feels his position "indescribably awkward" and wonders what he can say "that would not seem dictated by the most sordid motive" (002-031). The inheritance of an estate worth eleven thousand a year in land, with additional holdings bringing the total to fourteen thousand (003-002), makes refusal financially unthinkable.
- **La Chicot's Conspicuous Consumption (Ch.4)** : The ballet dancer is "fond of money, but only of money to spend in the immediate present; money for fine dresses, good dinners, wine that foamed and sparkled" (004-008). She spends lavishly on "the gross, sensual pleasures of dining and drinking" with no thought for "money for the future, for sickness, for old age" (004-008). Her greed is the immediate, unreflective greed of appetite — consumption without calculation.
- **Desrolles's Criminal Rapacity (Ch.45)** : The murderer's scheme is reduced to a cash transaction: "When Desrolles left the village under the shadow of Dartmoor, after bargaining for a handsome annuity, he meant to enter upon a new and delightful stage of existence" (045-002). Every human relationship, every moral consideration, collapses into a price — the annuity becomes the novel's starkest symbol of greed unmoored from any ethical constraint.
- **Laura Malcolm's Resistance to Mercenary Marriage (Ch.3)** : Laura Malcolm openly declares that she "would rather forego this fortune than force a hateful alliance" (003-075), distinguishing herself from the mercenary motives that govern everyone around her. Yet her very protest acknowledges the gravitational pull of the money — the fourteen thousand a year (003-008) is always the third party in every intimate conversation between John and Laura. John's own fears of being seen as "mercenary" (011-044) reveal how thoroughly financial calculation has contaminated the language of love.
- **Thomas Sampson's "Ardent Love of Moneymaking" (Ch.2)** : The young solicitor, described as possessing "an ardent love of moneymaking" (002-038), represents greed in its respectable, professionalized form. His own courtship calculations — weighing a farmer's daughter with "a tidy little bit of money" against Celia Clare — demonstrate that avarice operates at every social level, from the Treverton fortune to a provincial lawyer's marriage prospects.

## Key Passages

> Throughout the house there were indications of wealth that inspired an almost savage longing in this man's mind. What a changed life his would be if he should inherit only half of his cousin's possessions. He thought, with a weary sigh, of the wretched hand to mouth existence that he had led of late years, and then thought of the things that he would do if he came in for any share of the old man's money. He sat meditating thus until the clock in the great hall struck two, with a tone that had a certain sullen horror in its sound. (001-065)

> This was about as much as he would venture to say at this early stage of affairs. He felt his position indescribably awkward, and he wondered at Laura Malcolm's composure. What ought he to say or do? What could he say that would not seem dictated by the most sordid motive? What disinterested feeling could there ever arise between those two, who were bound together by their common interest in a great estate, who met as strangers to find themselves suddenly dependent upon each other's caprice? (002-031)

> La Chicot was not of a calculating temper. She was fond of money, but only of money to spend in the immediate present; money for fine dresses, good dinners, wine that foamed and sparkled, and plenty of promenading in hired carriages in the Bois de Boulogne. Money for the future, for sickness, for old age, for the innumerable necessities of life, she never thought of. (004-008)

> When Desrolles left the village under the shadow of Dartmoor, after bargaining for a handsome annuity, he meant to enter upon a new and delightful stage of existence. The world was changed for him. Assured of a handsome income, he felt as it were, newborn. He could go to Paris, to Vienna, to any of the cities in which a man may lead the life of dissipation he loved. (045-002)

| Thomas Sampson | The respectable face of avarice — a provincial solicitor with "an ardent love of moneymaking" (002-038) whose calculations reveal greed operating within the bounds of law and local society |

## Critical Perspective

Braddon's treatment of greed in *The Cloven Foot* is characteristic of the sensation novel's engagement with Victorian money-anxieties. Where earlier domestic fiction treats avarice as a simple moral failing — punished in villains, renounced by heroes — Braddon embeds greed so deeply in the novel's structure that no character escapes its taint. John Treverton is neither hero nor villain but a man whose better instincts are perpetually undermined by financial calculation. The conditional will functions as a diabolical device that makes disinterested love impossible: between two people "bound together by their common interest in a great estate" (002-031), every word risks sounding "dictated by the most sordid motive" (002-031).

The novel's title, *The Cloven Foot*, invokes the diabolical — and the devil's work in this story is accomplished not through supernatural means but through the corrosive force of money. La Chicot's murder is the logical endpoint of a world in which everything has a price: her killer negotiates his exit from England as a financial transaction (045-002), just as her husband had once bartered his self-respect for her earnings. Even the novel's provincial subplots — Thomas Sampson's matrimonial arithmetic, the vicar's careful counsels about settlements and income — reveal greed as a ubiquitous, low-grade hum beneath every social interaction. Braddon, who wrote at extraordinary speed to support her own household, knew firsthand that in Victorian England money was not an abstraction but the medium in which all lives were lived.
