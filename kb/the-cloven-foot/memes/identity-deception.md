# Identity Deception

The narrative engine of *The Cloven Foot* — the systematic deployment of concealed identities, assumed names, and hidden pasts that drives the novel's murder mystery and exposes the fragility of Victorian social identity.

## Definition

In *The Cloven Foot*, identity deception functions not merely as a plot mechanism but as the novel's central thematic preoccupation. Nearly every major character operates under a false or suppressed identity: John Treverton hides his past as Jack Chicot; Desrolles moves through the novel under multiple aliases; La Chicot conceals the nature of her marriage; and the murder investigation itself turns on whether the suspected caricaturist and the respectable heir are in fact the same man. Braddon constructs a world in which "one may lose one's identity" (004-023), where social identity proves as erasable and reassignable as a stage name.

The novel's title invokes the diabolical — the cloven hoof that reveals the devil beneath a pleasing exterior — and this metaphor governs its treatment of identity: every character with a respectable surface threatens to reveal a hidden, often criminal, self beneath. Identity deception in *The Cloven Foot* is thus both a practical strategy for survival and an index of moral corruption, the "cloven foot" that betrays the concealed self.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **John Treverton / Jack Chicot (dual identity)** : The novel's central identity deception. The heir to the Treverton estate lived for years in London as "Jack Chicot," a struggling caricaturist married to a former ballet dancer. When he returns to claim his inheritance, he must suppress this past entirely — his Bohemian life "under an assumed name" creates "no small sensation" when revealed (046-001). The alias becomes the pivot of the murder investigation: "I, John Treverton, alias Chicot" (031-024) is the confession upon which the plot turns.

- **Desrolles / Mansfield / Malcolm (multiple aliases)** : The novel's most prolific identity deceiver. "Mr. Desrolles, alias Mansfield, alias Malcolm" (040-062) operates under at least three names, each linked to a different phase of his criminal career. His aliases are not protective coloration but instruments of fraud, enabling him to move freely through the novel's social world while evading the consequences of his past actions.

- **La Chicot's concealed marriage** : The dancer Zaïre Chicot constructs a public identity as a glamorous theatre performer while hiding the "false position" of her marriage to Jack (019-017). Her identity deception is both social survival — maintaining theatrical status despite domestic squalor — and the proximate cause of her death.

- **The Mysterious Visitor (Ch.3)** : A man with "the lower part of his face hidden in the folds of a woollen scarf" (003-044) visits Hazelhurst under cover of darkness, his identity deliberately obscured. This visual motif of concealment — the hidden face, the secret visit — establishes the novel's governing pattern of obscured identity from its opening chapters.

- **The Inquest and the Identity Question (Ch.27)** : The novel's legal and public investigation explicitly frames identity as a question to be resolved: "You deny the identity of John Treverton and Chicot, the caricaturist" (027-042). Identity becomes not a fact but a proposition to be proven or disproven, with the machinery of the law deployed to decide who a person "really" is.

- **The Cloven Foot as Identity Metaphor** : The novel's title provides its governing figure for identity deception. As the devil's cloven hoof reveals his true nature beneath a pleasing human form, so the concealed identities of Treverton, Desrolles, and others are the "cloven foot" that betrays the hidden self — the mark that the respectable surface cannot fully suppress.

## Critical Perspective

Braddon's treatment of identity deception in *The Cloven Foot* belongs to the sensation novel's broader preoccupation with the instability of social identity in Victorian Britain. The mid-Victorian period witnessed acute anxiety about the knowability of persons: urbanization created conditions in which, as the novel observes, "nobody knows one, one knows nobody" (004-023); the expansion of credit economies made financial identity a matter of reputation rather than substance; and legal reforms to marriage and inheritance law destabilized traditional markers of social position.

Braddon intensifies these anxieties by making identity deception not the province of a single villain but the condition of nearly every significant character. Unlike the detective fiction that would follow — in which identity is a puzzle to be solved and restored — *The Cloven Foot* suggests that identity is inherently multiple, that "alias" is not a deviation from the norm but a latent possibility in every self-presentation. The novel's resolution does not so much restore true identity as choose which version of the self will be allowed to stand: John Treverton survives; Jack Chicot is buried. The "cloven foot" of the title is thus not merely the mark of the devil but the inescapable trace of the other self — the identity that cannot be fully suppressed, the past that will not stay hidden.
