# The Discovery of Desrolles's Identity

The discovery of [[Desrolles]]'s true identity is the climactic event of *The Cloven Foot*, the moment toward which the novel's elaborate architecture of concealment, suspicion, and investigation has been building. It is the event that transforms the central question — "who is Desrolles?" — from a matter of speculation into a matter of established fact, with profound consequences for every major character.

## Background

Throughout the novel, Desrolles has been a figure of opacity. He appears as a lodger, a confidant to [[Jack-Chicot]], a man of languid manner and knowing silences. The question of his identity — his real name, his past, his connection to the Treverton family — is inextricably linked to the question of [[John-Treverton]]'s identity. If Desrolles is not who he appears to be, then what does that imply about everyone connected to him?

The discovery is prepared for by numerous earlier scenes: Desrolles's cryptic conversations with Chicot, his knowing manner with a female lodger ("my soul, you ought not" [014-008]), and the growing body of evidence that the identity of "Desrolles" is a constructed one.

## Consequences

The discovery transforms the narrative landscape. Questions that had been matters of suspicion become matters of knowledge. The legal implications — for the Treverton inheritance, for any crimes that may have been committed, for the social standing of those connected to Desrolles — are immediate and far-reaching. The discovery also has profound emotional consequences: characters must reckon with what they knew, what they suspected, and what they chose not to see.

The event also resolves (or transforms) the novel's governing theme of identity. If [[Identity]] has been the question — can we know who anyone really is? — then the discovery provides an answer: yes, eventually, identity will out. The "cloven foot" will show itself.

## Key Passages

> "You deny the identity of John Treverton and Chicot" ([027-042])

Though not the discovery itself, this earlier confrontation sets the terms for the eventual revelation.

> "His denial of the identity between the two men went for very little" ([028-005])

The narrator's commentary anticipates the discovery: denial alone cannot withstand the accumulating evidence.

> "proof of Jean Kergariou's identity with the sailor whose corpse——" ([037-002])

The forensic dimension of identity — proving who someone is through physical evidence — foreshadows the discovery's method.
