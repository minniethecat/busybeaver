# La Chicot

La Chicot is the [[murder-concept|murder]] victim whose violent death drives the central mystery plot of *The Cloven Foot*. Born Zaïre Chicot in the province of Brittany, she rises from provincial obscurity to become a celebrated *premiere danseuse* in Paris and later [[london|London]], where her name appears in gigantic yellow capitals on hoardings across [[london|the city]]. Her marriage to [[jack-chicot|Jack Chicot]] — the alias under which the protagonist [[John-Treverton]] conceals his [[identity]] — links her [[fate]] irreversibly to the novel's dual-[[identity]] structure. When she is found stabbed to death in her bed at [[cibber-street|Cibber Street]], Leicester Square, her husband's disappearance and the [[the-discovery|discovery]] of a weapon in his paint-box make him the prime suspect, launching the [[murder-concept|murder]] investigation that occupies the novel's second half.

## Role in the Novel

La Chicot appears only in the novel's extended flashback (Chapters 4 through 17) and in posthumous references throughout the present-time narrative, but her [[murder-concept|murder]] is the engine of the plot. Her death provides the [[reputation-and-scandal|scandal]] that threatens to destroy [[john-treverton|John Treverton]]'s chance at claiming the Hazlehurst [[inheritance]] and marrying [[Laura-Malcolm]]. She is first introduced in [[ch04-la-chicot]], where Braddon devotes an entire chapter to her backstory, career, and marriage — a significant structural investment that underscores her narrative weight. She is a [[sensation-fiction|sensation-novel]] archetype: the beautiful, reckless, doomed woman whose glamour and moral looseness make her simultaneously alluring and pitiable. Her death is the "dark gap" in the protagonist's past that must be explained, and the mystery of who killed her threads through the present-day chapters as gossip, newspaper speculation, and finally a legal inquiry.

## Biography

### Background

Zaïre Chicot was born in the province of Brittany, in the town of Auray, amid "fig-trees and saintly shrines" (004-004). She was of provincial origin — not Parisian by birth — and her accent and turns of phrase betrayed this to the "enlightened ear" of her compatriots, despite her claim to be "all that there was of the most Parisian" (004-004). Until her nineteenth year she had never seen Paris and its "long, dazzling boulevards stretching into unfathomable distance" (004-004).

She came to Paris to earn her living, finding work among laundresses on the quay before discovering a far more congenial path: the corps de ballet (004-005). Paris taught her "that she was beautiful" and that it was "pleasanter to whirl and bound among serried [[class-and-status|rank]]s of other Chicots... clad in scantiest drapery, but sparkling with gold and spangles... than to toil among laundresses on the quay" (004-005).

### Career as Ballet Dancer

La Chicot rose rapidly in the Parisian ballet world. She was soon known as "the belle of the ballet" and attracted the notice of the ballet-master, who "taught her steps which he taught to none other of the sisterhood under his tuition" and "pushed her to the front" (004-006). Following his advice, she migrated from a large theatre where she was a nobody to a smaller house in the students' quarter on the left bank of the Seine, between the School of Medicine and the Sorbonne, where she "soon became everybody" (004-006).

Her dancing was distinguished by "audacity rather than… high art" (004-003). She was no follower of the Taglioni school — "she would have 'mocked herself of you' if you had talked to her about [[edward-clare|the poet]]ry of motion" (004-003). But for "flying bounds across the stage — for wild pirouettings on tiptoe — for the free use of the loveliest arms in creation — for a bold backward curve of a full white throat more perfect than ever sculptor gave his marble bacchanal, La Chicot was unrivalled" (004-003).

As time passed, her dancing grew more extreme: "La Chicot was growing more and more like a Thracian Maenad as time went on. Her dancing was more audacious, her gestures more electrical. There was a kind of inspiration in those wild movements, but it was the inspiration of a Bacchante" (004-011).

After her star waned in Paris, she was engaged by Mr. Smolendo at the [[the-prince-frederick-theatre|Royal Prince Frederick Theatre and Music Hall]] in [[london|London]], where she became the theatre's main draw. Her coarser style suited the house: "what La Chicot wanted in training she made up for with dash and audacity" (004-019). Smolendo predicted she would take the town "by storm" for three seasons before burning out, "like one of those high-stepping horses that knock themselves to pieces in a year or two" (004-020). She was advertised everywhere: "Chicot. In gigantic yellow capitals on a black ground... Her name was on the walls in capitals three feet high, her bold bright face smiled on the foot passenger at every turn in the road" (008-014).

She was severely injured in a stage accident at [[the-theatre-company-prince-frederick|the Prince Frederick]], when the telescopic iron apparatus that lifted her through an artificial "coral cave" collapsed — the scene-shifter being drunk (008-023). She lay near death for months with a compound leg fracture and suspected brain injury, but eventually recovered and returned to the stage for the Christmas season, appearing in "half-a-dozen costumes, all equally original, expensive, and audacious" as a "fountain of golden water," a "fish-woman," and other spectacles (014-006).

### Marriage to Jack Chicot

At the Students' Theatre in Paris, La Chicot met the man who would become her husband. He was "an Englishman, leading a rather wild life in this students' quarter of Paris, living from hand to mouth, very poor, very clever, very badly qualified to get his own living" (004-007). He was handsome, well-bred, with "that unmistakable air of gentle birth which neither poverty nor Bohemianism can destroy, and in the opinion of La Chicot the most fascinating man she had ever seen" (004-008).

It was "an infatuation on both sides — his first great passion and hers" (004-008). She married him recklessly, knowing he had barely enough money for their wedding dinner. La Chicot, "a strong-minded young woman" who had made her own way unaided, was "quite resigned to the idea that she was to be the breadwinner" (004-008). This "child of the people set a curious value upon the name [[class-and-status|gentleman]]. The fact that her husband belonged to a superior race made up, in her mind, for a great many shortcomings" (004-008). He was soon known by her name and "spoken of everywhere as Monsieur Chicot" (004-008).

The first two years of their marriage in Paris were a "strange kind of life... an odd, improvident, dissipated life, in which night was turned into day, and money spent like water, and nothing desired or obtained out of existence except pleasure" (004-009). But the marriage soured rapidly after their move to [[london|London]]. By the winter of Chapter 6, Jack had "grown weary of his wife and his circumstances" and the couple quarrelled violently. Yet La Chicot retained a fierce attachment to her husband, becoming "desperately jealous of his least civility to another woman" even as he grew "gloomy" and distant (004-063).

### Intemperate Habits

Braddon delineates La Chicot's drinking as both a symptom and cause of her degradation. In Paris, she "d[[class-and-status|rank]] more champagne than was good for her constitution or her womanhood; grew a shade coarser every year; never opened a book or cultivated her mind in the smallest degree" (004-009). Her fellow performers noted the decline: "'La Chicot begins to drink like a fish,' said Antoine, of the orchestra... 'I wonder whether she beats her husband when she has had too much champagne?'" (004-012).

After the move to [[london|London]], her drinking accelerated: "She had a bottle of champagne in her dressing-room every night, and finished it before she went on for her great pas. So long as she abstained from brandy this meant sobriety... When she felt that its sustaining power was insufficient she fortified it with brandy, and then La Chicot was a creature to be shunned" (006-005). The stage accident that nearly killed her occurred when both she and the stage-carpenter were drunk (008-015). Her anonymous admirer had sent her a case of Roederer champagne, which she consumed freely, believing that a pint could harm nobody (008-015). On the night of the accident, "the weather being unusually sultry for the season, she d[[class-and-status|rank]] two bottles in the course of her toilet" and "could hardly stand" when she went down to the wings (008-021).

### Mysterious Past and Anonymous Admirer

La Chicot received an anonymous gold bracelet from a secret admirer, which she refused to return despite her husband's demand, causing "very hard words spoken on both sides" (006-044). [[jack-chicot|Jack Chicot]] tried and failed to trace the purchaser. The admirer, later revealed to be the wealthy financier [[joseph-lemuel|Joseph Lemuel]], also sent a case of champagne and offered her a carriage, a house in Mayfair, and a settlement through love letters she kept in her workbasket (014-010 to 014-017). Despite these offers, and despite [[Desrolles]] urging her to accept, La Chicot insisted, "I am an honest woman. And then, I am too proud" (014-027).

### Murder

On a winter night, at about one o'clock in the morning, La Chicot was stabbed to death in her bed at the [[cibber-street|Cibber Street]] lodgings. [[jack-chicot|Jack Chicot]] returned home after eleven to find "hideous stains on the counterpane, an awful figure lying in a heap among the bedclothes, a long loose coil of raven hair, curved like a snake round the rigid form" (017-007). The wound was "a deep stab; a violent thrust with some sharp, thin, and narrow instrument, which had pierced the hollow of her neck, and penetrated in a slanting direction to the lungs" (018-005).

The surgeon found evidence of a struggle: "on the round, white wrist of the dead a purple bruise showed where a savage hand had gripped that lovely arm; on the right shoulder... appeared the marks of strong fingers that had fastened their clutch there" (018-007). In her clenched hand, the landlady [[mrs-evitt|Mrs. Evitt]] discovered a crucial piece of evidence: "a little tuft of iron-grey hair, which looked as if it had been torn from a man's head" (017-058 to 017-059).

[[jack-chicot|Jack Chicot]] raised the alarm, then went to fetch a policeman and never returned. His disappearance, combined with the [[the-discovery|discovery]] of an Italian dagger in his paint-box — a weapon "just such... as that with which the wife had been stabbed" (018-016 to 018-017) — immediately made him the prime suspect.

### Press Portrayal

The [[murder-concept|murder]] became a sensation across [[london|London]]. "The more popular of [[the-press|the penny dailies]] went into convulsions of indignation against everybody concerned" (018-045). The newspapers "depicted the whole scene as vividly as if it had been shown to them in a charmed sleep" (018-052):

> They gushed as they described the beauty of the wife; they wept as they told of her intemperate habits. The husband they painted in the darkest dyes of iniquity. A man who had battened on his wife's earnings — a poor creature — a led captain — idle, luxurious, intemperate, since it was doubtless his example which had taught that glorious creature to drink. (018-052)

The leader-writers painted in "a blaze of lurid light, the scene of the [[murder-concept|murder]]": the husband's midnight return from haunts of vice, the wife's recriminations, "hot words on both sides," and "the husband brutalised by drink... snatches the dagger... and plunges the blade into his wife's bosom" (018-052). These sensationalized accounts turned [[jack-chicot|Jack Chicot]] into a public villain and "the talk for the next three weeks" of [[london|London]] (018-052).

La Chicot was buried at Kensal Green Cemetery, her funeral drawing "a tremendous crowd... to see the foreign dancing woman laid in her untimely grave" (018-043). Mr. Smolendo himself laid a wreath of white camellias on the coffin, and actors from [[the-theatre-company-prince-frederick|the Prince Frederick]], as well as much of "dramatic and equestrian [[london|London]]," attended (018-043).

### Fate / Resolution

La Chicot is dead before the novel's midpoint. Her [[murder-concept|murder]] is never definitively solved on the page in the traditional sense — the [[identity]] of her killer, strongly implied to be [[desrolles|Desrolles]], is part of the mystery that shadows [[john-treverton|John Treverton]] through the remainder of the novel. Her legacy is twofold: she is the "dark gap" in Treverton's history that must be explained and overcome before he can claim his [[inheritance]] and marry [[laura-malcolm|Laura Malcolm]], and she is a representative figure of the female casualties of bohemian [[london|London]], beautiful women destroyed by drink, fame, and the moral laxity of the theatrical demi-monde.

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

La Chicot is driven by immediate gratification: "money for fine dresses, good dinners, wine that foamed and sparkled, and plenty of promenading in hired carriages" (004-008). She has no sense of the future: "Money for the future, for sickness, for old age, for the innumerable necessities of life, she never thought of" (004-008). Braddon characterizes her philosophy as profoundly Horatian — "to snatch the pleasure of the day, and let tomorrow take care of itself" (004-008). Her attachment to the idea of her husband's gentility — race — is perhaps her deepest psychological need, compensating for her own humble origins. She is fiercely loyal to him, "flying at him like a fury when she was betwixt sobriety and intoxication, calling him her angel, or her cat, or her cabbage, with imbecile tenderness, when she was comfortably tipsy" (006-005).

Her pride is real but contradictory. She refuses Lemuel's offers despite their material appeal, declaring, "I am an honest woman. And then, I am too proud" (014-027). Yet she accepts his anonymous gifts without qualm, finding no moral difficulty in wearing a bracelet from an unknown man while rejecting its giver.

### Role in the Mystery

La Chicot is the victim around whom the novel's central mystery revolves. Her [[murder-concept|murder]] — the method (a thin, flexible Italian dagger), the circumstances (a locked room, a sleeping household), the evidence (a tuft of iron-grey hair), and the suspect's disappearance — provides the sensational crime at the novel's heart. The [[the-discovery|discovery]] of the dagger in [[jack-chicot|Jack Chicot]]'s paint-box by [[george-gerard|George Gerard]] (018-016 to 018-017) and Gerard's letter to *The Times* (018-051) turn public opinion decisively against the missing husband. The mystery of who really killed her — the evidence points toward [[desrolles|Desrolles]] rather than [[jack-chicot|Jack Chicot]] — is ultimately resolved in the novel's climactic chapters.

## Portrayal in the Text

> La Chicot was a weed grown on Gallic soil... She was soon known as the belle of the ballet, and speedily made herself obnoxious to the principal dancers, who resented her superior charms as an insolence. (004-004, 004-006)

> She earned her money gaily, and spent it royally; domineered over her husband on the strength of her superb beauty; basked in the sunshine of temporary prosperity; drank more champagne than was good for her constitution or her womanhood; grew a shade coarser every year; never opened a book or cultivated her mind in the smallest degree; scorned all the refinements of life... (004-009)

> Her dancing was more audacious, her gestures more electrical. There was a kind of inspiration in those wild movements, but it was the inspiration of a Bacchante, not the calm grace of dryad or sea-nymph. (004-011)

> She looked lovely, in an attitude more perfect than anything that was ever done in marble — her round white arms lifted above her head, flinging back the loose branches of coral, her black hair covering her like a mantle. That long rich hair was one of her chief beauties — something to be remembered where all was beautiful. (006-025)

## Significance

La Chicot serves multiple functions in Braddon's sensation narrative. Structurally, she is the victim whose [[murder-concept|murder]] generates the novel's suspense plot and places the hero under a cloud of suspicion that must be dispelled before the comic resolution (marriage, [[inheritance]]) can occur. Thematically, she embodies the dangers of the demi-monde — a woman consumed by vanity, drink, and love of pleasure, who rises on her beauty alone and falls through the vices her circumstances have fostered. She is a darker variation on the fallen-woman trope: not a repentant Magdalene but an unrepentant Bacchante, defiant to the end.

Her French nationality and provincial origins, her career as a dancer, and her intemperate habits mark her as a figure of the Continental Other in the moral geography of the novel — an exotic, dangerous presence in respectable English life. Her [[murder-concept|murder]] in a [[london|London]] lodging-house dramatizes the collision between the bohemian theatrical world and the respectable world of [[inheritance]] and landed property that [[john-treverton|John Treverton]] seeks to enter.
