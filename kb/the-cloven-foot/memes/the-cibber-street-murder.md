# The Cibber Street Murder

In the dead of a winter night at the Cibber Street lodging-house in Leicester Square, La Chicot — a stage dancer and the wife of Jack Chicot (John Treverton's alias) — was found murdered in her bed, her throat cut, with a tuft of iron-grey hair clutched in her dead hand.

## Summary

At three o'clock in the morning, a cry of "Murder!" rang through the Cibber Street lodging-house. Jack Chicot stood on the first-floor landing, candle in hand, his face "like the face of a ghost," announcing that his wife had been murdered. A thin thread of crimson had crept from under the bedroom door across the grey carpet to the threshold. [[PN017-003]][[PN017-004]][[PN017-006]][[PN017-007]]

Inside the room, La Chicot lay in a heap among the bedclothes, her throat cut, "a long loose coil of raven hair, curved like a snake round the rigid figure." Chicot went to fetch a policeman — and vanished. The surgeon later determined she had been dead since approximately one o'clock, two hours before the alarm was raised. [[PN017-007]][[PN017-013]][[PN017-062]]

### Preceding Events

## Detailed Account

The cry of "Murder!" woke the house at three o'clock. Mrs. Rawber heard it in her back bedroom; Mrs. Evitt in the front kitchen. They converged on the first-floor landing where Jack Chicot stood, candle in hand, his pallid countenance looking "like the face of a ghost." [[PN017-003]][[PN017-004]]

> "My wife has been murdered. My God, it is too awful! See — see —" [[PN017-006]]

Chicot pointed to the thin thread of crimson that had crept along the carpet. He claimed he had entered the house only three minutes before and discovered the horror. When someone suggested fetching a policeman, Chicot volunteered — and never returned. [[PN017-010]][[PN017-013]][[PN017-014]]

> "I begin to think he has made a bolt of it," said Desrolles. "That looks rather bad." [[PN017-015]]

Mrs. Evitt was immediately convinced of Chicot's guilt: "It must 'ave been her 'usband done it. You was often a-quarrelling, you know you was." [[PN017-008]]

A police sergeant arrived, viewing the scene with "stolid calm." The surgeon determined the time of death was approximately one o'clock — two hours before Chicot raised the alarm. [[PN017-029]][[PN017-062]]

### The Crucial Evidence

After the policeman left, Mrs. Evitt re-entered the death chamber alone. She forced open La Chicot's tightly clenched hand and discovered "a little tuft of iron-grey hair, which looked as if it had been torn from a man's head." She carefully folded the hairs into an old letter and concealed the packet. [[PN017-058]][[PN017-059]]

The expression of her face had altered. "There was a new intelligence in her dull grey eyes. Her countenance and bearing were as of one whose mind is charged with the weight of an awful secret." [[PN017-061]]

## Consequences

The murder set in motion the chain of events that would ultimately lead to John Treverton's arrest: the inquest, the newspaper speculation, and the suspicions that clung to "Chicot" for nearly a year. Jack Chicot's disappearance after the murder became "one of the social mysteries of the year." [[PN031-020]]

The tuft of iron-grey hair found in La Chicot's hand would later prove to be the key piece of physical evidence — hair that did not match John Treverton's.

## Significance

### Plot Function

The Cibber Street murder is the central crime of the novel, the event around which all suspicion, investigation, and eventual exoneration revolve. It is discovered in Chapter 17 but its shadow extends across the entire narrative, from John Treverton's confession to Laura through to the trial.

### Thematic Resonance

The murder crystallises the novel's investigation of hidden identity and circumstantial evidence. Jack Chicot's flight — which to the other lodgers appears as proof of guilt — is revealed to be the desperate act of a man who knows how damning the appearances are. The tuft of grey hair, hidden by Mrs. Evitt, becomes the novel's emblem of concealed truth: the evidence that could exonerate an innocent man, suppressed by a woman who relished her secret.
