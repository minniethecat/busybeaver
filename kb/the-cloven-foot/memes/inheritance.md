# Inheritance

The financial mechanism that drives the plot of *The Cloven Foot* — Jasper Treverton's conditional will, the Treverton Manor estate worth fourteen thousand a year, and the ever-present threat of disinheritance that shapes every major character's motivations and choices.

## Definition

In *The Cloven Foot*, inheritance is not a passive transfer of wealth but an active narrative force — a "singular condition" (002-007) that compels characters into impossible positions. Jasper Treverton's will stipulates that his distant kinsman John Treverton can only come into the estate if he marries Laura Malcolm, the old man's adopted daughter, within one year of the testator's death (002-006). This condition transforms inheritance from a straightforward legal mechanism into a crucible of character: the fortune is "a fair estate, but a sad incumbrance" (002-013) that forces John into a marriage he never sought. The estate is valued at eleven thousand a year in land plus additional holdings, making fourteen thousand a year total (003-002) — a spectacular Victorian fortune that no man in his position could easily refuse.

The threat of disinheritance haunts the novel from the opening chapters. Laura Malcolm herself holds the power to frustrate the will by refusing to marry John, in which case "she would deprive you of the estate" (003-008). This inversion of conventional power relations — where the penniless adopted daughter holds the key to the great estate — is characteristic of Braddon's sensation plotting.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **Jasper Treverton's Will (Ch.2)** : The central document of the novel. Drawn up in the presence of Mr. Clare the vicar, Laura Malcolm, and John Treverton, it bequeaths the estate to John on condition he marry Laura within a year, with legacies to servants and a bequest of old plate to Theodore Clare (002-005 – 002-006).
- **The Fourteen-Thousand-a-Year Estate** : The Treverton Manor estate comprises land worth about eleven thousand a year, with additional property bringing the total to fourteen thousand (003-002). This figure is repeated throughout the novel as a marker of John's transformed social standing — "a country gentleman with fourteen thousand a year" (021-025).
- **The Marriage Condition** : The will's "singular condition" (002-007) that John must marry Laura Malcolm creates the central dramatic tension. John's position is "awkward" and "embarrassing" because he is bound to a woman who may not want him, and whose compliance is essential to his fortune (002-007 – 002-015).
- **The Disinheritance Threat** : Laura Malcolm openly contemplates refusing the marriage, telling John she "would rather forego this fortune than force a hateful alliance" (003-075). Her independence creates a persistent anxiety that the inheritance could collapse at any moment.
- **Jasper's Eccentricity** : The old man's character explains the will's strangeness. Jasper Treverton was a man of "eccentric temper" (001-043) who had quarrelled with John's father and deliberately created a will that would torment his heir. As one character observes, "it's a relief to him to disinherit somebody" (013-007).
- **The Settlement** : The legal machinery of the estate — trusts, income, and property — shapes the material world of the novel, from the great house John inspects (002-031) to the servants and dependents "fed at the expense of the estate" (005-013).

## Key Passages

> "The will was then read, and it was found to contain a very simple disposal of the testator's property. After bequeathing a few small legacies to his old servants, a bequest of old plate to Theodore Clare, the vicar, and his absolute forgiveness to John Treverton the elder, he said John Treverton the younger should marry his dearly-beloved adopted daughter, Laura Malcolm, within one year of his decease. The estate was to be held in trust during that interval." (002-006)

> "You see, Mr. Treverton, the inheritance which seemed so nearly within your grasp may slip away from you after all." (002-003)

> "The situation was an awkward one. John Treverton felt that it was so, and was involved in the singular condition which attached to his inheritance." (002-007)

> "I would rather forego this fortune than force a hateful alliance upon you." (003-075)

## Critical Perspective

Inheritance in *The Cloven Foot* exemplifies the sensation novel's characteristic treatment of money as destiny. Where the realist novel of the mid-Victorian period (Dickens, Eliot) treats inheritance as a mechanism of moral resolution — the lost heir restored, the deserving rewarded — Braddon presents it as a source of instability and menace. Jasper Treverton's will does not settle anything; it creates a year-long crisis during which the fortune could vanish at Laura Malcolm's whim.

This treatment reflects the Victorian era's deep ambivalence about inherited wealth. The fourteen thousand a year is both a glittering prize and a moral trap, forcing John into a marriage he cannot freely choose. The novel repeatedly frames the inheritance not as a blessing but as an "incumbrance" (002-013) and a source of "embarrassment" (002-015). For Laura Malcolm, the fortune is a "hateful alliance" (003-075) that would commodify her consent. For Desrolles, it is a prize to be seized by criminal means, revealing the corrupting gravitational pull of great wealth.

Braddon herself knew the precariousness of literary fortune — she wrote *The Cloven Foot* at extraordinary speed to support her household, and her own experience of living by the pen informs the novel's unsentimental view of money. The inheritance plot is not a fairy tale of deserved wealth but a sensation-engine of anxiety, coercion, and violence.
