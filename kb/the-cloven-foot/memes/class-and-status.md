# Class and Status

The intricate Victorian class system as it operates in *The Cloven Foot* — a hierarchy that separates the country gentleman from the London theater dancer, the inherited fortune from the earned living, and the respectable surface from the inconvenient knowledge held by servants — and the sensation novel's exposure of the fragility and hypocrisy of these social boundaries.

## Definition

In *The Cloven Foot*, class and status function not as a static social backdrop but as a dynamic and contested field. The novel occupies a specific moment in Victorian social history: the old landed gentry, represented by Treverton Manor and the Beechampton gentry, coexists uneasily with new forms of wealth and social mobility. John Treverton's inheritance of fourteen thousand a year elevates him overnight from a "gentleman at large" living a "wretched hand to mouth existence" (001-003, 001-065) to a country gentleman of substance. This transformation is never complete or secure — the novel continually tests the boundaries of gentlemanliness, respectability, and class belonging.

Braddon's sensation plot exploits the permeability and anxiety of class boundaries. La Chicot, a French ballet dancer earning a generous salary, occupies an ambiguous social space between the demimonde of the London theater and the respectable world. The servant class — the old manservant Jacob, the "upper servants of the Manor House" (002-005), the landlady Mrs. Evitt — possesses knowledge that can destabilize the positions of their social superiors. The novel suggests that class is less a fixed structure than a performance, dependent on money, manners, secrets, and the compliance of those below.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **The Gentleman at Large (Ch.1)** : John Treverton arrives at Treverton Manor as "a gentleman at large, living as best he might on the proceeds of his commission" (001-003). He is a gentleman by birth — "a gentleman by birth and education" (004-008) — but without money, that birthright is precarious. His survey of the Manor's wealth inspires "an almost savage longing," the desire of a man who has "begun his career with a small fortune" and watched it dwindle (001-003, 001-065).
- **The Fourteen-Thousand-a-Year Transformation** : The inheritance converts John from a man of uncertain means to "a country gentleman with fourteen thousand a year" — a transformation that changes how he is perceived by everyone from Laura Malcolm to the Beechampton gentry (002-003, 003-002). The income is repeated as a social marker, the financial basis of his new class position.
- **La Chicot's Social Ambiguity (Ch.4)** : La Chicot "called herself" Parisian, but her accent betrays provincial origins (004-004). She occupies a world of "gross, sensual pleasures" — champagne, hired carriages, the Bois de Boulogne — that marks her as outside respectable society. As a ballet dancer, she earns and spends at a scale her birth would never have permitted, crossing class boundaries through the theater (004-008 – 004-009).
- **The Servant Class and Its Knowledge** : The old servant Jacob knows the household's secrets; the "upper servants of the Manor House" are given legacies in Jasper Treverton's will (002-005 – 002-006). Mrs. Evitt, the Cibber Street landlady, discovers the crucial clue of the iron-grey hair and manages her knowledge strategically. Servants are witnesses to the lives of their betters — a structural feature of Victorian class anxiety that the sensation novel exploits.
- **Beechampton Gentry vs. London Theater World** : The novel sets the "bluff red-cheeked gentleman-farmer" (003-040) and the rural world of Beechampton against the London theater demimonde of Smolendo and his dancers. The "agricultural class" (013-005) represents rooted, traditional social identity, while the theater world represents mobility, performance, and moral ambiguity.
- **Respectability as Performance** : The language of respectability saturates the novel — "respectable people" in the village (002-029), the "respectable" streets where proper people live (004-028 – 004-029), the aspiration to "live a respectable life" (026-112). But the novel consistently shows respectability to be a fragile surface, maintained by money, secrecy, and the suppression of inconvenient truths.
- **Middle-Class Respectability Measured by the Inch** : The novel satirizes the rigid metrics of middle-class life, the life lived "by the inch-measure of middle-class respectability" (026-057). This phrase captures Braddon's critique of a class system that values conformity over character.

(001-065)

> Well, sir, he is a gentleman — everyone can see that — but a gentleman who has turned his back on fortune. (004-054)

> I should not make a bad country gentleman, if I were happy. (003-043)

> La Chicot was not of a calculating temper. She was fond of money, but only of money to spend in the immediate present; money for fine dresses, good dinners, wine that foamed and sparkled, and plenty of promenading in hired carriages in the Bois de Boulogne. Money for the future, for sickness, for old age, for the innumerable necessities of life, she never thought of. (004-008)

## Critical Perspective

Braddon's treatment of class in *The Cloven Foot* is characteristic of the sensation novel's engagement with Victorian social anxieties. Unlike the reassuring class resolutions of Dickens — where the lost heir is restored and the deserving poor elevated — Braddon presents class as a system of instability and potential humiliation. The "gentleman at large" of the opening chapter is always one step from ruin; the country gentleman of the middle chapters is one revelation away from disgrace.

The novel's interest in the servant class is particularly sharp. Servants are not background figures but instruments of plot: Jacob the old manservant conveys information between characters, Mrs. Evitt discovers crucial evidence, and the "upper servants of the Manor House" are recognized in the will itself (002-005). This attention to the servant's knowledge reflects a specifically Victorian anxiety — the fear that those who empty the chamber pots also know the chamber's secrets.

The theater world — La Chicot, Smolendo, the "Students' Theatre" — represents a space where class boundaries are deliberately blurred, where a provincial girl can become a Parisian dancer and command a salary her birth would never justify. But this mobility comes at a price: the theater demimonde is also the space of moral danger, of "gross, sensual pleasures" (004-008) and ultimately of murder. The novel treats social mobility not as opportunity but as threat.

The phrase "middle-class respectability" (026-057) recurs as both aspiration and accusation. For Braddon, who wrote the novel at extraordinary speed to support herself and her household, the question of what money can and cannot buy — respectability, love, identity — was not abstract. *The Cloven Foot* offers no comfortable resolution to its class questions because the Victorian class system itself offered none.
