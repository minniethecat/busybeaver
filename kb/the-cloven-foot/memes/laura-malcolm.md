# Laura Malcolm

Laura Malcolm is the emotional and moral center of *The Cloven Foot*. As [[Jasper-Treverton]]'s adopted daughter and the primary beneficiary of his will, she stands at the intersection of the novel's central conflicts: love versus duty, [[inheritance]] versus merit, and [[identity]] versus appearance. Throughout the novel, she navigates these tensions with a composure and moral clarity that distinguish her from almost every other character.

## Role in the Novel

Laura Malcolm appears in the very first chapter, seated at the bedside of the dying [[Jasper-Treverton]], "looking very wan and pale" ([001-102]). She is immediately established as the object of [[jasper-treverton|Jasper Treverton]]'s deepest affection — "the only creature I really love" ([001-078]) — and the central figure in his testamentary scheme. The will's requirement that [[John-Treverton]] marry her within one year of Jasper's death places Laura at the center of a legal, romantic, and existential drama.

Her role evolves across the novel from passive object (the [[inheritance]] to be won) to active moral agent. She must judge the character of those around her, decide whom to trust, and ultimately determine her own [[fate]] — even as the language of "[[fate]]" and "[[fate|destiny]]" surrounds her every choice.

## Biography

### Background

Laura Malcolm was adopted by [[Jasper-Treverton]] under circumstances that the novel reveals gradually. She is not his blood relative, yet he calls her "his dearly-beloved adopted daughter" ([002-006]) and makes her the primary beneficiary of his will. Her origins — who her biological parents were, how she came to be adopted — form part of the novel's mystery structure.

### The Deathbed Vigil

Laura attends [[jasper-treverton|Jasper Treverton]] through his final illness. She is present at his death, holding his hand as he dies — "with his hand in Laura Malcolm's, and his face turned toward——" ([002-003]). She is present at the reading of [[inheritance|the will]], alongside [[mr-clare|Mr. Clare]] the vicar and the upper [[the-servants|servants]] ([002-005]), bearing witness to the legal instrument that will define her future.

### The Courtship

In the months following [[jasper-treverton|Jasper Treverton]]'s death, Laura must navigate the attentions of [[John-Treverton]] — a man she barely knows but whom [[inheritance|the will]] has designated as her future husband. Their conversations, walks, and growing intimacy are shadowed by [[inheritance|the will]]'s conditions: is John courting her for love or for [[inheritance]]? Laura's discernment — her ability to see through appearances to the truth of character — becomes the novel's central moral faculty.

### The Revelation

As the [[identity]] mystery unfolds, Laura's position becomes increasingly complex. Her knowledge of [[john-treverton|John Treverton]]'s true [[identity]], or her suspicion of it, places her in a position of terrible responsibility. Her choices in this phase of the novel determine not only her own [[fate]] but the [[fate]] of everyone connected to the Treverton [[inheritance]].

## Character Analysis

### Moral Discernment

Laura Malcolm's defining trait is her ability to perceive truth. In a novel where [[identity]] is perpetually in question, where men may be not who they seem, Laura serves as the moral compass. The narrator's description of her at [[inheritance|the will]] reading — "he wondered at Laura Malcolm's composure" ([002-031]) — [[the-arrest|capture]]s the quality that sets her apart: while others are agitated, confused, or deceptive, Laura remains steady, observant, and clear-sighted.

### Between Agency and Fate

Laura is caught between two competing frameworks. On one hand, she is the object of others' designs: [[jasper-treverton|Jasper Treverton]]'s will, [[john-treverton|John Treverton]]'s courtship, the community's expectations. The language of [[fate]] surrounds her: "the very turning point of her [[fate|destiny]]" ([012-050]). On the other hand, she consistently exercises agency within these constraints — questioning, judging, and ultimately choosing. Her story is a negotiation between the [[fate]] imposed by others and the [[fate|destiny]] she claims for herself.

## Key Passages

> "Not the faintest," Laura Malcolm answered, very sadly ([001-039])

An early response to a question (likely about hope or expectation), establishing her characteristic tone of clear-eyed sadness.

> "I love him dearly," she answered, looking down at her folded hands ([001-042])

Speaking of [[jasper-treverton|Jasper Treverton]], Laura reveals the depth of her attachment to the dying man — an attachment that makes her position as his heir both natural and burdensome.

> "his dearly-beloved adopted daughter, Laura Malcolm, within one year of his decease" ([002-006])

The will's language fixes Laura's [[identity]] in legal terms: she is "dearly-beloved" and "adopted," both terms carrying emotional weight within a legal framework.

> he wondered at Laura Malcolm's composure ([002-031])

An outsider's observation that [[the-arrest|capture]]s what makes Laura exceptional — her self-possession in circumstances that would unsettle anyone.

## Literary Significance

Laura Malcolm represents a distinctive type of Victorian heroine: not the passive angel of the house, nor the sensational fallen woman, but a figure of moral intelligence whose power lies in perception rather than action. She is closer to Elizabeth Bennet than to Aurora Floyd — a woman whose judgments shape the novel's outcomes even when she appears to be merely waiting for others to decide. Braddon's achievement is to make this quiet discernment dramatically compelling, turning the question "what does Laura think?" into the novel's central suspense.
