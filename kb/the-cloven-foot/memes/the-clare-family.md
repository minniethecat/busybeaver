# The Clare Family

The Clare family occupies a distinctive position in *The Cloven Foot*: neither central to the inheritance plot nor marginal to it, they function as the novel's moral chorus — witnesses to key events, confidantes to the principals, and occasionally the vehicle for the kind of comic relief that sharpens rather than dissipates narrative tension.

## Members

| Member | Role |
|--------|------|
| Mr. Clare | Vicar of Beechampton, present at the will reading, the wedding, and other key moments |
| [[Celia-Clare]] | The vicar's daughter, [[Laura-Malcolm]]'s confidante and verbal sparring partner |
| Other daughters | Mentioned but less fully characterized |

## Functions in the Novel

### Moral Witness

Mr. Clare's presence at the reading of [[Jasper-Treverton]]'s will ([002-005]) establishes the family's role as official witness. The church — through its representative — observes and implicitly validates (or withholds validation from) the legal and social transactions that drive the plot. This is not a merely ceremonial role: in a world where identity is contested, the testimony of a clergyman carries weight.

### Confidante and Conscience

[[Celia-Clare]] serves as [[Laura-Malcolm]]'s closest friend and most honest interlocutor. Their conversations — "Come here, Celia," cried Laura ([007-051]) — provide the novel with its most direct access to Laura's thoughts and feelings. Celia's irreverent, practical manner ("I'm afraid we should starve, Celia, and that our children's only——" [005-045]) offers a counterpoint to the high Romantic rhetoric of fate and destiny that surrounds the central love plot.

### Social Anchor

The Clare family anchors the novel's social world. As the vicar's family, they represent the established order of the village — the world that [[Jasper-Treverton]]'s will disrupts and that the novel's events must eventually restore or reconfigure. They are, in a sense, the novel's "normal" against which the eccentricities and transgressions of the Treverton household are measured.

## Literary Significance

The clerical family is a staple of Victorian fiction — from the Grants in *Mansfield Park* to the Crawleys in *Framley Parsonage* — and Braddon uses the type with economy and skill. The Clares are never the focus of the narrative, but they are essential to its moral architecture. Through them, the novel maintains contact with the world of ordinary judgment, everyday morality, and the kind of common sense that the sensational plot constantly threatens to overwhelm.
