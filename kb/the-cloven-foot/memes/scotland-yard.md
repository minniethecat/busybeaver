# Scotland Yard

The headquarters of London's Metropolitan Police and the law enforcement body responsible for investigating the murder of La Chicot in *The Cloven Foot*. Scotland Yard represents institutional authority in the novel, but its effectiveness is consistently undermined by the narrative -- the police fail to find the killer, circulate a wildly inaccurate description of the suspect, and ultimately arrest the wrong man.

## Description

Scotland Yard is the seat of the Metropolitan Police's detective branch, the "combined intelligence" to which the novel's London public looks for the solution to the Chicot murder. It is not a character but an institutional presence that looms over the second half of the novel. Braddon's portrayal of Scotland Yard reflects the sensation novel's characteristic scepticism toward official authority: the police are well-meaning but incompetent, their investigations tainted by press sensationalism and their own limited methods.

The police first appear at the crime scene in Cibber Street shortly after the murder, conducting a search of the premises that fails to uncover either the crucial dagger hidden in Jack Chicot's paint-box (discovered instead by George Gerard) or the iron-grey hair clutched in the dead woman's hand (discovered by Mrs. Evitt). This dual failure -- the police miss the principal physical evidence in the case -- establishes the novel's theme of institutional blindness. [018-016][018-013]

## Role in the Novel

Scotland Yard intervenes at three critical junctures in the narrative:

- **The crime scene investigation (Ch.17--18).** A police-sergeant and a detective examine the Cibber Street premises. According to the hospital nurse assigned to guard the death chamber, "whatever they've discovered they've kept to themselves" but "I don't believe it's much." The detectives overlook the Italian dagger concealed in Jack Chicot's paint-box, a failure that Gerard notes with astonishment: "Strange that the detectives should have overlooked this." [018-013][018-019]

- **The fruitless manhunt (Ch.18).** After Jack Chicot's disappearance, Scotland Yard circulates a verbal description of the missing husband based on Desrolles's account, producing an image "no more like Jack Chicot than Jack Chicot was like the Emperor of China." The detectives hunt this imaginary Chicot "assiduously in all the worst parts of London" -- dining at low eating-houses, playing billiards in dubious taverns, riding penny steamers and railways -- always to find "that, although sufficiently disreputable, he is not Jack Chicot." Despite "working thus conscientiously," they cannot "pierce the gloom of the mystery." [018-048][018-046][018-049]

- **The arrest of John Treverton (Ch.38--39).** A detective from Scotland Yard travels to Hazlehurst Manor and arrests John Treverton as he is about to ride out to begin his day's work. The detective, speaking in "a low and serious tone," informs Treverton: "I'm a police officer from Scotland Yard, and I am here to arrest you on suspicion of having murdered your wife, known as Mademoiselle Chicot." A subordinate waits in the corridor outside while Treverton says goodbye to Laura. The detective allows Treverton the dignity of traveling in his own trap to the station. When Treverton asks who "set Scotland Yard on my heels," the detective replies with professional opacity: "That's a kind of thing we never tell. We got the straight tip." [038-173][038-180][038-181][038-182][039-002]

- **The man from Scotland Yard at Hazlehurst (Ch.39).** Following the arrest, "the man from Scotland Yard" remains present at Hazlehurst throughout Treverton's final interview with Laura, while his subordinate paces the corridor outside. The detective escorts Treverton to London and the House of Detention at Clerkenwell. [039-002][039-012]

## The Press and Scotland Yard

The penny newspapers reserve "their most scathing denunciations for the police," demanding to know: "Where are the police? What are they doing that this scoundrel has not been found?" Letters pour in to the *Morning Shrieker* "by the bushel, every correspondent suggesting his own particular and original method for catching a murderer." The contrast between the press's lurid certainty and the police's actual bafflement is one of the novel's sharpest satirical points. [018-045][018-047]

## Significance

Scotland Yard functions in the novel as a symbol of official justice that consistently falls short. The police do their duty "conscientiously" but cannot see what is before their eyes -- a dagger in a paint-box, a tuft of hair in a dead hand. The real detective work in the novel is performed by amateurs: George Gerard discovers the murder weapon, Mrs. Evitt withholds and then reveals the crucial physical evidence, and Edward Clare -- for all his malicious motives -- pieces together the identity puzzle that the professionals cannot solve. In the sensation novel's moral economy, institutional authority is no guarantee of truth.

Braddon's treatment of Scotland Yard also participates in a broader Victorian cultural anxiety about the new detective police. The Metropolitan Police's Detective Department had only been established in 1842, less than forty years before the novel's publication, and public confidence in plain-clothes detection remained uncertain. By showing the Yard's detectives failing at every turn -- missing evidence, chasing phantoms, arresting the wrong man -- Braddon taps into contemporary doubts about whether a professional police force could ever substitute for the moral certainties of community knowledge and providential justice. Ultimately, the novel's resolution comes not through police work but through the convergence of personal conscience (Mrs. Evitt's belated testimony), accidental discovery (the grey wig pulled from Desrolles's head in Paris), and narrative form itself.
