# The Press

The collective newspaper industry of London that covers -- and helps create -- the sensation surrounding the murder of La Chicot. In *The Cloven Foot*, the press functions not as a dispassionate recorder of events but as an active participant in the drama, constructing lurid narratives that shape public opinion, influence the course of the police investigation, and torment the novel's central characters.

## Description

The press in *The Cloven Foot* spans a range of publications from the respectable *Times* to the sensational *Morning Shrieker* to the comic weekly *Folly as It Flies*. Braddon, herself a prolific journalist and magazine editor before turning to novel-writing, portrays the newspaper industry with insider knowledge -- she knows both its power and its venality. The novel's press is a Janus-faced institution: on one side the sober factual record of the *Times* (which publishes George Gerard's letter identifying the murder weapon and prints Laura's marriage announcement), on the other the lurid fictions of the penny dailies (which "painted, in a blaze of lurid light, the scene of the murder" [018-052]).

Several named publications appear: ***Folly as It Flies***, the comic weekly where Jack Chicot's caricatures are published, signed simply "Chicot" [015-037]; the ***Morning Shrieker***, Braddon's satiric invention representing the lowest sensationalist press, which "went into convulsions of indignation against everybody concerned" and receives "letters by the bushel" from amateur detectives [018-045][018-047]; and the ***Times***, *Telegraph*, and *Standard*, the real newspapers that carry reports of the case and advertisements for Desrolles [040-070]. In Paris, the *Moniteur Universel* and "the Parisian journals" carry news of the case and of Desrolles's death [036-033][045-064].

## Role in the Novel

The press intervenes in the narrative at multiple levels:

- **At the crime scene (Ch.18).** On the morning after the murder, "newspaper reporters forced their way into her house in defiance of her protests, and finding her slow to answer their questions, got hold of Mr. Desrolles, who was very ready to talk and to drink with every comer." The press's intrusion into the domestic space of Cibber Street is the first step in transforming a private tragedy into a public sensation. [018-002]

- **The construction of Jack Chicot's guilt (Ch.18).** The leader-writers "painted, in a blaze of lurid light, the scene of the murder" -- the husband's midnight return from haunts of vice, the wife's recriminations, "the husband brutalised by drink, stung to fury by the wife's well-merited reproaches, snatches the dagger from the table... and plunges the blade into his wife's bosom." This image, entirely speculative, becomes "the talk for the next three weeks": "At street corners and on the roofs of omnibuses the public talk... was of Jack Chicot's crime." [018-052]

- **The condemnation of the authorities (Ch.18).** The press presents itself as the voice of outraged public morality. "They reviled the Coroner; they denounced the surgeon as a simpleton; they insinuated dark things about the landlady; they banded the witnesses as perjurers; but they reserved their most scathing denunciations for the police." [018-045]

- **Gerard's intervention via the *Times* (Ch.18).** George Gerard writes to the *Times* describing the Italian dagger found in Jack Chicot's paint-box. His letter prompts the famous surgeon Sir John Pelham to examine the wound and confirm the match, publishing his own letter the next day. The *Times* here functions as a conduit for expert testimony that bypasses the official investigation. [018-051]

- **Edward Clare at the dinner party (Ch.22).** Edward, freshly returned from London, introduces the press-constructed narrative of "the Chicot murder" into the Trevertons' dinner conversation, using newspaper coverage as a weapon. He speaks of "the newspapers" wearing a subject "to tatters" and of "every newspaper in London" being "free to brand him as a coward and a murderer." Laura detects his malice. [022-045][022-057]

- **The papers as a threat to Treverton (Ch.31--39).** As the plot accelerates toward John Treverton's arrest, the press looms as an agent of exposure. Treverton confesses to Laura: "Do you remember the story of a man whose name was bandied about in the newspapers nearly a year ago... a man whose wife was murdered, and whom some of the London papers plainly denounced as the murderer?" [031-020] After his arrest, he says bitterly: "The newspapers will make my story public." [039-029]

- **Desrolles reads of his peril (Ch.45).** In Paris, "one morning chance flung an English newspaper in his way, and he read the report of John Treverton's first appearance at the Bow Street Police-court" -- discovering that the legal process is closing in on him. The same newspapers that once helped him evade justice by vilifying Chicot now become the instrument of his own exposure. [045-025]

- **The final report (Ch.45).** After Desrolles's death, "the English newspapers of a later date contained the account of the pursuit and arrest of Desrolles, his desperate resistance, and awful death." The press that constructed the false narrative of Jack Chicot's guilt now publishes the true story of Desrolles's crimes. [045-065]

## Significance

The press in *The Cloven Foot* embodies the sensation novel's ambivalent relationship with its own medium. Braddon was writing a sensation novel serialized in a magazine -- she herself was part of the press industry she satirizes. The novel's treatment of journalism is therefore a knowing self-critique: the same appetite for lurid crime, sexual scandal, and domestic violence that sells newspapers also sells sensation fiction. When the leader-writers "painted, in a blaze of lurid light, the scene of the murder," they are doing exactly what Braddon herself does as a novelist.

Yet the novel draws a clear distinction between press fiction and novelistic truth. The newspapers construct a false Jack Chicot -- a drunken, brutal wife-killer -- whom the novel's readers know to be innocent. The "verbal picture" circulated by the police and press is "no more like Jack Chicot than Jack Chicot was like the Emperor of China" [018-048]. Against this institutional falsehood, the novel posits its own narrative as the true account: the sensation novel claims to correct the distortions of sensation journalism, to tell the story that the newspapers get wrong.

The press also serves as a mechanism of class anxiety. John Treverton's past as a newspaper caricaturist and "scribbler of newspaper paragraphs" [031-026] marks him as déclassé -- a gentleman fallen into Grub Street. His fear that "the newspapers will make my story public" is not merely fear of legal jeopardy but of social exposure: the respectable Squire of Hazlehurst revealed as the Bohemian husband of a murdered ballet dancer, his private history converted into a penny-daily headline.
