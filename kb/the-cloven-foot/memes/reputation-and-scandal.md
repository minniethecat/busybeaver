# Reputation and Scandal

The entwined forces that govern social life in *The Cloven Foot* — reputation as the fragile currency of Victorian respectability, and scandal as its explosive destruction, crystallized in the press frenzy over "the Chicot murder" and the public judgment that threatens to annihilate John Treverton's claim to identity and inheritance.

## Definition

In the world of *The Cloven Foot*, reputation is not merely personal esteem but a structural necessity — the "outer shell of intense respectability" (022-049) without which a character cannot function in society. Respectability can be "bought" with income (022-025), measured in "inch-measure of middle-class respectability" (026-057), and can separate a man from his former self (006-063). It is simultaneously the novel's most prized asset and its most fragile.

Scandal is reputation's antithesis — the public destruction of social standing through exposure, gossip, and press sensationalism. The novel opens with Jasper Treverton claiming he has "lived too remote from the world to hear its scandals" (001-078), establishing scandal as the worldly force that his isolated estate has held at bay. The murder of La Chicot transforms this latent threat into the novel's central social drama: "the newspapers nor the public would ever get tired of the subject" (022-045). Scandal in *The Cloven Foot* operates at every scale — from village gossip (026-131) to metropolitan press frenzy — and threatens to destroy lives not through legal conviction but through the court of public opinion.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **The Press Frenzy (Ch.18, Ch.22)** : After La Chicot's murder, "newspaper reporters forced their way into the house" (018-002), and the public's appetite for the "Chicot murder" proves insatiable. Jack Chicot fears that "every newspaper in London [is] free to brand him as a murderer" (022-057). The press does not merely report the crime; it constructs a narrative of guilt that preempts the legal process.

- **Village Scandal and Gossip (Ch.26)** : The novel explicitly warns that "Villages are given to scandal" (026-131), and the small community of Hazlehurst exemplifies how reputation operates through local talk. Laura Malcolm "loved scandal better" than she loved her friend (023-029), illustrating how the appetite for scandal corrodes even intimate social bonds.

- **Respectability as Social Shell** : The novel repeatedly figures respectability as a surface — an "outer shell" (022-049) that may conceal moral vacancy. John Treverton observes that men can be separated from respectability by degrees (006-063), while Celia's household operates on "the beaten tracks of respectability" (008-102). The idiom of measurement — "inch-measure" (026-057), "pattern" (004-043), "punctilious piece" (026-085) — suggests reputation is a commodity that can be acquired, sized, and lost.

- **The Disgrace Threshold (Ch.37–38)** : Celia declares she can "bear anything except his disgrace" (037-019), defining disgrace as worse than poverty, hardship, or even death. When Desrolles believes he has "no longer to fear the disgrace which must have attached to him" (038-004), the novel reveals how profoundly disgrace shapes the calculus of every character's actions.

- **The Erosion of Public Attention (Ch.46)** : By Chapter 46, the scandal has grown "stale and almost worn threadbare" (046-001), demonstrating that even the most consuming scandal has a half-life — but not before it has done its damage.

- **The Identity-Reputation Nexus** : Treverton's "spotless reputation" (005-016) as heir is premised on the erasure of his Bohemian past. When his alias is exposed, reputation and identity collapse together — proving that in the novel's world, who you are *is* what society believes about you.

## Key Passages

> "I have lived too remote from the world to hear its scandals; and I know not whether you have been a saint or a sinner during the years that are past." (001-078 — Jasper Treverton, establishing scandal as the currency of the world beyond Hazelhurst)

> "Villages are given to scandal. If you were to be seen at the house of a married woman whose husband was away, you would have the whole parish up in arms." (026-131 — warning that reputation is policed at the most local level)

> "I thought neither the newspapers nor the public would ever get tired of the subject." (022-045 — Jack Chicot on the press frenzy)

> "I can bear anything except his disgrace." (037-019 — Celia, defining the ultimate social horror)

> "He had no longer to fear the disgrace which must have attached to him had the crime been brought home to the real offender." (038-004 — Desrolles's relief reveals how disgrace functions as a constant, shaping presence)

> "It buys him respectability, that forty pounds, which nothing else could buy him." (022-025 — respectability as a purchasable commodity)

## Critical Perspective

Braddon's treatment of reputation and scandal in *The Cloven Foot* is both a sensation-novel convention and a sharp critique of Victorian social mores. The novel exposes reputation as a commodity — something that "buys him respectability" (022-025), that can be measured in inches (026-057), and that exists as an "outer shell" (022-049) independent of inner moral worth. This commodification of character is the dark insight at the novel's heart: in a society where reputation can be purchased, scandal can be manufactured.

The press functions as scandal's primary engine. Braddon, who knew the periodical world intimately from her own career, depicts newspaper reporters not as neutral observers but as invasive forces who "forced their way into the house" (018-002) and whose narratives have the power to "brand" a man before any legal verdict (022-057). The novel thus anticipates modern anxieties about trial-by-media while critiquing the Victorian sensation press's appetite for lurid crime.

The village gossip economy (026-131) provides the local counterpart to metropolitan press frenzy, suggesting that scandal operates identically at every scale — only the medium changes. Laura Malcolm, who "loved scandal better" than love (023-029), is the novel's emblem of how the scandal appetite corrupts even the most intimate relationships. The novel's resolution — the scandal growing "stale" (046-001) — offers no moral reckoning, only exhaustion, suggesting that scandal in the Victorian world was not defeated but simply outlasted.
