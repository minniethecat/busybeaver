# Sensation Fiction

The literary genre of the 1860s Victorian sensation novel — defined by its fusion of criminal violence with domestic realism — as it is exemplified and extended by M.E. Braddon's *The Cloven Foot*, a novel that places murder, concealed identity, and press sensationalism at the centre of an inheritance plot set among the country houses and London lodging-houses of mid-Victorian England.

## Definition

Sensation fiction was the dominant popular literary genre of the 1860s, pioneered by Wilkie Collins (*The Woman in White*, 1859; *The Moonstone*, 1868), Mary Elizabeth Braddon (*Lady Audley's Secret*, 1862), and Ellen Wood (*East Lynne*, 1861). The genre's defining innovation was to bring the crimes, scandals, and conspiracies of the Gothic and Newgate traditions into the contemporary English household — to locate bigamy, murder, and fraud not in a remote castle or foreign city but behind the lace curtains of a respectable country house. As Henry James observed, sensation novels "introduced into fiction those most mysterious of mysteries, the mysteries which are at our own doors."

*The Cloven Foot* (serialised in 1863 as *John Marchmont's Legacy* before its 1879 book publication) is a textbook example of the sensation novel in its mature phase. The novel opens with a conditional will that compels its heir into marriage, establishes a cast of characters divided between a country estate and the bohemian theatres and lodging-houses of London, and pivots at its midpoint on a shocking murder — La Chicot killed in her Cibber Street bedroom — that transforms a domestic inheritance drama into a criminal investigation spanning the remaining twenty-eight chapters.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **Crime at the Heart of Domesticity** : The murder of La Chicot occurs not in a dark alley but in "a house in which almost every room was occupied" (018-046) — a Cibber Street lodging-house where the ordinary rituals of sleeping, eating, and dressing continue alongside the "hideous stains on the counterpane" (017-007). The novel refuses any spatial separation between safety and danger, respectability and violence. When the household members gather at the inquest, they are described as "a curious assembly" within "a long low room, used on more ordinary occasions as a clubroom for the patrons of the Prince of Wales's Feathers" (018-024) — the instruments of justice (the coroner, the jury, the witnesses) occupying the same quotidian space as public-house conviviality.

- **Hidden Identities and the Double Life** : The novel's central sensation mechanism is John Treverton's concealed identity as Jack Chicot, the caricaturist. The heir to a fourteen-thousand-a-year estate has lived for years in Bohemian London under an assumed name, married to a ballet dancer, earning his bread by comic sketches. The revelation that John Treverton "had lived for some years in London under an assumed name" creates "no small sensation among a community which rarely enjoyed such opportunities for gossip" (046-001). This dual identity is the pivot on which the entire plot turns — it is the secret whose exposure threatens to destroy the marriage plot, the inheritance, and John Treverton's social standing, and it is the mystery that must be solved before the murder can be understood.

- **The Press as Narrative Agent** : The novel embeds within itself a critique of the very medium in which it originally appeared. The newspaper reporters "forced their way into the house in Cibber Street" (018-002) within hours of La Chicot's death. The leader-writers "painted, in a blaze of lurid light, the scene of the murder" (018-052), imagining dialogue and motive in the absence of facts. The novel depicts the press constructing a sensation narrative that competes with — and sometimes overwhelms — the novel's own plot: "I thought neither the newspapers nor the public would ever get tired of the Chicot murder" (022-045). Braddon, who knew the periodical press from the inside, renders the mechanics of sensation journalism with the precision of a practitioner.

- **The Melodramatic Aesthetic** : The novel repeatedly draws on the language and conventions of theatrical melodrama. Jack Chicot's sketch of the murder scene is described as "rude and apparently hasty," yet possessed of "a certain melodramatic power" (018-016). The corpse of La Chicot is rendered with Gothic theatricality: "a long loose coil of raven hair, curved like a snake round the rigid form" (017-007). Edward Clare frames his pursuit of Treverton's secret as "a drama" whose scenes he orchestrates — visiting Blackfriars Road, interrogating witnesses, casting himself as the hero of a vindication plot (028-003).

- **Scandal and the Surveillance Society** : The novel depicts village life as a machinery of gossip where private lives are relentlessly scrutinised. "Villages are given to scandal" (026-131) is both an observation and a warning — the same community that provides the novel's pastoral setting is also the audience that will consume and judge John Treverton's hidden past. The "no small sensation" (046-001) created by his exposure is the pleasure of a readership within the novel that mirrors the readership without — a recursive structure characteristic of sensation fiction's self-consciousness about its own appeal.

- **The "Extraordinary" as Narrative Principle** : The novel repeatedly marks its own events as extraordinary — the inquest is described as "an extraordinary inquiry" (019-014), John Treverton's conduct is "extraordinary" (012-047), the entire sequence of events is "this extraordinary state of affairs" (015-002). This self-designation is a hallmark of sensation fiction: the novel insists on the exceptional nature of its own material, staking its claim on the reader's appetite for the unprecedented even as it follows genre conventions with machine-like precision.

## Key Passages

> "The newspaper reporters forced their way into the house in Cibber Street, greatly to Mrs. Evitt's aggravation. They examined the room in which the murder had been committed — the furniture, the paper on the walls, the view from the window — anything for a descriptive paragraph." (018-002)

> "They painted, in a blaze of lurid light, the scene of the murder. The husband's midnight return from haunts of vice — the wife's recriminations — her natural outbreak of jealousy — hot words on both sides." (018-052)

> "Here was an atrocious murder committed in the very heart of civilised London; in the midst of a calmly slumbering household; in a house in which almost every room was occupied; and yet the murderer is suffered to escape." (018-046)

> "It was likewise true of Mr. Desrolles, alias Mansfield, alias Malcolm. It was almost impossible to bring any charge home to so many-sided an individual." (040-062)

> "The fact that Mr. Treverton had lived for some years in London under an assumed name, and had been married to a ballet-dancer, and had more or less to do with the murder of that ballet-dancer, and so on, had created no small sensation among a community which rarely enjoyed such opportunities for gossip." (046-001)

> "Murder! an awful word under the most ordinary circumstances of everyday life — an awful word even when spoken of an event that happened long ago, or afar off. But what a word shouted in the dead of night, through the close darkness of a sleeping house, thrilling the ear of slumber, freezing the blood in the half-awakened sleepers' veins." (017-002)

> "His death would cause a sensation, his absence would make a blank." (023-007)

## Critical Perspective

*The Cloven Foot* is not merely an example of sensation fiction; it is a novel that thinks about sensation fiction from within the form. Braddon embeds within her plot a running commentary on how sensation is produced, circulated, and consumed — the newspaper reporters who "forced their way into the house" (018-002), the leader-writers who "painted" imaginary scenes in "a blaze of lurid light" (018-052), the village community for whom John Treverton's double life is a delicious scandal (046-001). This recursive structure — a sensation novel that depicts the making of sensation — is characteristic of Braddon's sophistication as a practitioner of the form.

The novel's governing title metaphor, the cloven foot, is itself a figure for the sensation aesthetic. The cloven hoof is the mark that reveals the devil beneath a pleasing surface — the trace of the hidden, the criminal, the scandalous that the respectable facade cannot fully suppress. Sensation fiction operates by the same logic: it promises to show the reader the cloven foot beneath the drawing-room carpet, the murder in the lodging-house, the bigamy behind the marriage settlement. The genre's pleasure — and its moral ambiguity — lies precisely in this exposure: the thrill of discovering that the country house, the vicarage, the newspaper office all conceal passions as violent as any Gothic dungeon.

Braddon's position within the sensation genre was both central and contested. *Lady Audley's Secret* (1862) had made her one of the most famous — and notorious — novelists in England, and critics routinely attacked sensation fiction for its alleged coarseness, its appeal to "the morbid and the prurient," and its supposed corrupting influence on female readers. Margaret Oliphant's famous review of sensation fiction in *Blackwood's Magazine* (1867) condemned the genre for making crime "the staple of domestic interest." *The Cloven Foot*, serialised in the midst of this critical firestorm (1863 in *Belgravia*, the magazine Braddon herself edited), can be read as Braddon's implicit defence of her genre: a novel in which the press is shown to be as sensation-hungry as any novelist, in which a vicar's son is driven by jealousy as violent as any murderer's, in which the line between the respectable and the criminal is shown to be not a wall but a permeable membrane. The novel implies that sensation fiction does not invent the violence it depicts — it merely refuses to look away from the violence that respectable society prefers to ignore.

The novel also exemplifies the sensation genre's distinctive relationship to serialisation. Braddon wrote *The Cloven Foot* at extraordinary speed — she was famously prolific, producing novels in a matter of weeks to meet serial deadlines — and the novel's structure bears the marks of serial composition: cliffhanger chapter endings, reversals that reorient the reader's understanding of previous events, a proliferating cast of witnesses whose testimony complicates rather than resolves the central mystery. The serial form demanded that each instalment deliver a "sensation" — a shock, a revelation, a reversal — sufficient to bring the reader back for the next number. *The Cloven Foot* delivers these sensations with machine-like precision: the mysterious visitor of Chapter 3, the revelation of the marriage condition in Chapter 2, the murder in Chapter 17, the exposure of Treverton's alias in the novel's second half. The novel is a case study in the sensation novel's narrative economy — the conversion of domestic material into serial suspense.

For Braddon personally, the sensation genre was also a vehicle for exploring the precariousness of female identity and reputation. Braddon's own life — her long cohabitation with the married publisher John Maxwell, the scandal this occasioned, her eventual marriage to Maxwell after his first wife's death — made her acutely sensitive to the ways in which a woman's social identity could be made and unmade by public narrative. The "sensation" created by John Treverton's hidden past (046-001) is, in displaced form, the sensation that Braddon's own life had created in the periodical press. The novel's obsessive concern with the gap between public reputation and private truth — with what a community "knew" about a person and what that person actually was — reflects Braddon's lived experience of being the subject of sensation journalism even as she was one of its most successful practitioners.

Finally, *The Cloven Foot* belongs to a specific historical moment in the development of the Victorian novel. The sensation novel of the 1860s occupies the ground between the domestic realism of the mid-Victorian period (Dickens, Gaskell, Trollope) and the detective fiction that would emerge in the 1880s and 1890s (Conan Doyle). Unlike detective fiction, which posits a rational investigator who can restore order by solving the crime, the sensation novel is fundamentally agnostic about the possibility of resolution. The inquest in *The Cloven Foot* does not solve the murder — it multiplies the mystery. The novel's resolution (the hunt for Desrolles) does not restore a stable social order so much as choose which version of events will be allowed to stand. This epistemological uncertainty — the sense that the truth may be irrecoverable, that identity is inherently multiple, that respectability is a performance rather than a fact — is the sensation novel's most radical inheritance.
