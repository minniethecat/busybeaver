# Justice

The arc of accusation, investigation, and vindication that structures the second half of *The Cloven Foot* — from the discovery of La Chicot's body through the inquest, John Treverton's arrest, the Bow Street inquiry, and his eventual discharge — embodying the sensation novel's central tension between the machinery of legal process and the moral imperative of poetic justice.

## Definition

In *The Cloven Foot*, justice operates on two distinct registers. On one level, it is the apparatus of the law: the inquest convened because "there'll have to be an inquest" (017-032), the evidence of witnesses, the police-court examination at Bow Street, the formal discharge of the prisoner.

On another level, justice is a narrative and moral demand — the insistence that innocence be vindicated and guilt exposed, even when the legal machinery proves slow, myopic, or actively hostile. Braddon traces the gap between these two registers across nearly thirty chapters, from the policeman's first examination of the scene through to the final exposure of Desrolles as the real murderer.

The novel insists that legal process alone cannot deliver justice; it requires the persistence of individual truth-tellers and the intervention of narrative form itself, which arranges events toward a satisfying close.

Braddon's narrator poses the question explicitly: "Was Laura, whose love had been obtained by an audacious fraud, to live for ever under the shadow of a yet darker wrong — the wrong to others, which John Treverton, while his wife believed him guiltless, was to pass undetected and unpunished?" (028-003–028-004).

This formulation captures the novel's double standard of justice: the legal question of guilt for murder and the moral question of accountability for fraud, both demanding resolution before domestic harmony can be restored.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **The Discovery and the First Inquest (Ch. 17–18)**: The policeman who examines La Chicot's body declares, "There'll have to be an inquest" (017-032), and the coroner's inquiry is held at noon the next day at the Prince of Wales's Feathers public house, where "the news of the murder had spread far and wide already" (018-002). Witnesses include the doctor, the police-sergeant, and the detective (018-024). Mrs. Evitt's evidence is taken, though the coroner warns her, "This isn't evidence" (018-032), and Desrolles is examined as the last witness, his testimony throwing "no new light on the subject" (018-040). The inquest is adjourned for a week in hope of further evidence (018-041), but the public has already reached its own verdict through the newspapers.

- **The Presumption of Guilt and Jack Chicot's Disappearance**: The most damaging circumstantial fact against the missing husband is his "unaccountable disappearance" after he went out to summon the police (018-020). The public and press cry out: "If the husband was not guilty, why should he disappear the moment the crime was discovered?" (022-051). The evidence of Desrolles, the last witness examined, confirms the inquest's impression: "the most remarkable feature in the case — the disappearance of the husband, after having given the alarm and expressed his intention of summoning the police" (018-024). The press takes over the work of prosecution, and "for the next two or three weeks" the obsession was "Jack Chicot's crime, and the miserable stupidity of the police in not being able to find him" (018-052).

- **The Dagger as Evidence**: George Gerard discovers the Italian dagger hidden in Jack Chicot's colour-box. He "took up the dagger and looked at it curiously — a deadly weapon in a strong hand" (018-017). The surgeon's evidence at the inquest confirms the weapon matches the wound (018-006). Yet George Gerard himself conceals this evidence from the inquest, saying to himself as he replaces the dagger, "Strange that the detectives should have overlooked this" (018-019). His decision to withhold evidence complicates the novel's treatment of justice: personal loyalty to John Treverton overrides the legal duty of disclosure.

- **The Bow Street Inquiry and Arrest (Ch. 38–41)**: John Treverton is arrested by a police officer from Scotland Yard "on suspicion of having murdered your first wife" (038-173). Laura insists he face the charge: "Better to be put upon your trial, and prove yourself innocent, as I know you can, than to live all your life under the shadow of a groundless suspicion" (039-004). The inquiry is held at Bow Street, where several witnesses give evidence (040-002), and Treverton's appearance draws considerable attention, as those present "saw that the inquiry had not been adjourned" (041-064). The medical testimony about the dagger is contested, and the question of the prisoner's guilt hangs on whether further evidence will emerge.

- **Mrs. Evitt's Testimony and the Turn Toward Truth**: The landlady who had been "sly enough" to observe everything (006-055) finally comes forward, her conscience pricked by the injustice of Treverton's imprisonment. She declares in court that her former lodger "oughtn't to be a prisoner" and that she "ought to have up and spoke before" (044-008). Her evidence, combined with Joseph Lemuel's testimony about the imitation diamonds and Mr. Mosheh's identification of Desrolles as the man who tried to sell the fake stones, shifts the weight of the evidence entirely. The court finds for the prisoner, and "the prisoner was discharged" (044-043).

- **Poetic Justice: The Death of Desrolles (Ch. 45)**: The pursuit of Desrolles to Paris ends not in a courtroom but in a fall that functions as Providence's verdict. Desrolles, who had "a loyal regard for truth and justice" only when it suited his schemes (032-051), is the man upon whom "the brand of crime itself could scarcely have done more" than his own life of concealment (006-063). The narrative delivers a justice beyond the reach of the law: the murderer is destroyed by the very decayed building in which he has hidden, a collapse that enacts the metaphor implicit in the novel's title — the rotten structure that concealed evil crumbles under its own weight.

## Critical Perspective

Braddon's treatment of justice in *The Cloven Foot* exemplifies the sensation novel's characteristic scepticism toward legal institutions. The inquest at the Prince of Wales's Feathers produces no verdict — it adjourns "in the hope that further evidence would be forthcoming" (018-041).

The press fills the gap with lurid fictions about "Jack Chicot's crime" (018-052). The police construct and circulate an image of the suspect that "was no more like Jack Chicot than Jack Chicot was like the Emperor of China" (018-048).

Even Scotland Yard, for all its institutional authority, cannot "pierce the gloom of the mystery" (018-046). The medical experts at Bow Street offer diametrically opposed interpretations of the same evidence. In Braddon's world, legal institutions are systematically unreliable — they generate suspicion rather than certainty, public sensation rather than truth.

Against this institutional failure, the novel posits poetic justice as a counter-principle: a narrative logic in which guilt is exposed and innocence vindicated not through legal procedures but through the convergence of personal conscience, providential accident, and narrative form itself.

Mrs. Evitt's delayed testimony — "I ought to have up and spoke before" (044-008) — is an act of individual moral awakening, not institutional procedure.

The death of Desrolles, the man who "had a loyal regard for truth and justice" only instrumentally (032-051), is brought about not by a court sentence but by the collapse of the rotten building in which he has hidden — an architectural judgment that literalises the novel's title metaphor.

This double structure — legal process versus poetic resolution — reflects the sensation novel's broader challenge to mid-Victorian confidence in institutional authority. Where the realist novel typically affirmed the law's capacity to discern truth, Braddon's fiction insists that justice requires resources the law cannot supply.

Those resources include the loyalty of a friend who withholds evidence, the conscience of a landlady who has been "sly enough" to watch everything (006-055), and the faith of a wife who insists her husband face trial.
