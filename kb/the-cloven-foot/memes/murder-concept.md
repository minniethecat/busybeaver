# Murder

The central crime of *The Cloven Foot* — the violent killing of La Chicot (Jack Chicot's wife) in her Cibber Street lodging — and the narrative's driving mystery through which the sensation novel explores themes of guilt, identity, and justice.

## Definition

In the context of *The Cloven Foot*, murder is not a mere plot device but the structural pivot of the entire narrative. The killing of La Chicot in Chapter 17 transforms the novel from a domestic drama of inheritance and courtship into a sensation mystery, launching a criminal investigation that spans the remainder of the book's 45 chapters. The murder operates simultaneously as a literal crime requiring detection and as a symbolic rupture in the novel's social order — exposing hidden identities, secret pasts, and the fragility of Victorian respectability.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **The Crime Scene (Ch.17)** : Jack Chicot discovers his wife dead in their Cibber Street bedroom at three in the morning, with "hideous stains on the counterpane" and "a long loose coil of raven hair, curved like a snake round the rigid form" (017-007). The body bears a stab wound from a dagger found on the scene.
- **The Public Sensation (Ch.18)** : The murder becomes a media spectacle. Newspaper leader-writers "painted, in a blaze of lurid light, the scene of the murder" (018-052), and the public obsession with "the Chicot murder" (022-045) dominates London conversation for weeks.
- **The Investigation** : The inquest at the Prince of Wales's Feathers public house (018-024) hears evidence from the surgeon, police, and household members, while Jack Chicot's disappearance makes him the prime suspect.
- **The Clue of the Grey Hair** : Mrs. Evitt discovers "a little tuft of iron-grey hair" clutched in the dead woman's hand (017-058), a piece of physical evidence that suggests a struggle and points toward a male assailant.
- **The Motive Question** : The novel systematically presents and undermines various theories — jealous husband, mysterious stranger, professional rivalry — keeping the reader in suspense about who actually committed the murder.

## Key Passages

> "Murder! an awful word under the most ordinary circumstances of everyday life—an awful word even when spoken of an event that happened long ago, or afar off. But what a word shouted in the dead of night, through the close darkness of a sleeping house, thrilling the ear of slumber, freezing the blood in the half-awakened sleepers' veins." (017-002)

> "My wife has been murdered. My God, it is too awful! See—see—" Chicot pointed with a trembling hand to a thin thread of crimson that had crept along the dull grey carpet to the very threshold. (017-006 – 017-007)

> "Here was an atrocious murder committed in the very heart of civilised London; in the midst of a calmly slumbering household; in a house in which almost every room was occupied; and yet the murderer is suffered to escape." (018-046)

## Critical Perspective

Braddon's treatment of murder in *The Cloven Foot* exemplifies the sensation novel's distinctive engagement with crime. Unlike the detached detective fiction that would later dominate Victorian literature, Braddon embeds the murder within a dense web of domestic relations — the victim is a wife, the suspect her husband, the witnesses their fellow lodgers. The novel's title itself, *The Cloven Foot*, invokes the diabolical — the "cloven hoof" of the devil — suggesting that murder is not merely a legal transgression but a manifestation of hidden evil within the apparently respectable surfaces of Victorian society. The press frenzy surrounding "the Chicot murder" (018-046) also reflects Braddon's critique of sensation journalism, which she knew intimately from her own career as a periodical writer.
