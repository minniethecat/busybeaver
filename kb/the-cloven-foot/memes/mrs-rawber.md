# Mrs. Rawber

Mrs. Rawber is a lodger at [[mrs-evitt|Mrs. Evitt]]'s boarding-house in [[cibber-street|Cibber Street]], a professional actress who plays "leading business" at the Shakespeare Theatre. Her theatrical background and practical temperament make her a distinctive voice among the witnesses to the novel's central crime, providing both comic relief and credible testimony.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Rawber appears in the chapters following the [[cibber-street|Cibber Street]] [[murder-concept|murder]], where she is one of the three residents of the lodging-house awoken by [[jack-chicot|Jack Chicot]]'s cry in the night. As a long-term lodger — she has been with [[mrs-evitt|Mrs. Evitt]] "close upon five year" — she represents the established, respectable stratum of the boarding-house's precarious world [017-018]. Her theatrical profession gives her a flair for the dramatic, but her evidence at [[the-coroners-court|the inquest]] is straightforward and corroborative, confirming the hour of the disturbance and the strange conduct of [[jack-chicot|Jack Chicot]] [017-065].

## Biography

### Background

Mrs. Rawber is an actress of some standing in the [[london|London]] theatrical world, performing "leading business" at the Shakespeare Theatre [004-036]. She has lodged at [[mrs-evitt|Mrs. Evitt]]'s for close to five years, suggesting a settled domestic life despite the itinerant associations of her profession. She is described as "quite the lady" by her landlady, and [[la-chicot|La Chicot]]'s mother is reported to have said that no one could have been "greater in the part than Mrs. Rawber" [004-040].

### Key Actions

- Is awoken at three in the morning by [[jack-chicot|Jack Chicot]]'s terrible cry, which she hears from her back bedroom on the ground floor [017-003].
- Comes out onto the stairs in a yellow cotton dressing-gown, shivering with cold and fear [017-024].
- Refuses to return to her bedroom, sitting on the stairs instead because she fears the house is haunted [017-038].
- Notes the time of the cry as ten minutes past three from the clock on her bedroom mantelpiece [017-043].
- Is advised by [[scotland-yard|the police]] sergeant to follow [[desrolles|Desrolles]]'s example and return to her room [017-046].
- Fortifies herself with a glass of hot gin-and-water — she is "a temperate woman" but fond of what she calls her "little comforts" [017-052].
- Gives evidence at [[the-coroners-court|the inquest]] held at the [[the-prince-of-waless-feathers|Prince of Wales's Feathers]], confirming [[mrs-evitt|Mrs. Evitt]]'s account of the night's events and the ghastly look on [[jack-chicot|Jack Chicot]]'s face [017-065].
- Attends [[la-chicot|La Chicot]]'s funeral at Kensal Green, alongside much of "dramatic and equestrian [[london|London]]" [017-072].
- Protests vigorously when police search her drawers and cupboards, revealing "mysteries of theatrical attire," and demands compensation if her Lady Macbeth dresses are damaged [017-062].

### Fate / Resolution

Mrs. Rawber's role in the narrative is confined to the domestic aftermath of the [[murder-concept|murder]] and [[the-coroners-court|the inquest]]. She survives the events unscathed, though she briefly fears she will have to leave the lodging-house, which she believes has acquired the name of being haunted [017-055]. Her final appearance is at [[la-chicot|La Chicot]]'s funeral, where she forms part of the crowd of theatrical [[london|London]] that turns out for the occasion [017-072].

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

Mrs. Rawber is primarily motivated by self-preservation and a practical concern for her domestic comfort. She fears ghosts in her bedroom after the [[murder-concept|murder]], but is quickly soothed by "company in a fire" and a glass of gin-and-water. Her professional pride surfaces when police search her belongings.

### Role in the Mystery

Mrs. Rawber serves as a corroborating witness whose testimony helps establish the timeline of the [[murder-concept|murder]] and the suspicious behaviour of [[jack-chicot|Jack Chicot]]. Her evidence at [[the-coroners-court|the inquest]] agrees with [[mrs-evitt|Mrs. Evitt]]'s account of the hour of the alarm and Chicot's "ghastly look" and eagerness to fetch [[scotland-yard|the police]] — a detail that contributes to the puzzle of whether Chicot is [[murder-concept|murder]]er or innocent party [017-065].

## Portrayal in the Text

> "It's a very disagreeable form," said Mrs. Rawber, "and if you tallow-grease my Lady Macbeth dresses, I shall expect you to make them good." [017-064]

## Significance

Mrs. Rawber belongs to the novel's rich portrait of [[london|London]]'s theatrical and lodging-house world — a social stratum that sensation fiction often mined for colour and contrast. Her combination of professional dignity, comic vulnerability, and practical good sense makes her more than a mere plot functionary: she is a vivid minor character whose presence helps ground the sensational events in recognisable human detail. Her yellow cotton dressing-gown, her fear of ghosts, and her Lady Macbeth dresses collectively suggest a woman who lives between two worlds — the glamour of the stage and the shabbiness of a [[cibber-street|Cibber Street]] back bedroom.
