# The Servants

The servants in *The Cloven Foot* form a shadow household that parallels and penetrates the world of their employers. Present at key events (the will reading includes "the upper servants of the household" [002-005]), privy to private conversations, and mobile between social worlds in ways their employers are not, the servants constitute an informal information network whose knowledge can confirm, complicate, or contradict the official version of events.

## Functions in the Novel

### Witnesses

The servants' presence at the will reading is legally significant: they are witnesses to a document whose interpretation will be contested. Their testimony — what they saw, what they heard, what they know of the household's private life — is a resource the novel holds in reserve. The question of what the servants know, and whether they will tell, is one of the novel's subsidiary tensions.

### Information Carriers

In a novel structured around secrets — [[John-Treverton]]'s past, [[Desrolles]]'s identity, [[Jasper-Treverton]]'s true intentions — servants are the weak point in every wall of silence. They move between rooms, between households, between the upstairs world of their employers and the downstairs world of the village. The novel acknowledges, without making explicit, that the servants know more than they say.

### Class Commentary

The servants also serve a thematic function, reminding readers of the class structure that underpins the novel's world. The Treverton inheritance is not merely a family matter — it affects the livelihoods and futures of everyone in the household. The servants' stake in the outcome is economic rather than romantic, practical rather than existential, and their perspective offers an implicit critique of the emotional excesses of their employers.

## Literary Significance

The servants in Victorian fiction are a well-established device for providing access to private domestic spaces — from the servants in *Wuthering Heights* (Nelly Dean is the most famous example) to the household staff in Dickens. Braddon's use of servants in *The Cloven Foot* is more restrained than either, but the principle is the same: those who clean the rooms, serve the meals, and attend the dying hear everything. The novel's restraint in not making a servant the primary narrator or informant is itself a narrative choice — the servants know, but their knowledge remains implicit, a reservoir of potential revelation that the novel never fully taps.
