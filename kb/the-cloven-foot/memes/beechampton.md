# Beechampton

Beechampton is the market town near Hazlehurst Manor in Devonshire, home to the Clare family and a significant secondary setting in *The Cloven Foot*. It is the seat of the vicarage, the local shopping destination, and the railway station that connects the remote Devonshire countryside to London — making it a crucial node in the novel's geography of rural and urban worlds.

## Location

Beechampton is situated in Devonshire, "adjacent" to the village of Hazlehurst and within a short drive of Hazlehurst Manor (003-002). The town is described as bordering closely on the manor estate: the Treverton lands "bordered closely on the town of Beechampton" (003-002). Celia Clare dismisses the town as "hideously behind the age" (011-015), suggesting a sleepy rural community untouched by metropolitan fashion. The railway station at Beechampton is a key transit point — John Treverton departs from it, and Edward Clare and George Gerard travel from it "across a stretch of heath and moor to Camelot" (020-043).

## Description

Beechampton is rendered as a modest provincial town with the typical features of a Devonshire market centre. It has a chief grocer "in whose plate-glass windows there beamed all that was newest and brightest in the way of glass and china" (024-009). It has a pony carriage service that conveys visitors and goods between the town and Hazlehurst (011-014). The town also possesses an ironmonger (030-011) and a station where "obese farmers" gather (028-027).

Celia Clare takes Laura Malcolm to Beechampton for shopping in the pony carriage, "to buy a collection of frivolities" (024-002). But the town's inadequacies are clear to Celia: "Beechampton is hideously behind the age" (011-015), indicating the limitations of rural life for spirited young women. John Treverton at one point owns a block of houses in Beechampton that brings in ground rents — a detail that underscores the economic geography of the Treverton estate (037-050).

## Significance in the Novel

### Plot Function

Beechampton is primarily the home of the Clare family, whose members are pivotal to the novel's romantic and investigative subplots. The vicar, Theodore Clare, is a friend and trustee of the late Jasper Treverton, and his role in the will and its execution originates from the vicarage at Beechampton (002-003, 002-005). His son Edward Clare is Laura Malcolm's disappointed suitor, who later becomes the investigator of John Treverton's hidden past. His daughter Celia Clare is Laura's closest friend and confidante.

Beechampton also serves as a transit hub: its railway station connects the isolated Devonshire setting to London and the wider world. Key journeys originate or pass through Beechampton station:
- John Treverton takes the train to London, via Beechampton, in his investigation of the murder.
- Edward Clare and George Gerard take the seven o'clock train from Beechampton, "catch the Exeter express for London" (032-120) on their voyage of discovery.
- The newlyweds John and Laura arrive in a fly hired at Beechampton after their ill-omened Camelot wedding (021-002).

### Thematic Role

Beechampton represents provincial English life — ordered, hierarchical, and, from the perspective of characters like Celia Clare, stiflingly limited. The town is "hideously behind the age" (011-015), a phrase that captures the divide between rural tradition and metropolitan modernity that runs through the novel. Yet Beechampton is also the seat of the vicarage, and thus of moral authority: Mr. Clare's moderate, kindly wisdom provides a counterpoint to the novel's violence and deception. The town's shops, its station, its ironmonger and grocer, constitute the ordinary world that the novel's sensation events disrupt.

The fact that John Treverton owns property in Beechampton (037-050) ties him materially to the community even before he has morally earned his place in it — a detail that reflects the novel's recurring concern with the relationship between money, land, and social standing.
