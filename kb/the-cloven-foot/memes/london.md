# London

London in *The Cloven Foot* is more than a backdrop — it is the enabling condition of the novel's central mystery. Its lodging-houses allow men to live under assumed names. Its legal institutions — the courts, [[Scotland-Yard]], the coroner's office — provide the machinery for investigating and adjudicating identity. Its social anonymity makes it possible for [[John-Treverton]] to disappear for seven years and return as [[Jack-Chicot]], for [[Desrolles]] to inhabit a false identity, and for the boundaries between truth and appearance to blur.

## Geographical and Social Character

The London of the novel is not the London of fashionable squares and aristocratic mansions. It is the London of Cibber Street — a world of lodgings, shared staircases, and transient residents. [[Cibber-Street]] itself functions as a microcosm of urban anonymity: a place where strangers live in close proximity while knowing almost nothing of each other's true selves.

The city's institutional geography matters too. The courts, the police, and the morgue are all London institutions, and the novel's plot moves between them as identity is contested, investigated, and (attempted to be) fixed.

## Literary Significance

London in Victorian sensation fiction is consistently figured as a space of danger and possibility — a place where the old certainties of rank, name, and character dissolve. Braddon's London is of a piece with Wilkie Collins's and Charles Dickens's: a labyrinth where identity is performative, detection is necessary, and the truth is always one lodging-house door away.
