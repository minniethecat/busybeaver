# John Treverton

John Treverton, heir to [[Jasper-Treverton]]'s Devonshire estate at [[Hazlehurst]], leads a double life through much of the novel under the alias [[jack-chicot|Jack Chicot]] — a struggling [[london|London]] comic artist married to a Parisian dancer. His concealed past as Chicot, the seven-year gap between his departure from England and his return to claim the Treverton [[inheritance]], and his position as the prime suspect in [[La-Chicot]]'s [[murder-concept|murder]] together form the central mystery of *The Cloven Foot*.

## Identity

John Treverton is a [[class-and-status|gentleman]] by birth but a fallen [[class-and-status|gentleman]] by circumstance. He began his career "with a small [[inheritance|fortune]] and a commission in a crack regiment" (001-003), but wasted his patrimony and sold his commission, reducing himself to "a [[class-and-status|gentleman]] at large, living as best he might, no one but himself knew how" (001-003). He is the sole surviving relative of [[jasper-treverton|Jasper Treverton]] of [[hazlehurst-manor|Hazlehurst Manor]] in Devonshire, summoned to the old man's deathbed in Chapter 1 after years of estrangement between their families (001-004).

His dual [[identity]] as [[jack-chicot|Jack Chicot]] is the novel's central structural device. For seven years between leaving Hazlehurst as a disappointed heir and his eventual return, Treverton lived in Paris and [[london|London]] under the alias Chicot, working as a comic illustrator for periodicals, marrying the dancer [[la-chicot|Zaïre Chicot]], and sinking into the bohemian demi-monde of [[cibber-street|Cibber Street]], Leicester Square. This concealed period constitutes the "dark gap" in his history that the novel slowly illuminates through flashback chapters — Chapters 4 through 17 — before returning to the novel's present-time narrative.

Braddon characterizes him with deliberate ambiguity: "He was not an utterly bad fellow, this John Treverton, though his better and purer feelings had been a good deal blunted by rough contact with the world" (001-007). He possesses "a f[[class-and-status|rank]] winning manner, and a handsome face" but suffers from "a self-indulgent nature," "an easy temper, a habit of looking at the pleasanter side of things," and "a chronic avoidance of all serious thought — qualities which do not serve to make up a strong character" (001-007).

## Biography

### Background

John Treverton's father Richard had quarrelled irrevocably with his cousin [[jasper-treverton|Jasper Treverton]], the wealthy Devonshire landowner, and never sought reconciliation despite his own reduced circumstances (001-032). John inherited neither wealth nor connection from this estrangement. After dissipating his small patrimony and selling his army commission, he drifted without fixed occupation or income, living by means known only to himself (001-003).

In Chapter 1, he receives a telegram summoning him to [[jasper-treverton|Jasper Treverton]]'s deathbed at [[hazlehurst-manor|Hazlehurst Manor]] — the first contact between the branches of the family in decades (001-004). Despite suspecting that the old man's adopted daughter [[Laura-Malcolm]] would inherit everything, he makes the journey to Devonshire.

### Key Actions

**The Will and the Marriage Condition.** After [[jasper-treverton|Jasper Treverton]]'s death, [[inheritance|the will]] is read in Chapter 2. To John's dismay, [[inheritance|the estate]] — worth some fourteen thousand pounds a year (003-002) — is left to him only on condition that he marry [[laura-malcolm|Laura Malcolm]] within one year. If the marriage does not take place, the entire [[inheritance|fortune]] passes to trustees for the erection of a hospital (002-006). John's face "assumed a sudden pallor" at this announcement (002-007), and he retreats to [[london|London]] after touring [[inheritance|the estate]] with the lawyer [[Thomas-Sampson]].

**The Mysterious Visitor.** Before leaving Hazlehurst, John witnesses [[laura-malcolm|Laura Malcolm]] secretly admitting a tall, muffled stranger through the garden door at night (003-044 to 003-047). When he confronts her, Laura claims the man is a relation, but John remains suspicious — "If we were differently circumstanced, if I loved her, I should insist upon a fuller explanation" (003-077).

**Life as [[jack-chicot|Jack Chicot]].** The novel's extended flashback (Chapters 4 through 17) reveals Treverton's seven-year exile. In Paris, as [[jack-chicot|Jack Chicot]], he met and fell in love with [[La-Chicot]] (Zaïre), a beautiful dancer at the Students' Theatre. "He was a good-tempered fellow, with a sublime faith in his own capacity to get a living" (004-018). They married and moved to [[london|London]], where Zaïre became the star dancer at the Royal [[the-theatre-company-prince-frederick|Prince Frederick Theatre]], her name flaunted on six-sheet posters across [[london|the city]] (004-003).

**Marriage in Decline.** The marriage soured rapidly. Chicot worked as a comic illustrator while Zaïre's career flourished, her drinking increased, and their [[cibber-street|Cibber Street]] lodgings became a battlefield. "Those two are devils when they quarrel," a neighbour observed (004-013). By the winter chronicled in Chapter 6, "the longest, dullest winter that [[jack-chicot|Jack Chicot]] had ever lived through," he had grown weary of his wife and his circumstances (006-002).

**Zaïre's Accident.** In Chapter 8, [[la-chicot|La Chicot]] is gravely injured in a stage accident at [[the-prince-frederick-theatre|the Prince Frederick Theatre]] — a fall caused by a collapsing iron apparatus during her ascent through artificial coral caves (008-023). She lies near death for months, and [[jack-chicot|Jack Chicot]] becomes her night nurse. When the surgeon [[George-Gerard]] tells him she may die, Jack cannot conceal a look of "ineffable relief" (008-091). Gerard confronts him: "This poor creature, lying here, has for some time past been a burden and an affliction to you. If Providence were to remove her quietly, you would thank Providence" (008-098). Zaïre ultimately recovers.

**[[the-murder-of-la-chicot|The Murder of La Chicot]].** On the night of the [[murder-concept|murder]] (Chapter 17), [[jack-chicot|Jack Chicot]] returns to [[cibber-street|Cibber Street]] after eleven o'clock and discovers his wife dead in their bedroom, her throat cut, "hideous stains on the counterpane" and "a long loose coil of raven hair, curved like a snake round the rigid form" (017-007). He raises the alarm — "My wife has been [[murder-concept|murder]]ed. My God, it is too awful!" (017-006) — then goes to fetch a policeman and never returns (017-013 to 017-014). His disappearance immediately brands him the prime suspect: "It must 'ave been her 'usband done it" (017-008). [[mrs-evitt|Mrs. Evitt]], the landlady, discovers a crucial piece of physical evidence: "a little tuft of iron-grey hair" clutched in the dead woman's hand, torn from an assailant's head (017-058).

### Fate / Resolution

John Treverton is eventually exonerated of the [[murder-concept|murder]]. The true [[identity]] of the killer is revealed in the novel's final chapters, and Treverton's concealed past — which he had hidden to protect his claim to the Hazlehurst [[inheritance]] — is brought into the open. He is ultimately able to marry [[Laura-Malcolm]] and claim the Treverton [[inheritance|fortune]], though the resolution is hard-won and shadowed by the years of concealment and the [[reputation-and-scandal|scandal]] of the Chicot [[murder-concept|murder]] trial.

## Literary Significance

John Treverton exemplifies several hallmark devices of the Victorian sensation novel. His dual [[identity]] — the [[class-and-status|gentleman]] heir who is also the bohemian [[jack-chicot|Jack Chicot]] — embodies the genre's preoccupation with concealed pasts, social performance, and the porous boundary between [[class-and-status|respectability]] and criminality. Braddon constructs him as a deliberately flawed protagonist whose moral weakness (the self-indulgence, the avoidance of serious thought, the secret wish for his wife's death) generates narrative suspense without forfeiting reader sympathy.

The structural device of the extended flashback — Chapters 4 through 17, which excavate the seven-year gap in Treverton's history — is one of the novel's most ambitious narrative techniques. By deferring the [[the-discovery|revelation]] of Treverton's Chicot [[identity]] until the reader has already invested in him as the heir of Hazlehurst, Braddon creates an information asymmetry that drives the mystery plot: the reader discovers the dangerous past that the characters in the present-time narrative do not know.

Treverton also functions as a study in the sensation novel's interrogation of guilt. He is legally innocent of the [[murder-concept|murder]] yet morally compromised by his acknowledged desire for his wife's death. His disappearance after discovering the body — ostensibly to fetch a policeman — reads simultaneously as panic and as a quasi-confession of culpability, placing the reader in the position of detective and judge.
