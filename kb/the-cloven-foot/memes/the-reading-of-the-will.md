# The Reading of Jasper Treverton's Will

The formal reading of Jasper Treverton's last will and testament, held in the drawing-room of Hazlehurst Manor shortly after the old man's funeral, sets the entire plot of *The Cloven Foot* in motion. The event binds together the fates of its two principal characters — John Treverton and Laura Malcolm — through the dead man's eccentric condition that his heir must marry his adopted daughter or forfeit the entire estate.

## Summary

After Jasper Treverton's quiet funeral, conducted "in accordance with Jasper Treverton's express desire" (002-004), the will was read in the white-and-gold panelled drawing-room of Hazlehurst Manor. The lawyer Thomas Sampson presided, with the vicar Theodore Clare, Laura Malcolm, and the upper servants in attendance (002-005). The will provided small bequests to servants, a legacy to the vicar, and a complimentary sum to the lawyer — and then delivered its bombshell: the entire remainder of the estate, "real and personal," was left to John Treverton "provided the said John Treverton should marry his dearly-beloved adopted daughter, Laura Malcolm, within one year of his decease" (002-006). If the marriage did not take place, the fortune would pass entirely to the trustees for the erection of a hospital in Beechampton (002-006).

## Context

### Preceding Events

Jasper Treverton had summoned his estranged kinsman John Treverton to his deathbed in Chapter 1, ending a decades-long family feud (001-004). The old man lingered nearly a week, during which John "could not help wishing the old man would make a speedy end of it" (002-002), his thoughts always filled with "the inheritance which seemed so nearly within his grasp" (002-003). After Jasper's death, there followed "three or four days of wearisome delay" for the funeral arrangements, during which John avoided the darkened house as much as possible, spending his time in "long rambles out into the open country" (002-003). The funeral itself took place under a "drizzling rain and a low, lead-coloured sky," with John's thoughts "far away as he stood by the open grave" (002-004).

## Detailed Account

The will reading commenced immediately after the funeral, the mourners returning to a house where "it was a relief to find the blinds drawn up and the dull grey winter light in the rooms" (002-005). Mr. Sampson seated himself at a table in the drawing-room — "a very handsome room — with white and gold panelling, six long windows, and a fireplace at each end" (002-005). The upper servants took their places "in a little group near the door" (002-005).

The will began with the smaller bequests: a small annuity to Andrew Trimmer the butler, sums varying from fifty to two hundred pounds to the coachmen and women servants, a complimentary legacy of a hundred guineas to Thomas Sampson, and a bequest of old plate to Theodore Clare the vicar (002-006). Then came the pivotal clause.

The testator left the remainder of his property to John Treverton on the condition that he marry Laura Malcolm within one year. The estate was to be held in trust during this interval by Clare and Sampson. Should the marriage not occur, the entire estate would pass to the trustees for the erection of a hospital in Beechampton (002-006).

The reaction was immediate and telling. "Miss Malcolm looked up with a startled expression as this strange bequest was read. John Treverton's face assumed a sudden pallor that was by no means flattering to the lady whose fate was involved in the singular condition which attached to his inheritance" (002-007). Braddon notes that "the situation was an awkward one for both" and Laura "rose directly the reading of the will was finished, and left the room without a word" (002-007).

> "Allow me to congratulate you, Mr. Treverton," said Thomas Sampson, folding up the will, and coming to the fireplace by which John Treverton was seated: "you will find yourself a very rich man." (002-008)

In the conversation that followed between John, the vicar, and the lawyer, John's true feelings emerged. He spoke "in a slow, meditative way, and he was pale to the very lips. There was no appearance of triumph or gladness — only an anxious, disappointed expression, which made his handsome face look strangely worn and haggard" (002-012). He protested that Jasper would have done better "by dividing his property between Miss Malcolm and myself, leaving us both free" (002-011).

Mr. Clare rebuked this hesitation: "There are not many men who would think Laura Malcolm an encumbrance to any fortune, Mr. Treverton. I think you will be happier in the possession of such a wife than in the enjoyment of your cousin's wealth, large as it is" (002-013).

## Consequences

The will's marriage condition creates the novel's central tension: two strangers are bound together by a dead man's eccentric codicil, each dependent on the other's consent for their fortune. For John Treverton, it means he must court a woman he barely knows, his every advance shadowed by the suspicion of mercenary motive (002-031). For Laura Malcolm, it means her marriage has become a public condition of inheritance, her private feelings entangled with financial obligation.

The immediate consequence is John's departure to stay with Mr. Sampson at "The Laurels," avoiding the awkwardness of remaining at the Manor with Laura (002-024). Their parting conversation in the study — "a very pale, sad face" on her side, painful embarrassment on his — establishes the emotional register of their relationship for the chapters to come (002-025 to 002-034). The condition itself, with its one-year time limit, becomes the structural deadline against which all subsequent events unfold: the courtship, the wedding, John's disappearance, the murder investigation, and the eventual exoneration.

## Significance

### Plot Function

The will reading is the inciting incident of the novel. Without this event and its marriage condition, there would be no plot — no reason for John Treverton to pursue Laura Malcolm, no need to conceal his identity as Jack Chicot, no pressure that drives the narrative toward its eventual crisis and resolution. The condition transforms what might have been a straightforward inheritance story into a sensation novel, introducing themes of coercion, disguise, and the entanglement of love with money.

### Thematic Resonance

The will crystallizes the novel's central thematic concern with [[marriage-market|marriage as financial transaction]]. Jasper Treverton treats his adopted daughter and his fortune as interchangeable assets to be bundled together in a single bequest. The condition echoes the sensation novel's preoccupation with wills as instruments of control from beyond the grave — the dead hand reaching into the lives of the living. The pallor that "assumed" John Treverton's face (002-007) foreshadows the guilt and concealment that will define his character, while Laura's silent exit marks the beginning of her role as a woman whose fate is determined by men's legal instruments.
