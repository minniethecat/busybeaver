# Desrolles

Desrolles is the primary antagonist of *The Cloven Foot*. A broken-down [[class-and-status|gentleman]] with a cavalry past, he lives under the alias Desrolles as a lodger at No. 14 [[cibber-street|Cibber Street]], Leicester Square, present on the night of [[La-Chicot]]'s [[murder-concept|murder]]. He is eventually revealed as the true killer — [[la-chicot|La Chicot]]'s secret lover, motivated by the diamond necklace he had given her and his fear of exposure — and dies resisting [[the-arrest|arrest]] in Paris.

## Identity

Desrolles operates under multiple [[identity-deception|assumed name]]s throughout the novel. In [[cibber-street|Cibber Street]] he is "Mr. Desrolles," the second-floor lodger, a sufferer from "some chronic ailment" who is "a good lodger except his hours, which were dreadful" (008-108 to 008-110). When he visits [[hazlehurst-manor|Hazlehurst Manor]] to blackmail the Trevertons, he presents himself as "Colonel Mansfield" (026-004). He corrects [[john-treverton|John Treverton]]'s greeting: "Mansfield. I have changed the name of Desrolles" (026-069). His true [[identity]] is eventually established as "Mr. Desrolles, alias Mansfield, alias Malcolm" (040-062) — the same "Malcolm" surname borne by [[laura-malcolm|Laura Malcolm]], because Desrolles claims, whether truthfully or opportunistically, to be Laura's father.

Braddon establishes his untrustworthiness early. No one "who would have trusted Mr. Desrolles in any virtuous enterprise" (006-063). He had once been "Captain Desmond" with "something of the old 'long sword, saddle, bridle' swagger, which had been peculiar to him twenty years ago, when he called himself Captain Desmond, and had not yet forgotten his youthful days in a cavalry regiment" (045-043).

## Biography

### Background

Desrolles is a [[class-and-status|gentleman]] by birth who fell into dissolution. He had served in a cavalry regiment as a young man and retained the military bearing and manners of that life. By the time the novel opens, he has descended into the bohemian underworld of [[london|London]] and Paris — a brandy-drinker, a frequenter of low cafes and billiard rooms, living by his wits and his capacity for manipulation. He had spent his "most reckless years in Paris; he knew [[london|the city]] by heart, with all her charms, with all her vices" (045-003).

In [[cibber-street|Cibber Street]], he occupies the second-floor rooms at [[mrs-evitt|Mrs. Evitt]]'s lodging house, directly below the Chicots' rooms on the third floor and above [[mrs-rawber|Mrs. Rawber]] the tragedienne on the first. He is [[jack-chicot|Jack Chicot]]'s confidant and [[la-chicot|La Chicot]]'s "usual escort" home from the theatre (016-053). [[mrs-evitt|Mrs. Evitt]] later testifies that he was "a good lodger" and that his hours were dreadful (008-110).

### Key Actions

**The Diamond Necklace.** Desrolles's central secret is that he was [[la-chicot|La Chicot]]'s anonymous admirer — the mysterious giver of the diamond necklace and later the pearl bracelet. When Lemuel Grundy presents [[la-chicot|La Chicot]] with the diamond necklace at [[the-prince-frederick-theatre|the Prince Frederick Theatre]], Desrolles is standing beside her, his face marked by "a passing distortion" at the sight of the jewels (016-037). He urges her to keep them: "If your husband were to see them he would take them away and sell them" (016-034). The diamonds are in fact imitation paste — revealed in the novel's final [[the-discovery|revelation]]s as a key to Desrolles's motive.

**[[the-murder-of-la-chicot|The Murder of La Chicot]].** On the night of the [[murder-concept|murder]] in Chapter 17, Desrolles appears to have "slept more soundly than his neighbours" (017-003). He is the coolest head at the scene: it is Desrolles who says "He has made a bolt of it. That looks rather bad," referring to [[jack-chicot|Jack Chicot]]'s disappearance (017-015). He suggests sending for a policeman (017-011). Throughout [[the-coroners-court|the inquest]] he gives evidence that "threw no new light upon the [[murder-concept|murder]]" (018-040). He stands "beside the grave, decently grave and sympathetic" at [[la-chicot|La Chicot]]'s funeral, placing white camellias on her coffin (018-043).

Crucially, [[mrs-evitt|Mrs. Evitt]] later discovers physical evidence linking Desrolles to the crime: in the dead woman's hand was found "a little tuft of iron-grey hair" — "just the colour of Desrolles' hair. I could swear to it" (041-109). The [[murder-concept|murder]]er had been dragged by the hair during the struggle, and the iron-grey hair matches Desrolles exactly. A bloodstained chintz dressing-gown belonging to Desrolles is also found (041-123).

**The Blackmail of the Trevertons.** Desrolles's most brazen act is his journey to [[hazlehurst-manor|Hazlehurst Manor]] in Chapter 26 ("A Disinterested Parent"). Presenting himself as "Colonel Mansfield," he confronts Laura Treverton with the claim that he is her father and extracts a hundred pounds from [[john-treverton|John Treverton]], plus a promise of a thousand pounds a year (026-131 to 026-132). He had learned that [[jack-chicot|Jack Chicot]] and [[john-treverton|John Treverton]] were the same man, and he exploits this knowledge ruthlessly. "He was respectabilised by a full purse" after leaving the [[hazlehurst-manor|Manor House]] (027-002).

**Flight and Hunt.** After the Treverton [[murder-concept|murder]] inquiry resumes and a reward of ten pounds is advertised for his whereabouts (040-071), Desrolles flees to Paris. Chapter 45 ("[[the-hunt-for-desrolles|The Hunt for Desrolles]]") traces his last days: he takes up residence in his old haunt in the Students' Quarter, drinks absinthe, gambles, and plans an [[the-arrest|escape]] to South America under the alias "Colonel Mowbray." He books passage on the *Reine Blanche*, bound for Valparaiso (045-034 to 045-036).

### Fate

Unaware that he has been tracked by an English detective, Desrolles returns to his lodging on the Boulevard Louis Capet in a drunken state. The detective confronts him: "I [[the-arrest|arrest]] you on suspicion of being concerned in the [[murder-concept|murder]] of [[la-chicot|La Chicot]]" (045-049). Desrolles draws a knife and fights with animal desperation — he had "been a famous bruiser in days of old" and wrestles "as only rage and despair can wrestle" (045-055 to 045-056). During the struggle, he is hurled against the papered wall of his room. The "frail lath and plaster partition split asunder, the rotten wood crumbled," and "with one hoarse shriek, Desrolles rolled backwards into empty air" (045-062). He falls from the fifth storey, striking the timber shoring on the way down, and dies on the pavement below. The narrator delivers the verdict: "It was a bad end of a bad man" (045-063).

## Literary Significance

Desrolles functions as the sensation novel's [[class-and-status|class]]ic hidden villain — the [[murder-concept|murder]]er concealed in plain sight among the witnesses and mourners. Braddon employs him to embody several key themes of the genre: the porous boundary between gentility and criminality, the weaponization of family secrets, and the danger posed by men who have fallen from their [[class-and-status|class]]. His multiple aliases (Desrolles / Mansfield / Malcolm / Desmond / Mowbray) make him a figure of radical instability — a man with no fixed [[identity]], and therefore no fixed moral character.

His role as [[la-chicot|La Chicot]]'s secret lover and anonymous gift-giver makes him a study in obsession and possession. The diamond necklace — revealed to be imitation paste — crystallizes the novel's concern with surfaces and deceptions: Desrolles himself is as counterfeit as the jewels he gave, a hollow imitation of a [[class-and-status|gentleman]]. His death by falling through a rotted wall literalizes his own structural decay: the man and the building collapse together, both rotten from within.

Structurally, Desrolles is the answer to the novel's central mystery. While [[john-treverton|John Treverton]] shoulders the weight of suspicion through the trial chapters, the real killer has been hiding among the same [[cibber-street|Cibber Street]] witnesses who gave evidence at [[the-coroners-court|the inquest]]. Braddon's management of this concealed villain — present in the text from Chapter 4 but never overtly implicated until the final [[the-discovery|revelation]]s — demonstrates her mastery of the delayed-disclosure technique that defines sensation fiction.
