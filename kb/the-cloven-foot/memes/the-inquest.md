# The Inquest into La Chicot's Murder

The coroner's inquest into the violent death of [[la-chicot|La Chicot]] was held at the Prince of Wales's Feathers — "more popularly known as the Feathers" — a public-house at the corner of Cibber Street and Woodpecker Court (018-024). Held at noon on the day following the murder, the inquest was the formal legal proceeding that gathered witness testimony, established the circumstances of the crime, and identified the missing husband Jack Chicot as the prime suspect.

## Summary

The inquest was convened at the Prince of Wales's Feathers public house, where the coroner examined the witnesses who had been present in the Cibber Street lodging-house on the night of the murder: the doctor, the police-sergeant, the detective who assisted in the examination, [[desrolles|Desrolles]], [[Mrs. Evitt]], and [[Mrs. Rawber]] (018-024). The most important witness — Jack Chicot, the dead woman's husband — "had not been seen since he left the house under the pretence of summoning the police," a disappearance that "was the most remarkable feature in the case, and puzzled the Coroner" (018-024). After hearing the testimony, which "threw no new light upon the mystery," the Coroner "adjourned the inquiry for a week, in the hope that further evidence would be forthcoming" (018-041).

## Context

### Preceding Events

The murder had been discovered at three o'clock on the previous morning. Jack Chicot, returning to Cibber Street, found his wife dead in their bed and raised the alarm, then left to summon the police and never returned. The police had conducted a thorough search of the premises, examining every room and questioning the inhabitants, but had found no conclusive evidence (017-063 to 017-069). By morning, the news "had spread far and wide," and "there was a crowd gathered round the house in Cibber Street all the morning" (018-002). George Gerard called at the house between nine and ten o'clock, and it was he who discovered the Italian dagger in Chicot's paint-box — a discovery that would prove decisive (018-016 to 018-017).

## Detailed Account

The inquest was held at the Feathers public house, where witnesses were examined one by one. The press descended on the case: "The newspaper reporters forced their way into her house in defiance of her protests" (018-002), and finding Mrs. Evitt slow to answer, "got hold of Mr. Desrolles, who was very ready to talk and to drink with every comer" (018-002).

The Coroner questioned Mrs. Evitt closely about the Chicots' domestic life. She testified that they quarrelled frequently, but that the violence was always La Chicot's: "I have heard her violent, but never him" (018-027). She offered the deeply suggestive testimony that La Chicot had once told her, "Mrs. Evitt, that man hates me too much to strike me. If he was once to give way to his temper he'd be the death of me" (018-031). The Coroner cut her off — "Come, come, we can't hear anything about your impressions. This isn't evidence" (018-032) — but her words had their effect.

Mrs. Rawber's evidence "merely confirmed Mrs. Evitt as to the hour at which they had been aroused, and the conduct of Jack Chicot," with both women agreeing as to "the ghastly look of his face" (018-038).

> Desrolles was the last witness examined. As he stood up to answer the Coroner, he caught sight of a familiar face in the crowd near the doorway. It was the countenance of Joseph Lemuel, the stock broker, sorely changed since Desrolles had seen it last. Close by Mr. Lemuel's side appeared a well-known criminal lawyer. Desrolles' bister complexion grew a shade grayer at sight of these two faces, both intently watchful. (018-039)

Desrolles's testimony was carefully crafted to incriminate the husband without appearing to do so. He said the Chicots were "both excellent creatures, but not suited to each other. They did not live happily together." He had "never seen Jack Chicot guilty of any act of absolute violence towards his wife," but believed "there was a good deal of bitterness in his mind." The marriage, he concluded, was simply "an ill-assorted marriage, and they were a very unhappy couple" (018-040).

Throughout the inquest, George Gerard watched from "a crowded corner of the room, but he held his peace as to that discovery of the dagger in Jack Chicot's colour-box" (018-042). His silence at this critical moment — motivated perhaps by his own complicated feelings about La Chicot, or by uncertainty about what his discovery meant — delayed the full investigation.

After hearing all testimony, the Coroner "adjourned the inquiry for a week, in the hope that further evidence would be forthcoming." The prevailing sentiment was clear: "There was a feeling in the court that a very strong suspicion attached to the dead woman's husband, and that if he did not turn up speedily he would have to be looked for" (018-041).

## Consequences

The inquest's failure to produce definitive findings left the case unresolved, and the press filled the vacuum with sensational speculation. George Gerard eventually wrote to *The Times* describing the dagger, and the famous surgeon who had treated La Chicot confirmed "such a wound could have been inflicted by just such a dagger" (018-051). The newspapers seized on this: "They depicted the whole scene as vividly as if it had been shown to them in a charmed sleep" (018-052).

The public pressure to find Jack Chicot intensified. The inquest, by establishing him as the prime suspect in an official legal proceeding, set in motion the machinery of investigation that would eventually lead not to Chicot's conviction but to the unraveling of the true story — the exposure of Desrolles as the murderer and John Treverton's exoneration in the novel's final chapters.

Furthermore, the inquest's adjournment without resolution created the period of suspended judgment during which the novel's central tensions could develop: John Treverton's concealment at Hazlehurst, his secret marriage to Laura Malcolm at Camelot Church, and the slow accumulation of evidence that would ultimately redirect suspicion toward the true killer.

## Significance

### Plot Function

The inquest functions as the novel's first formal attempt to impose legal order on the chaos of the murder. It gathers the scattered testimony into an official narrative, and in doing so, it entrenches the wrong narrative — that Jack Chicot murdered his wife. This misdirection is essential to the sensation novel's structure: the legal system, far from discovering truth, perpetuates error, and the real detective work must be done outside official channels by characters like Edward Clare and George Gerard.

### Thematic Resonance

The inquest embodies the novel's critique of surface-level justice. Desrolles, the true murderer, testifies "with the hypocrite's visage" as a cooperative witness, while the innocent man is condemned in absentia by newspaper leader-writers who "painted, in a blaze of lurid light, the scene of the murder" (018-052). The Prince of Wales's Feathers — a public house rather than a courtroom — highlights the porous boundary between official justice and public entertainment in the sensation-novel world, where murder becomes theatre and inquest testimony is consumed alongside gin.
