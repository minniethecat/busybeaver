# Mr. Clare

Mr. Clare is the vicar of the parish near [[camelot-church|Camelot]], a clergyman of the Church of England who serves, alongside [[thomas-sampson|Thomas Sampson]], as one of the two trustees of [[jasper-treverton|Jasper Treverton]]'s will. His dual role as spiritual counselor and legal executor places him at the intersection of moral and temporal authority in the novel's exploration of [[inheritance]], [[justice]], and family secrets.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Clare appears primarily in the novel's opening chapters, where his presence at [[inheritance|the will]]-reading establishes the legal and moral framework within which the subsequent drama unfolds. As a vicar, he brings the weight of ecclesiastical authority to the proceedings; as a trustee, he bears legal responsibility for executing a will whose terms create evident in[[justice]]. His claret-drinking habits and his patient, measured manner make him a distinctly human figure rather than a mere institutional representative.

## Biography

### Background

Mr. Clare is the vicar of the parish that includes the Treverton estate. He had known [[jasper-treverton|Jasper Treverton]] in life and was trusted sufficiently to be named a trustee. He is fond of a particular claret, though he admits he "had never been quite sure" of its quality [002-036]. This small self-deprecation suggests a man who is conscientious without being pompous.

### Key Actions

- Makes the arrangements for [[jasper-treverton|Jasper Treverton]]'s funeral [002-003].
- Is present at the reading of [[inheritance|the will]] alongside [[laura-malcolm|Laura Malcolm]] and [[thomas-sampson|Thomas Sampson]] [002-005].
- Encourages the heir to accept the [[inheritance|fortune]], saying "I think you will be happier for not having any choice" — a remark that suggests both pastoral wisdom and an uncomfortable complicity in [[inheritance|the will]]'s in[[justice]] [002-013].
- Acknowledges but does not challenge the impropriety of a will that disinherits [[laura-malcolm|Laura Malcolm]], saying merely that it would be well to carry out the testator's wishes [002-018].
- As co-trustee, wishes the heir to come to [[london|London]] [002-028].

## Significance

Mr. Clare represents the established Church's entanglement with property and [[inheritance]] in Victorian England. As a vicar and trustee, he is both spiritual guide and legal functionary — roles that the novel suggests are not always compatible. His patient, accommodating manner masks a deeper failure: he never questions the moral legitimacy of a will that robs an orphaned ward of her [[inheritance]]. His claret and his measured phrases make him likeable, but his complicity in the novel's foundational in[[justice]] makes him a more morally complex figure than he first appears.

[[laura-malcolm|Laura Malcolm]]'s cool response to the suggestion that she might consider him as more than a friend — she likes him "as a friend, and all that kind of thing" — hints at a romantic subplot that the novel raises and dismisses, keeping the focus on the darker currents of the Treverton [[inheritance]] [003-025].
