# The Reading of Jasper Treverton's Will

The formal reading of Jasper Treverton's last will and testament, which took place in the drawing-room of Treverton Manor shortly after his funeral, revealed a startling condition: John Treverton would inherit the estate only if he married Laura Malcolm within a twelvemonth.

## Summary

Following Jasper Treverton's funeral, his will was read by the lawyer Thomas Sampson in the drawing-room of Treverton Manor, in the presence of the vicar Theodore Clare, Laura Malcolm, and the upper servants. The will contained small bequests to servants and friends, but the bulk of the estate went to John Treverton on one extraordinary condition — he must marry Laura Malcolm within twelve months, or forfeit the inheritance. [[PN002-005]][[PN002-006]]

The condition shocked both principal parties. Laura Malcolm rose and left the room directly the reading was finished, without saying a word. John Treverton turned pale, and later expressed to the vicar and lawyer his wish that Jasper had simply divided the property between them, leaving both free. [[PN002-007]][[PN002-011]]

## Context

### Preceding Events

Jasper Treverton, the master of Treverton Manor, had been ill and bedridden for nearly a week after the arrival of his cousin John Treverton. During this time John waited impatiently for the inheritance that seemed within his grasp. [[PN002-001]][[PN002-002]]

After six days, Jasper Treverton died, with Laura Malcolm's hand in his and his face turned toward her. A quiet funeral followed, in accordance with his express wishes. [[PN002-003]][[PN002-004]]

## Detailed Account

The will was read in the drawing-room of Treverton Manor, a handsome room with white and gold panelling, six long windows, and a fireplace at each end. Thomas Sampson seated himself at a table, with Mr. Clare, Laura Malcolm, and the upper servants in attendance. [[PN002-005]]

The will was simply worded. It began with bequests to the old servants, a small annuity to Andrew Trimmer the butler, sums of fifty to two hundred pounds to the coachmen and women servants, a complimentary legacy of a hundred guineas to Thomas Sampson, and a bequest of old plate to Theodore Clare the vicar. After these legacies, the testator left the remainder of his property, real and personal, to his cousin John Treverton — but on the condition that he marry Laura Malcolm within a twelvemonth. [[PN002-006]]

> "Allow me to congratulate you, Mr. Treverton," said Thomas Sampson, folding up the will, and coming to the fireplace by which John Treverton was seated: "you will find yourself a very rich man." [[PN002-008]]

John Treverton responded doubtfully, noting that the inheritance was conditional upon Miss Malcolm accepting him as her husband, "which she may not be." He expressed his view that Jasper would have done better by dividing the property between Laura and himself, leaving them both free. [[PN002-009]][[PN002-011]]

The vicar reassured him: "There are not many men who would think Laura Malcolm an encumbrance to any fortune, Mr. Treverton. I think you will be happier in the possession of such a wife than in the enjoyment of your cousin's wealth, large as it is." [[PN002-013]]

## Consequences

The will's condition created an intensely awkward situation between John Treverton and Laura Malcolm. Before leaving the Manor, John paid Laura a farewell visit in the twilight. She was calm but pale, and he found the conversation painfully embarrassing. He accepted Thomas Sampson's invitation to stay at "The Laurels" to tour the estate. [[PN002-024]][PN002-025]][[PN002-031]]

The condition fundamentally shaped the entire plot of the novel, binding John Treverton and Laura Malcolm together in a marriage neither had chosen freely, and setting in motion the chain of events that would lead to the Cibber Street murder and its aftermath. [[PN002-035]]

## Significance

### Plot Function

The will reading is the inciting event of the novel. It transforms John Treverton from a hopeful heir into a man trapped by a condition he cannot refuse, and it places Laura Malcolm in the humiliating position of being a condition attached to a fortune. Every subsequent event in the novel — the marriage, the London life, the murder of La Chicot, the trial — traces back to this moment.

### Thematic Resonance

The will embodies the novel's central theme of marriage as transaction. Jasper Treverton treated Laura Malcolm not as an adopted daughter but as a piece of property to be disposed of alongside his estate. John Treverton's visible discomfort at the reading — his pallor and anxious expression — signals that he understands the moral queasiness of the arrangement even as he cannot afford to refuse it.
