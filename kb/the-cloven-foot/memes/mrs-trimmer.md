# Mrs. Trimmer

Mrs. Trimmer is the cook and housekeeper at [[hazlehurst-manor|Hazlehurst Manor]], the wife of [[andrew-trimmer|Andrew Trimmer]], the butler. Together with her husband, she has served the Treverton family for decades, forming part of the old servant establishment that embodies continuity and domestic order at the [[hazlehurst-manor|Manor House]]. Her loyalty to [[laura-malcolm|Laura Malcolm]] and her practical wisdom make her a voice of grounded common sense amid the novel's emotional upheavals.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Trimmer appears as a background figure in the Hazlehurst chapters, her presence marking the rhythms of domestic life — the baking and roasting, the preparation of wedding breakfasts, the anxious watching over the family's [[inheritance|fortune]]s. Her retort to her husband's suggestion that Laura might marry for money — "it isn't in Miss Laura to marry a man she wasn't fond of, not for all the [[inheritance|fortune]]s in Christendom" — is one of the novel's clearest statements of Laura's integrity [009-055].

## Biography

### Background

Mrs. Trimmer has been cook and housekeeper at [[hazlehurst-manor|Hazlehurst Manor]] for nearly as long as her husband has been butler — close to thirty years. She witnessed [[laura-malcolm|Laura Malcolm]]'s arrival at the Manor as a delicate child and has watched her grow into the mistress of the house. Her deep loyalty to "Miss Laura" is matched by a practical investment in the household's stability: like her husband, her livelihood depends on [[inheritance|the estate]] remaining a private residence.

### Key Actions

- Retorts sharply to her husband's [[greed|mercenary]] suggestion about marriage, defending Laura's character: "It isn't in Miss Laura to marry a man she wasn't fond of, not for all the [[inheritance|fortune]]s in Christendom" [009-055].
- Roasts herself and her poultry late into the night before the wedding, despite [[laura-malcolm|Miss Malcolm]] having said "a very simple meal would be wanted" [011-049].
- Welcomes Laura Treverton back to the [[hazlehurst-manor|Manor House]] with warm eagerness, saying it "will be happiness to have him settle down among us" [037-005].
- Tells Laura, in a moment of confidential friendliness, that the separation from her husband "must have been a sore trial" [037-007].
- Advises Laura on household matters with the settled authority of long service, declaring "nothing like old ways" [037-011].
- Is distressed when the household falls into irregular patterns, declaring "there was no pleasure in cooking dinners that nobody cared to eat" [021-002].

### Fate / Resolution

Mrs. Trimmer remains at [[hazlehurst-manor|Hazlehurst Manor]], her position secured by the survival of the Treverton marriage. Her final appearances show her in her accustomed role — managing the household, offering advice, and taking quiet satisfaction in the restoration of domestic order after the disruptions of [[john-treverton|John Treverton]]'s absence.

## Significance

Mrs. Trimmer, like her husband Andrew, belongs to the tradition of loyal family retainers in Victorian fiction. Her role is modest but significant: she provides a servant's-eye view of the gentry's emotional dramas, and her plain-spoken defence of Laura's character cuts through the calculations of [[inheritance]] and money that dominate the novel's central plot. Her anxiety about the household's [[fate]] — "what's to become of you and me?" — reminds the reader that the melodrama of wills and marriage has consequences for people whose lives depend on the stability of the world above stairs.
