# Cibber Street

Cibber Street is a narrow London street in the Leicester Square district whose lodging-house becomes the scene of the central crime of *The Cloven Foot* — the murder of [[La-Chicot]]. It is a shabby-genteel quarter of London, housing a transient population of theatrical people, and its lodging-house at the corner of Woodpecker Court is where the novel's pivotal violent event occurs.

## Location

Cibber Street is situated in the "close and smoky region of Leicester Square" (008-081), a densely populated urban quarter of central London. The lodging-house stands at the corner of Cibber Street and Woodpecker Court (018-024). The street is described as part of "that essentially dramatic, mimic, and demonstrative region" (008-002), close to the Royal Prince Frederick Theatre where La Chicot performs. The public-house at the corner serves as the local gathering point and the scene of the inquest that follows the murder (018-024).

## Description

The houses in Cibber Street are "two hundred years old" (018-016). The Chicots' lodging-house is a typical rooming establishment of the period, run by the landlady [[mrs-evitt]]. The interior includes:

> "For these houses in Cibber Street were two hundred years old — there stood the old oak bureau with a sloping front and a receptacle for stationery and an inkstand at the top, in the recess of which Gerard discovered the weapon" (018-016)

The lodging is modest, with Jack Chicot spending "very little of his life in the Cibber Street lodging. There was not even the pretence of a home in the two shabby rooms" (014-040). The front kitchen on the ground floor serves as Mrs. Evitt's sleeping quarters (017-003). The upstairs bedroom is the scene of the murder, where La Chicot is found stabbed in her bed.

The window-seat in the front room contains a hidden recess — an old oak bureau — where the murder weapon (an Italian dagger) is discovered after the crime (018-016). The street itself is characterized by its theatrical population and "that essentially dramatic" quality: excitement and agitation were its "normal condition" (008-002).

## Significance in the Novel

### Plot Function

Cibber Street is the location of the novel's central crime: the murder of La Chicot. On a winter night, at about three o'clock in the morning, the inhabitants of the lodging-house are "scared... by the sudden ringing of bells, violent and long continued" (017-003). Jack Chicot, having returned home after eleven o'clock (008-107), discovers his wife's body and raises the alarm before disappearing.

The murder scene itself is vividly rendered. The surgeon finds evidence of a violent struggle: "on the round, white wrist of the dead a purple bruise showed where a savage hand had gripped that lovely arm; on the right shoulder... appeared the marks of strong fingers" (018-007). In the murdered woman's clenched hand, Mrs. Evitt discovers "a little tuft of iron-grey hair, which looked as if it had been torn from a man's head" (017-058 to 017-059).

The subsequent inquest is held at the public-house at the corner of Cibber Street and Woodpecker Court (018-024). The discovery of the Italian dagger in Jack Chicot's paint-box, hidden in the old oak bureau, makes him the prime suspect (018-016 to 018-017).

After the murder, the street becomes a site of morbid public curiosity: "the whole of Cibber Street turned out for the occasion" of the funeral, and a crowd gathered round the house all morning (018-002, 018-043).

### Thematic Role

Cibber Street represents the hidden/secret London of Braddon's sensation fiction — the shabby-genteel underworld where respectable facades crumble. It is the antithesis of Hazlehurst Manor: where the manor is spacious, landed, and ancestral, Cibber Street is cramped, rented, and transient. The street embodies the novel's theme of dual identity — it is where John Treverton lives under the alias "Jack Chicot," his true identity concealed from both his wife and his later Devonshire life. The lodging-house, with "not even the pretence of a home" (014-040), symbolizes the moral rootlessness that leads to catastrophe.

The "dramatic, mimic, and demonstrative" character of the neighborhood (008-002) also underscores the novel's preoccupation with performance and authenticity: in a street of actors, the murder itself becomes a public spectacle.

### Key Rooms / Areas

- **The upstairs bedroom**: The murder scene; La Chicot is found stabbed in her bed, the counterpane bearing "hideous stains" (017-007).
- **The front room / office**: Contains the old oak bureau with the hidden recess where the murder weapon is discovered (018-016).
- **The front kitchen**: Mrs. Evitt's sleeping quarters, where she is roused by the alarm (017-003).
- **The public-house at the corner**: Scene of the inquest and the gathering point for the neighborhood (018-024).
- **Woodpecker Court**: The adjoining court at whose corner the lodging-house stands (018-024).
