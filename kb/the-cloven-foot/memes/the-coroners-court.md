# The Coroner's Court

The legal body charged with investigating the violent death of La Chicot and determining whether a crime has been committed and by whom. In *The Cloven Foot*, the coroner's court convenes at the Prince of Wales's Feathers public-house and represents the first official attempt to impose legal order on the chaos of the murder -- an attempt that, like all institutional responses in the novel, proves insufficient.

## Description

A coroner's court in Victorian England was a distinct legal institution with a specific jurisdiction: to inquire into sudden, violent, or suspicious deaths and to determine, with the aid of a jury, the cause of death and the identity of any responsible party. Unlike a criminal trial, a coroner's inquest was investigative rather than prosecutorial -- its purpose was to establish facts, not to convict. The inquest in *The Cloven Foot* is held on the day after the murder, at noon, at the Prince of Wales's Feathers public-house at the corner of Cibber Street and Woodpecker Court [018-002][018-024].

The coroner himself is unnamed in the novel. He is characterized by his impatience with gossipy testimony and his insistence on facts -- repeatedly interrupting Mrs. Evitt when her recollections drift from evidence to impressions. His manner is brusque and procedural: "Come, come, we can't hear anything about your impressions. This isn't evidence" [018-032]; "I want to hear facts, not assertions" [018-034]. Despite this rigour, his inquiry produces no result. The adjournment "in the hope that further evidence would be forthcoming" [018-041] distinguishes the coroner's court from the Bow Street Police-Court proceedings that follow later in the novel (Chapters 40 and 44), which operate under the authority of a magistrate and result in Treverton's discharge.

## Role in the Novel

The coroner's court occupies a single densely narrated chapter (18), but its influence extends across the entire novel:

- **The inquest convenes (Ch.18).** The court assembles at the Feathers. There is already "a crowd gathered round the house in Cibber Street" and newspaper reporters have forced their way into Mrs. Evitt's lodgings. The public appetite for the inquest is voracious. [018-002]

- **The witnesses are called.** The court hears from the doctor (who established time of death), the police-sergeant, the detective who searched the premises, Desrolles, Mrs. Evitt, and Mrs. Rawber. Each witness contributes fragments of information, but the crucial witness -- Jack Chicot, the husband -- is absent, having vanished after going out to summon the police. This disappearance is "the most remarkable feature in the case" and "puzzled the Coroner." [018-024]

- **The testimony paints a picture.** Mrs. Evitt describes a troubled marriage but stops short of accusing Jack Chicot of violence. La Chicot, she says, told her: "Mrs. Evitt, that man hates me too much to strike me. If he was once to give way to his temper he'd be the death of me" -- words that, while hearsay, suggest a husband capable of murder [018-031]. Yet when pressed directly, Mrs. Evitt concedes she has never seen Jack Chicot commit an act of violence. The Coroner, frustrated, cuts her off: "We can't hear anything about your impressions." [018-032]

- **Desrolles testifies.** As the last witness, Desrolles gives carefully non-committal evidence: the Chicots were "both excellent creatures, but not suited to each other... an ill-assorted marriage... much to be pitied, both." His testimony "threw no new light upon the mystery." But his composure is shaken when he spots Joseph Lemuel and a criminal lawyer in the crowd -- faces whose presence suggests forces beyond the scope of the coroner's inquiry. [018-039][018-040]

- **Gerard remains silent.** George Gerard watches the inquest "from a crowded corner of the room, but he held his peace as to that discovery of the dagger in Jack Chicot's colour-box." The most incriminating piece of physical evidence in the case -- the thin Italian dagger whose blade matches the wound -- goes unmentioned in the official record because a single witness chooses silence. [018-042]

- **The adjournment.** Unable to reach a finding, the Coroner "adjourned the inquiry for a week, in the hope that further evidence would be forthcoming." The court's failure to produce a verdict is the structural pivot of the novel: from this moment, the search for truth passes from official institutions to private individuals. The coroner's court formally yields to the press (which fills the void with lurid speculation), to Edward Clare (who pursues his own malicious investigation), and eventually to Mrs. Evitt (whose guilty conscience reveals the truth that the court could not discover). [018-041]

- **The court of public opinion.** The press, in its subsequent coverage, effectively substitutes its own judgment for the coroner's. "There was a feeling in the court that a very strong suspicion attached to the dead woman's husband, and that if he did not turn up speedily he would have to be looked for." This "feeling" -- not a verdict but an atmosphere -- becomes the basis for the manhunt and, eventually, for Treverton's arrest. The coroner's court, by refusing to close the case, inadvertently empowers the press to close it in its own way. [018-041]

## Legal Context

The Victorian coroner's inquest was a distinctive feature of English legal procedure, combining the functions of a modern medical examiner, a grand jury, and a public inquiry. Coroners were elected officials, not necessarily trained in law or medicine, and their courts operated with considerably less formality than the assizes or magistrates' courts. The practice of holding inquests in public-houses was common throughout the nineteenth century, particularly in London, reflecting both the absence of dedicated court buildings in many districts and the traditional role of the pub as a community gathering space.

Braddon's choice of the Feathers as the inquest venue is not incidental but pointed. By placing the legal inquiry in a public-house -- a space associated with gossip, drink, and informal social exchange -- she implicitly questions the dignity and effectiveness of the proceeding. The coroner's authority is diluted by the environment: the crowd pressing at the door, the beer on the tables, the landlady's meandering testimony. The result is a court that hears but cannot judge, that assembles evidence but cannot make sense of it.

## Significance

The coroner's court represents the first failure of institutional justice in the novel. It is followed by the failure of Scotland Yard (which cannot find Jack Chicot or uncover the true murderer), the failure of the press (which constructs a false narrative), and ultimately the near-failure of the Bow Street proceedings (which would have convicted an innocent man had Mrs. Evitt not belatedly disclosed her evidence). In Braddon's sensation-novel world, legal institutions are systematically unreliable: they process information but cannot perceive truth, they hear testimony but cannot weigh it, they adjourn where they should conclude.

The coroner's inquest also establishes the novel's distinctive temporality. Justice in *The Cloven Foot* is always deferred: the inquest adjourns, the trial is postponed, the arrest comes late, the crucial evidence is withheld. This temporal structure creates the suspense on which the sensation novel depends, but it also carries a thematic argument: that official processes of truth-finding are inherently incomplete, that the resolutions provided by courts and inquests are always provisional, and that real justice -- the exposure of the true murderer and the vindication of the falsely accused -- requires resources that institutional proceedings cannot supply.
