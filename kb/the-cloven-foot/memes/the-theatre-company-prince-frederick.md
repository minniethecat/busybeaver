# The Royal Prince Frederick Theatre

The Royal Prince Frederick Theatre and Music Hall, situated near Leicester Square in London, is the theatrical venue managed by the enterprising impresario Mr. Smolendo. Originally a music hall, lately licensed as a theatre, it becomes the stage on which La Chicot's meteoric London career is performed -- and the engine that drives the novel's Bohemian subplot, bringing together dancer and surgeon, stockbroker and caricaturist, in a demimonde of spectacle, champagne, and moral danger that stands in sharp contrast to the rural gentry world of Hazlehurst.

## Description

The Prince Frederick is introduced in Chapter 4 as a house of popular entertainment that has recently expanded its ambitions. It occupies a position in the theatrical landscape somewhere between the legitimate West End theatres (like the Prince of Wales's, where Edward Clare considers taking a stall) and the rougher music halls. Smolendo has converted it from a music hall into a theatre proper, and his business model depends on importing Parisian sensation: "All the most notorious Parisian successes in the way of spectacular drama, all the fairy extravaganzas and demon ballets and comic operettas were reproduced by Mr. Smolendo at the Prince Frederick" [004-018].

The theatre is located in the heart of theatrical London, near Leicester Square -- a neighbourhood "essentially dramatic, musical, and terpsichorean" [008-002], crowded with actors, dancers, and the trades that serve them. The Chicots' lodgings in Cibber Street are chosen specifically because Smolendo recommends the area and because "the Prince Frederick is situated" nearby [004-024].

Smolendo's management philosophy is both commercial and perfectionist: "He had found out that perfection always pays." His productions are expensively mounted, his talent "the best in every line, from his prima donna to his gasman" [006-014], and his productions designed entirely around his star.

## Role in the Novel

The Prince Frederick is the institutional centre of the novel's theatrical world and the catalyst for several crucial plot developments:

- **La Chicot's London career (Ch.4).** Smolendo discovers La Chicot at the declining Students' Theatre in Paris and brings her to London, calculating that "she'll take the town by storm, and she'll draw better for her first three seasons than any star I've had since I began management" [004-020]. She is billed as "Mademoiselle Chicot, premiere danseuse at the Royal Prince Frederick Theatre and Music Hall" -- "quite the handsomest woman in London" [004-003].

- **The fashionable destination (Ch.4--6).** The theatre attracts a cross-class audience: "the fine flower of the aristocracy... met and mingled in the stalls at the Prince Frederick" alongside medical students, stockbrokers, and the merely curious. La Chicot's performances turn the Prince Frederick into the most fashionable venue in London: "the theatre was nightly crowded to suffocation. There were morning performances every Saturday. Stalls and boxes were booked a month in advance" [014-004].

- **The spectacular burlesque (Ch.6).** Smolendo produces a grand fairy spectacle with La Chicot in six costumes -- fountain of golden water, fish-woman, bayadere, debardeur, wood nymph, odalisque -- all "equally original, expensive, and audacious" [014-006]. The climactic tableau of the coral caves features an iron telescopic lift that carries La Chicot upward through "an ocean of blue gauze." Jack Chicot protests the dangerous machinery, but Smolendo dismisses his concerns: "My dear fellow, these men all drink... But Roberts is a treasure. He's always sober in business" [006-031].

- **The stage accident (Ch.8).** The ironwork gives way during a performance. "The ironwork creaked, groaned, trembled, and then gave way. There was a shrill scream from the dancer, a cry of horror from the men at the wings, and La Chicot was lying in the middle of the stage, a confused heap of tumbled gauze and silver, silent and unconscious, while the green curtain came down with a run" [008-023]. The accident breaks La Chicot's leg and introduces George Gerard, the medical student in attendance, into the narrative -- a meeting that will have profound consequences for the plot.

- **Gerard's obsession.** Among the frequenters of the Prince Frederick is George Gerard, who attends nightly -- his fellow students marvel that a man who "despised ballet dancing" now watches "with his attention as fixed as if he were watching Paget using the knife." Gerard's attendance at the Prince Frederick is the origin of his obsessive love for La Chicot, which in turn drives his presence at the crime scene, his discovery of the dagger, and his eventual recruitment by Edward Clare. [004-065][004-066]

- **Joseph Lemuel in the stalls.** The wealthy stockbroker Joseph Lemuel is another regular at the Prince Frederick, watching La Chicot with an intensity that Edward Clare notes: "There was a man with a pale Jewish face and dark earnest eyes who never took his gaze off the dancer." Lemuel becomes La Chicot's anonymous admirer, sending champagne, a bracelet, and eventually diamonds with an offer of a house in Mayfair -- the false diamonds that will prove to be the murderer's motive. [015-080]

- **The company at the funeral (Ch.18).** After La Chicot's murder, "All the company from the Prince Frederick was there, besides much more of dramatic and equestrian London" at her funeral at Kensal Green. Mr. Smolendo "with his own hands, placed a wreath of white camellias on the coffin" [018-043].

- **The theatre's collapse (Ch.18).** Without its star, the Prince Frederick collapses. Smolendo finds "an all-accomplished lady to take La Chicot's place in the burlesque; but the public did not believe in the all-accomplished lady -- who was old enough to have been La Chicot's mother -- and Mr. Smolendo saw his theatre a desert of empty benches." The fate of the Prince Frederick mirrors the fate of its star: her death extinguishes the enterprise built entirely upon her celebrity. "The tide of fashion rolled away to other theatres; and the bark that carried Mr. Smolendo's fortunes was left stranded on the shore" [018-044].

## The Students' Theatre, Paris

Before the Prince Frederick, La Chicot danced at the Students' Theatre (Theatre des Etudiants) on the Left Bank of Paris, "amidst a labyrinth of narrow streets and tall houses between the School of Medicine and the Sorbonne" [004-006]. It was here that Jack Chicot, an Englishman "leading a rather wild life in this students' quarter of Paris, living from hand to mouth, very poor, very clever," first saw and fell in love with her [004-007]. When her popularity waned in Paris -- "the parterre grew thin, the students yawned or talked to each other in loud whispers while the dancer was executing her most brilliant steps" [004-015] -- Smolendo's offer of a London engagement rescued her from professional decline. The Students' Theatre is the origin point of the novel's central relationship, and its decline foreshadows the transience of all theatrical celebrity.

## Significance

The Prince Frederick represents the theatrical demi-monde that Braddon knew intimately from her own career as an actress before she became a novelist. It is a world of precarious glamour, of talents briefly celebrated and rapidly exhausted, of commercial calculation disguised as art. Smolendo's assessment of La Chicot -- "She won't last many seasons. She's like one of those high-stepping horses that knock themselves to pieces in a year or two" -- is chillingly accurate, and his prediction is fulfilled more literally than he imagined: she is dead within three seasons, murdered in her own lodgings [004-020].

The theatre also functions as a space of class transgression and moral ambiguity. In the stalls of the Prince Frederick, the aristocracy rubs shoulders with medical students and stockbrokers; on its stage, a Breton provincial girl becomes a London star earning a salary her birth would never justify; at its stage door, millionaires and shabby-genteel confidence men wait together. The theatre dissolves the social boundaries that structure the rest of the novel, creating a zone of fluid identity that is both exhilarating and dangerous -- a space where men fall in love with dancers, where millionaires send anonymous diamonds, and where the machinery that lifts a woman to the sky can just as easily let her fall.

In the novel's moral geography, the Prince Frederick stands as the antithesis of Hazlehurst Manor: the one a world of spectacle, drink, and sexual display; the other of inheritance, marriage, and rural respectability. John Treverton's journey from one to the other -- from the wings of the Prince Frederick to the master of Hazlehurst -- is the novel's central transformation, and the question of whether he can ever fully escape the theatre's taint drives the mystery plot from its first chapter to its last.
