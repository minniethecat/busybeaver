# Mrs. Evitt

Mrs. Evitt is the landlady of the [[cibber-street|Cibber Street lodging-house]], a woman of lower-middle-[[class-and-status|class]] [[class-and-status|respectability]] whose [[the-discovery|discovery]] of [[la-chicot|La Chicot]]'s stabbed body makes her a crucial witness in the [[murder-concept|murder]] investigation. Her mixture of shrewdness, timidity, and self-interest provides a vivid portrait of the Victorian [[london|London]] lodging-house keeper drawn into sensational events.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Evitt runs the [[cibber-street|Cibber Street]] house where [[jack-chicot|Jack Chicot]] and his wife [[la-chicot|La Chicot]] take lodgings. She sleeps on an ancient bed in the front kitchen [017-003]. Though she is "sly enough" according to one character's assessment [006-055], her world is fundamentally that of the respectable poor — concerned with propriety, rent, and her own standing. The [[the-discovery|discovery]] of the [[murder-concept|murder]] in her house shatters this world. Her shrieks of "[[murder-concept|Murder]] has been done in my house!" [017-008] [[the-arrest|capture]] her horror at finding her property and [[reputation-and-scandal|reputation]] implicated in violent crime.

## Biography

### Background

Mrs. Evitt is a widow or single woman maintaining herself through the letting of lodgings — a common occupation for women of her [[class-and-status|class]] in Victorian [[london|London]]. She had been in the house on the night of the [[murder-concept|murder]] but claimed she "dared not go anigh her" when she discovered something was wrong [017-028].

### Key Actions

- On the night of the [[murder-concept|murder]], she is called to the scene and finds [[la-chicot|La Chicot]] dead, triggering her hysterical reaction [017-003].
- She wraps herself in a waterproof and stands with another woman at the door, too frightened to enter the room where the body lies [017-024].
- In a "torrent of words" she tells the authorities everything she knows [017-033].
- She is left alone upstairs while the investigation proceeds, drawing hairs from the dead woman's head in a macabre act of curiosity or evidence-gathering [017-059].
- She later declares plaintively that no harm had ever come of the lodgings before [017-063].
- She asks the doctor nervously about laying out the body, showing her preoccupation with the practical and respectable handling of death [017-070].

### Fate / Resolution

Mrs. Evitt's role is primarily concentrated in the immediate aftermath of the [[murder-concept|murder]]. Her testimony helps establish the facts of the [[the-discovery|discovery]], but as a minor character she recedes from the narrative once the investigation moves beyond [[cibber-street|Cibber Street]]. Her alarmed countenance at the doctor's words [017-075] suggests she remains fearful of the consequences the crime may bring upon her establishment.

## Significance

Mrs. Evitt embodies the collision between sensational crime and ordinary lower-middle-[[class-and-status|class]] life that is a hallmark of sensation fiction. Her concern is not with [[justice]] but with the practical and [[reputation-and-scandal|reputation]]al damage the [[murder-concept|murder]] does to her and her property. Her drawing of hairs from the dead woman's head, whether from morbid curiosity or an instinct to gather evidence, adds a gothic touch to her otherwise mundane character. As a witness, her torrent of words provides the authorities with the initial account of the crime's [[the-discovery|discovery]].
