# Identity and Disguise

Identity and disguise form the central thematic engine of *The Cloven Foot*, operating at every level of the narrative: from the protagonist [[John-Treverton]]'s deliberate concealment of his past, to the enigmatic [[Desrolles]] whose very name may be a mask, to the legal and social identities contested through [[inheritance]] and the reading of [[Jasper-Treverton]]'s will.

## Definition

In the world of *The Cloven Foot*, identity is never a stable given — it is something that can be lost, stolen, hidden, or fabricated. The novel explores identity across three interlocking dimensions: **legal identity** (who one is in the eyes of the law, particularly regarding inheritance), **social identity** (who one is perceived to be by others), and **moral identity** (who one truly is beneath all masks). The "cloven foot" of the title itself functions as a marker of concealed truth — the devilish trace that betrays a hidden nature.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The identity theme permeates the novel through multiple interconnected strands:

- **John Treverton / Jack Chicot**: The central identity puzzle. John Treverton disappeared for seven years and returned as "Jack Chicot." The question of whether these two names refer to the same man drives much of the investigative plot. When confronted directly, Chicot issues a flat denial: "You deny the identity of John Treverton and Chicot" ([027-042]). Yet this denial is itself treated with suspicion — "His denial of the identity between the two men went for very little" ([028-005]).

- **Desrolles's Enigma**: The mysterious lodger Desrolles embodies the theme of concealed identity. He appears throughout the early chapters as a confidant to Jack Chicot and a fellow lodger, yet his background remains opaque. His languid, knowing manner suggests someone with secrets to keep. He tells a female lodger, "my soul, you ought not" to trust appearances ([014-008]).

- **The Dancer's Identity**: The question of "the identity of the dancer, Mademoiselle C——" ([036-029]) introduces yet another layer — the professional performer whose stage identity may differ from her legal one.

- **The Corpse Question**: The novel confronts identity in its most literal form with the discovery of a corpse that requires proof of "Jean Kergariou's identity with the sailor whose corpse" was found ([037-002]). Here identity becomes a matter of forensic evidence and legal determination.

- **Proving One's Self**: The pragmatic question "how did you prove your identity?" ([026-120]) recurs, highlighting that in this world, identity is not self-evident but must be demonstrated through documents, witnesses, and testimony.

## Key Passages

> "in which one may lose one's identity. Nobody knows one, one knows nobody" ([004-023])

This passage from early in the novel establishes the existential dimension of the identity theme — the terror of anonymity in a world where recognition is everything.

> "You deny the identity of John Treverton and Chicot" ([027-042])

The direct challenge, posed in legal or quasi-legal interrogation, crystallizes the central mystery. The verb "deny" implies an accusation — the identity is assumed, and the burden of disproof falls on the accused.

> "His denial of the identity between the two men went for very little" ([028-005])

The narrator's aside reveals the community's skepticism. Denial alone cannot dissolve the suspicion that Treverton and Chicot are one and the same.

> "had you no doubt as to his identity? You were fully assured that the man you saw was the man you had known?" ([026-163])

The relentless questioning about the reliability of eyewitness identification underscores how fragile the basis of identity recognition can be.

## Critical Perspective

The Victorian sensation novel, of which *The Cloven Foot* is a prime example, was deeply preoccupied with questions of identity. The period's rigid class structure, complex inheritance laws, and growing urban anonymity created a cultural anxiety about who people really were. Braddon's novel exploits this anxiety through the figure of the returnee (John Treverton), the impostor (potentially Desrolles), and the disguised performer (the dancer). The novel suggests that in a world of mobility and masquerade, the question "who are you?" may have no simple answer — and that the attempt to fix identity through law, evidence, and testimony is always unstable.

The "cloven foot" — the devil's distinguishing mark — serves as the novel's governing metaphor for identity. Just as the devil can disguise himself but cannot hide his cloven hoof, so the characters in this novel may conceal their true selves, but some tell-tale sign will eventually betray them. The question the novel poses is whether that betrayal is inevitable — whether identity, like the cloven foot, will always find a way to reveal itself.
