# The Prince of Wales's Feathers

A public-house at the corner of Cibber Street and Woodpecker Court in Leicester Square, London, known colloquially as "the Feathers." It serves as the venue for the coroner's inquest into the murder of La Chicot, transforming an ordinary working-class drinking establishment into the seat of a legal inquiry that will determine the course of the novel's central mystery.

## Description

The Prince of Wales's Feathers is a typical London public-house of the Victorian period, of the kind that served as informal community centres for their neighbourhoods. Located at the intersection of Cibber Street and Woodpecker Court, it stands in the heart of the "essentially dramatic, musical, and terpsichorean nook" of Leicester Square [008-002] -- a district crowded with actors, dancers, theatrical folk, and the tradespeople who serve them. The Feathers is a fixture of working-class and bohemian London, a place where the local community gathers and where, on the day after the murder, a very different kind of crowd assembles.

The pub's full name invokes the heraldic badge of the Prince of Wales -- three ostrich feathers -- a common pub name across England. Its shortened form, "the Feathers," suggests the easy familiarity with which the neighbourhood regards it.

## Role in the Novel

The Prince of Wales's Feathers appears at a single but structurally crucial moment in the narrative -- the coroner's inquest in Chapter 18. The pub serves as more than a backdrop; as the setting for the official inquiry, it becomes the stage on which the novel's central questions of guilt, identity, and justice are first publicly aired:

- **The inquest setting (Ch.18).** "The Prince of Wales's Feathers -- more popularly known as the Feathers -- a public-house at the corner of Cibber Street and Woodpecker Court, was the scene of the inquiry." The choice of a public-house rather than a formal courtroom as the venue for the inquest is significant: it places the legal proceeding in the same informal, working-class space where the witnesses and suspects live their daily lives, blurring the boundary between official justice and communal gossip. [018-024]

- **The witnesses.** The Feathers hosts the testimony of the doctor, the police-sergeant, the detective who examined the premises, Desrolles (the fellow lodger), Mrs. Evitt (the landlady), and Mrs. Rawber (the actress on the ground floor). Jack Chicot, "the most important witness of all," is absent -- his disappearance being "the most remarkable feature in the case" and the detail that "puzzled the Coroner." The absent husband haunts the proceedings at the Feathers as surely as his missing person haunts the police investigation. [018-024]

- **The crowd.** The Feathers is packed with spectators. George Gerard watches "from a crowded corner of the room" but holds his peace about the dagger. Joseph Lemuel, the wealthy stockbroker and La Chicot's anonymous admirer, is present in the crowd with a well-known criminal lawyer at his side -- his presence alarming Desrolles, whose "bister complexion grew a shade grayer at sight of these two faces." The pub is thus the site where the novel's disparate social worlds -- the demi-monde of theatre and lodging-houses, the wealth of the City, and the machinery of the law -- converge in a single room. [018-042][018-039]

- **The indefinite outcome.** The Coroner, finding insufficient evidence to reach a verdict, "adjourned the inquiry for a week, in the hope that further evidence would be forthcoming." The Feathers inquest thus produces not resolution but suspension: the case remains open, the suspicion attaches to the missing husband, and the press fills the void with lurid speculation. The adjournment at the Feathers is the moment at which the novel's legal plot stalls, ceding the field to the press and to private investigation. [018-041]

- **The atmosphere.** Braddon conveys the crowding and noise of the Feathers during the inquest through small details: the Coroner's impatience with Mrs. Evitt's meandering testimony, the press of bodies in the room, the watchful presence of interested parties in the crowd. The pub is not a neutral space: it is a site of performance, of competing narratives, of information offered and withheld.

## Connection to the Broader Plot

The Feathers inquest is the hinge between the novel's first and second halves. Before Chapter 18, the narrative is primarily a domestic drama of inheritance, courtship, and Bohemian marriage. The inquest transforms the murder from a private tragedy into a public event, converting Jack Chicot's disappearance into a de facto admission of guilt in the eyes of the law and the public. Everything that follows -- Edward Clare's investigation, Gerard's recruitment, Treverton's arrest, the Bow Street proceedings -- flows from the failure of the Feathers inquest to resolve the case.

The pub also embodies the class dynamics of the Victorian legal system. Coroners' inquests were routinely held in public-houses in the nineteenth century, particularly in working-class districts where no dedicated court building existed. The choice of the Feathers rather than a formal courtroom places the inquiry squarely in the world of the suspects and witnesses -- a world of actors and landladies and fellow lodgers, not of judges and barristers. The legal process is embedded in the community it investigates, its authority diluted by the beer stains on the tables and the crowd pressing at the door.

## Significance

The Prince of Wales's Feathers represents the democratisation -- and the limitations -- of Victorian justice. The inquest is public, the witnesses are ordinary Londoners, and the room is open to anyone who can push their way in. But this accessibility produces no truth: the Coroner adjourns without a verdict, the crucial evidence remains undiscovered, and the wrong man is suspected. The Feathers inquest demonstrates that bringing people together in a room to testify does not guarantee that the truth will emerge -- particularly when the most important witness has vanished and a second witness (Gerard) chooses silence.

In the novel's symbolic geography, the Feathers stands at the intersection of two worlds: the domestic space of Cibber Street (where the murder occurred) and the public space of the press and the law (which will consume and distort the case). It is the threshold across which private tragedy passes into public sensation.
