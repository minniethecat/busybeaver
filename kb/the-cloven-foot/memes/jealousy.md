# Jealousy

A pervasive and destabilising emotional force throughout *The Cloven Foot*, operating across romantic, professional, and public registers to drive plot, define character motivation, and expose the fragility of Victorian social surfaces.

## Definition

In *The Cloven Foot*, jealousy functions as more than a private passion; it is a narrative engine. Braddon deploys jealousy in three distinct but interlocking modes: the romantic jealousy of a rejected suitor (Edward Clare's bitterness toward John Treverton over Laura Malcolm), the professional jealousy and simmering rivalries within La Chicot's bohemian circle (Desrolles's ambiguous ambitions, La Chicot's own competitive vanity), and the public's prurient, voyeuristic consumption of the Chicot murder as spectacle. Together, these strands reveal jealousy as the dark underside of Victorian sentimentality: a passion that masquerades as love, duty, or moral outrage, but in each case corrodes the bonds it claims to honour.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **Edward Clare's Rejected-Suitor Jealousy (Ch.1, Ch.23, Ch.28, Ch.37)** : Edward Clare — the vicar's sleek, white-handed son — had himself hoped to marry Laura Malcolm and claim Hazlehurst Manor. When Jasper Treverton's will makes Laura's inheritance contingent upon marriage to the unknown heir John Treverton, Edward's thwarted ambition curdles into sustained jealousy. He frames his malice as chivalrous concern, but the Vicar perceives its true nature: "He ascribed the young man's malice to the jealousy of a rejected suitor; and he knew that from jealousy to hatred was but a step" (037-024).

- **John Treverton's Initial Suspicion (Ch.1)** : Upon arriving at Hazlehurst Manor, John Treverton instinctively interprets Laura Malcolm's earnest welcome as hypocrisy — "What interest could she have in his arrival? What feeling could there be between them but jealousy?" (001-036). This early, preemptive jealousy colours his perception of the woman he will later marry.

- **The Public's Prurient Jealousy — The Chicot Murder as Spectacle (Ch.18)** : The leader-writers of the popular press reconstruct the murder scene as lurid entertainment, imagining the "wife's recriminations — her natural outbreak of jealousy — hot words on both sides" (018-052). This mode of jealousy is vicarious: a public that envies and consumes the private passions of others, transforming a domestic tragedy into mass sensation.

- **The Jealous-Husband Theory of the Crime (Ch.18)** : George Gerard arrives at the Cibber Street murder scene expecting to find "the husband stretched lifeless beside the wife he had sacrificed to his jealous fury" (018-003). The jealous-husband narrative — plausible, familiar, satisfying — is precisely the story the public and press most readily believe, whether or not it corresponds to the truth.

- **Edward Clare's Self-Deceiving Jealousy (Ch.28)** : In the novel's most psychologically acute exploration of jealousy, Edward Clare rationalises his hatred of John Treverton: "It was no base envy of another's prosperity; it was not even jealous anger against a rival, Edward told himself. No, it was a chivalrous ardour in the defence of the woman he had loved" (028-003). The narrator's irony exposes the self-deception at jealousy's core.

- **La Chicot's Possessive Jealousy (Ch.28)** : In a crucial speculative passage, George Gerard and Edward Clare debate the killer's motives. Gerard proposes that "he may have believed it possible to so arrange his life that no one would ever recognise Jack Chicot in the country Squire. He may have thought that he could buy his freedom from Madame Chicot. Perhaps it was only when he found that her love, or her jealousy, was not to be hoodwinked that he conceived the idea of murder" (028-111). Here, a woman's jealous refusal to be discarded becomes, in the speculative logic of the men discussing the case, the catalyst for homicide.

## Critical Perspective

Braddon's treatment of jealousy in *The Cloven Foot* reflects the sensation novel's characteristic interest in the gap between public profession and private passion. Jealousy in the novel is almost never called by its true name — it is dressed as chivalry, as moral outrage, as journalistic duty, as justice. Edward Clare's self-deception in Chapter 28 is the exemplary case: Braddon's narrator exposes the irony of a man who "told himself" his motives were noble even as the reader recognises the jealousy he refuses to name. This pattern aligns with the sensation novel's broader project of exposing the hypocrisies of Victorian respectability — the discovery that beneath the smooth surfaces of the vicarage, the country house, and the newspaper office, there pulse the same violent passions the culture officially disavows.

The novel also participates in a distinctively mid-Victorian discourse about jealousy and crime. The "jealous husband" theory of the Chicot murder — the version George Gerard expects and the press sensationalises — draws on contemporary anxieties about domestic violence and masculine passion, anxieties given legal form in the Matrimonial Causes Act of 1857 and cultural expression in the sensation fiction of the 1860s. Braddon, who had herself been the subject of scandal as Mary Elizabeth Maxwell's common-law wife, was acutely attuned to the ways in which "jealousy" could be wielded as a narrative weapon — a story told about others that was always also a story told to police desire, especially women's desire.

La Chicot's jealousy, as speculatively reconstructed by George Gerard (028-111), is perhaps the novel's most subversive representation of the emotion: a woman's jealous refusal to be discarded that becomes, in the masculine imagination, sufficient provocation for her own murder. The novel does not endorse this logic, but it registers its cultural availability with chilling clarity.
