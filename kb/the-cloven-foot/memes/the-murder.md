# The Murder (The Cloven Foot)

The murder of La Chicot — a stage dancer living at the Cibber Street lodging-house — is the central crime of *The Cloven Foot*, the event whose investigation, concealment, and eventual resolution drives the novel's entire plot.

## Summary

The murder of La Chicot occurred in the early hours of a winter morning at the Cibber Street lodging-house near Leicester Square. She was found with her throat cut, and her husband Jack Chicot (the alias of John Treverton) became the prime suspect after he disappeared while supposedly fetching a policeman. The murder was discovered at three o'clock in the morning, but the surgeon determined death had occurred around one o'clock — two hours before the alarm was raised. [[PN017-001]][[PN017-062]]

The murder transformed John Treverton's life. Already living a double existence as the respectable heir of Treverton Manor and the husband of a dancer in a London lodging-house, the suspicion of murder drove him to abandon his London identity entirely and flee to Hazlehurst, where he married Laura Malcolm under the terms of Jasper Treverton's will. [[PN031-020]][[PN031-028]]

## Its Role in the Plot

The murder functions as the novel's absent centre — an event that occurred before the main action of the narrative begins (John Treverton's arrival at Hazlehurst) but whose reverberations shape every subsequent development.

### Before the Murder

La Chicot and Jack Chicot lived at the Cibber Street lodging-house alongside Desrolles and Mrs. Rawber. Their marriage was marked by quarrels and unhappiness. Mrs. Evitt, the landlady, later testified that she had "seen it in his looks" that Chicot hated his wife. [[PN017-008]][[PN017-016]]

### After the Murder

Jack Chicot vanished. The newspapers branded him a murderer. Months later, a man calling himself John Treverton arrived at Hazlehurst to claim his inheritance — a man whose secret history would eventually catch up with him. [[PN031-020]]

### The Resolution

The discovery that the iron-grey hair in La Chicot's dead hand did not match John Treverton's hair colour was one of the factors leading to his ultimate exoneration. The true identity of the murderer remains one of the novel's central mysteries.

## Significance

The murder is the novel's organising secret — the truth that everyone either seeks to uncover or struggles to conceal. It links the two worlds of the novel (fashionable Hazlehurst and seedy Cibber Street) through the figure of John Treverton/Jack Chicot, a man who belongs to both and is at home in neither. The tuft of grey hair — hidden by Mrs. Evitt, sought by investigators — becomes the novel's central symbol of truth suppressed and justice deferred.
