# Camelot Church

Camelot Church is a remote parish church on the edge of Dartmoor in Devonshire, where [[John-Treverton]] and [[Laura-Malcolm]] are secretly married in Chapter 20. Isolated, damp, and sepulchral, the church is the setting for what the characters and narrator repeatedly call "an ill-omened wedding" — a marriage undertaken in haste and secrecy that seems doomed from its beginning.

## Location

Camelot is a remote parish accessible by train from Beechampton, "across a stretch of heath and moor" (020-043). John Treverton selects it precisely because of its obscurity: "I never heard of the place in my life till I looked at the Ordnance map this morning... Here is Camelot, for instance. I never heard of Camelot before" (019-066). It is "some remote parish" (019-066) so isolated that John can be confident of escaping the notice of Hazlehurst gossip. Laura has never heard of it, and Celia has "nothing to say against Camelot" (019-067), confirming its utter anonymity. The journey from Beechampton takes the couple across moorland, arriving at Camelot's "narrow street on the slant of a hill" at about four o'clock in deepening gloom (020-043, 020-045).

## Description

Camelot itself is a "narrow street on the slant of a hill" (020-045), and "there was no church that it can call its own" — the church is an outlying structure separate from the little settlement (019-066). The church is old and grim:

> "The old church smelt rather like a vault when they went in out of the fresh afternoon air" (020-049)

The interior has "queer corners and dark oaken pews" (020-049) — a description echoed in the Hazlehurst church description elsewhere. The building is ancient, damp, and sepulchral: the vault-like smell and the darkness within contrast sharply with the fresh moorland air outside, reinforcing the funereal atmosphere of the ceremony. The wedding party is small and furtive: John, Laura, the clergyman, and the witnesses they have brought with them. After the ceremony, the couple and their luggage are to go back across the moor, returning to the world they came from (020-049).

John himself later reflects with bitter irony on his choice:

> "There was less difficulty in fixing upon our wedding place than I was in fixing upon Camelot," said John, as they walked slowly back to the little street (020-048)

The choice of Camelot — a place plucked from the Ordnance map for its anonymity — underscores the haste and secrecy that surround the marriage.

## Significance in the Novel

### Plot Function

Camelot Church is the setting for the wedding of John Treverton and Laura Malcolm at the end of Chapter 20, "The Church Near Camelot." The marriage is the fulfilment of Jasper Treverton's will condition — John must marry Laura within one year of the testator's death to inherit the estate (002-013). But the secrecy of the ceremony (a remote parish chosen from a map, with no announcement and no celebration beyond the immediate party) already signals that something is profoundly wrong.

The wedding is repeatedly described as "ill-omened" (011-022, 012-005, 012-063, 015-002, 023-025). Celia Clare says: "I know I am superstitious for saying so, but I do call this an ill-omened wedding" (012-005). John Treverton later reflects with a "bitter smile: 'It was an ill-omened marriage'" (012-063). The phrase becomes a refrain, and Camelot Church is its physical embodiment — a church that "smelt rather like a vault" (020-049), presaging the death-haunted secret that will soon engulf the marriage.

The banns were put up at one of the Camelot churches, and John arranged to meet Laura at the remote church where the ceremony would take place (019-067). The wedding party "went on foot to the outlying church where John Treverton was to take his second bride" (020-047), the phrase "second bride" itself a loaded reference to La Chicot, whom none of the Devonshire party knows exists.

### Thematic Role

Camelot Church embodies the theme of concealment that runs through the novel. Its very selection — a place unknown even to lifelong Devonshire residents, found on a map, chosen for its obscurity — mirrors John Treverton's concealment of his past. The name "Camelot," with its Arthurian connotations of idealized chivalry, is grimly ironic: this is no court of noble knights but a damp, dark church where a wedding founded on a hidden crime takes place.

The vault-like smell of the church interior is the novel's most concentrated Gothic image of a marriage-as-death. The building is not merely old but sepulchral; it anticipates both the literal vaults in Hazlehurst churchyard where the Trevertons are buried (002-004, 019-045) and the figurative grave of trust that John Treverton digs for his marriage. That the wedding occurs in Chapter 20 — the midpoint of a 45-chapter novel — structurally marks it as the pivot from hope to ruin.
