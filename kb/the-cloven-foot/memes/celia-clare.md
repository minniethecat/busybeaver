# Celia Clare

Celia Clare is the lively, talkative daughter of the Vicar of Hazlehurst, the devoted friend and confidante of [[Laura-Malcolm]], and the sister of [[Edward-Clare]]. She serves as both comic relief and emotional support throughout the novel, her cheerful frivolity providing contrast to the darker currents of the mystery plot. Her own romantic storyline — a triangle involving the worthy but impoverished surgeon [[George-Gerard]], the self-interested solicitor [[Thomas-Sampson]], and her own [[greed|mercenary]] instincts — culminates in a genuine love-match that mirrors the novel's larger movement from deception to sincerity.

## Role in the Novel

Celia first appears in Chapter 5, where she is established as [[laura-malcolm|Laura Malcolm]]'s chosen companion during the year of waiting for [[john-treverton|John Treverton]]'s decision: "I want no companion, except Celia now and then" (005-017). She is the daughter of [[mr-clare|Mr. Clare]], the Vicar of Hazlehurst, and the sister of [[edward-clare|Edward]] Clare, the idle Oxonian poet who is himself in love with Laura. Celia functions as Laura's closest friend and emotional outlet — it is to Celia that Laura confides her anxieties about [[john-treverton|John Treverton]], and it is Celia who provides the practical, worldly counterpoint to Laura's romantic idealism.

Throughout the novel, Celia's irrepressible gaiety and flippancy serve a structural purpose, providing moments of levity that relieve the accumulating tension of the central mystery. Braddon characterizes her as "a joyous, improvident, idle creature, with the unreasoning love of life for its own sake which makes a Neapolitan beggar happy in the sunshine" (009-020). She is described as having a "canary head" of fair hair (009-021), a pretty, pouting face, and a "philosophical air" that she affects in conversation (005-044). Her chatter about bonnets, cuffs, collars, and the horrors of rural dullness provides much of the novel's comic texture.

Her most significant contribution to the narrative architecture comes through her own romantic subplot, which develops in the novel's final third and provides a parallel resolution to the central love story of [[john-treverton|John Treverton]] and [[laura-malcolm|Laura Malcolm]].

## Biography

### Background

Celia Clare is the daughter of the Vicar of Hazlehurst and his good-natured wife. She has one brother, [[edward-clare|Edward]] Clare, an Oxford dropout who fancies himself a poet. The Clares are genteel but not wealthy — Celia has "been subject to occasional attacks [of poverty] towards the end of the quarter ever since I've been an independent being with a fixed allowance" (030-073). She has lived all her life in Hazlehurst, within earshot of the church clock, and chafes against the narrowness of village existence. Her one great [[the-arrest|escape]] has been occasional visits to a well-to-do aunt at Brighton, which she considers "the highest form of terrestrial bliss that had ever come within Celia's experience" (009-003).

She is [[laura-malcolm|Laura Malcolm]]'s closest friend, the two having grown up together in the small community. Celia affects an elder-sister air toward Laura "that was pleasant in its absurdity" (009-013), despite being the more frivolous of the two. She is genuinely loyal, hurrying back from Brighton when Laura's engagement to [[john-treverton|John Treverton]] is announced, and standing by her friend through the crisis of Treverton's [[the-arrest|arrest]].

### Key Actions

**Laura's Companion.** During the year of waiting after [[jasper-treverton|Jasper Treverton]]'s death, Celia is Laura's principal companion at the [[hazlehurst-manor|Manor House]]. She acts as intermediary between Laura and her brother [[edward-clare|Edward]], conveying Laura's wish that [[edward-clare|Edward]] restrict his visits now that she is effectively pledged to [[john-treverton|John Treverton]] (005-030 to 005-032). Celia also serves as a sounding-board for both Laura's anxieties (005-036) and [[edward-clare|Edward]]'s frustrated ambitions (005-039 to 005-044).

**The Confidante.** When Laura becomes engaged to [[john-treverton|John Treverton]], Celia rushes back from Brighton to assist with the wedding preparations, [[reputation-and-scandal|scandal]]ized by Laura's indifference to her trousseau: "No wedding gown! No trousseau, and no wedding gown! This is indeed an ill-omened marriage!" (011-022). She serves as Laura's sole bridesmaid at the quiet wedding (011-048) and remains at the [[hazlehurst-manor|Manor House]] during the early days of the Trevertons' marriage, observing [[john-treverton|John Treverton]]'s curiously melancholy manner toward his bride (011-042 to 011-045).

**The Children's Party.** At the Christmas Eve party for [[the-village|the village]] children (Chapter 25), Celia works energetically, "flew hither and thither with plates of cake, spread innumerable slices of bread and jam, tied the strings of a score of pinafores" (025-005). She seats herself at the front during [[edward-clare|Edward]]'s reading of "The Jackdaw of Rheims" and "started all the laughter and applause" (025-034).

**Meeting [[george-gerard|George Gerard]].** In Chapter 29, [[edward-clare|Edward]] brings his [[london|London]] acquaintance, the young surgeon [[george-gerard|George Gerard]], to stay at the Vicarage. Celia is immediately intrigued by this "stern-looking creature" whose smile transforms his severe face (029-028 to 029-029). During Gerard's visit, Celia gives herself up to "the task of making that one day agreeable to him, with the utmost benevolence and amiability" (034-026). Their moorland ramble together — during which [[edward-clare|Edward]] absents himself — becomes the foundation of her romantic feelings for him (034-068 to 034-092).

**Refusing Tom Sampson.** In Chapter 38, the solicitor [[thomas-sampson|Thomas Sampson]], walking back to the [[hazlehurst-manor|Manor House]] with Celia, proposes marriage and is firmly refused: "I never, never, never could think of you in any other character than that of a trustworthy friend" (038-126). She later confesses to Laura that she is "desperately in love with a pauper" — [[george-gerard|George Gerard]] — despite her lifelong conviction that nothing could induce her to marry a poor man (038-039).

**The Crisis.** When [[john-treverton|John Treverton]] is [[the-arrest|arrest]]ed on suspicion of [[murder-concept|murder]] (Chapter 39), Celia is bewildered and distressed by the sudden departure of her friends for [[london|London]] (039-018 to 039-025). She remains in Hazlehurst, loyal to Laura throughout the crisis.

### Fate / Resolution

In the novel's final chapter (Chapter 46), Celia becomes engaged to [[george-gerard|George Gerard]] during a summer visit to [[hazlehurst-manor|Hazlehurst Manor]]. Standing before a Druidic altar on the moor, Gerard asks her to wait for him, and the transformed Celia answers meekly, "Yes, George" (046-015). [[john-treverton|John Treverton]], grateful for Gerard's role in his exoneration, purchases the retiring village doctor's practice and gives it to Gerard as a free gift (046-026), enabling the couple to settle near the Trevertons at Hazlehurst. Celia's vision of the future includes "Harley Street, a landau, and a pair of handsome grays" (046-019) — her characteristic blend of romance and worldliness intact to the end.

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

Celia is driven by a tension between her romantic nature and her worldly realism. She openly declares that she could never marry a poor man — she is "as particular as I am about collars and cuffs" (038-043) and dreams of pony carriages and the best dressmakers in Exeter. Yet she falls in love with [[george-gerard|George Gerard]] precisely because his hard-working intelligence and masculine determination appeal to something deeper than her taste for comfort. She is a study in the movement from girlish frivolity to genuine emotional commitment, without ever losing her essential character.

### Role as Comic Relief

Celia is Braddon's chief instrument for tonal variation in a novel otherwise dominated by [[murder-concept|murder]], deception, and existential dread. Her chatter about bonnets and gloves, her complaints about rural dullness, her comic indignation at Laura not having a proper wedding gown — all serve to leaven the narrative. She is the character who can say "Brighton was quite too delightful" (011-005) in the same breath that the novel is building toward a [[murder-concept|murder]] accusation.

## Portrayal in the Text

> "Celia was the most frivolous of beings, affected a talent for business, and assumed an elder-sister air towards Laura Malcolm that was pleasant in its absurdity." (009-013)

> "Celia would have chatted as merrily over a cup of tea and a herring in a garret at five shillings a week, as amidst the fleshpots of Hazlehurst Manor. She was a joyous, improvident, idle creature, with the unreasoning love of life for its own sake." (009-020)

> "Now Celia Clare was not fond of poverty. She considered it a necessary evil, but liked to give it as wide a berth as possible." (029-043)

> "Darling, will you wait for me?" he asked, looking down at her, with eyes brimming over with love. / "Yes, George," she answered, meekly, quite a transformed Celia, all her pertness and flippancy gone. (046-014 to 046-015)

## Significance

Celia Clare serves several connected functions in the novel. As Laura's confidante, she provides the reader with an external perspective on the central relationship between Laura and [[john-treverton|John Treverton]], voicing the doubts and questions that the more trusting Laura suppresses. As comic relief, she prevents the novel from becoming oppressively dark. As the heroine of the secondary love plot, she demonstrates that genuine feeling can overcome worldly calculation — her engagement to the impoverished [[george-gerard|George Gerard]] mirrors Laura's own leap of faith in marrying [[john-treverton|John Treverton]], and both women are rewarded with happiness.

Structurally, Celia's courtship by Tom Sampson (rejected), her brother [[edward-clare|Edward]] (a cautionary figure of idle selfishness), and [[george-gerard|George Gerard]] (chosen) forms a miniature romantic economy that echoes the novel's larger themes: the insufficiency of mere financial security, the danger of [[greed|mercenary]] motives, and the ultimate triumph of sincere affection.
