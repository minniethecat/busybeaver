# Fate and Destiny

Fate pervades *The Cloven Foot* as both a plot mechanism and a thematic preoccupation. Characters repeatedly invoke fate to explain their circumstances, justify their decisions, and resign themselves to outcomes they cannot control. From the opening chapters through the final revelations, the language of fate — "it must be as fate wills" ([008-093]), "I must submit to fate" ([005-033]) — provides the novel's governing emotional register of tragic inevitability.

## Definition

In *The Cloven Foot*, fate operates on three levels. **Narrative fate** governs the plot: the terms of [[Jasper-Treverton]]'s will, the discovery of hidden identities, and the convergence of seemingly unrelated events all suggest a universe where outcomes are predetermined. **Character fate** shapes individual trajectories: [[Laura-Malcolm]]'s adoption, [[John-Treverton]]'s seven-year exile, and [[La-Chicot]]'s violent death are each framed as fated events. **Symbolic fate** is embodied in the novel's title — the "cloven foot" as the mark of the devil, the tell-tale sign that hidden truths will inevitably surface.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **La Chicot's Tragic Fate**: The most concentrated cluster of fate language surrounds La Chicot. She "met with her fate" at Cibber Street ([004-007]), and "her unhappy fate cast a gloom over the house" ([018-044]). Her story serves as the novel's cautionary tale — a woman whose destiny was sealed by circumstances beyond her control.

- **The Will as Fate's Instrument**: [[Jasper-Treverton]]'s will functions as an engine of fate, dictating the terms under which characters may inherit, marry, and live. "My fate depends on you," a character tells another, referring to the disposition of the will ([009-051]). The legal document becomes fate made concrete — an inescapable condition imposed from beyond the grave.

- **Romantic Fate**: The love story between [[Laura-Malcolm]] and [[John-Treverton]] is saturated with the language of fate. They sit "face to face with fate" ([010-002]), and each declares that "no stroke of time or fate can change" their love ([011-063], [011-064]). Fate here is both a threat and a promise — something to be defied through the strength of love.

- **Resignation and Anger**: Characters respond to fate in two modes. Some submit: "I suppose I must submit to fate" ([005-033]), "It must be as fate wills" ([008-093]). Others rage: "He was angry with fate and Jasper Treverton for the will which had made Laura's wealth a barrier between them" ([028-003]).

- **The Turning Point of Destiny**: In a pivotal scene, a character stands at "the very turning point of her destiny. All must depend upon what Jo——" ([012-050]), the sentence breaking off at the crucial moment, suggesting that destiny hangs on a single decision.

## Key Passages

> "it must be as fate wills" ([008-093])

This simple declarative statement, uttered in the context of La Chicot's fate being discussed in Cibber Street, captures the novel's fatalistic worldview: there is a higher will at work, and human agency is limited.

> "I must submit to fate. Miss Malcolm no doubt considers——" ([005-033])

The interrupted sentence suggests that fate and social judgment are intertwined — to submit to fate is also to submit to how others perceive one's situation.

> "He was angry with fate and Jasper Treverton for the will which had made Laura's wealth a barrier between them" ([028-003])

The conflation of "fate" with "Jasper Treverton" is telling: the dead man's legal instrument has become indistinguishable from cosmic destiny. Fate in this novel is not abstract — it is enacted through specific human decisions, documents, and institutions.

> "the very turning point of her destiny. All must depend upon what Jo——" ([012-050])

The broken sentence dramatizes the moment of choice. Destiny is not simply imposed; it crystallizes at moments of decision.

## Critical Perspective

The Victorian sensation novel's preoccupation with fate reflects broader nineteenth-century tensions between religious providentialism and secular determinism. Braddon's handling of fate in *The Cloven Foot* is notably secular: fate operates through legal documents (the will), social structures (class and inheritance), and psychological compulsions (love, guilt, fear) rather than through divine intervention. The "cloven foot" — though not literally mentioned in the corpus — functions as the novel's master trope for fate: the hidden mark that will eventually betray itself, the devil in the details that cannot be permanently concealed.

The frequency of the word "fate" (20 occurrences across the novel's chapters) is striking, as is its distribution: it clusters at moments of decision (the will, the wedding, the proposal) and moments of retrospection (characters looking back on La Chicot's death). Fate is thus both prospective and retrospective — a way of facing the future and a way of making sense of the past.
