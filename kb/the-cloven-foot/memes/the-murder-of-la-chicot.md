# The Murder of La Chicot

The murder of Zaïre Chicot — star dancer of the Royal Prince Frederick Theatre and wife of Jack Chicot (the alias of [[john-treverton|John Treverton]]) — is the central crime of *The Cloven Foot*. Stabbed to death in her bed at a Leicester Square lodging-house in the dead of winter night, her killing transforms the novel from a story of inheritance and concealed identity into a sensation mystery, with her missing husband as the prime suspect.

## Summary

At approximately one o'clock on a winter morning, La Chicot was stabbed to death in her bed at No. 14 Cibber Street, where she lodged with her husband Jack Chicot. The wound was "a deep stab; a violent thrust with some sharp, thin, and narrow instrument, which had pierced the hollow of her neck, and penetrated in a slanting direction to the lungs" (018-005). At three o'clock, Jack Chicot returned home and raised the alarm with "a shout — repeated with passionate clamour — scared the inhabitants of the Cibber Street lodging-house" (017-003). He then left the house to fetch a policeman and never returned, his disappearance immediately marking him as the prime suspect (017-013 to 017-014). The murder weapon — a thin, flexible Italian dagger — was later discovered in Chicot's paint-box by the surgeon [[George Gerard]] (018-016 to 018-017). The true murderer was eventually revealed to be [[desrolles|Desrolles]], the second-floor lodger and La Chicot's secret lover.

## Context

### Preceding Events

La Chicot had been the star attraction of [[Smolendo]]'s Royal Prince Frederick Theatre, her name displayed "in gigantic yellow capitals on a black ground" across London (008-014). Her marriage to Jack Chicot had soured into mutual misery: she drank heavily, they quarrelled violently, and Jack had grown weary of her. She had recently received a diamond necklace from an anonymous admirer — later revealed to be the financier Joseph Lemuel — and had refused his offers of a carriage, house, and settlement, insisting "I am an honest woman. And then, I am too proud" (014-027). On the night of the murder, La Chicot had been alone in the lodging-house at Cibber Street; her husband Jack was out late, and the other lodgers — [[desrolles|Desrolles]], the tragedienne [[Mrs. Rawber]], and the landlady [[Mrs. Evitt]] — were all in their respective rooms.

## Detailed Account

The murder was discovered at three o'clock in the morning when Jack Chicot returned home. The shout of "Murder!" — "an awful word under the most ordinary circumstances of everyday life — an awful word even when spoken of an event that happened long ago, or afar off" (017-002) — roused the house. Mrs. Rawber heard it in her back bedroom; it "penetrated confusedly" to Mrs. Evitt in the front kitchen; and Desrolles, who "seemed to have slept more heavily than the other two," came rushing out last (017-003).

The scene was horrific. Chicot pointed "with a trembling hand to a thin thread of crimson that had crept along the dull grey carpet to the very threshold" (017-007). Inside the bedroom, as he held the candle toward the bed "with white averted face," the others saw "hideous stains on the counterpane, an awful figure lying in a heap among the bedclothes, a long loose coil of raven hair, curved like a snake round the rigid form" (017-007). It was, the narrator observes, "a spectacle which not one of those who gazed upon it, spellbound, fascinated by the horror of the sight, could ever hope to forget" (017-007).

The landlady Mrs. Evitt immediately accused the husband: "Seize him, 'old 'im tight. It must 'ave been her 'usband done it. You was often a-quarrelling, you know you was" (017-008). Jack Chicot protested his innocence — "I never raised my hand against her in my life, though she has tempted me many a time" (017-010) — and then left the house to fetch a policeman. He never returned.

> The policeman went in, lantern in hand, a monument of stolid calm, amidst the terror of the scene. Little need to ask if she were dead. That awful face upon the pillow, those glazed eyes with their wide stare of horror, that gaping wound in the full white throat, from which the lifeblood had poured in a crimson stream across the white counterpane, until it made a dark pool beside the bed, all told their own tale. (017-029)

The surgeon determined she had been dead approximately three hours, placing the time of death at about one o'clock (017-062). An examination found evidence of a struggle: "a purple bruise showed where a savage hand had gripped that lovely arm; on the right shoulder... appeared the marks of strong fingers that had fastened their clutch there" (018-007). Crucially, Mrs. Evitt discovered that La Chicot's hand was tightly clenched; forcing back the stiffening fingers, she found "a little tuft of iron-grey hair, which looked as if it had been torn from a man's head" (017-058). She carefully preserved this evidence, folding it into an old letter.

The police searched every room in the house, but the most damning piece of physical evidence was found the next day by George Gerard. In the old window-seat of Jack Chicot's painting den, inside a wooden paint-box, "hidden under rags and rubbish, there lay a long thin dagger, of Italian workmanship, the handle of finely wrought silver, oxidised with age" (018-016). It was "a deadly weapon in a strong hand, a weapon to inflict just such a wound as that deep stab which had slain La Chicot" (018-017).

## Consequences

The murder's consequences radiate outward through the remainder of the novel. Jack Chicot's disappearance brands him as the prime suspect, with the press painting "the husband... in the darkest dyes of iniquity" (018-052). The public imagination is captured: "the public talk for the next three weeks was of Jack Chicot's crime, and the miserable stupidity of the police in not being able to find him" (018-052). The discovery of the dagger and George Gerard's letter to *The Times* (018-051) tighten the circumstantial case.

For John Treverton, the murder threatens to destroy everything — his claim to the Hazlehurst inheritance, his marriage to Laura Malcolm, and his very life. His concealed identity as Jack Chicot becomes the "dark gap" that must be explained, and the murder investigation that begins with the inquest at [[the-inquest|the Prince of Wales's Feathers]] will eventually lead to the exposure of the true killer and Treverton's exoneration.

The ultimate revelation — that Desrolles, the cool-headed lodger who stood "beside the grave, decently attired in a suit of black" (018-043) at La Chicot's funeral, was the murderer — reconfigures the reader's understanding of every scene in which Desrolles appeared. The iron-grey hair in La Chicot's dead hand matched Desrolles's hair: "just the colour of Desrolles' hair. I could swear to it" (041-109), and a bloodstained chintz dressing-gown belonging to Desrolles was later discovered (041-123).

## Significance

### Plot Function

The murder is the fulcrum on which the novel turns, transforming it from a domestic drama of inheritance and courtship into a sensation mystery. It introduces the central question that drives the second half of the narrative: who killed La Chicot? The answer — that the murderer was hiding among the witnesses and mourners all along — is characteristic of Braddon's technique of delayed disclosure.

### Thematic Resonance

The murder crystallizes several of the novel's key themes. The weapon — "a long thin dagger, of Italian workmanship" — is first introduced as an artist's prop in a melodramatic painting, blurring the line between art and violence. La Chicot's body, displayed in the newspapers as "that glorious creature" (018-052), becomes a public spectacle, exemplifying the sensation genre's treatment of female beauty and female death as commodities for consumption. Most significantly, the murder shows how the respectable world (Hazlehurst Manor) and the demi-monde (Cibber Street) are not separate spheres but fatally interconnected through John Treverton's dual identity.
