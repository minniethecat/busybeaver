# Theodore Clare

Theodore Clare is the vicar of the parish that includes the Treverton estate at [[hazlehurst-manor|Hazlehurst Manor]]. Though referenced throughout the novel as "the vicar" or "[[mr-clare|Mr. Clare]]," his Christian name appears only once — in [[jasper-treverton|Jasper Treverton]]'s will, where he receives "a bequest of old plate" for his service as friend and spiritual counselor [002-006]. Along with [[thomas-sampson|Thomas Sampson]], he is named co-trustee of [[inheritance|the will]], placing him at the centre of the legal and moral machinery that governs the novel's central [[inheritance]] plot.

## Role in the Novel

Theodore Clare — invariably referred to as [[mr-clare|Mr. Clare]] — appears primarily in the opening chapters, where his clerical authority and friendship with [[jasper-treverton|Jasper Treverton]] give him standing in the affairs of the [[hazlehurst-manor|Manor House]]. He arranges the funeral, witnesses [[inheritance|the will]], and becomes one of the two trustees charged with administering an [[inheritance]] whose conditions create immediate moral tension. His presence recurs at key domestic moments: the dinner before [[john-treverton|John Treverton]]'s departure for [[london|London]], the wedding preparations, and Laura Treverton's crisis after her husband's disappearance.

## Biography

### Background

[[mr-clare|Mr. Clare]] is the vicar of the parish and an old friend of [[jasper-treverton|Jasper Treverton]], who attended the dying man in his final hours and read the prayers for the sick [001-102]. He is the father of [[edward-clare|Edward]] Clare, the Oxonian who harbours hopes of marrying [[laura-malcolm|Laura Malcolm]], and of [[celia-clare|Celia Clare]], Laura's chosen friend. His wife is mentioned as being present at the wedding to "represent the bride's mother" [011-048]. He is fond of a particular claret, though he admits he had "never been quite so good a judge as he had believed himself to be" [002-036].

### Key Actions

- Attends [[jasper-treverton|Jasper Treverton]] on his deathbed, reading the prayers for the sick [001-102].
- Makes the arrangements for the funeral [002-003].
- Is present at the reading of [[inheritance|the will]] alongside [[laura-malcolm|Laura Malcolm]] and the upper [[the-servants|servants]] [002-005].
- Tells the heir "I think you will be happier for not having any choice" regarding the [[inheritance|fortune]] [002-013].
- Acknowledges that the position forced on [[laura-malcolm|Laura Malcolm]] is "much more embarrassing for her than for you" [002-016].
- Persuades Laura to remain at the [[hazlehurst-manor|Manor House]] rather than take lodgings in [[the-village|the village]] [005-002].
- Tells Laura she is free on her part to refuse the marriage if she cannot be happy in it [005-007].
- Hosts the wedding-eve dinner at the vicarage [011-048].
- Is the person Laura thinks of turning to in her distress after [[john-treverton|John Treverton]]'s desertion — "it would be easier for her to write to her old friend the vicar from a distance" [011-075].

### Fate / Resolution

[[mr-clare|Mr. Clare]]'s role in the narrative is confined to the Hazlehurst portion of the story. His gentle, accommodating manner makes him a sympathetic figure, though the novel subtly questions whether his willingness to go along with [[inheritance|the will]]'s unjust provisions amounts to moral complicity. He remains at his post as vicar, a steady presence in the parish as the drama of the Treverton [[inheritance]] unfolds elsewhere.

## Significance

[[mr-clare|Mr. Clare]] embodies the mid-Victorian churchman as a figure of moderate virtue and institutional complicity. He is kind, patient, and well-meaning, but he never questions the fundamental in[[justice]] of a will that disinherits [[laura-malcolm|Laura Malcolm]]. His favourite claret and his self-deprecating humour humanise him, but they also point to a certain comfortable worldliness that the novel repeatedly associates with the established clergy. In thematic terms, he represents the social order that sensation fiction characteristically exposes as fragile or compromised: a man whose goodness is real but whose moral imagination is limited by the system he serves.
