# The Cloven Foot (Motif)

The novel's title image — the cloven hoof that in folklore reveals the devil beneath a pleasing human guise — functions as the governing metaphor of *The Cloven Foot*, structuring its treatment of concealed criminality, respectable hypocrisy, and the inescapable trace of hidden evil.

## Definition

In Christian folklore, the devil can assume a handsome human form, but his cloven hoof remains — the one feature he cannot disguise, the mark that betrays his true nature no matter how fair his appearance. M.E. Braddon adopts this image as the organizing symbol of her sensation novel. The cloven foot is not a literal physical deformity in the novel but a pervasive figurative pattern: the hidden moral blemish, the suppressed criminal past, the secret self that the respectable surface cannot fully conceal. Every character who presents a fair exterior — John Treverton's gentility, Desrolles's avuncular charm, Edward Clare's chivalrous pretensions — harbours a "cloven foot" that threatens exposure. The motif thus unifies the novel's thematic preoccupations with identity deception, murder, greed, and the fragility of Victorian social reputation.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **The Title as Thematic Key**: The novel's title is itself the first and most important manifestation of the motif. By naming her work *The Cloven Foot*, Braddon signals from the outset that the narrative will be concerned with the exposure of hidden evil — the reader is primed to look for the diabolical trace beneath every respectable surface.

- **Diabolical Temperament (Ch.4)** : La Chicot's moral degeneration is rendered in explicitly diabolical language. After performing to dwindling audiences at the Theatre des Etudiants, "she used to come home from the theatre in a diabolical humour" (004-017). Her violence — the flying cups and saucers, the rages directed at her husband — is the cloven foot of her own nature: the wildness that her theatrical glamour cannot fully mask.

- **Edward Clare's Diabolical Sneer (Ch.22)** : At the Hazlehurst dinner party, Edward Clare speaks of the Trevertons' happiness with "a diabolical sneer" (022-021). His outward posture of urbane wit conceals vindictive malice — he is a "village Iago" whose smooth surface hides a determination to destroy Treverton. He later speculates that the Chicot murderer remains "discreetly hidden under some outer shell of intense respectability" (022-049), unwittingly articulating the motif's central proposition while exemplifying it himself.

- **The Mask and the Hypocrite (Ch.28)** : The motif finds its most explicit articulation in Edward Clare's mission "to pluck the mask from this smooth hypocrite's face" (028-003). The language of mask-stripping directly echoes the folkloric pattern — the devil's pleasing human form (the mask) must be removed to reveal the cloven hoof beneath (the true self). Clare believes he is exposing Treverton's hidden evil; the novel's irony is that Clare's own motivations are equally suspect.

- **Desrolles Unmasked (Ch.45)** : In the novel's climactic confrontation, the diabolical erupts into full visibility. When cornered by the detective, Desrolles's expression "was diabolical; a wild beast — a beast of a low type, not your kingly lion or your lordly tiger — at bay and knowing escape impossible, might so look; the thin lips curling upward above the long sharks' teeth; the grizzled brows contracted — the eyes emitting sparks of lurid light" (045-053). This is the cloven foot made manifest — the criminal nature that Desrolles's multiple aliases and respectable lodgings had concealed, now exposed in its full animal ferocity.

- **Respectability as Concealment**: Throughout the novel, the language of respectability functions as the human guise that hides the cloven foot. John Treverton's fourteen-thousand-pound estate, his country-squire manner, his charitable works — all constitute the fair exterior that must be scrutinized. The novel repeatedly poses the question (022-049): what lies "discreetly hidden under some outer shell of intense respectability"?

## Critical Perspective

Braddon's deployment of the cloven-foot motif participates in the sensation novel's broader project of exposing the hidden corruptions of respectable Victorian society. The 1860s sensation novel — of which Braddon was the acknowledged master after *Lady Audley's Secret* (1862) — specialized in revealing criminality within the domestic sphere: bigamy behind the facade of fashionable marriage, murder within the middle-class household, fraud beneath the surface of inherited wealth. The cloven-foot image concentrates this project into a single, vivid figure: the devil is not elsewhere, in some Gothic castle or foreign clime, but here, in the English country house, wearing the mask of a gentleman.

The motif also carries a specifically gendered dimension. The "cloven foot" in the novel is associated disproportionately with male characters (Treverton, Desrolles, Clare), while the female characters (Laura, Celia) are positioned as the deceived — those who fail to see the hoof beneath the polished boot. La Chicot is the exception: a woman whose diabolical nature is as visible as her dancing, and who is punished with death. The novel thus uses its governing motif both to expose masculine criminality and to police feminine transgression, reflecting the asymmetrical moral economy of Victorian gender ideology.
