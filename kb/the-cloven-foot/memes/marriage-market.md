# Marriage Market

The reduction of marriage to a financial transaction in *The Cloven Foot* — from Jasper Treverton's conditional will to the mercenary language of courtship, engagement, and settlement — and Braddon's sustained critique of a Victorian society in which love and money are inextricable.

## Definition

In *The Cloven Foot*, the marriage market is not a metaphor but a literal mechanism: Jasper Treverton's will conditions John Treverton's inheritance of a fourteen-thousand-a-year estate upon his marriage to Laura Malcolm within a year of the testator's death (002-006). The phrase captures the novel's structural transformation of courtship into commerce — a world in which proposals are brokered by a dead man's eccentricity, in which "hateful alliance" (003-075) and "marriage settlement" (010-063) crowd out the language of love, and in which no character can be entirely sure whether affections are genuine or mercenary. The marriage market operates at every social level in the novel: the gentry match engineered by the will, the provincial solicitor's calculating courtship, the rejected suitor's resentment at being outbid by a wealthier rival, and the public's appetite for the spectacle of mismatched unions.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **The Conditional Will as Marriage Broker (Ch.2)** : Jasper Treverton's will is the novel's ultimate marriage-market instrument. It does not simply transfer property; it compels a union between two strangers — John Treverton and Laura Malcolm — for whom "what disinterested feeling could there ever arise between those two, who were bound together by their common interest in a great estate" (002-031). The will transforms marriage into the price of inheritance: John must marry Laura or lose everything (002-003).

- **Laura Malcolm as Conscripted Commodity (Ch.2–Ch.5)** : Laura Malcolm's consent is simultaneously essential and irrelevant. The will's language treats her as a prize — "his dearly-beloved adopted daughter" (002-006) — yet her own preferences are reduced to a binary whose exercise destroys the fortune. She protests that she "would rather forego this fortune than force a hateful alliance" (003-075), asserting a moral agency that the will structurally denies her.

- **The Mercenary Vocabulary of Courtship (Ch.10–Ch.13)** : When John Treverton and Laura Malcolm negotiate their engagement, the language is relentlessly commercial. John asks Laura an anxious question: whether she would have consented "if there had been no such thing as my cousin Jasper's will" (010-004). The marriage settlement — a legal document specifying financial arrangements — becomes a substitute for romantic declaration. John proposes to settle his entire estate upon Laura as a postnuptial trust (010-060), an act that attempts to disentangle money from love by immersing love entirely in money.

- **The Public Framing: "Princes and Princesses" (Ch.5)** : John Treverton defends the contractual nature of their marriage by invoking royal precedent: royal couples get on "as well together as the princes and princesses whose marriages are arranged by cabinet councils" (005-010). This is the marriage market rationalized — the claim that dynastic calculation yields as much happiness as romantic choice, a proposition the novel systematically troubles.

- **The "Ill-Omened" Marriage (Ch.11–Ch.12)** : From the moment the engagement is announced, the marriage is framed as cursed. Celia Clare declares "This is indeed an ill-omened marriage!" (011-022) when Laura refuses a wedding gown. The phrase "ill-omened" recurs throughout the novel — at the wedding, in retrospect after the murder, and in the parish gossip of Hazlehurst (012-005, 012-063, 015-002, 023-025) — linking the marriage's commercial origins to its disastrous consequences.

- **The Wedding That Was No Wedding (Ch.11)** : Laura Malcolm refuses all the trappings of a conventional wedding — no trousseau, no wedding gown (011-022). This refusal is both personal pride and narrative commentary: a marriage contracted under financial compulsion does not deserve festive celebration. The wedding itself is "a very quiet affair" (010-052) conducted in an atmosphere of dread rather than joy.

- **Celia Clare's Perspective on the Market (Ch.5)** : Celia expresses bafflement that Laura might not wish to marry a man with John Treverton's prospects, suggesting that in the marriage market, the question of "her own inclinations" (005-044) is secondary to financial advantage. Her practical view of marriage — economic security above sentiment — reflects one pole of the Victorian marriage-market spectrum.

- **The Rejected Suitor as Failed Speculator (Ch.1, Ch.23, Ch.37)** : Edward Clare's bitterness toward John Treverton is partly romantic jealousy and partly the resentment of a man who has lost a matrimonial prize. The Vicar sees his son's malice as that of "a rejected suitor" (037-024) — a phrase that frames Edward as a failed bidder in the marriage market, his chivalric pretensions concealing economic disappointment.

## Critical Perspective

Braddon's treatment of the marriage market in *The Cloven Foot* belongs to the sensation novel's broader critique of Victorian matrimonial economics. The marriage plot was the master-plot of nineteenth-century fiction, from Austen's ironic marriage-market comedies to the Brontes' passionate rewritings of the Cinderella fable. But where the realist novel typically resolves the tension between love and money through some form of moral accounting — the heroine who marries well but not too mercenarily, the hero who proves his worth by earning rather than inheriting — Braddon refuses such consolations.

The conditional will makes sincerity structurally impossible. John Treverton cannot prove that his affections are genuine because no act of love can be disentangled from the fortune that depends upon it. Laura Malcolm cannot exercise genuine choice because her refusal would destroy the inheritance for everyone. The novel's repeated use of the phrase "ill-omened marriage" (011-022, 012-063, 015-002, 023-025) insists upon a causal connection between the marriage's commercial origins and its disastrous consequences: the domestic misery, social scandal, and violent crime that follow.

Braddon wrote *The Cloven Foot* in 1879, at a moment when the Married Women's Property Act was beginning to reshape the legal framework of Victorian marriage. The novel's intense focus on settlements, trusts, and the financial architecture of matrimony reflects a culture in which marriage was being reconceived — from a sacrament that subsumed a wife's legal personhood into her husband's, to a contract between parties with separable economic interests. Laura Malcolm's insistence on a postnuptial settlement, and John Treverton's willingness to place his entire estate in trust for her, gesture toward this new model. Yet the will's coercive structure remains the novel's master-document, and the "ill-omened" refrain darkens every gesture of reform.

The sensation novel's distinctive contribution to the marriage-market theme is its insistence on the violence latent in the commercial model. When marriage is a transaction, the rejected party is a disappointed creditor. When love is priced, fraud is inevitable. The Cloven Foot of the title — the devil's footprint — is perhaps nowhere more visible than in the conditional will that claims to sanctify a union while commodifying every party to it.
