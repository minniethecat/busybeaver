# Nature

## Definition

In Baudelaire's poetic universe, **Nature** (*la Nature*) occupies an ambiguous position — neither the benign mother of Romanticism nor the indifferent mechanism of the natural sciences, but a **sacred text written in symbols**. Nature is the visible register of the invisible: every tree, scent, and color is a word in a language that only the poet-priest can decipher. This theory, expounded in the sonnet **Correspondances** (004), is the philosophical foundation of all of Baudelaire's poetry and, by extension, of the Symbolist movement that followed him.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

The definitive statement on Nature appears in **Correspondances** (004), arguably the most influential sonnet Baudelaire ever wrote:

> La Nature est un temple où de vivants piliers
> Laissent parfois sortir de confuses paroles… (004-001)

Nature is a *temple* — not a forest, not a garden, but a sacred architecture. The "living pillars" (*vivants piliers*) are trees, but they are also the columns of a church. The forest is a cathedral, and the poet is its celebrant. The "confused words" (*confuses paroles*) that emerge from this temple are the symbols of the natural world — scents, colors, sounds — which to the uninitiated are mere sensations but to the poet are a coherent language.

This is Baudelaire's theory of **correspondences**: the doctrine that the sensory world is a system of analogies, where each sensation corresponds to an idea, and all sensations correspond to one another. The famous examples in the sonnet — "Les parfums, les couleurs et les sons se répondent" (perfumes, colors, and sounds answer one another) — establish a synesthetic universe in which the boundaries between the senses dissolve:

> Comme de longs échos qui de loin se confondent
> Dans une ténébreuse et profonde unité… (004-005)

The "dark and deep unity" (*ténébreuse et profonde unité*) is the metaphysical ground beneath the sensory manifold. Nature is not an end in itself — it is a **medium**, a system of signs pointing beyond itself to the realm of the Ideal.

In **Élévation** (005), the relationship between Nature and transcendence is dramatized. The poet rises above "les étangs" (ponds), "les vallées" (valleys), "les montagnes" (mountains), "les bois" (woods), "les nuages" (clouds), and "les mers" (seas) — the entire inventory of the natural world — to reach a region "où l'air pur est l'oubli" (where the pure air is oblivion). Nature here is what must be transcended, not what must be embraced.

Yet in the same movement, the poetry of nature in Baudelaire retains a sensuous intensity that belies any simple rejection. In **La Chevelure** (018), the lover's hair opens onto a tropical landscape:

> Quand la nature, grande en ses desseins cachés,
> De toi se sert, ô femme, ô reine des péchés… (018-060)

Nature uses the woman's body — her hair, her perfume — as its instrument. The woman is not natural in the Romantic sense (innocent, unadorned); she is nature's artifice, its most elaborate and deceptive work. This is Baudelaire's reversal of the Romantic nature/woman equation: nature is not simple and pure; it is **complex and intoxicating**, and it reveals itself through the most artificial of its products.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's theory of Nature as a temple of symbols has three major consequences for his poetics:

**1. The poet as translator.** If Nature is a text, the poet is its translator — the one who renders the "confused words" of the living pillars into the articulate language of verse. This elevates the poet from entertainer to priest: the poem is not an expression of personal feeling but a **decoding of cosmic meaning**. The Symbolist movement (Mallarmé, Verlaine, Rimbaud) would take this idea as its founding principle.

**2. Nature as system, not essence.** Baudelaire's Nature is not a substance but a **structure** — a network of analogies and correspondences. This distinguishes him from both the Romantics (for whom Nature was a living presence, a *natura naturans*) and the Parnassians (for whom Nature was a collection of picturesque objects). For Baudelaire, Nature is meaningful, but its meaning is encrypted — it requires the poet's labor to become legible.

**3. The distance from Rousseau.** Baudelaire's Nature is definitively **post-lapsarian**. There is no return to an innocent state of nature; the natural is always already fallen. This is the deep agreement between Baudelaire's aesthetics and his Catholicism: the natural world is beautiful, but it is beautiful as a *sign* of something else — and that something else (God, the Ideal, the absolute) is inaccessible. Nature is a temple, but the god has departed; the pillars speak, but their words are "confuses."

> En l'isolant de l'immense nature. (028-033)

This line from **L'Irrémédiable** (028) — "isolating it from the immense nature" — describes the Baudelairean condition in reverse: the fallen self is *cut off* from the symbolic order of nature, adrift in a world where correspondences have broken down.
