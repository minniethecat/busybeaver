# Angel

## Definition

In Baudelaire's symbolic vocabulary, the **Angel** (*l'Ange*) represents the ascending current of existence — the pull toward purity, transcendence, and the ideal. Angels appear throughout *Les Fleurs du Mal* as figures of the beloved, as guardians of spiritual possibility, and as reminders of what the poet has lost or can never attain. Yet Baudelaire's angels are never simply good: they are often ambiguous, poised between heaven and the abyss, capable of falling.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

Angels appear most frequently as metaphors for the beloved. In **Hymne à la Beauté** (017), the poet asks whether Beauty is angel or siren — "Ange ou Sirène" (017-025) — and answers that it does not matter. The beloved can be angelic in appearance and demonic in effect.

In **Réversibilité**, the poet asks the beloved-angel to pray for his sinful soul: "Ange plein de gaieté, connaissez-vous l'angoisse?" — Angel full of gaiety, do you know anguish? The angel here is innocent, untouched by the poet's suffering, and the distance between them is what makes the prayer both necessary and futile.

In **Le Flambeau Vivant** (The Living Torch), the beloved's eyes are compared to angels: "Ce sont des yeux d'anges" — they are angel's eyes — but these angelic eyes lead the poet not to heaven but into the labyrinth of desire. Baudelaire's angels lead downward as often as upward.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's angels reflect his broader pattern of spiritual inversion. Traditional Christian angelology places angels in a clear hierarchy between God and humanity, as messengers of divine will. Baudelaire's angels have fallen into ambiguity. They are beautiful but possibly dangerous, pure but possibly indifferent, heavenly but possibly irrelevant to the poet's earthly suffering.

This ambiguity is central to Baudelaire's treatment of the ideal. If angels can fall, then purity is not stable. If the angelic beloved can be cruel, then beauty is not morally reliable. The angel, like all Baudelairean symbols, carries its opposite within itself.
