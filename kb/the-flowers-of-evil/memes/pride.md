# Pride

## Definition

**Pride** (*l'orgueil*) in Baudelaire's work is not a sin to be confessed but a force to be claimed. It is the Satanic refusal — the erect spine of the creature who will not kneel, the dignity preserved even in damnation, and the psychological engine that converts suffering into art. Baudelaire inherits the Christian condemnation of pride as the deadliest of sins and deliberately inverts it: pride becomes the poet's most essential quality, the thing that distinguishes him from the timid conformity of the bourgeois herd and aligns him with the great rebels of myth and history.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

The most concentrated expression of Baudelairean pride appears in the poem **Bénédiction** (Blessing), where the poet's mother curses the child she has borne — a poet destined for suffering — and the poet's response is not shame but exaltation:

> S'écria, transporté d'un orgueil satanique:
> « Je sais que la douleur est la noblesse unique… » (013-010)

"Satanic pride" is not a confession of evil but a declaration of independence. The poet *chooses* his alignment — with the fallen angel rather than the reigning God — because suffering is the source of nobility, and Satan is the archetype of the sufferer who refuses to repent. This is Baudelaire's Luciferian theology: pride is the refusal to accept unearned forgiveness, the insistence that suffering must be *transformed* rather than absolved.

The triad that recurs throughout the collection binds pride to its companion forces. In the line "Et contient mon orgueil, ma douleur et ma gloire!" (And contains my pride, my pain, and my glory!, 059-042), pride precedes pain and glory — it is the first term, the initiating energy from which the other two flow. Pain is the cost, glory is the reward, but pride is the *cause*.

This pride takes institutional form in the figure of the poet as "prêtre orgueilleux de la Lyre" (proud priest of the Lyre, 060-027). The poet is not a servant of his audience but a priest of his art — proud because the Lyre demands it, because only pride can sustain the solitary labor of creation against a world that does not understand.

The darkest formulation appears in the line "Un peu de l'orgueil des damnés" (A little of the pride of the damned, 061-025). Even the damned, in Baudelaire's vision, retain their pride — it is the one possession that cannot be taken, the "trésor de toute gueuserie" (treasure of every beggary, 094-013). When everything else is stripped away — wealth, reputation, love, hope — pride remains. It is the ontological minimum of human dignity.

Baudelaire's cats — "Les chats puissants et doux, orgueil de la maison" (Powerful and gentle cats, pride of the house, 048-003) — embody a domesticated, aestheticized form of pride. The cat's pride is self-contained, indifferent to approval, at once powerful and gentle. It is the pride Baudelaire admires: not aggressive self-assertion but sovereign self-possession.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's rehabilitation of pride must be understood against the background of mid-nineteenth-century French Catholicism and bourgeois morality, both of which treated pride as the root of all evil and humility as the highest virtue. Baudelaire saw in this endorsement of humility a mechanism of social control — the demand that the exceptional individual bow to the mediocre majority. His Satanic pride is a counter-force: the refusal to be flattened.

The theological problem Baudelaire poses is genuinely difficult. If God allows the poet to suffer — as the mother's curse in **Bénédiction** makes explicit — then is the poet's pride in refusing consolation a sin or a form of integrity? Baudelaire's answer, consistently, is that it is integrity. The poet who accepted consolation would be a poet no longer. Pride is not the *cause* of his suffering but his *response* to it — and a nobler response than the servile gratitude demanded by conventional piety.

The triad pride-pain-glory (059-042) is Baudelaire's most important contribution to the psychology of creation. It breaks with the Romantic model of the artist as passive vessel of inspiration and replaces it with an active, agonistic model: the artist *wrests* glory from pain through an act of pride. The work of art is not a gift received but a victory won.
