# Rire

## Definition

**Rire** (laughter) occupies a unique place in Baudelaire's thought: it is not a simple expression of joy but a metaphysical problem. In his essay "De l'essence du rire" (On the Essence of Laughter), Baudelaire argues that laughter is essentially satanic — it arises from the perception of superiority, the sudden recognition of one's own elevation over another's fall. The primordial laugh is the laugh of the demon at human weakness. Yet this satanic origin does not make laughter worthless; on the contrary, it makes it the most human of expressions, the mark of our fallen grandeur. The child's innocent laugh is the fleeting exception — a paradise lost with the onset of self-consciousness.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

The collection's deployment of laughter maps its full moral spectrum, from the demonic to the desperate. The most explicitly satanic register appears in **L'Irrémédiable**: "Reconnaissez Satan à son rire vainqueur" (Recognize Satan by his victorious laugh, 059-027). Satan's laugh is *vainqueur* — triumphant — because it signals the successful corruption of souls. Laughter here is the audible sign of damnation.

At the opposite pole is the forced gaiety of the damned in **Le Jeu** (Gambling): the gamblers are "Au rire éternel condamnés, / Et qui ne peuvent plus sourire!" (Condemned to eternal laughter, / And who can no longer smile! 072-028–029). The crucial distinction between *rire* and *sourire* (smile) structures this couplet: *rire* is mechanical, compulsive, a punishment; *sourire* is voluntary, genuine, a grace. The damned are condemned to laugh but incapable of smiling — they have the external form of joy without its inner substance.

The **Bénédiction** poem opens the collection with an image of maternal mockery that sets the tone for all that follows: "Et ton rire trempé de pleurs qu'on ne voit pas" (And your laughter steeped in tears one cannot see, 007-013). The mother's laugh at her poet-child is cruelty dressed as mirth — the paradigm of Baudelairean laughter as aggression. The tears "qu'on ne voit pas" (that one cannot see) hidden within the laugh prefigure the entire Spleen cycle: beneath every apparent joy lurks an invisible sorrow.

The grotesque dimension of Baudelairean laughter crystallizes in **Danse Macabre**: the skeletal figure displays "Le sourire éternel de tes trente-deux dents" (The eternal smile of your thirty-two teeth, 087-040). The grin of the death's-head is the limit case of Baudelaire's laughter theory — pure, unceasing, contextless mirth that mocks all human pretension by its very existence.

A rare moment of untroubled beauty appears in **Le Beau Navire** (The Beautiful Ship): "Le rire joue en ton visage" (Laughter plays on your face, 112-003). Here laughter is not satanic but aesthetic — a feature of the beloved's beauty, as innocent as light on water. The verb *joue* (plays) is the antonym of the *condamnés* earlier: play is free, condemnation is compelled.

The primal scene of Baudelairean laughter theory appears in **Les Métamorphoses du Vampire**: "Et fais rire les vieux du rire des enfants" (And make the old laugh with the laughter of children, 115-008). The vampire — the beloved — possesses the power to restore to the aged the lost laughter of childhood. But this restoration comes at the cost of being consumed. The child's laugh is the ideal, but it cannot be recovered innocently; it can only be borrowed from a predatory source.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's theory of laughter is inseparable from his theology of the Fall. Laughter is the sign of our distance from God: angels do not laugh, animals do not laugh, only fallen beings — humans and demons — laugh. This places Baudelaire in direct opposition to the Romantic cult of joy. For Baudelaire, joy is not the natural state but an intermittent reprieve; laughter is not liberation but a symptom of our condition.

Yet Baudelaire does not simply condemn laughter. His essay insists that the comic is a legitimate artistic mode, and *Les Fleurs du Mal* is rich in satirical and grotesque effects. The key is self-awareness: the Baudelairean artist laughs while knowing why laughter is possible. This doubles the satanic structure — the artist participates in the demonic while anatomizing it.

The tears hidden within the laugh (007-013) provide the collection's deepest insight into laughter's dual nature. Baudelaire never separates laughter from sorrow because he never separates the human from the fallen. Every laugh is "trempé de pleurs" — steeped in tears — whether the laugher knows it or not.
