# Tears

## Definition

**Tears** (*les larmes, les pleurs*) in Baudelaire's poetic economy are never merely symptoms of sadness. They are bodily signs that mark the threshold between the inner and outer worlds, involuntary confessions of a soul that language cannot fully express. In a poetic universe where irony and misanthropy dominate the surface, tears are the moments when the mask slips — when suffering becomes visible, when the cold poet reveals that he too has been wounded.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

Tears appear at critical junctures throughout the collection, often in association with the moon, with children, and with figures of exile. They mark the moments when Baudelaire's characteristic hardness gives way to a vulnerability that is all the more powerful for being rare.

The first tear in the collection belongs not to the poet but to Ennui. In **Au Lecteur** (poem I):

> C'est l'Ennui! — L'œil chargé d'un pleur involontaire, (002-037)

"It is Ennui! — the eye charged with an involuntary tear." Ennui, the monster who dreams of scaffolds while smoking his hookah, sheds a single tear — involuntary, as tears always are in Baudelaire. The tear is the sign that Ennui is not mere apathy but a suffering so deep it has forgotten its own name. This tear, shed at the threshold of the entire collection, establishes tears as witnesses to a pain that cannot be articulated.

The moon is Baudelaire's great weeper. In **Tristesse de la Lune** (poem LXVII), the moon is personified as a grieving woman:

> Elle laisse filer une larme furtive, (047-009)

She lets a furtive tear slip — the adjective *furtive* is crucial. The moon's tear is stolen, hidden, a grief that does not wish to be seen. The poet, "ennemi du sommeil" (enemy of sleep), receives this tear in the hollow of his hand:

> Dans le creux de sa main prend cette larme pâle, (047-012)

The tear is *pâle* (pale) — the color of the moon itself. The poet becomes the custodian of the moon's grief, collecting her tear as if it were a precious stone. The tear here is not a loss but a gift: the moon shares her sorrow with the poet, and the poet preserves it in his hand, transforming it into art.

This lunar grief is implicitly contrasted with human unfeeling. The moon's tear is furtive because the world does not deserve to see it; the poet is the only one awake, the only one worthy of receiving it. The tear is thus a sign of election — the poet is chosen to witness what others cannot see.

In **Le Vampire** (poem XXXI), tears become a weapon:

> Si, quelque soir, d'un pleur obtenu sans effort (023-036)

The speaker imagines obtaining a tear from the beloved "without effort" — but immediately adds that this tear would only revive the vampire-beloved's power over him. The tear is a poison disguised as balm; the beloved's weeping is another form of predation. Baudelaire here inverts the conventional association of tears with sincerity: the beloved's tear is manipulation, and the speaker's vulnerability to it is his curse.

In **Le Mort Joyeux** (poem LXXII), the speaker rejects tears entirely:

> Plutôt que d'implorer une larme du monde, (054-050)

"Rather than implore a tear from the world," the speaker would prefer to be devoured by worms while alive. The world's tear — its pity — is more horrible than death itself. This is the Baudelairean rejection of consolation: the poet does not want the world's tears, because the world's pity is an insult. Tears from the wrong source are contamination, not purification.

This theme returns in **Le Cygne** (poem LXXXIX), where the exiled Andromache weeps by a "Simoïs menteur" (lying Simois):

> Ce Simoïs menteur qui par vos pleurs grandit, (078-004)

The false river grows by her tears — but the tears are real even if the river is not. The exile's grief is not diminished by being poured into an imitation; if anything, the imitation deepens the grief by underscoring what has been lost. Tears here are the currency of exile, the only authentic response to a world of false rivers and borrowed landscapes.

In **Lesbos** (poem CXIII), tears become eroticized and collective:

> Je sèche tous les pleurs sur mes seins triomphants (115-007)

The speaker — Lesbos personified — dries all tears on her triumphant breasts. The tear is here absorbed into the body of the erotic mother, transformed from sign of grief into sign of belonging. To weep is to be gathered into the community of the damned, and the drying of tears is a form of erotic welcome.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's tears are the most reliable index of his ambivalent relationship to sentiment. He is, famously and programmatically, the poet of "le mot cruel" (the cruel word) and the "sourire amer" (bitter smile); but his tears are the counter-current to this cruelty, the evidence that the bitterness is itself a form of woundedness. The shed tear is involuntary (002-037), furtive (047-009) — never theatrical, never demanded. Baudelaire's poetics of the tear is a poetics of the unwilled sign.

The moon in **Tristesse de la Lune** is the figure of this unwilled grief. The moon does not choose to weep — she "lets" a tear slip — and the poet does not solicit it — he receives it. This mutuality of involuntary giving and passive receiving is Baudelaire's model of true compassion, as opposed to the false pity that he rejects in **Le Mort Joyeux** ("implorer une larme du monde"). The world's pity is an insult because it is given from above, from a position of superiority. The moon's tear is a gift because it is given in secret, from one sufferer to another, in the dark.
