# Chair

## Definition

In Baudelaire's lexicon, **Chair** (*la Chair*) is never merely the body. It is the **theological battlefield** where spirit and matter contend — the site of the Fall and, paradoxically, the sole medium through which any redemption can be felt. Baudelaire inherits the Christian dualism of flesh-versus-spirit but subverts it: his "spiritual flesh" (*chair spirituelle*) is not a contradiction but a synthesis, a body that has become the vehicle of transcendence rather than its obstacle. The flesh is damned and sacred at once, and this double nature is the generative tension of the entire *Fleurs du Mal* project.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

The duality of flesh is established early. In **Les Phares** (006), Rubens — the painter of abundant, overflowing bodies — is characterized through a startling metaphor:

> Rubens, fleuve d'oubli, jardin de la paresse,
> Oreiller de chair fraîche où l'on ne peut aimer,
> Mais où la vie afflue et s'agite sans cesse, (006-001)

"Pillow of fresh flesh where one cannot love, / But where life flows and stirs without cease." The flesh here is not a site of love but of **anonymous vitality** — life as pure biological surge, indifferent to the individual soul. Rubens' bodies are "fresh" (*fraîche*) but loveless: they pulse with vitality that has no spiritual dimension.

The transformation of flesh from mere matter to spiritual medium reaches its apex in **Tout Entière** (033):

> Sa chair spirituelle a le parfum des Anges,
> Et son œil nous revêt d'un habit de clarté. (033-031)

"Her spiritual flesh has the perfume of Angels" — the phrase *chair spirituelle* is a deliberate oxymoron, a reconciliation of opposites. Flesh, the seat of sin, becomes "spiritual" (*spirituelle*) without ceasing to be flesh. The beloved's body does not transcend materiality; it **transfigures** it. Her flesh smells not of decay but of angels — the same verb that elsewhere describes the stench of the corpse.

In **Chanson d'Après-Midi** (042), the erotic dimension of flesh is rendered through the circuit of perfume:

> Sur ta chair le parfum rôde
> Comme autour d'une fleur. (042-013)

"On your flesh the perfume prowls / As around a flower" — the verb *rôder* (to prowl, to lurk) introduces an animal, almost predatory quality. Perfume does not rest on this flesh; it circles it, hunts it. The flesh is a flower, but one that attracts predators.

The body-as-temple appears in **La Prière d'un Païen** (057), where the speaker, addressing a pagan deity of pleasure, describes the body in architectural terms:

> Faîte de chair et de velours. (057-011)

"Summit of flesh and velvet" — the word *faîte* means ridgepole, the highest point of a roof. The body is a **building**, a sacred architecture of flesh crowned with velvet. The pagan prayer consecrates what Christianity condemns: the flesh as a site of worship rather than sin.

But Baudelaire never lets the other face of flesh recede for long. In **L'Imprévu** (059), the flesh resists all moral warning:

> J'avertis en vain la chair infecte.
> L'homme est aveugle, sourd, fragile, comme un mur. (059-018)

"I warn the infected flesh in vain" — the adjective *infecte* (infected, foul) is the antonym of *spirituelle*. Flesh that refuses purification is flesh that putrefies. The "wall" simile (*comme un mur*) reduces the human being to blunt, insensate materiality — matter that no longer channels spirit but blocks it.

In **Danse Macabre** (087), the lovers of flesh are those who cannot understand death's seduction:

> Qui ne comprennent pas, amants ivres de chair,
> L'élégance sans nom de l'humaine armature. (087-018)

"Lovers drunk on flesh" — the phrase *ivres de chair* recalls the wine imagery that runs through the collection. To be drunk on flesh is to be intoxicated by the surface, to miss the "nameless elegance of the human armature" — the skeleton beneath, the death that gives form to life.

The most violent treatment of flesh comes in **Femmes Damnées** (114):

> Fait claquer votre chair ainsi qu'un vieux drapeau. (114-100)

"Makes your flesh crack like an old flag" — flesh reduced to fabric, to a worn-out banner snapped by the wind. The simile strips flesh of all its sacred mystery: it is cloth, material, a thing among things.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's treatment of flesh maps onto the broader architecture of *Les Fleurs du Mal*. The collection moves — broadly — from the ideal (Spleen et Idéal) through the urban (Tableaux Parisiens), the artificial paradises (Le Vin), the condemned (Fleurs du Mal / Révolte), and finally to death (La Mort). Flesh follows this arc: in the early poems it is capable of spiritualization (*chair spirituelle*); by the condemned and death poems, it reverts to infected matter, the flag that cracks in the wind.

This trajectory is not simply a descent. Baudelaire's originality lies in his refusal to resolve the flesh/spirit dialectic. Christian orthodoxy demands that flesh be mortified so spirit can ascend. Baudelaire, by contrast, insists that **spirit must pass through flesh, not around it**. The "spiritual flesh" of Tout Entière is not a compromise but a poetics — a way of saying that the only transcendence available to embodied beings is one that takes embodiment seriously.

The flesh is also the site of Baudelaire's most intense synesthetic experiments. In Chanson d'Après-Midi, perfume "prowls" on flesh; in Tout Entière, flesh exudes the scent of angels. Flesh is not silent matter but a **broadcasting station** for sensory signals — it emits perfumes, reflects light, produces music (the breath that "makes music" in 033-023). The flesh is the body's interface with the invisible world.
