# Cain

## Identity

**Cain** (*Caïn*) is a biblical figure from Genesis 4 — the first son of Adam and Eve, who murdered his brother Abel and was cursed by God to wander the earth as a fugitive. In orthodox tradition, Cain is the archetype of sin: the first murderer, the inaugurator of human violence.

The Romantic movement radically reinterpreted Cain. Byron's *Cain* (1821) recast him as a Promethean rebel who defies a tyrannical God. Baudelaire inherits this Romantic Cain and deepens him psychologically: the poet identifies with Cain not as villain but as the marked outsider, condemned yet unrepentant, whose suffering is the condition of his vision.

## Connection to Baudelaire

For Baudelaire, Cain is a figure of identification — the poet's double. The poet, like Cain, is marked and set apart from ordinary humanity. His sensitivity is a curse that isolates him; his transgression is not crime but vision, seeing what others refuse to see.

Baudelaire's own life mirrors Cain's exile. Expelled from school, estranged from his family, prosecuted for his poetry, living in debt and disgrace — Baudelaire knew what it meant to wander under a mark. The alignment of poet and murderer is characteristically Baudelairean: transgression, not virtue, is the source of creative power.

Cain also represents a response to suffering that Baudelaire admired: the refusal to repent. Cain never apologizes to God. His suffering is real but his dignity lies in not begging for forgiveness. This proud endurance, this insistence on one's own truth against divine and social condemnation, is the Baudelairean ideal.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

Cain appears most prominently in **Abel et Caïn**, a poem that contrasts the two lineages. The race of Abel prospers under God's favor — "Race d'Abel, dors, bois et mange; / Dieu te sourit complaisamment." The race of Cain suffers — "Race de Caïn, dans la fange / Rampe et meurs misérablement."

The poem's sympathies are unmistakably with Cain. The race of Abel enjoys comfort purchased by divine favor; the race of Cain earns dignity through suffering. The final stanza calls for the race of Cain to ascend to heaven and cast God down — an apocalyptic inversion of the biblical order.

This is Baudelaire at his most politically and theologically radical. The poem was among those condemned in the 1857 trial, partly because its Cainite sympathies were read as blasphemy against the established religious order.

Cain's presence also resonates in poems about exile, guilt, and the artist's alienation — **Bénédiction** (003), where the poet is cursed from birth, and **Le Voyage** (108), where the poet seeks escape from the world that condemned him.

## Literary Significance

Baudelaire's Cain belongs to a tradition of Romantic biblical revisionism alongside Blake, Byron, and Hugo. Each of these writers rehabilitated a biblical villain — Satan, Cain — as a hero of rebellion. Baudelaire's contribution is psychological: his Cain is not a cosmic rebel but an intimate one, a figure for the artist's own condition.

The Cain identification also illuminates Baudelaire's aesthetics of evil. The poet is not good; he is truthful. Cain becomes the patron saint of a poetry that refuses to flatter its audience with moral comfort, that insists on describing the world as it is — fallen, unjust, haunted by suffering — rather than as it should be.
