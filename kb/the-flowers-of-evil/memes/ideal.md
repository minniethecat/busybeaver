# The Ideal

## Definition

In Baudelaire's poetic universe, the **Ideal** (*l'Idéal*) is the transcendent pole of human experience — the realm of perfect beauty, spiritual aspiration, and artistic perfection that stands in permanent tension with the crushing weight of Spleen. It is not a stable destination but a direction of movement: the vertical ascent toward which the poet's consciousness ceaselessly reaches, even as gravity — in the form of boredom, despair, and the material world — pulls it back down. The Ideal is what makes Spleen bearable, and Spleen is what makes the Ideal necessary.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

The Ideal is explicitly named in the largest section of *Les Fleurs du Mal* — "Spleen et Idéal" — which comprises the majority of the collection's poems. This structural choice is itself a statement: the entire poetic enterprise oscillates between these two poles, and every poem is situated somewhere on the axis between them.

In the dedicatory preface **Au Lecteur** (To the Reader), Baudelaire establishes the Ideal as a shared human aspiration, however corrupted:

> La sottise, l'erreur, le péché, la lésine,
> Occupent nos esprits et travaillent nos corps… (002-001)

The catalogue of sins that opens the poem is the anti-Ideal — everything that drags humanity downward. The preface to the 1857 edition articulates the aspiration explicitly, acknowledging the poet's goal of reaching "l'idéale perfection" while confessing to having fallen far short (001-055).

In the poem **L'Idéal** (015), Baudelaire rejects the conventional beauties of classical art and literature — the "pâles vignettes" of an effete tradition — in favor of a darker, more violent ideal embodied in the figure of Lady Macbeth and the monumental paintings of Michelangelo:

> Je laisse à Gavarni, poète des chloroses,
> Son troupeau gazouillant de beautés d'hôpital…
> Ce qu'il faut à ce cœur profond comme un abîme,
> C'est vous, Lady Macbeth, âme puissante au crime… (015-001)

The Ideal here is not gentle or soothing — it is grandiose, terrifying, and tinged with evil. Baudelaire's Ideal is inseparable from the abyss.

Throughout the "Spleen et Idéal" section, the Ideal manifests in multiple forms: erotic beauty (the beloved woman as celestial vision), artistic perfection (the poem itself as a triumph over chaos), and spiritual transcendence (the longing for a world beyond the material). But it always remains just out of reach — sensed, desired, glimpsed, but never possessed.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's treatment of the Ideal is fundamentally **anti-Platonic**. For Plato, the Ideal exists in a realm of pure forms, accessible through reason and contemplation. For Baudelaire, the Ideal is accessible only through the senses — through intoxication, erotic passion, or aesthetic rapture — and it is always already contaminated by the very materiality that makes it perceptible. Beauty, Baudelaire writes, is made of "un élément éternel, invariable" and "un élément relatif, circonstanciel" — an eternal element and a relative, circumstantial one. The Ideal is always alloyed with the real.

This alloying is what distinguishes Baudelaire from the Romantics. Where a Romantic poet might achieve transcendence through communion with nature, Baudelaire finds nature itself a "temple" whose "vivants piliers" speak only in "confuses paroles" (Correspondances). The Ideal is never pure — it flickers through the urban crowd, the prostitute's gaze, the drunkard's staggering step. It is a **modern Ideal**: fragmented, fleeting, and indissociable from the city that produces it.

The structural pairing of Spleen and Ideal is Baudelaire's great contribution to modern poetics. The two are not opposites in a simple binary but interdependent terms in a dialectic. Without Ideal, Spleen would be mere depression; without Spleen, Ideal would be mere daydream. The tension between them is the engine of Baudelaire's poetry — and, in his diagnosis, of modern consciousness itself.
