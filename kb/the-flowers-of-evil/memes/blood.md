# Blood

## Definition

**Blood** (*le sang*) in Baudelaire's poetic vocabulary is far more than a bodily fluid. It is the substance of sacrifice, the medium of passion, the dark ink in which the poet writes, and the "poison noir" (black poison, 072-019) that circulates through the veins of melancholy. Baudelaire's blood is always *spent* — never merely contained — spilled in the act of creation, the ecstasy of love, or the violence of history. To write a poem is to bleed; to love is to lose blood; to live intensely is to be drained.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

Blood saturates Baudelaire's imagery from the opening poems to the final pages. Its most concentrated appearance is in the famous characterization of Delacroix in **Les Phares** (poem VI):

> Delacroix, lac de sang hanté des mauvais anges (006-029)

Delacroix is a "lake of blood haunted by evil angels" — an image of terrifying beauty that defines the Romantic artist as a reservoir of dark, sacrificial energy. The lake suggests both depth and stillness; the blood suggests violence and life; the evil angels suggest that the source of this art is not benign. Delacroix's paintings are not made from talent but from blood — drawn from a wound that never closes.

The same poem ends with the famous image of the artist's collective cry reaching God:

> Que cet ardent sanglot qui roule d'âge en âge
> Et vient mourir au bord de votre éternité! (006-043)

The "ardent sob" — which is also a *sanglot*, a word that contains *sang* (blood) within it — rolls across the ages and dies at the shore of God's eternity. The artist's cry is a blood-cry, a sob that carries the substance of suffering across centuries.

In **La Muse malade** (poem VII), the poet's creative power is figured as blood-loss:

> Du sang que nous perdons croît et se fortifie (008-014)

From the blood we lose, something grows and strengthens. The economy is sacrificial: blood spent is not wasted but transformed into strength. The poet's depletion is the poem's enrichment — a vampiric logic where art feeds on the life of its creator.

The erotic dimension emerges in **Parfum exotique** (poem XXII, actually from **A une Madone** etc.) and more darkly in the poems of sadistic love. In **Le Poison** (poem XXXX):

> Mais respirer le parfum de ton sang (026-014)

"To breathe the perfume of your blood" — the lover's blood becomes a fragrance, an intoxicant, a poison that is also a pleasure. The erotic and the violent converge in the image of blood-as-perfume.

The historical dimension appears in **Le Cygne** and surrounding poems, but most powerfully in the apocalyptic imagery of **Spleen** (poem LXXVIII):

> Au bord d'un lac de sang sous un grand tas de morts (053-013)

"At the edge of a lake of blood under a great pile of dead" — this is the spleen landscape at its most extreme, where the internal depression projects itself outward as a battlefield. Blood here is not passionate but stagnant, not sacrificial but wasted — the blood of the anonymous dead, meaningless in its abundance.

In **Duellum** (poem XXXXV), the lovers' combat draws real blood — love is figured as a duel where both parties bleed, and the blood is the proof of passion's seriousness.

The most personal note is struck in **L'Héautontimorouménos** (poem LXXXIII):

> C'est tout mon sang, ce poison noir! (072-019)

"This black poison — it is all my blood!" The poet's blood has turned to poison within him. He is both the torturer and the tortured, the one who strikes and the one who bleeds. The blood that was once the medium of passion and creation has become a black venom circulating in a closed circuit of self-destruction.

And in the death-haunted **Le Voyage**:

> Et qui, soûl de son sang, préférerait en somme
> La douleur à la mort et l'enfer au néant! (086-023)

"Drunk on his own blood, would prefer, all things considered, pain to death and hell to nothingness!" — the blood-drunk voyager prefers suffering to extinction. Blood intoxication is a form of life-affirmation, even when the life affirmed is one of torment.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's blood imagery operates across three registers: the **sacrificial** (the artist bleeds for art), the **erotic** (blood as the ultimate intimacy between lovers), and the **historical** (blood as the substance of violence that saturates human time). These registers are not separable in practice — the erotic is always sacrificial, the historical is always eroticized, the sacrificial is always tinged with historical violence.

The wordplay between *sang* (blood) and *sanglot* (sob) — a connection built into the French language — is essential to Baudelaire's poetics of blood. The "ardent sanglot" (006-043) is the cry that carries blood in its very name. To weep is to bleed vocally; to bleed is to weep corporeally. The boundary between bodily fluid and emotional expression dissolves.

This dissolution is the key to Baudelaire's treatment of blood: it is the substance in which body and soul, passion and violence, creation and destruction become indistinguishable. Blood is the common currency of all intense experience — the poet bleeds in ecstasy, in suffering, in love, and in art, and the blood is the same in every case. To live at the intensity Baudelaire demands is to be perpetually hemorrhaging.
