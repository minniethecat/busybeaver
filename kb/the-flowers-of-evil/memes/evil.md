# Evil

## Definition

In Baudelaire's moral universe, **Evil** (*le Mal*) is not a privation but a presence — an active, creative force that opposes and complements the good. The title *Les Fleurs du Mal* (The Flowers of Evil) announces this inversion: flowers, traditionally symbols of natural beauty and innocence, are harvested from evil soil. Baudelaire's evil is aestheticized, made into an object of fascination and a source of artistic power.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

Evil is the very substance from which the collection is woven. In **Au Lecteur** (002), the poet catalogues humanity's vices — "la ménagerie infâme de nos vices" (002-032) — and names Ennui (boredom) as the worst of them all. Evil is universal, intimate, and strangely banal.

Throughout the *Spleen et Idéal* section, evil appears as a necessary condition of beauty. The poem **Le Mal** explicitly confronts the problem of evil: why do the innocent suffer while the guilty prosper? Baudelaire offers no theodicy, only the stark acknowledgment that evil exists and must be metabolized into art.

In **Les Fleurs du Mal** as a whole, evil takes many forms: the cruelty of lovers, the corruption of the city, the violence of nature, and most insidiously, the ennui that drains life of meaning. The poet does not condemn evil from a moral height; he confesses his own complicity. This honesty — the refusal to exempt the self — gives Baudelaire's treatment of evil its unsettling power.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's engagement with evil reflects his Catholic heritage filtered through Romantic Satanism. Unlike earlier poets who treated Satan as a villain, Baudelaire treats him as a tragic figure — the rebel who chose knowledge over obedience. This is the Satan of the *Litanies de Satan*, a poem that scandalized 19th-century readers with its liturgical praise of the devil.

The aestheticization of evil was one of the charges brought against Baudelaire in the 1857 obscenity trial. The prosecution argued that the poems glorified vice; Baudelaire and his defenders insisted that depicting evil was not endorsing it. This tension — between moral condemnation and aesthetic appropriation — remains at the heart of Baudelaire's legacy.
