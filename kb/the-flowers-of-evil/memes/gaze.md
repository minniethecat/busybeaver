# Gaze

## Definition

**The gaze** (*le regard*) in Baudelaire's poetic universe is an instrument of power, not a passive faculty of perception. To look is to act; to be looked at is to be acted upon. Baudelaire's gaze can "aiguiser les frimas" (sharpen the frost, 080-018), "allumer le désir" (ignite desire, 027-010), or render the beholder "soudainement renaître" (suddenly reborn, 084-010). The eyes are not windows but weapons — and the most dangerous gaze of all belongs to Beauty herself, whose regard is "infernal et divin" (infernal and divine, 017-002), mixing poison and paradise in a single glance.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

The gaze is a central dramatic mechanism throughout the collection. Poems turn on moments of looking — the exchange of glances that changes everything for both parties.

The definitive statement comes early, in **Hymne à la Beauté** (poem XXI):

> Ô Beauté? Ton regard, infernal et divin,
> Verse confusément le bienfait et le crime (017-002)

Beauty's gaze is infernal *and* divine — it pours out benefit and crime simultaneously, without distinguishing between them. To be looked at by Beauty is to be blessed and damned in the same instant. This dual nature of the gaze — its capacity to give and to take, to save and to destroy — is the foundation of Baudelaire's visual poetics.

In **A une passante** (poem XCIII), the gaze becomes the medium of an entire erotic drama compressed into a single urban moment. The poet and a woman in mourning exchange glances in a crowded street:

> Un éclair... puis la nuit! — Fugitive beauté
> Dont le regard m'a fait soudainement renaître (084-010)

A lightning flash — then night. The woman's gaze makes the poet "suddenly reborn" — an instantaneous resurrection triggered by a stranger's glance. This is the modern gaze at its purest: fleeting, anonymous, and transformative. The poet will never see her again, but the moment of mutual looking has changed him permanently. The gaze here is an event, not a condition — it happens, it passes, and its effects outlast its duration.

In **Les Petites Vieilles** (poem XCI), the gaze turns cruel and diagnostic:

> Son regard aiguisait les frimas (080-018)

"Her gaze sharpened the frost" — the old woman's look has the power to intensify the cold of the world, to make winter keener by the very act of seeing it. The gaze here is not neutral registration but active transformation: the world becomes sharper, colder, more hostile under the pressure of this looking.

In **Le Jeu** (poem XCVI), the gamblers' gazes are the theater of their souls:

> Ces visages, ces regards, mystérieux et grands (094-003)

Faces and gazes, mysterious and vast — the gambling table is a gallery of looking, where every glance reveals (and conceals) a destiny.

The seductive gaze appears in many forms. In **Le Serpent qui danse** (poem XXVIII):

> Ton regard, comme ta personne,
> Est charmant et dangereux (027-009)

The beloved's gaze, like her person, is charming and dangerous — the two adjectives that most perfectly capture Baudelaire's aesthetic ideal. To charm is to enchant; to be dangerous is to threaten. The gaze that does both is the gaze that matters.

In **Le Chat** (poem XXXIV), the cat's gaze becomes the model of the poetic gaze itself:

> Et, de ses yeux perçants, contemple
> Les astres d'or (034-007)

The cat contemplates the golden stars with piercing eyes — a gaze that combines precision (piercing) with reverie (the stars). This is what Baudelaire asks of the poet: to look at the world with the cat's intensity and the cat's detachment, at once absorbed in vision and sovereign over what is seen.

The dark side of the gaze emerges in **Une Charogne** (poem XXIX), where the decomposing carcass "regardait d'un œil fâché" (looked with an angry eye, 021-034) — the dead looking back at the living, the object of the gaze becoming its subject, reversing the power relation.

And in **L'Irrémédiable** (poem LXXXIV), the gaze turns inward and nihilistic:

> Où la mégère se regarde (072-021)

Where the shrew looks at herself — the self-regarding gaze as mirror of self-loathing.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's treatment of the gaze is profoundly modern in its recognition of the power asymmetry inherent in looking. Before Foucault and before the theory of the male gaze, Baudelaire understood that to look is to possess and to be looked at is to be possessed — but he also understood that the possession can go both ways, that the one who looks can be captured by the one who is looked at.

The gaze in Baudelaire is always **doubled**: it is active and passive, wounding and wounded, dominating and dominated. Beauty's gaze is infernal *and* divine (017-002); the beloved is charming *and* dangerous (027-009); the stranger's glance is a lightning flash *and* then night (084-010). This doubling is not ambiguity but precision — the gaze is not one thing *or* another; it is precisely the simultaneity of opposites.

This makes the gaze a privileged figure for Baudelaire's aesthetic generally. The artist looks at the world, and the world looks back. The poem is the record of that mutual looking — the visual encounter in which subject and object exchange positions and powers. To read Baudelaire is to be looked at by Baudelaire, and the experience is as unsettling as it is beautiful.
