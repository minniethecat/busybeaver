# Volupté

## Definition

**Volupté** in Baudelaire's lexicon resists easy translation. The English "voluptuousness" or "sensual pleasure" captures the bodily dimension but misses the spiritual charge that Baudelaire consistently gives the word. Volupté is pleasure that has been refined, intensified, and — crucially — sanctified. The collection's programmatic pairing of "les saintes voluptés" (the saintly voluptuous pleasures, 003-060) establishes the paradox that governs all of Baudelaire's deployments of the term: the flesh is not the enemy of the spirit but its potential ally. Volupté is the point where sensuality becomes indistinguishable from prayer.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

The theological framing of volupté is established early, in **Bénédiction**: suffering "prépare les forts aux saintes voluptés" (prepares the strong for saintly voluptuous pleasures, 003-060). The adjective *saintes* does real work here — these are not profane pleasures but consecrated ones, earned through suffering and reserved for "les forts" (the strong). Baudelaire reverses the Christian ascetic formula: rather than renouncing pleasure to reach God, suffering itself prepares one for a higher, holier form of pleasure.

The contemplative dimension of volupté appears in **La Vie antérieure** (The Previous Life): "C'est là que j'ai vécu dans les voluptés calmes" (It is there that I lived in calm voluptuous pleasures, 009-009). The adjective *calmes* (calm) distinguishes this volupté from the frantic, addictive pleasure-seeking that Baudelaire elsewhere anatomizes. The volupté of the anterior life is steady, oceanic, inseparable from the vast rhythms of the sea and sky. It is pleasure without anxiety — exactly what the modern subject cannot sustain.

The temporal dimension of volupté is explored in **Le Balcon** (The Balcony): the poet speaks of evenings where the beloved's breath "creuse la volupté" (hollows out voluptuous pleasure, 036-008). The verb *creuser* (to dig, to hollow) makes volupté into something carved into time — not a fleeting sensation but a permanent excavation. Memory deepens pleasure; each return to the balcony scene reopens and enlarges the original volupté.

The intellectual register of volupté is claimed in the opening of **Les Chats** (The Cats): they are "Amis de la science et de la volupté" (Friends of science and of voluptuous pleasure, 048-005). The cats — those emblems of Baudelairean aesthetics — unite what the modern world has separated: knowledge and pleasure. The pairing of *science* and *volupté* is a quiet manifesto. True volupté is not ignorance but a form of knowing; true knowledge is not ascetic but a form of pleasure.

The divine register reaches its peak in **Hymne** (Hymn): "J'aspire, volupté divine!" (I inhale, divine voluptuous pleasure! 061-016). Volupté is no longer merely "saintly" but "divine" — it has completed its ascent from the profane to the sacred. The verb *aspire* (I inhale) makes volupté into breath itself, as necessary and involuntary as respiration. The beloved's presence is the atmosphere the poet breathes; to be separated from her would be to suffocate.

In the condemned poems, volupté takes on a more dangerous valence. **Femmes damnées** (Damned Women) speaks of "De vastes voluptés, changeantes, inconnues" (Vast voluptuous pleasures, shifting, unknown, 108-023) — pleasures that exceed categorization, that transform faster than language can capture them. The adjectives *changeantes* and *inconnues* mark the limit of Baudelaire's sanctification strategy: there are voluptés that resist consecration, that remain wild and unnameable.

The sterile form of volupté is anatomized in **Lesbos**: "Qui font qu'à leurs miroirs, stérile volupté" (Which makes, before their mirrors, a sterile voluptuous pleasure, 113-017). Here volupté turns back on itself — self-regarding, narcissistic, incapable of generating anything beyond its own reflection. The adjective *stérile* (sterile) is Baudelaire's most damning critique: volupté that does not open outward toward the divine is merely a loop, a prison of pleasure.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's rehabilitation of volupté is one of his most significant departures from Catholic orthodoxy. The Church had spent centuries teaching that pleasure — and especially sensual pleasure — was a snare, a temptation, an obstacle to salvation. Baudelaire does not deny the danger; his poetry is full of pleasures that destroy. But he insists on the possibility of a pleasure that is not destructive, that can be "sainte" and even "divine." This is not hedonism — the Baudelairean voluptuary is not a libertine but a kind of mystic, using the body as an instrument of transcendence rather than an end in itself.

The price of this sanctification is suffering. "Les saintes voluptés" are prepared by suffering (003-060); they are not given freely but earned. This is Baudelaire's answer to the moralists: pleasure is dangerous, yes, which is precisely why it must be approached through suffering, which purifies the seeker and distinguishes the strong from the weak. The weak seek pleasure to escape suffering; the strong accept suffering as the price of a higher pleasure.
