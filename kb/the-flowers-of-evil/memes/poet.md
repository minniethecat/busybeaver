# Poet

## Definition

**The poet** (*le poète*) in Baudelaire's work is never merely a writer of verses. He is a metaphysical type: the consecrated being whose divine gift condemns him to exile among ordinary men, the interpreter of the symbolic forest of the world, and the figure who transforms suffering into beauty. Baudelaire inherits the Romantic idea of the poet as prophet and outcast but darkens it — his poet is not Shelley's unacknowledged legislator but a cursed creature, mocked by the world, sustained only by the ironic knowledge that his suffering is the price of vision.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

The figure of the poet appears throughout the collection, from the opening poem **Bénédiction** (poem I) to the final condemnations. The vision is established in the very first line of the dedicatory poem:

> Le Poète apparaît en ce monde ennuyé (003-002)

The poet *appears* — he does not arrive or enter, but manifests, as if from another order of being. And the world he enters is *ennuyé* (bored), the same Ennui that in **Au Lecteur** is described as the century's reigning monster. The poet's relationship to the world is thus defined at the outset as one of fundamental opposition: he is not-of-this-world, and this world is governed by the deadliest of sins.

In **Bénédiction**, the poet's mother curses him at birth, and his wife tears out his heart to feed it to a beast. Yet the poem's final movement transfigures this suffering:

> Le Poète serein lève ses bras pieux, (003-054)

The serene poet raises his pious arms toward heaven, sustained by the knowledge that suffering is the crucible of his art and that a throne awaits him among the "Légions des Saints" (003-058). The poem's closing vision confirms:

> Je sais que vous gardez une place au Poète (003-067)

This is Baudelaire's central claim about the poetic vocation: the poet is consecrated, his suffering is sacred, and his reward is not in this world.

The most famous formulation of the poet's condition comes in **L'Albatros** (poem II). The albatross, "prince des nuées" (prince of the clouds), is majestic in flight but grotesque on the ship's deck where sailors mock and cripple it. The poem's final stanza makes the allegory explicit:

> Le Poète est semblable au prince des nuées (004-013)

The poet, like the albatross, is exiled in the world of ordinary men — his "ailes de géant" (giant wings) prevent him from walking. This image fuses the poet's grandeur and his misery into a single figure: the very attribute that makes him sublime is what makes him ridiculous on the ground.

In **La Beauté** (poem XVII), the poet's relationship to beauty is presented as a form of service. Beauty speaks as a "rêve de pierre" (dream of stone), an idol indifferent to the poet's devotion:

> Est fait pour inspirer au poète un amour (014-003)

Beauty is made to inspire in the poet a love that is eternal and mute — the poet is the worshipper, and beauty the unresponsive goddess. This asymmetry is essential: the poet must love without being loved in return, and this unrequited devotion is itself the source of art.

The nocturnal aspect of the poet appears in **Tristesse de la Lune** (poem LXVII):

> Un poète pieux, ennemi du sommeil, (047-011)

The pious poet, enemy of sleep, catches the moon's tear in his hand. Here the poet is figured as a nocturnal solitary, a watcher while the world sleeps, his piety directed not toward God but toward the moon — a symbol of the imagination itself.

In the darkest register, the poet appears in **Spleen** (poem LXXVI) broken and spectral:

> L'âme d'un vieux poète erre dans la gouttière (054-050)

The soul of an old poet wanders in the gutter, voiceless and ghostly, his creative power reduced to a sad and frightened moan. This is the terminal point of the Baudelairean poet: the Spleen that devours all has devoured even the one who gave it voice.

But in **Le Soleil** (poem LXXXVII), the solar poet fights back:

> Quand, ainsi qu'un poète, il descend dans les villes, (087-019)

The sun, like a poet, descends into the cities and ennobles the most vile things. Here the poet is identified not with the victim of the world but with the transmuting force of light itself — the poet as alchemist, turning the filth of the modern city into gold.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's conception of the poet operates through a series of paradoxes that are never resolved, only deepened. The poet is at once **consecrated and cursed**: blessed by heaven (003-067) but mocked by the world (004-013). He is at once **powerful and powerless**: his wings are giant but they prevent him from walking. He is at once **the servant of beauty and the creator of beauty**: he loves beauty without being loved (014-003), yet his act of loving creates the art that embodies beauty.

This paradoxical structure distinguishes Baudelaire's poet from the Romantic poet-hero. Where Victor Hugo's poet is a prophet leading the people toward progress, Baudelaire's poet is a pariah whose very exclusion from society is the condition of his vision. The poet does not save the world — he suffers it, transfigures it, and offers the transfiguration as art. Baudelaire thus inaugurates the modern figure of the artist as outsider, a figure that would dominate twentieth-century literature from Rimbaud to the Surrealists.

A final and crucial dimension: the poet is always, in Baudelaire, a figure of **the double**. The poet who appears in Bénédiction is both the consecrated child and the cursed adult; the poet of L'Albatros is both the prince of the clouds and the ridiculous cripple on deck. This doubleness is not a flaw in the conception — it is the conception. The poet's greatness and his misery are the same thing seen from different angles, and Baudelaire's art consists in holding both angles in view at once.
