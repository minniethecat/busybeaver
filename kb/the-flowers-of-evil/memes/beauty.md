# Beauty

## Definition

In Baudelaire's poetics, **Beauty** (*la Beauté*) is a complex, paradoxical force — not the classical, harmonious ideal of the Renaissance or the Enlightenment, but a modern beauty born from the tension between the sublime and the sordid, the eternal and the ephemeral. Baudelaire's beauty is inseparable from suffering, evil, and mortality; it draws its power precisely from what traditional aesthetics would exclude.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

Beauty appears throughout *Les Fleurs du Mal* as both theme and personified figure. In the sonnet **La Beauté** (017), Baudelaire gives Beauty a voice — she speaks as a cold, imperious idol, a "monstre énorme, effrayant, ingénu" (017-022), whose gaze is both "infernal et divin" (017-002). This fusion of opposites is the hallmark of Baudelairean beauty.

The concept recurs across the collection. In **Les Phares** (006), Baudelaire praises the artist who can "ramasser la beauté des goujats" — gather beauty from the lowly (006-018). In **Une Charogne** (021), the poet addresses his beloved beside a rotting carcass, telling her that after death, the worms will preserve "la forme et l'essence divine / De mes amours décomposés" — beauty outlasts decay (021-045). In **Hymne à la Beauté** (016), the poet asks whether Beauty comes from heaven or the abyss, answering that it doesn't matter as long as it opens the door to an infinite he loves (016-019).

The poem **Tout Entière** (038) explicitly explores the paradox: the beloved contains "les diverses beautés" (038-002) that adorn her youth, and the poet resolves to paint both her charms and her flaws. Beauty is not perfection but totality.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's conception of beauty marks a decisive break from classical aesthetics. Where tradition sought the beautiful in proportion, harmony, and moral goodness, Baudelaire locates it in contradiction and excess. His beauty is **dualistic**: it originates equally from God and Satan (017-021), from heaven and the abyss (017-001). This duality reflects the broader structure of *Les Fleurs du Mal*, organized around the tension between **Spleen** (melancholy, despair, the pull toward death) and **Idéal** (transcendence, purity, the aspiration toward the infinite).

The poet's insistence that beauty can be extracted from the ugly — from carcasses, prostitutes, and urban squalor — establishes a new aesthetic for modernity. It anticipates Symbolist and Decadent movements and fundamentally reshapes what poetry can address.
