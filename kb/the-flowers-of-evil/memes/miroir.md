# Miroir

## Definition

The **miroir** (mirror) in Baudelaire's poetic system is never a neutral surface. It is an agent of transformation — sometimes revelation, sometimes distortion, always dangerous. Unlike the Romantic mirror, which reflects the soul's nobility or nature's sublimity, Baudelaire's mirrors are "obscurcis et plaintifs" (darkened and plaintive, 003-076) — they do not clarify but trouble; they do not confirm identity but unsettle it. The mirror is the site where the Baudelairean self confronts its own multiplicity and discovers that it is not one but many, not whole but scattered across a thousand reflective fragments.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

The collection's foundational mirror image appears in **Bénédiction**: the poet-martyr's cries "Ne sont que des miroirs obscurcis et plaintifs" (Are only darkened and plaintive mirrors, 003-076). The poet's art does not directly express suffering; it *reflects* it, but through a darkened and lamenting surface. The adjectives are crucial: *obscurcis* (darkened) denies the mirror its traditional clarity; *plaintifs* (plaintive) gives it a voice, a tone of lament. The mirror does not merely show — it mourns.

The cosmic dimension of the mirror is established in **L'Homme et la Mer** (Man and the Sea): "La mer est ton miroir; tu contemples ton âme" (The sea is your mirror; you contemplate your soul, 011-002). The free man contemplates his soul not through introspection but through the external mirror of the ocean. Baudelaire's mirror here mediates between self and world — self-knowledge is not achieved by turning inward but by finding the right external surface in which to see oneself reflected.

The transformative power of the mirror is celebrated in **La Beauté** (Beauty): "De purs miroirs qui font toutes choses plus belles" (Pure mirrors that make all things more beautiful, 014-013). The poet's eyes — those "purs miroirs" — do not merely record the world; they transfigure it. The mirror, when pure, is an aesthetic instrument: it elevates whatever it reflects. This is the mirror in its Baudelairean ideal — not a passive surface but an active transformer.

The self-reflective mirror takes on a sinister cast in **Le Cygne** (The Swan): Andromache is seen bent "Qui vers son miroir penche un lourd amas d'années" (Who toward her mirror leans a heavy mass of years, 076-013). The mirror here is the instrument of temporal reckoning — it shows not the face but the accumulated weight of time. Looking in the mirror is an act of mourning for the self one used to be.

The interpersonal mirror appears in **Confession**: "Dans nos deux esprits, ces miroirs jumeaux" (In our two minds, these twin mirrors, 105-008). The lovers' minds reflect each other in perfect symmetry — but the poem's tone is elegiac, and the next line speaks of "Les miroirs ternis et les flammes mortes" (The tarnished mirrors and the dead flames, 105-014). The twin mirrors have tarnished; the reflection has failed. Baudelaire's mirrors are subject to entropy — they darken, they tarnish, they eventually cease to reflect at all.

The interior mirror reaches its darkest expression in **L'Héautontimorouménos** (The Self-Tormentor): "Je suis le sinistre miroir" (I am the sinister mirror, 072-020). Here the self *becomes* the mirror — but a mirror that reflects only the beloved's cruelty back at her. The speaker is not a person but a reflective surface, and the reflection he offers is *sinistre* — ominous, left-handed, wrong. To become a mirror is to lose one's own substance and become merely the negative of another.

The sterile mirror of self-regard is condemned in **Lesbos**: "Qui font qu'à leurs miroirs, stérile volupté" (Which makes, before their mirrors, a sterile voluptuous pleasure, 113-017). The Lesbians before their mirrors exemplify a closed circuit of desire — reflection without production, pleasure without issue. This is the mirror as prison: one looks and sees only oneself, endlessly.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's mirrors chart a trajectory from revelation to entrapment. The "purs miroirs" of Beauty (014-013) that make all things more beautiful stand at one pole; the "sinistre miroir" of the Self-Tormentor (072-020) and the "stérile volupté" of Lesbos (113-017) stand at the other. The difference is not in the mirror but in what it reflects and how the reflection is used. A mirror that reflects only the self becomes a prison; a mirror that reflects the world becomes a window.

This duality maps onto Baudelaire's larger dialectic of Spleen and Idéal. The mirror under Spleen is "obscurci" and "plaintif" — it darkens, distorts, laments. The mirror under Idéal is "pur" — it clarifies, beautifies, elevates. But the crucial Baudelairean insight is that the same mirror can serve both functions. The poet's art is itself a mirror: it can descend into the darkness of Spleen or ascend toward the clarity of Idéal, and most of Baudelaire's greatest poems do both at once.

The tarnishing of mirrors over time (105-014) introduces a temporal dimension unique to Baudelaire's treatment. Mirrors are not eternal; they age, they cloud, they eventually become opaque. This is the fate of all reflective surfaces — including consciousness itself. The mind that once reflected the world with clarity eventually turns dark and plaintive, showing nothing but its own fatigue.
