## Definition

*Langueur* in Baudelaire's poetic lexicon denotes a state of voluptuous languor, a sweet and painful slowing of the spirit that hovers between pleasure and melancholy. It is the body's translation of Spleen — not the sharp despair of *angoisse* but a softer, more insidious torpor. The term carries strong decadent connotations: the exquisite exhaustion of over-refined sensibilities, the beautiful fatigue of those who have felt too much. Unlike the English "languor," the French *langueur* retains a distinctly erotic charge, suggesting a body made heavy by unsatisfied desire.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

The word *langueur* appears at key moments across *Les Fleurs du Mal*, each time inflected differently. In "Le Vin du Solitaire," the poet evokes "ce silence et cette langueur" (034-038) alongside a "confidence horrible chuchotée / Au confessionnal du cœur." Here langueur is paired with silence and terrible confession — the languorous state is also a state of heightened psychological vulnerability, the moment when hidden truths rise to the surface.

In "Tristesses de la Lune," the moon "parfois sur ce globe, en sa langueur oisive, / Elle laisse filer une larme furtive" (047-009). The moon's languor is idle, dreamy, producing a single tear that a pious poet catches in his hand. This is langueur as cosmic condition — the celestial body itself succumbs to the sweet paralysis that afflicts the Baudelairean soul.

The poem "Chant d'Automne" immerses the reader "en un flot de triste langueur" (067-020), linking langueur explicitly with autumn and sadness. The season of decline and decay is the season of langueur — the world itself grows slow and heavy as it approaches its end.

In "Femmes Damnées," the condemned women display "de douces langueurs et des frissons amers" (098-004), sweet languors paired with bitter shivers. This juxtaposition captures the essence of Baudelairean langueur: it is never pure pleasure but a compound state in which sweetness and bitterness interpenetrate.

"Le Poison" describes perfume that "charge les esprits d'amour et de langueur" (101-012), positioning langueur as an intoxicant — like wine, like opium, like poetry itself, it is one of the altered states through which Baudelairean consciousness navigates its world.

## Analysis

Langueur occupies a critical position in Baudelaire's emotional spectrum, standing between the active pleasures of *volupté* and the paralyzing despair of *spleen*. It is Spleen in its sensual mode — the body's way of experiencing metaphysical exhaustion. This somatic quality is essential: langueur is felt in the flesh, a heaviness of limb and slowing of pulse that externalizes the soul's condition.

The concept also functions as a temporal marker. Langueur is the feeling-tone of decadence, of a civilization (or a soul) that has passed its peak and now drifts in the long afternoon of decline. Baudelaire's langueur anticipates the fin-de-siècle decadent sensibility that he helped create — the exquisite fatigue of cultures grown too old, too refined, too aware of their own mortality.

The link with autumn (*automne*) is particularly significant. Langueur is the autumn of the soul — not the sharp shock of winter (which would be *spleen* in its acute form) but the slow, beautiful decay of a season that is dying into its own richness.
