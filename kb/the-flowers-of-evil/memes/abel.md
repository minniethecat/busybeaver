# Abel

## Identity

**Abel** is the second son of Adam and Eve in the Book of Genesis, a shepherd whose offering was accepted by God while his brother Cain's was rejected. Enraged by this divine preference, Cain murdered Abel — the first death in biblical history. In the Judeo-Christian tradition, Abel is the prototype of the innocent victim, the righteous man whose blood cries out from the ground for justice.

## Connection to Baudelaire

If Cain represents the Baudelairean poet — rebellious, suffering, marked — then Abel represents everything the poet refuses to be: compliant, favored, uncomplicated. Baudelaire's treatment of Abel in *Abel et Caïn* is notably unsympathetic. The race of Abel prospers: "La race d'Abel, dors, bois et mange; / Dieu te sourit complaisamment." The race of Abel sleeps, drinks, and eats; God smiles on them complacently.

This portrait of Abel as the beneficiary of divine favoritism is central to Baudelaire's inversion of conventional morality. In the poet's moral universe, it is better to be Cain — guilty but conscious, damned but aware — than to be Abel, whose innocence is indistinguishable from stupidity. The complacency of the righteous is, for Baudelaire, more repellent than the anguish of the sinner.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

In **Abel et Caïn**, Abel is the unmarked brother — the one who has never been wounded, never been exiled, and therefore never been fully alive. The poem's structure — contrasting the two races stanza by stanza — makes clear that Baudelaire's identification is with Cain. Abel is necessary to the poem as Cain's opposite, but he is not its hero.

## Literary Significance

Baudelaire's Abel demonstrates his characteristic inversion of traditional moral hierarchies. The victim is not necessarily sympathetic; the righteous are not necessarily admirable. By making Abel the representative of a complacent, bourgeois piety, Baudelaire critiques the Christianity of his time — a religion that has lost its existential urgency and become a prop for social respectability.
