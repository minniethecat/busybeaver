# Wine

## Definition

In Baudelaire's poetics, **Wine** (*le Vin*) is the primary agent of **artificial paradise** — a substance that temporarily dissolves the suffocating grip of Spleen and opens a window onto the Idéal. Unlike the Romantics, who often treated wine as a convivial social lubricant, Baudelaire elevates it to a quasi-religious sacrament: wine transfigures the lowest forms of human existence — the ragpicker, the murderer, the solitary — into moments of dignity, transcendence, or terrible lucidity. It is the poor man's opium, the proletarian's mystical ascent, and the poet's metaphor for the alchemy of creation itself.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

Wine occupies an entire section of *Les Fleurs du Mal* — **"Le Vin"** — comprising five poems that trace a descending arc from cosmic generosity to solitary despair. The section functions as a pivot between the urban tableaux and the final meditations on death, suggesting that intoxication is a threshold experience that points toward the ultimate journey.

In **L'Âme du Vin** (The Soul of Wine), wine itself speaks in the first person, declaring its divine lineage and its mission to console human misery:

> Un soir, l'âme du vin chantait dans les bouteilles:
> « — Homme, vers toi je pousse, ô cher déshérité,
> Sous ma prison de verre et mes cires vermeilles,
> Un chant plein de lumière et de fraternité! » (091-001)

The poem's theology is subversive: wine, not God, offers the laborer his reward, transforming "le sordide bouge" (the sordid hovel) into a paradise through the alchemy of intoxication (036-001).

In **Le Vin des Chiffonniers** (The Wine of Ragpickers), Baudelaire paints a heroic portrait of the urban poor who, drunk on cheap wine, become "princes des pavés" — princes of the cobblestones. Wine is the ragpicker's revenge against a society that has discarded him, a ritual of dignity in squalor.

**Le Vin de l'Assassin** descends into darkness: a murderer, fortified by wine, boasts of his crime. Here wine is not consolation but accomplice — it enables the act and numbs the conscience. The poem shocked contemporaries by refusing moral judgment, presenting the murderer's monologue with clinical detachment.

**Le Vin du Solitaire** depicts the solitary drinker for whom wine replaces human connection — "le regard singulier d'une femme galante" and "le regard d'un homme qui souffre et qui attend" are equally unavailable to him. Wine becomes the only companion that does not betray.

Finally, **Le Vin des Amants** closes the cycle with an erotic ascent: lovers, intoxicated by wine, ride together toward a "paradis bleu" — a blue paradise that lies beyond the constraints of ordinary consciousness.

> Je crois boire un vin de Bohême,
> Amer et vainqueur,
> Un ciel liquide qui parsème
> D'étoiles mon cœur! (020-033)

## Analysis

Baudelaire's wine cycle operates as a **sacramental inversion**: wine consecrates not the faithful but the marginal. The ragpicker, the murderer, the solitary, the lover — each receives a form of communion through intoxication that the Church withholds from them. This is Baudelaire's radical democracy of the artificial paradise: transcendence is available to all, regardless of virtue, through the body rather than the soul.

The cycle's structure mirrors Baudelaire's larger poetic arc. The five poems move from cosmic generosity (L'Âme du Vin) through social realism (Le Vin des Chiffonniers) and moral darkness (Le Vin de l'Assassin) to existential solitude (Le Vin du Solitaire), culminating in erotic transcendence (Le Vin des Amants). This is the same trajectory that *Les Fleurs du Mal* itself traces: from the high ideal through the sordid real toward the possibility of escape through death. Wine prefigures death as the ultimate voyage — both are intoxications that dissolve the boundaries of the self.

The comparison of beauty to wine in **Hymne à la Beauté** (017-004) — "On ne peut pour cela te comparer au vin" — is paradoxical: the poet cannot compare beauty to wine, yet the very act of saying so performs the comparison. Beauty and wine share the same ambivalence: both are "infernal et divin" (017-002), both promise paradise and risk damnation.

For Baudelaire, wine is also the **poet's metaphor for poetry itself**. The alchemical transformation of grapes into wine — of raw suffering into intoxicated transcendence — mirrors the poet's transformation of experience into art. When Baudelaire writes that wine "sait revêtir le plus sordide bouge / D'un luxe miraculeux" (036-001), he describes poetry's own power to transfigure the ugly into the beautiful.
