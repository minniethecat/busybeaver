# Art

## Definition

In Baudelaire's poetic vocabulary, **Art** (*l'Art*) carries a double charge. It names the **craft of immortalization** — the artist's power to fix fleeting beauty in lasting form — and simultaneously the **problem of the surface**: every artwork is a mask, a fiction, a "blasphemy" against nature that paradoxically reveals a higher truth. Art is not the opposite of reality but its concentration. For Baudelaire, the true artist does not copy nature but corrects it.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

The problem of art is confronted most directly in **Le Masque** (016), a poem subtitled "Statue allégorique dans le goût de la Renaissance." The speaker approaches a sculpted female figure of perfect beauty, only to discover that beneath the mask of the face lies a second face — one of anguish:

> O blasphème de l'art! ô surprise fatale!
> La femme au corps divin, promettant le bonheur,
> Par le haut se termine en monstre bicéphale! (016-020)

"O blasphemy of art!" — the exclamation is ambivalent. Is art a blasphemy because it deceives, or because it reveals what nature conceals? The two-headed monster (*monstre bicéphale*) is the truth of the statue: beauty above, anguish beneath. Art's blasphemy is its honesty — it shows us what we would rather not see.

In **Le Balcon** (026), art is the technique of resurrection through memory:

> Je sais l'art d'évoquer les minutes heureuses,
> Et revis mon passé blotti dans tes genoux. (026-021)

"I know the art of evoking happy minutes" — the verb *évoquer* is precise: it means to call forth, to summon, as a magician summons spirits. Art is a **necromancy of the past**, a power to make what is dead live again through the incantation of poetic language. The repetition of this line at the poem's close (026-025) transforms it from statement into ritual — art as incantation.

In **Une Charogne** (021), art appears as the explicit rival to death. A carcass rots by the roadside, but the poet, addressing his beloved, declares that her beauty will escape decomposition through verse:

> Alors, ô ma beauté! dites à la vermine
> Qui vous mangera de baisers,
> Que j'ai gardé la forme et l'essence divine
> De mes amours décomposés! (021-045)

The artist is described earlier in the poem as one who works on a "forgotten canvas" (*la toile oubliée*, 021-031) — art as the patient completion of what time has abandoned. The painter's canvas and the poet's page are parallel surfaces on which mortal beauty is preserved against decay.

In **La Beauté** (014), Beauty herself speaks, declaring her nature as an artistic ideal:

> Je suis belle, ô mortels! comme un rêve de pierre,
> Et mon sein, où chacun s'est meurtri tour à tour,
> Est fait pour inspirer au poète un amour
> Eternel et muet ainsi que la matière. (014-001)

Beauty is "a dream of stone" (*un rêve de pierre*) — eternal, mute, material. The poet's love for beauty is modeled on the sculptor's love for marble: it does not seek reciprocity but inspiration. Art is this **one-sided devotion**, the worship of a silent goddess.

The preface itself, written by Henry Frichet, frames Baudelaire as an artist first and a man of commerce second — "érudit et en artiste beaucoup plus qu'en commerçant" (001-004). Even in the paratext, the identity of "artist" is positioned as the higher calling, opposed to the commercial logic of the marketplace.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's conception of art is shaped by his historical position at the intersection of Romanticism and early Modernism. From Romanticism he inherits the cult of the artist as exceptional being, the genius whose suffering authorizes his vision. But Baudelaire adds a crucial twist: the artist is not a transparent medium of nature or emotion but a **technician of the surface**. Art is made, not poured forth — it requires skill, craft, the "art" of evocation that Le Balcon names.

This technical understanding of art is inseparable from Baudelaire's fascination with artificiality. In his critical writings (especially *Le Peintre de la vie moderne*), he argues that beauty is composed of two elements: an eternal, invariable element (the soul of beauty) and a relative, circumstantial element (the fashion, the epoch, the "mask"). Art's task is to distill the eternal from the circumstantial without destroying the latter — for without the circumstantial, beauty becomes abstract and lifeless.

The mask, then, is not a lie but a **necessary fiction**. Le Masque's "blasphemy" is that art tells the truth precisely by not pretending to be nature. The sculpted allegory reveals the anguish beneath beauty because it is a made thing, not a found one. Artifice is the condition of revelation.
