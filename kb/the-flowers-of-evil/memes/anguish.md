# Anguish

## Definition

In Baudelaire's emotional taxonomy, **Anguish** (*l'Angoisse*) is the bodily experience of metaphysical distress — not the abstract sadness of melancholy nor the paralyzed blankness of Ennui, but a **visceral constriction** that seizes the chest, accelerates the heart, and transforms the weight of existence into a physical symptom. If Spleen is the landscape of despair and Ennui its paralysis, Anguish is its somatic scream. It is the point at which psychological suffering becomes indistinguishable from physical pain.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

Anguish appears across *Les Fleurs du Mal* as a sudden, overwhelming presence — less a sustained mood than a **crisis event** within the poem. It is the Baudelairean equivalent of the panic attack: a surge of terror that transforms the world in an instant.

In **L'Irréparable** (039), the poet addresses a spirit "comblé d'angoisse" — filled to the brim with anguish:

> A cet esprit comblé d'angoisse
> Et jusqu'au faîte de son front… (039-012)

The spatial metaphor is precise: anguish fills the self from within, rising like water in a vessel, threatening to overflow. It is an interior flood.

In **La Cloche Fêlée** (061), the cracked bell's voice is compared to a wounded soldier dying beside a lake of blood:

> Ton angoisse, trop lourde, perce
> Et meurt… (061-014)

The anguish is "too heavy" (*trop lourde*) — it is a weight that breaks through, then dies. The poem's sonic imagery (the cracked bell, the dying groan) makes audible what is otherwise interior and silent.

In **Femmes Damnées (Delphine et Hippolyte)** (103), the theme takes an erotic and therapeutic turn. Hippolyte addresses Delphine as a healer:

> Guérisseur familier des angoisses humaines… (103-008)

The lover becomes the "familiar healer of human anguishes" — a doctor of the soul whose touch alleviates the existential symptom. But the poem's tragic arc makes clear that this healing is temporary: the anguish returns, and the healer becomes part of the illness.

In **Lesbos** (073), the island's exiled poetess Alcaeus is described as:

> Et luttant, angoisses funèbres! (073-009)

"Struggling, funeral anguishes!" — the exclamation mark turns the phrase into a cry. Anguish is "funèbre" — funereal, death-colored — because its ultimate object is always mortality.

The most concentrated treatment of Anguish appears in the prose poem **L'Angoisse** from *Le Spleen de Paris* (054 in the verse numbering). Here anguish is personified as a "despotic" force:

> Vaincu, pleure, et l'Angoisse atroce, despotique,
> Sur mon crâne incliné plante son drapeau noir. (054-075)

"Vanquished, weep, and atrocious, despotic Anguish / Plants its black flag on my bowed skull." The military metaphor — the black flag of conquest planted on the defeated skull — makes Anguish not a feeling but an **occupying power**. The self is territory conquered by an alien force.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's Anguish is distinguishable from his other negative affects by its **physical intensity**. Spleen is atmospheric (gray skies, low ceilings); Ennui is behavioral (the yawn, the shrug); Remorse is parasitic (the worm, the archer). Anguish is **circulatory** — it moves through the body, accelerates the pulse, constricts the breath. It is the closest Baudelaire comes to a purely somatic account of psychological suffering.

This somatization is significant because it resists the Cartesian split between mind and body. For Baudelaire, the soul suffers in the flesh — there is no purely spiritual anguish just as there is no purely physical pleasure. This is why his Anguish poems always involve bodily imagery: bowed skulls, heavy weights, cracked bells, struggling breath.

The relationship between Anguish and **Time** is crucial. Anguish is almost always future-oriented — it is the anticipation of suffering rather than its memory. Remorse looks backward; Anguish looks forward. This temporal orientation links it to Kierkegaard's concept of anxiety (*Angest*) as "the dizziness of freedom" — the vertigo experienced when contemplating the open future. Baudelaire would not have read Kierkegaard, but the convergence suggests a shared nineteenth-century discovery: that the modern self is constitutively anxious, not because of specific threats but because of the structure of consciousness itself.
