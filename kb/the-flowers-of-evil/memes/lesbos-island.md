# Lesbos

## Location

**Lesbos** (Greek: Λέσβος) is a Greek island in the northeastern Aegean Sea, the third-largest after Crete and Euboea. In antiquity, it was the home of **Sappho** (c. 630–570 BCE), the lyric poet whose verses celebrated love between women.

For Baudelaire, Lesbos is not a geographical reality but a mythological space — the symbolic homeland of female desire, a "terre des nuits chaudes et langoureuses" (land of hot and languorous nights) where Sappho's legacy licenses an eroticism freed from Christian judgment. It is his most explicit engagement with what his century could only name obliquely.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

Lesbos is the subject of the condemned poem **Lesbos** (chapter 113), one of six pieces banned by the 1857 obscenity trial (001-041). The poem opens with an invocation that establishes the island's dual nature as a paradise of the senses:

> Lesbos, où les baisers languissants ou joyeux,
> Chauds comme les soleils, frais comme les pastèques,
> Font l'ornement des nuits et des jours glorieux (113-002)

The kisses of Lesbos — languorous and joyful, hot as suns, cool as watermelons — erase the boundary between pleasure and devotion. The island is a sacred space where women love women without shame.

The poem's tutelary spirit is Sappho herself, "la mâle Sapho, l'amante et le poëte" — male in creative power, female in erotic object, a figure who transcends gender. The poem's theological audacity peaks in its challenge: "Qui des Dieux osera, Lesbos, être ton juge?" (113-035). No god has standing to condemn Lesbos.

Yet the poem ends in lament: "Et c'est depuis ce temps que Lesbos se lamente" (113-071). Like Eden, Lesbos is a paradise whose beauty is inseparable from its loss. The golden age of Sapphic love is past; the present is a fallen time.

## Symbolism

Lesbos functions as the **counter-space to Paris**. Paris is the city of men, commerce, and judgment. Lesbos is the island of women, pleasure, and freedom from the masculine gaze. The 1857 trial made this opposition literal: Paris judged Lesbos and found it obscene.

The island embodies the Baudelairean dialectic of paradise and loss. Like the Garden of Eden, Lesbos is fallen — the poem celebrates and mourns simultaneously. For Baudelaire, the most beautiful paradises are always already lost.

The poem's treatment of female homosexuality is doubly legible. Baudelaire celebrates Lesbos while also exoticizing it as a male fantasy. The poem is subversive in its refusal of moral judgment and appropriative in its ventriloquism of desire Baudelaire could not directly experience. This doubleness marks the limits of even Baudelaire's radicalism.
