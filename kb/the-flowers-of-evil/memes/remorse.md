# Remorse

## Definition

In Baudelaire's moral psychology, **Remorse** (*le Remords*) is not the healthy contrition that leads to repentance and amendment of life — it is a **parasitic consciousness** that feeds on the past without ever redeeming it. It personifies itself: Remorse is a predator ("aux traits empoisonnés" — with poisoned features), a worm that gnaws, an intimate torturer more reliable than any external judge. Baudelaire's Remorse is the dark twin of memory: memory preserves the pleasure of sin; Remorse preserves its poison.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

Remorse enters the collection in the opening poem, **Au Lecteur**, as one of the invisible labors of the soul:

> Et nous alimentons nos aimables remords,
> Comme les mendiants nourrissent leur vermine. (002-003)

The adjective *aimables* (lovable, kindly) is characteristically Baudelairean in its perversity. We "nourish" our remorses — not in the sense of cultivating a healthy conscience, but as beggars feed their lice. Remorse is a parasite we have grown attached to. The simile is deliberately disgusting: what should lead to moral improvement becomes instead a form of self-degradation.

The fullest treatment of Remorse appears in **L'Irréparable** (039), where the concept is personified with ferocious intensity:

> Pouvons-nous étouffer le vieux, le long Remords,
> Qui vit, s'agite et se tortille,
> Et se nourrit de nous comme le ver des morts,
> Comme la chenille du chêne?
> Pouvons-nous étouffer l'implacable Remords? (039-001)

Remorse is "old" (*vieux*) and "long" (*long*) — it outlives the sin that produced it. It "lives, stirs, and writhes" (*vit, s'agite et se tortille*), feeding on us as the death-worm feeds on corpses. The rhetorical question — "Can we stifle implacable Remorse?" — is answered by its very form: the repetition, the accumulation of similes, the helplessness of the interrogative. The answer is no.

Later in the same poem:

> Connais-tu le Remords, aux traits empoisonnés,
> Celui dont notre cœur sert de cible et de proie? (039-033)

Remorse has "poisoned features" (*traits empoisonnés*), and the heart is its "target and prey" (*cible et de proie*). The metaphor shifts from the worm to the archer, but the violence remains: Remorse is an attacker and the self its victim.

Other poems extend the imagery. In **Le Flacon** (024), the memory of a lost love is like a perfume bottle that, once opened, releases a haunting — and "le ver rongera ta peau comme un remords" (024-014): the worm will gnaw your skin like a remorse. In **L'Héautontimorouménos** (052), the self-torturer rushes toward his ruin "sans remords" (052-012) — without remorse — which is itself presented as a sign of his damnation. In the poem's logic, the absence of remorse is the ultimate remorse.

In **Le Poison** (071), the poet warns that art itself — the "musique" that "cueille des remords dans la fête servile" (071-007) — harvests remorses from the slavish feast. Beauty is not innocent; it produces its own form of guilt.

The theological dimension appears in **La Voix** (092):

> Dieu, touché de remords, avait fait le sommeil… (092-031)

God himself, "touched by remorse," created sleep. This extraordinary image suggests that remorse is not merely human but cosmic — that even the Creator feels something akin to guilt for the world He has made. Sleep, the nightly oblivion, is God's gift of temporary absolution.

And in **Le Vin des Amants** (098):

> O Bacchus, endormeur des remords anciens! (098-016)

Wine is the "putter-to-sleep of ancient remorses" — intoxication as anesthesia for conscience. The remorses are "anciens" — they predate the present moment, they are the accumulated guilt of a lifetime.

The final appearance, in **Le Voyage** (114), closes the arc. The damned souls are:

> Et, pleine de remords et d'horreur, et livide… (114-071)

Full of remorse and horror, and livid. Remorse is the final companion — the one feeling that survives the voyage to the end.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's Remorse is a **secularized version of the Catholic sacrament of penance** — but with a crucial difference: there is no absolution. In the Catholic framework, remorse (imperfect contrition) or true contrition leads to confession, which leads to forgiveness. Baudelaire's Remorse leads nowhere. It is a closed circuit of pain: the sinner remembers the sin, suffers the remorse, and continues to sin. The suffering is real, but it is sterile.

This sterility is what distinguishes Baudelaire's Remorse from, say, Dostoevsky's. In *Crime and Punishment*, Raskolnikov's remorse leads (eventually) to confession and redemption. Baudelaire's speakers never confess — or if they do, as in "Au Lecteur," the confession is a trap that implicates the reader rather than absolving the speaker.

The **personification** of Remorse is central to Baudelaire's method. By making Remorse a predator — a worm, an archer, a torturer — he externalizes an internal feeling, giving it the objectivity and visual intensity required by his poetics. This is the same technique he applies to Spleen, Ennui, and Anguish: psychological states become characters in a drama, and the self becomes the stage on which they fight.
