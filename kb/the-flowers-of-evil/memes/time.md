# Time

## Definition

In the cosmology of *Les Fleurs du Mal*, **Time** (*Le Temps*) is not a neutral measure but a malevolent agent — an insatiable devourer that is the ultimate source of all human suffering. Baudelaire's Time has a body: it eats, it grips, it grinds. It is the force that separates the self from its own past (see [[Memory]]) and guarantees the triumph of Spleen over Idéal. In the famous line from **L'Ennemi** (010), Time is personified as a predator: "Le Temps mange la vie" — Time eats life.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

No concept is more pervasive in Baudelaire's poetry than Time, and it appears in almost every register — from mythic personification to intimate bodily sensation.

In **L'Ennemi** (010), Time is the enemy of the soul's garden. The poet's youth was a "ténébreux orage" (dark storm) that ravaged the garden; now autumn has come, and the poet must prepare for new growth. But Time's hunger is bottomless:

> Et qui sait si les fleurs nouvelles que je rêve
> Trouveront dans ce sol lavé comme une grève
> Le mystique aliment qui ferait leur vigueur? (010-009)

The "mystic nourishment" (*mystique aliment*) that the new flowers need is precisely what Time has consumed. The poem's anguish is not that the garden was destroyed, but that it may never bloom again — Time eats not just the past but the future.

In **Spleen LXXVIII** (022), Time is figured as a spider spreading its web in the brain:

> Et de longs corbillards, sans tambours ni musique,
> Défilent lentement dans mon âme… (022-017)

But earlier in the same poem, the image is more intimate — the slow unspooling of existence:

> Tant l'écheveau du temps lentement se dévide! (022-014)

Time as a thread unwinding: the image captures the paradox of Baudelairean temporality — at once excruciatingly slow (the boredom of Spleen) and terrifyingly fast (the approach of death).

In **L'Horloge** (060), the clock speaks as a living god — "sinistre, effrayant, impassible" (sinister, frightening, impassive) — and delivers the definitive Baudelairean pronouncement on time:

> Souviens-toi que le Temps est un joueur avide
> Qui gagne sans tricher, à tout coup! c'est la loi. (060-019)

Time as an avid gambler who never loses: the image fuses the mechanical (the clock's inexorable hands) with the mythic (the god who plays with mortal lives). And the clock's final word is "Meurs, vieux lâche! Il est trop tard!" — Die, old coward! It is too late! (060-033)

The preface **Au Lecteur** (002) places Time in the catalog of cosmic forces aligned against the human soul:

> Mais parmi les chacals, les panthères, les lices… (002-029)

The key to Baudelaire's Time is its **passivity**. It does not strike like a murderer; it simply waits. And in waiting, it wins. The yawn of Ennui and the tick of the clock are the same gesture seen from different angles.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's treatment of Time marks a decisive break with both the classical and Romantic traditions. Classical Time (Horace's *carpe diem*, Ronsard's rose) urged enjoyment *because* time passes — the rose must be gathered today. Romantic Time (Lamartine's *Le Lac*) lamented time's passage but found consolation in memory and nature's permanence. Baudelaire's Time offers **no consolation at all**.

The distinction is theological. In the classical and Romantic views, some version of permanence remains — nature renews itself, memory preserves the beloved, God's eternity stands outside time. In Baudelaire, Christianity is a residual presence, but its promise of eternal life is absent. Time is not a passage toward God but a passage toward nothing. This is the specifically modern terror of Baudelaire's Time: it is secularized entropy, direction without destination.

The aesthetic response to Time is therefore crucial. If Time cannot be escaped, it can at least be **transmuted**. The poem itself — the fixed and beautiful artifact — is a victory over time, however temporary. Baudelaire's sonnets, with their rigorous formal discipline, are time-stopping machines: they freeze the flux of experience into a structure that resists dissolution. Art is the one thing Time cannot digest.

> Imposer tous les temps et tous les univers. (003-068)

This line from **Bénédiction** (003) — "to impose upon all times and all universes" — defines the artist's ambition as a conquest of temporality itself.
