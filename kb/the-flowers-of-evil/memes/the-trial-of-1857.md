# The Trial of 1857

## Overview

On **20 August 1857**, three months after the publication of *Les Fleurs du Mal*, Charles Baudelaire and his publisher Auguste Poulet-Malassis stood before the Sixth Correctional Chamber of the Tribunal de la Seine, charged with "offense to public morals and good manners" (*outrage à la morale publique et aux bonnes mœurs*, 001-040).

The prosecution, led by Ernest Pinard (who had prosecuted Flaubert's *Madame Bovary* earlier the same year), focused on thirteen poems. The court ultimately condemned six: **Lesbos**, **Femmes Damnées**, **Le Léthé**, **À Celle Qui Est Trop Gaie**, **Les Bijoux**, and **Les Métamorphoses du Vampire**. Baudelaire was fined 300 francs; Poulet-Malassis was fined 100 francs. The six poems were ordered suppressed and would not be legally republished in France until 1949.

## Significance for Baudelaire

The trial was the defining public event of Baudelaire's life. It transformed him from a relatively obscure poet into a cause célèbre — a martyr of artistic freedom whose persecution confirmed his self-image as the marked, condemned outsider.

Baudelaire was devastated by the verdict. He had hoped for acquittal, and the fine — though modest — added to his already crushing debts. More painfully, the condemnation seemed to validate his deepest fear: that the world would reject his vision, that the poet was indeed cursed to be misunderstood.

Yet the trial also served Baudelaire's legend. It gave *Les Fleurs du Mal* the aura of forbidden fruit, the glamour of the illicit. Readers who might have ignored a book of poems were drawn by the scandal. Baudelaire's defenders — including Sainte-Beuve, Barbey d'Aurevilly, and the critic Charles Asselineau — rallied publicly, and the trial became a landmark in the history of literary censorship.

The condemned poems themselves acquired a second life underground. They circulated in samizdat editions, were reprinted abroad, and became the most famous — if least legally accessible — poems in the collection. The trial ensured that Baudelaire's most transgressive work would never be forgotten.

## Background

The 1850s were a period of heightened moral surveillance in France. The regime of Napoleon III, authoritarian and Catholic, regarded literature as a potential threat to public order. The same prosecutor, Ernest Pinard, had pursued Flaubert's *Madame Bovary* earlier in 1857, though Flaubert was acquitted.

Baudelaire's case was different. *Madame Bovary* could be defended as a moral novel that punished its adulterous heroine. *Les Fleurs du Mal* offered no such consolation. Its poems did not condemn the sins they described; they aestheticized them. Baudelaire's famous defense — that he depicted evil to make it repellent, that the collection was a "theology of sin" — was only partially accepted. The court found that six poems crossed the line from depiction to celebration.

The prosecution also objected to the collection's religious content. **Les Litanies de Satan**, with its liturgical praise of the devil, was seen as blasphemous. **Le Reniement de Saint Pierre** (The Denial of Saint Peter) seemed to mock Christian redemption. These poems were not condemned, but they shaped the court's perception of Baudelaire as a poet hostile to religion.

## Aftermath

The trial had lasting consequences for French literature. It established a legal precedent for the prosecution of poetry — a genre previously considered too elite and obscure to threaten public morals. It also galvanized the literary avant-garde's self-understanding as a community of outsiders persecuted by the state.

For Baudelaire personally, the trial deepened his bitterness and financial precarity. He attempted to rehabilitate his reputation through essays and art criticism, but the condemnation followed him. When he died in 1867, the six condemned poems remained banned.

In 1949, the Cour de Cassation — France's highest court — posthumously overturned the 1857 verdict. The judgment declared that Baudelaire's poems, "far from offending public morals, are the work of a poet who sought only to describe the human heart in all its misery and its grandeur."
