# Chat

## Definition

The **chat** (cat) in Baudelaire's poetry is far more than a domestic animal. It is a mediator between worlds — between the human and the animal, the familiar and the mysterious, the sensual and the cerebral. Baudelaire's cats are emblems of the poetic condition itself: solitary, fastidious, capable of sudden violence and prolonged stillness, at home in interiors but dreaming of the infinite. The cat is the creature in which Baudelaire most clearly sees his own reflection — "Chat séraphique, chat étrange" (Seraphic cat, strange cat, 037-022), a being that belongs simultaneously to heaven and to the uncanny.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

The cat's definitive presentation occupies two sonnets — **Le Chat** (The Cat, poem XXXIV) and **Les Chats** (The Cats, poem LXVI) — along with a third prose poem. Together they form a triptych of feline contemplation unmatched in French poetry.

In the first **Le Chat** (XXXIV), the cat is an intimate presence on the speaker's breast: "Viens, mon beau chat, sur mon cœur amoureux" (Come, my beautiful cat, upon my amorous heart, 025-001). The cat's physical proximity is charged with erotic suggestion — the "cœur amoureux" (amorous heart) makes the cat the beloved's surrogate, or perhaps the beloved herself in feline form. The cat's gaze is described as "un froid venin" (a cold venom, 025-008) — dangerous, intoxicating, potentially lethal. To be looked at by the Baudelairean cat is to be poisoned, but the poison is also a drug.

The second **Le Chat** (LI) deepens the metaphysics. The cat's voice is compared to a musical instrument — "Que ta voix, chat mystérieux, / Chat séraphique, chat étrange" (That your voice, mysterious cat, / Seraphic cat, strange cat, 037-021–022). The triadic address — mystérieux, séraphique, étrange — maps the cat's ontological coordinates: it belongs to the unknown, to the angelic, and to the uncanny simultaneously. The cat is a chord struck across three orders of being.

The speaker's attraction to the cat is explicitly compared to the attraction of the abyss: "Quand mes yeux, vers ce chat que j'aime / Tirés comme par un aimant" (When my eyes, toward this cat I love / Drawn as by a magnet, 037-033–034). The cat exerts a gravitational pull — not chosen but submitted to, as irresistible as the pull of the void. Loving the cat is not a decision but a yielding to a force beyond the will.

**Les Chats** (LXVI) expands from the individual cat to the feline species as a philosophical ideal. The poem opens with a declaration: "Les chats puissants et doux, orgueil de la maison" (Powerful and gentle cats, pride of the house, 048-001). The pairing of *puissants* and *doux* — powerful and gentle — establishes the cat's dual nature. It is a creature of contradictions held in perfect balance.

The cats are described as "Amis de la science et de la volupté" (Friends of science and of voluptuous pleasure, 048-005). This is the poem's philosophical core: the cat unites what human civilization has separated. Science and volupté, knowledge and pleasure, the cerebral and the sensual — the cat embodies their reconciliation. The cat is Baudelaire's answer to the Cartesian division of mind and body: a creature that thinks with its senses and feels with its mind.

The final stanza grants the cats an almost cosmic stature. They "prennent en songeant les nobles attitudes / Des grands sphinx allongés au fond des solitudes" (take in dreaming the noble attitudes / Of great sphinxes stretched out in the depths of solitudes, 048-009–010). The domestic cat, dozing by the fire, is in truth a sphinx — an ancient riddle, a guardian of secrets, a being that belongs to the desert of eternity rather than the parlor of Paris. The transformation from household pet to cosmic emblem happens in the space of a single Alexandrine couplet.

The prose poem **Le Chat** extends the feline meditation into a different register. Here the cat's body becomes the site of a hallucinatory vision: the speaker sees in its fur the image of a woman — "Je vois ma femme en esprit" (I see my wife in spirit). The cat dissolves into the beloved, the beloved into the cat; the boundary between species, between lover and loved, collapses into a single reverie.

Beyond the dedicated cat poems, feline imagery infiltrates the collection at unexpected moments. In **Spleen** (LXXVIII), the speaker's cat "sur le carreau cherchant une litière" (on the floor seeking a litter, 054-005) is a detail of domestic desolation — the cat as witness to human misery, indifferent and therefore honest. In **Le Crépuscule du Soir** (Evening Twilight), cats pass furtively through the gathering darkness (034-010) — "Des chats passaient furtivement" (Cats passed furtively) — as emblems of the twilight world itself, beings that move between day and night as they move between the known and the unknown.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's cats are the creatures in which his aesthetics finds its fullest animal embodiment. The cat is solitary, fastidious, self-contained — all qualities Baudelaire valued in the artist. The cat refuses to perform; it is what it is, without apology or explanation. This is the Baudelairean ideal of beauty: something that exists for itself, indifferent to the spectator, all the more fascinating for its indifference.

The erotic dimension of Baudelaire's cats cannot be overstated. The cat is repeatedly associated with the beloved — its fur, its gaze, its voice, its magnetic pull all map onto the experience of desire. But the cat is also a safer object of desire than the human beloved. It can be loved without the risk of betrayal; its indifference is natural rather than cruel. The cat allows Baudelaire to explore the dynamics of desire — possession, obsession, intoxication — without the moral complications of human relationships.

The philosophical dimension, crystallized in "Amis de la science et de la volupté" (048-005), makes the cat an emblem of Baudelaire's entire poetic project. The goal of *Les Fleurs du Mal* is precisely to unite knowledge and pleasure — to extract beauty from evil, meaning from suffering, the ideal from the spleen. The cat, simply by being a cat, achieves what the poet must struggle to produce: a life in which thought and sensation are not at war.
