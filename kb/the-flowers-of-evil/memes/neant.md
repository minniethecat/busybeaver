# Néant

## Definition

In Baudelaire's poetic universe, ***le Néant*** (Nothingness, the Void) is a presence, not an absence. It is the **seductive face of annihilation** — not the blank zero of non-existence but a "madly adorned" (*follement attifé*) negation that exerts an aesthetic pull on the suffering consciousness. Where Spleen is the heavy, suffocating experience of meaninglessness, Néant is its light, almost alluring counterpart: the temptation to cease, to dissolve, to be released from the burden of being. It is Baudelaire's word for the **aesthetic attraction of death**.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

The Néant enters *Les Fleurs du Mal* as a jealous rival to the suffering self. In **Le Gouffre** (069), the abyss that haunts Pascal also haunts Baudelaire — but with a crucial difference:

> Pascal avait son gouffre, avec lui se mouvant.
> — Hélas! tout est abîme, — action, désir, rêve,
> Parole! et sur mon poil qui tout droit se relève
> Mainte fois de la peur je sens passer le vent.
> 
> J'ai peur du sommeil comme on a peur d'un grand trou,
> Tout plein de vague horreur, menant on ne sait où;
> Je ne vois qu'infini par toutes les fenêtres,
> 
> Et mon esprit, toujours du vertige hanté,
> Jalouse du néant l'insensibilité. (069-010)

"Jealous of nothingness's insensibility" — the verb *jalouser* (to envy, to be jealous of) is the poem's psychological key. The speaking self does not fear the void; it **envies** it. Nothingness feels nothing, and this immunity to suffering makes it an object of desire. The "great hole" (*grand trou*) of sleep terrifies because it resembles death, but the Néant that lies beyond death is coveted precisely for its numbness.

In **Le Jeu** (086), the gamblers' desperate hope is contrasted with the speaker's darker preference:

> La douleur à la mort et l'enfer au néant! (086-024)

"Pain to death and hell to nothingness!" — he would condemn pain to death and hell itself to annihilation. The line is a **double curse**: not only should pain die, but the very place of punishment (hell) should be reduced to nothing. The Néant is here a weapon, a wished-for obliteration of cosmic justice itself.

The most arresting treatment of Néant appears in **Danse Macabre** (087), where Death — figured as a skeletal coquette — is described with an oxymoron of seductive emptiness:

> — O charme d'un néant follement attifé! (087-016)

"O charm of a madly adorned nothingness!" The phrase *néant follement attifé* (nothingness madly decked out) is quintessential Baudelaire. Nothingness is not bare — it is **dressed up**, adorned, coiffed, made alluring by the very artifice of Death's finery. The skeleton wears jewelry; the void wears a gown. This is the aestheticization of annihilation at its most extreme: if nothingness can be charming, then the distinction between being and non-being collapses into style.

The lovers who watch the danse macabre cannot understand this charm:

> Qui ne comprennent pas, amants ivres de chair,
> L'élégance sans nom de l'humaine armature. (087-017)

"Lovers drunk on flesh" — those who are too attached to matter, to the warm body — cannot appreciate the "nameless elegance of the human armature" (the skeleton). The Néant's charm is visible only to those who have detached themselves from the flesh's claims.

Finally, in **Femmes Damnées** (114), the drive toward nothingness takes a directly erotic form. Delphine, addressing Hippolyte, declares:

> Je veux m'anéantir dans ta gorge profonde,
> Et trouver sur ton sein la fraîcheur de la tombe! (114-083)

"I want to annihilate myself in your deep throat, / And find on your breast the coolness of the tomb!" The verb *s'anéantir* — to annihilate oneself, to become nothing — is the active form of the Néant. The lover's body becomes the gateway to non-being; erotic consummation and death are fused. The "coolness of the tomb" (*fraîcheur de la tombe*) sought on the beloved's breast is the sensual equivalent of the "insensibility" envied in Le Gouffre.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's Néant occupies a position in his psychological landscape that has no exact parallel in earlier French poetry. The Romantic tradition (Lamartine, Hugo) had cultivated melancholy, the *mal du siècle*, the vague yearning for an infinite that the finite world cannot satisfy. But Baudelaire's Néant is something more specific: it is the **positive desire for non-being**, the wish not to transcend the world but to exit it entirely.

This desire has three distinct tonalities in *Les Fleurs du Mal*:

1. **Envy** (Le Gouffre): the Néant is coveted for its immunity to pain. Nothingness cannot suffer; therefore it is superior to consciousness.

2. **Aesthetic seduction** (Danse Macabre): the Néant is *follement attifé* — madly adorned, dressed in the elegance of death. It attracts not despite being nothing but because its nothingness is styled, composed, made into a work of art.

3. **Erotic fusion** (Femmes Damnées): the Néant is the endpoint of desire, the "little death" that sexual climax prefigures. To love completely is to wish for annihilation in the other.

These three tonalities correspond to three philosophical registers: the existential (envy of insensibility), the aesthetic (the charm of adorned emptiness), and the erotic (annihilation as consummation). Together they form a complete psychology of the death-drive avant la lettre — a drive that Freud would theorize half a century later in *Beyond the Pleasure Principle*.

The link between Néant and the broader project of *Les Fleurs du Mal* is structural. The collection's final section, *La Mort*, offers death as the only remaining voyage when all others — art, love, wine, revolt — have failed. The Néant is death's anteroom: the imagined state that makes the actual event of dying thinkable, even desirable. Baudelaire does not celebrate the Néant as a metaphysical truth (he is no nihilist) but renders it as a **psychological temptation** — the voice that whispers, in the midst of Spleen's suffocation, that there is a door, and beyond it, nothing, and that nothing would be a relief.
