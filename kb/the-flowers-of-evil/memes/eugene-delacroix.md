# Eugène Delacroix

## Identity

**Eugène Delacroix** (1798–1863) was the leading painter of the French Romantic movement, known for his dramatic use of color, dynamic compositions, and scenes drawn from literature, history, and exotic cultures. His masterpieces include *La Liberté guidant le peuple* (1830) and *La Mort de Sardanapale* (1827). Baudelaire considered Delacroix the greatest living artist and wrote extensively about his work in his art criticism.

## Connection to Baudelaire

Baudelaire's admiration for Delacroix was profound and sustained. In his *Salon* reviews, Baudelaire championed Delacroix as the exemplar of modern painting — an artist who combined technical mastery with psychological depth, who painted not just appearances but the inner life of his subjects. Delacroix, for Baudelaire, was the painterly equivalent of the poet he himself aspired to be.

Delacroix's treatment of literary subjects — scenes from Dante, Shakespeare, Goethe, and Byron — demonstrated the affinity between painting and poetry that Baudelaire celebrated. The two men knew each other, and Delacroix's death in 1863 was a profound loss for Baudelaire.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

Delacroix is invoked in **Les Phares** (006), Baudelaire's poem about great artists. Delacroix appears alongside Rubens, Leonardo, Rembrandt, Michelangelo, and others as one of the "phares" (beacons) who illuminate humanity. Baudelaire praises Delacroix as "un lac de sang hanté des mauvais anges" — a lake of blood haunted by evil angels — capturing the violent, passionate quality of his painting.

## Literary Significance

Baudelaire's writings on Delacroix are among the founding texts of modern art criticism. By treating painting as an art of psychological depth — not mere representation but expression of the invisible — Baudelaire articulated principles that would influence Symbolist and modernist aesthetics.
