# Music

## Definition

In Baudelaire's aesthetics, **Music** (*la Musique*) occupies a privileged position among the arts. It is not merely sound organized in time but the **acoustic manifestation of the theory of correspondances** — the means by which the invisible harmony of the universe becomes perceptible to the human ear. Music transports, dissolves boundaries, and, like perfume, operates directly on the soul without passing through rational understanding. For Baudelaire, the poet-musician relationship is not metaphor but method: poetry should aspire to the condition of music.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

The most concentrated treatment of music appears in the sonnet **La Musique** (050), which opens with a declaration of music's oceanic power:

> La musique souvent me prend comme une mer!
> Vers ma pâle étoile,
> Sous un plafond de brume ou dans un vaste éther,
> Je mets à la voile; (050-001)

Music "takes" the speaker — the verb *prendre* suggests seizure, capture, a loss of voluntary control — and the simile "like a sea" (*comme une mer*) establishes the central Baudelairean equation: music = sea = voyage = transcendence. The poet sets sail not on water but on sound.

In **La Chevelure** (018), the beloved's hair becomes a vessel for musical reverie, transporting the lover across sensory boundaries:

> Comme d'autres esprits voguent sur la musique,
> Le mien, ô mon amour! nage sur ton parfum. (018-009)

The phrase "other spirits sail upon music" implies that music is the normal, expected medium of spiritual transport — those who voyage on perfume (like the speaker) are the exception. Music is the **baseline metaphor for all other forms of sensory ecstasy**.

In **Tout Entière** (033), music, perfume, and mystical transformation converge in the beloved's body:

> O métamorphose mystique
> De tous mes sens fondus en un!
> Son haleine fait la musique,
> Comme sa voix fait le parfum! (033-021)

Here synesthesia reaches its most condensed expression: breath makes music, voice makes perfume — the senses are no longer distinct channels but a single fused organ of perception. The "mystical metamorphosis" (*métamorphose mystique*) is the Baudelairean ideal: not to perceive separately but to experience the world as a unified sensory field.

In **La Vie Antérieure** (009), music belongs to a lost paradise of the senses:

> Les tout-puissants accords de leur riche musique
> Aux couleurs du couchant. (009-007)

"All-powerful chords of their rich music" — the music of the anterior life is "all-powerful" (*tout-puissants*), a force that once ordered existence. The past tense of the poem frames this musical plenitude as something lost, a paradise from which the poet has fallen into the fragmentary world of modernity.

Music's absence is equally significant. In **Spleen** (054), the funeral cortège moves without it:

> Et de longs corbillards, sans tambours ni musique,
> Lentement dans mon âme; l'Espoir, (054-073)

"Without drums or music" — the funeral procession of hope passes in silence. If music is transcendence and transport, its absence is the very definition of Spleen: the soul immobilized, stripped of its acoustic wings.

In **Le Vin du Solitaire** (094), music returns but in a nervous, enervating key:

> Les sons d'une musique énervante et câline, (094-007)

This "enervating and caressing music" is the voice of wine, a false transcendence — seductive but ultimately part of the solitary drinker's self-deception.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's treatment of music operates on three interlocking levels. First, **music as transport**: across multiple poems, music is consistently figured as a medium of voyage — a sea to sail upon, a vehicle for spiritual departure. This aligns with the Romantic tradition (Hoffmann, in particular, whom Baudelaire admired) that placed music at the summit of the arts for its capacity to suggest the infinite.

Second, **music as synesthetic model**. In the theory of *correspondances*, the senses echo one another — perfume, color, and sound answer each other across an invisible web of analogies. Baudelaire repeatedly links music with perfume (La Chevelure, Tout Entière), creating a sensory circuit in which hearing and smelling become interchangeable modes of spiritual perception. Music is not one sense among others; it is the paradigm case of how a material sensation can open onto the immaterial.

Third, **music as poetic ideal**. When Baudelaire writes "Je sais l'art d'évoquer les minutes heureuses" (Le Balcon, 026-021), the "art" he claims is not merely memory but a specifically musical power of evocation — to conjure the past as music conjures emotion, without argument, by pure presence. Poetry's aspiration to the condition of music is a recurring Baudelairean theme: the poem should work on the reader as music works on the listener, bypassing intellection.

The silence of Spleen — the "sans tambours ni musique" of the funeral cortège — is the photographic negative of this ideal. Spleen is not the opposite of music but its withdrawal, the world drained of acoustic correspondance. In this sense, the entire *Fleurs du Mal* project can be read as an attempt to restore music to a world that has gone silent.
