# Charles Baudelaire

## Identity

**Charles Pierre Baudelaire** (9 April 1821 – 31 August 1867) was a French poet, essayist, art critic, and translator. His single collection, *Les Fleurs du Mal* (The Flowers of Evil, 1857), stands as one of the most influential works in Western poetry.

The book redefined what lyric poetry could accommodate: urban squalor, erotic frankness, metaphysical despair, and the pursuit of beauty through evil.

Baudelaire translated Edgar Allan Poe into French, introducing Poe to a European readership. He wrote some of the nineteenth century's most penetrating art criticism (*Salons*, *Le Peintre de la Vie Moderne*). His prose poems, collected posthumously as *Le Spleen de Paris*, extended his revolution of poetic form.

## Connection to Baudelaire

As the author of *Les Fleurs du Mal*, Baudelaire is both creator and implicit central character. The "je" that speaks throughout the poems is a constructed persona, drawing on Baudelaire's experiences with documentary intensity.

These include: his traumatic childhood after his father's death and his mother's remarriage; his life as a dandy and debt-ridden bohemian in Paris; his struggles with syphilis, opium, and poverty.

His lifelong obsession was the irreconcilable tension between spirituality and sensuality, between the aspiration toward the Ideal and the gravitational pull of Spleen.

Baudelaire's aesthetic theory — most fully articulated in *Le Peintre de la Vie Moderne* (1863) — informs every poem. He defined modernity as "the transient, the fleeting, the contingent; the one half of art, whose other half is the eternal and the immutable."

The poet's task was to extract the eternal from the ephemeral, to find beauty not in classical perfection but in the modern, urban, and even sordid. This aesthetic program is the theoretical foundation of *Les Fleurs du Mal*.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

The poet's biography and aesthetic are woven into the collection's very fabric.

The **Préface** (001), by Henry Frichet, provides the external narrative: Baudelaire's friendship with the publisher Poulet-Malassis, the search for the title, the 1857 publication, and the immediate prosecution (001-040).

Frichet describes Baudelaire as a poet whose "amour" of beauty was inseparable from suffering, whose genius lay in transforming the sordid into the sublime (001-080).

The poem **Bénédiction** (003) opens the collection with a thinly veiled autobiographical myth: the poet is born into a world that hates him, cursed by his own mother who "crispe ses poings vers Dieu" (003-004). He is marked from birth — like Cain — as one who suffers and sees differently. Yet he is also blessed, because suffering is the condition of vision:

> « Ah! que n'ai-je mis bas tout un nœud de vipères,
> Plutôt que de nourrir cette dérision!
> Maudite soit la nuit aux plaisirs éphémères
> Où mon ventre a conçu mon expiation! » (003-001)

The poet's voice throughout the collection moves between exaltation and despair, between the "ciel" of the Ideal and the "gouffre" of Spleen. This oscillation is not a flaw but the structural principle of Baudelaire's art — he is the poet of duality, of the irreconcilable.

## Biography

Baudelaire's life was shaped by rupture and rebellion.

Born in Paris in 1821 to a sixty-two-year-old father (a former priest turned civil servant) and a twenty-eight-year-old mother, he lost his father at age six. His mother's swift remarriage to Lieutenant Colonel Jacques Aupick — a rigid, ambitious military officer — traumatized the young Charles and established the pattern of Oedipal resentment that would fuel his poetry's darker energies.

After being expelled from the Lycée Louis-le-Grand for insubordination, Baudelaire briefly studied law but soon devoted himself to literature and the Parisian demimonde.

His stepfather, hoping to reform him, sent him on a voyage to India in 1841; Baudelaire disembarked at Mauritius and returned to Paris after ten months, but the journey's exotic impressions — tropical seas, "malabaraises" women, the scent of distant lands — would suffuse his poetry for the rest of his life.

Upon reaching his majority, Baudelaire inherited a considerable fortune from his father, but his extravagant spending (on art, clothes, wine, opium, and a mistress — the actress Jeanne Duval) alarmed his family.

In 1844, they placed his remaining inheritance under a legal guardianship (*conseil judiciaire*), reducing him to a meager allowance for the rest of his life. This financial humiliation deepened his identification with the marginalized and the damned.

The watershed year was 1857. *Les Fleurs du Mal* appeared in June to immediate scandal.

By August, Baudelaire and Poulet-Malassis were prosecuted for "offense to public and religious morals." Six poems were condemned and Baudelaire was fined 300 francs. The verdict devastated him, though he continued to write.

Expanded editions of *Les Fleurs du Mal* appeared in 1861 and posthumously in 1868. His prose poems (*Le Spleen de Paris*) accumulated throughout the 1860s.

In 1864, fleeing creditors and depression, Baudelaire moved to Brussels, where his health rapidly declined. A stroke in March 1866 left him aphasic and paralyzed. He was brought back to Paris, where he died in his mother's arms on 31 August 1867, aged forty-six. His last months were a silence — the poet whose entire life had been language could no longer speak.

## Literary Significance

Baudelaire is arguably the single most consequential poet of the nineteenth century. His influence radiates outward across every major literary movement that followed:

| Movement | Key Figures | Baudelaire's Influence |
|----------|------------|----------------------|
| **Symbolism** | Rimbaud, Verlaine, Mallarmé | The poem as musical suggestion, not statement; synesthesia |
| **Decadence** | Huysmans, Wilde, D'Annunzio | Beauty in decay, artificiality as spiritual discipline |
| **Modernism** | T.S. Eliot, Ezra Pound | The urban landscape as psychic terrain; fragmentation |
| **Critical Theory** | Walter Benjamin | *The Arcades Project* took Baudelaire as modernity's central figure |

T.S. Eliot's *The Waste Land* (1922) is unimaginable without Baudelaire's urban infernos. Eliot declared that Baudelaire "invented a new kind of romanticism."

Walter Benjamin's *Arcades Project* made Baudelaire the key to understanding nineteenth-century Paris. For Benjamin, Baudelaire was the first poet to register the shock experience of modern urban life.

Within the French tradition, Baudelaire opened the path that led from Romanticism's nature-worship to Symbolism's interior landscapes.

Rimbaud's famous letter on the "dérèglement de tous les sens" (derangement of all the senses) is a direct extension of Baudelaire's pursuit of artificial paradises. Mallarmé's hermetic difficulty continues Baudelaire's conviction that poetry should not describe but evoke — that the music of verse matters more than its discursive content.
