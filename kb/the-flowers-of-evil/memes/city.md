# City

## Definition

For Baudelaire, the **City** (*la Ville*) is the fundamental setting of modern existence — not a backdrop but an active force that shapes consciousness. Paris, after Haussmann's demolitions and reconstructions of the 1850s, was in a state of perpetual transformation: medieval quarters vanished overnight, broad boulevards replaced labyrinthine alleys, and the urban subject found himself estranged in his own birthplace. Baudelaire's city is the "fourmillante cité" (swarming city) of **Les Sept Vieillards** (071) — thronged with strangers, saturated with stimuli, and haunted by the rubble of the past.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

The city receives its most concentrated treatment in the section **Tableaux Parisiens** (Parisian Scenes), added in the 1861 edition. But urban imagery pervades the entire collection — the city is the atmosphere Baudelaire breathes, and its rhythms inflect even the most intimate lyrics.

The opening of **Les Sept Vieillards** (071) establishes the city as a scene of spectral multiplication:

> Fourmillante cité, cité pleine de rêves,
> Où le spectre en plein jour raccroche le passant! (071-001)

The swarming city is a city full of dreams — but *rêves* in Baudelaire's lexicon is ambiguous, meaning both dreams and specters. The city produces hallucinations; its density of human bodies generates unreal apparitions. The old man who multiplies into seven identical copies is the city's own nightmare logic made visible.

In **Le Cygne** (078), the city is seen through the lens of exile and transformation. The poem opens with an address to Andromache — the mythic model of exile — and immediately pivots to the new Paris:

> Le vieux Paris n'est plus (la forme d'une ville
> Change plus vite, hélas! que le cœur d'un mortel) (078-007)

The parenthetical is one of the most famous lines in Baudelaire: the form of a city changes faster than the human heart. The self, in Baudelaire's diagnosis, is too slow for modernity. The city's metamorphosis outpaces the psyche's ability to adapt, producing a structural nostalgia — a longing for what was here yesterday but is gone today.

In **Le Crépuscule du Soir** (075), the city appears as an organism with its own rhythms. Evening falls and the city transforms:

> La Prostitution s'allume dans les rues… (075-015)

The city at night is the city of illicit commerce, of bodies for sale, of "les hôpitaux" (hospitals) and "les grabats" (pallet beds). And yet in the same poem, the poet hears a different music:

> Cependant des démons malsains dans l'atmosphère
> S'éveillent lourdement, comme des gens d'affaire… (075-011)

The city is a double entity: the site of suffering and the site of vision, the place where the poet is most alienated and most at home.

## Analysis

Baudelaire is the first great poet of the modern city — before Eliot's London, before Rilke's Paris, before Crane's New York, there is Baudelaire's Paris. His innovation is to treat the city not as a collection of buildings but as a **psychological condition**. The city is inside the subject as much as the subject is inside the city.

Three features of Baudelaire's urban imagination deserve emphasis:

**1. The crowd as solitude.** The city multiplies human presences while abolishing human connection. The flâneur — Baudelaire's ideal urban observer — moves through the crowd without belonging to it, seeing all but touched by none. In **Les Foules** (the prose poem), the poet claims the "incomparable privilège" (incomparable privilege) of being at once himself and another — but this privilege is purchased at the cost of an intimate life.

**2. The city as ruin in progress.** Haussmannization meant that the Paris Baudelaire knew as a child was being demolished as he wrote. **Le Cygne** is the definitive poem of this experience: the swan escaped from its cage, dragging its white plumage on the dry pavement, is the image of all exiles — and the old Paris itself is in exile from the present. Baudelaire's urban poetry is an archaeology of the present, digging through the layers of what was just lost.

**3. The city as aesthetic material.** The city supplies Baudelaire with his distinctive subject matter — not the nightingale or the Grecian urn but the blind man, the ragpicker, the prostitute, the seven identical old men. This is the "héroïsme de la vie moderne" (heroism of modern life) that Baudelaire called for in his *Salon of 1846*: the conviction that the modern city contains a beauty equal to any classical subject, if only the artist can see it.

The city is the condition of possibility for Baudelaire's poetry. Without Paris — its crowds, its transformations, its estrangements — there would be no *Fleurs du Mal*.
