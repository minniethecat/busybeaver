# Paradise

## Definition

In Baudelaire's work, **Paradise** (*le Paradis*) operates on two distinct but interpenetrating levels. The first is the **lost Paradise** of Christian theology — the Eden from which humanity was expelled, the state of innocence that haunts the fallen world as a memory without a referent. The second is the **artificial Paradise** (*paradis artificiel*) — the temporary, chemically-induced transcendence achieved through wine, opium, or hashish, which offers a simulation of spiritual ascent while carrying within it the bitter knowledge of its own fraudulence. Between these two paradises — the one irretrievably lost, the other always already dissolving — Baudelaire situates the modern hunger for transcendence.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

The lost Paradise appears with surprising frequency in *Les Fleurs du Mal*, often in contexts that emphasize its inaccessibility. In **Le Balcon** (030), the poet addresses his beloved from the remembered space of their intimacy:

> Mère des souvenirs, maîtresse des maîtresses… (030-001)

The balcony becomes a surrogate Eden — a remembered space of plenitude that memory can revisit but time has sealed off.

In **Moesta et Errabunda** (056), the poet asks a devastating question:

> Mais le vert paradis des amours enfantines,
> L'innocent paradis, plein de plaisirs furtifs,
> Est-il déjà plus loin que l'Inde et que la Chine? (056)

The "green paradise of childhood loves" — "innocent paradise, full of furtive pleasures" — is rhetorically farther than India or China. The geographical names evoke the exotic distance that fascinated Baudelaire, but the real distance is temporal: childhood is a country from which we are all permanently exiled. The poem answers its own question with the cruel image of paradise dissolving:

> Et le paradis en enfer… (056-010)

Paradise becomes hell — the green landscape of innocence transforms into the urban inferno of the adult world.

The artificial paradise receives its most concentrated treatment in the poem **Le Vin des Amants** (098), where wine offers:

> Vers le paradis de mes rêves! (095-014)

The "paradise of my dreams" — already the phrase acknowledges its irreality. It is a paradise not of reality but of *rêves*, of dreams, accessible only through the altered consciousness that intoxication provides.

In **Le Voyage** (108), Paradise appears in its most degraded form — as a spectacle consumed by tourists:

> …le nez en l'air, de brillants paradis… (108-046)

These "brilliant paradises" are the exotic destinations marketed to travelers, commodified transcendence, paradise reduced to postcard. The poem's savage irony targets the modern delusion that changing geographical location can provide the novelty that the soul craves.

Yet Paradise also appears in a positive register. In **L'Invitation au Voyage** (095), the poet imagines a perfect domestic interior:

> Là, tout n'est qu'ordre et beauté,
> Luxe, calme et volupté. (095)

This is Baudelaire's achieved Paradise — not a supernatural realm but an aesthetic one, created through art, love, and the ordering of sensory experience. The incantatory refrain suggests that such a Paradise is possible, but only within the space of the poem itself.

The theological dimension appears in **Femmes Damnées (Delphine et Hippolyte)** (103):

> Du Paradis terrestre a chassés Dieu le Père… (103-044)

God the Father, she says, has chased them from the earthly Paradise — but what has replaced it? The poem's answer is ambiguous: the fallen world is both a punishment and, in its very fallenness, the source of a darker beauty.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's Paradise is structured by a fundamental **temporal paradox**: it can only be experienced as already lost. Childhood is Paradise, but one doesn't know it until one has left it. Intoxication provides Paradise, but the moment of peak intoxication is also the moment one knows it will fade. The poem itself creates Paradise, but only for the duration of reading. Paradise, in Baudelaire's universe, is always in the past tense or the subjunctive — never in the indicative present.

This structure explains why Baudelaire is drawn to the **artificial** paradise. If Eden cannot be regained, it can at least be counterfeited — and Baudelaire, the great theorist of modernity, understands that the counterfeit can be more interesting than the original. The artificial paradise is not simply a fake; it is a *conscious* fake, a simulation that knows it is a simulation. This self-knowledge gives it a poignancy that the original, innocent Paradise lacked.

The political dimension is worth noting. Baudelaire's artificial paradises are **democratically available**: wine costs a few sous, and the ragpicker can achieve a drunken dignity that matches the king's. This is a radical proposition in a class-stratified society: transcendence, chemically mediated, is available to all. The Church may withhold Paradise from the sinner, but the wine shop does not.

And yet, the trajectory of *Les Fleurs du Mal* as a whole suggests that no Paradise — natural or artificial — is sufficient. The collection ends with **Le Voyage**, whose final cry is not for Paradise but for the Unknown: "Au fond de l'Inconnu pour trouver du nouveau!" (108-146). Paradise, even the artificial one, is still a known quantity. What the Baudelairean soul ultimately craves is not happiness but novelty — and novelty requires the abyss, not Eden.
