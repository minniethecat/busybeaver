# Mystique

## Definition

In Baudelaire's poetry, the adjective ***mystique*** (mystical) and its cognates designate a **mode of perception and connection** that transcends ordinary sensory boundaries. It is not the mysticism of the cloister or the ecstasy of the saint but a specifically poetic mysticism — the experience of synesthetic fusion in which sight, sound, smell, and touch converge, and the barrier between subject and object momentarily dissolves. The *mystique* is Baudelaire's word for the **unity that lies beneath the diversity of appearances**, the hidden harmony that the theory of *correspondances* postulates and that poetry exists to reveal.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

The word *mystique* appears across the collection as a modifier — always attached to a noun, always transforming that noun from the mundane to the charged. Its distribution reveals the range of experiences Baudelaire considers susceptible to mystical transfiguration.

In **Bénédiction** (003), the poet's crown is woven from mystical suffering:

> Et qu'il faut pour tresser ma couronne mystique
> Imposer tous les temps et tous les univers. (003-067)

The "mystical crown" (*couronne mystique*) is the poet's martyrdom — his suffering is not merely personal but cosmic, "imposed on all times and all universes." The mystical here elevates the individual pain to universal significance.

In **L'Ennemi** (008), the mystical appears as the **food** that the spirit needs to survive the ravages of time:

> Le mystique aliment qui ferait leur vigueur? (008-011)

"The mystical nourishment that would give them vigor" — the flowers of poetry require supernatural sustenance. Time (*Le Temps*) "eats life" (008-012), and only mystical aliment can counter its consumption. The metaphor is sacramental: poetry feeds on what is not of this world.

In **La Vie Antérieure** (009), the mystical characterizes a lost mode of perception:

> Mêlaient d'une façon solennelle et mystique
> Les tout-puissants accords de leur riche musique
> Aux couleurs du couchant. (009-006)

They "mingled in a solemn and mystical way / the all-powerful chords of their rich music / with the colors of the sunset." The *façon solennelle et mystique* is the perceptual mode of the anterior life — a mode in which sound and color are not separate senses but aspects of a single experience. The mystical is **synesthetic by nature**.

In **Un Fantôme** (028), the mystical appears as a **chain** linking the lover to the absent beloved across the abyss of death:

> Et par un fraternel et mystique chaînon
> Reste comme je suis pendu. (028-064)

"By a fraternal and mystical link / I remain as I am, hanging." The "mystical chain" (*mystique chaînon*) is the bond that survives physical separation — love as a metaphysical tether that death cannot sever. The word *fraternel* (fraternal) gives this mystical connection a tenderness absent from the more theatrical agonies of the collection.

The most compressed expression of Baudelaire's mysticism occurs in **Tout Entière** (033):

> O métamorphose mystique
> De tous mes sens fondus en un! (033-021)

"O mystical metamorphosis / Of all my senses melted into one!" The two lines are a complete manifesto. *Métamorphose mystique*: the transformation is not incremental but total. *Tous mes sens fondus en un*: the five senses do not cooperate — they **fuse**, lose their separate identities, become a single organ of perception. This is the Baudelairean sublime: not the terror of the infinite but the ecstasy of sensory unification.

In **Les Chats** (048), the mystical characterizes the feline gaze:

> Etoilent vaguement leurs prunelles mystiques. (048-014)

"Their mystical pupils are vaguely starred" — the cats' eyes contain and project stars, miniature constellations. The mystical gaze is not empty but **star-filled**: it contains a cosmos, a depth that the ordinary eye lacks.

In **La Prière d'un Païen** (057), the mystical meets wine in a deliberately pagan context:

> Dans le vin informe et mystique, (057-013)

"In the formless and mystical wine" — the adjective *informe* (formless) is key. Wine at this stage of fermentation is both without fixed shape and without fixed meaning; it is a **liquid potential** that the mystical charges with significance. The pagan prayer consecrates what the Christian would condemn.

In **La Mort des Pauvres** (106), death itself is a mystical granary:

> C'est la gloire des Dieux, c'est le grenier mystique,
> C'est la bourse du pauvre et sa patrie antique, (106-012)

"It is the glory of the Gods, it is the mystical granary, / It is the purse of the poor and his ancient homeland." Death — the poor man's only wealth — is figured as a *grenier mystique*, a storehouse of the beyond. The mystical here grants dignity to the most abject: the poor who have nothing in life possess everything in death.

Finally, in **Femmes Damnées** (114), the mystical characterizes the impossible union of the damned lovers:

> Celui qui veut unir dans un accord mystique
> L'ombre avec la chaleur, la nuit avec le jour! (114-065)

"He who would unite in a mystical accord / Shadow with warmth, night with day!" The "mystical accord" (*accord mystique*) is a union of contraries — the alchemical marriage of opposites that defines the Baudelairean project as a whole. *Les Fleurs du Mal* is, from one angle, nothing other than this attempted accord: the extraction of beauty from evil, the grafting of the ideal onto the sordid.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's *mystique* occupies a carefully delimited semantic space. He almost never uses the noun form (*le mystique* as a person); the word is overwhelmingly adjectival. This is significant: Baudelaire is not interested in mystics (people who claim unmediated access to the divine) but in **mystical qualities** — experiences, perceptions, connections that bear the charge of the sacred without belonging to any specific religious tradition.

This adjectival mysticism is structurally identical to the theory of *correspondances*. When the senses "melt into one" (033-021), they are doing what correspondances do at the cosmic scale: revealing the hidden unity beneath apparent diversity. The *mystique* is correspondances experienced from the inside — not a doctrine but a **perceptual event**.

The range of nouns modified by *mystique* maps the scope of Baudelaire's spiritual ambition: crown, nourishment, way, chain, metamorphosis, pupils, wine, granary, accord. These are not conventionally religious objects. Baudelaire's mysticism is **dispersed across the mundane** — it can alight on a cat's eye, a glass of wine, or the prospect of death with equal intensity. This is what distinguishes Baudelaire from both the Catholic revival of his era (Chateaubriand, the later Verlaine) and the secularizing tendencies of naturalism. He wants the sacred without the church, the mystical without the mystic.
