# God

## Definition

**God** (*Dieu*) occupies an ambiguous position in Baudelaire's poetry: He is named often, invoked sincerely, yet never securely possessed as a source of consolation. Baudelaire's God is the God of post-Revolutionary Catholicism — present in the cultural imagination, embedded in the language, but no longer the unquestioned center of the cosmos. The poet's relationship to God is one of desperate hope rather than confident faith.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

God appears at critical moments throughout the collection. In **Bénédiction** (003), the poet's mother "crispe ses poings vers Dieu, qui la prend en pitié" (003-004) — clenches her fists toward God in a gesture of accusation. The poet himself later cries: "Soyez béni, mon Dieu, qui donnez la souffrance" (003-057) — blessed be God who gives suffering as a divine remedy.

In **Hymne à la Beauté** (017), the poet explicitly relativizes God by placing Him alongside Satan: "De Satan ou de Dieu, qu'importe? Ange ou Sirène" (017-025) — whether Beauty comes from Satan or God, what does it matter? This is perhaps Baudelaire's most characteristic theological statement: the origin of beauty is less important than its effect on the soul.

In **Franciscae Meae Laudes** (059), a Latin hymn parodied, God is declared the creator of the beloved's beauty: "Et naturellement, Dieu m'a faite très belle" (059-006) — God made me very beautiful, naturally. The word "naturellement" carries ironic weight, as if beauty required no supernatural explanation.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's theology is fundamentally dualistic and indebted to the Gnostic tradition. Good and evil, God and Satan, are not arranged in a hierarchy where God is supreme. Rather, they are equal and opposing forces, and the human soul is their battleground. This is the "deux postulations simultanées" that Baudelaire described in his journals.

This theological position has important consequences for Baudelaire's aesthetics. If God and Satan are equal, then the artist can draw creative power from either source. Beauty can be satanic, and evil can be sublime. This is the logic behind Baudelaire's most scandalous poems and the reason his collection was condemned by the French courts in 1857.

The poet's relationship to God is one of negotiation rather than submission. He blesses God for suffering, not despite it. He treats God as a cosmic artist whose methods the human artist can appropriate. In this, Baudelaire anticipates Nietzsche's proclamation of God's death while still inhabiting the structures of Christian feeling.
