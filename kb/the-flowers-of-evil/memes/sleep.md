# Sleep

## Definition

In Baudelaire's poetic universe, **Sleep** (*le Sommeil*) is charged with profound ambivalence. It is at once the most accessible form of **oblivion** — the daily reprieve from consciousness that Spleen makes unbearable — and an object of **terror** in its own right, for sleep is the abyss in miniature, a nightly rehearsal for death. Baudelaire inherits the classical association of sleep and death as brothers (Hypnos and Thanatos in Greek myth), but he intensifies it: in his poetry, sleep is not death's brother but death's understudy, and each night's surrender to unconsciousness is a small capitulation to the void.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

Sleep appears across the full span of the collection, from the desperate longing for its oblivion to the terrified recoil from its approach.

In **De Profundis Clamavi** (022), the poet addresses his beloved from the depths of despair, envying those who can achieve a brute, animal sleep:

> …qui peuvent se plonger dans un sommeil stupide… (022-013)

The adjective *stupide* is crucial: it is not peaceful sleep the poet envies but stupid, mindless sleep — the sleep of creatures without consciousness. The poet's tragedy is that he thinks too much to sleep well; his consciousness pursues him even into the bed.

In **Le Guignon** (032), ordinary souls are described as able to:

> Et sommeiller longtemps à l'ombre de vos cils… (032-014)

To "slumber long in the shadow of your lashes" — sleep as erotic refuge, the lover's body as a shelter from the world.

However, in **Le Gouffre** (069), Baudelaire explicitly names his terror of sleep:

> J'ai peur du sommeil comme on a peur d'un grand trou,
> Tout plein de vague horreur, menant on ne sait où… (069-009)

"I fear sleep as one fears a great hole, / All full of vague horror, leading one knows not where." This is the most explicit formulation of sleep-as-abyss. The "great hole" is the gouffre — the same word Baudelaire uses throughout the collection for the metaphysical void. Sleep is terrifying precisely because it is a fall into ungraspable darkness, a journey without destination. The phrase "on ne sait où" (one knows not where) captures the essential horror: sleep dissolves the self into a space without coordinates.

In **La Voix** (092), Baudelaire offers a startling theological origin for sleep:

> Dieu, touché de remords, avait fait le sommeil… (092-031)

"God, touched by remorse, created sleep." Sleep is not a natural rhythm but a divine gift born of guilt — God's compensation to humanity for the burden of consciousness He imposed. This is a strangely tender image in Baudelaire's usually harsh theological register. Sleep becomes a daily act of divine mercy.

The collection's ambivalence about sleep reaches its peak in **Le Voyage** (106):

> Le sommeil et le don des rêves extatiques… (106-010)

"Sleep and the gift of ecstatic dreams" — here sleep is positive, the source of dreams that transcend ordinary consciousness. But this positivity is set against the terror of "Le Gouffre." Baudelaire refuses to resolve the contradiction: sleep is both gift and terror, both refuge and rehearsal for death.

In **Le Crépuscule du Soir** (055), the poet addresses his own heart in one of the collection's most despairing imperatives:

> Résigne-toi, mon cœur; dors ton sommeil de brute. (055-005)

"Resign yourself, my heart; sleep your brute's sleep." The command to sleep "like a brute" echoes the earlier envy of animal unconsciousness. The verb *résigne-toi* (resign yourself) reveals the pre-condition of sleep: one must first give up, surrender the will, accept the diminishment of consciousness.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's treatment of sleep is structured by a **paradox of control**. Sleep is the one form of oblivion that is both necessary and involuntary — one must sleep, and one cannot will oneself to sleep. This makes sleep a microcosm of the human condition as Baudelaire understands it: we are subject to forces beyond our control, yet we must somehow consent to them. The imperative "dors ton sommeil de brute" is both a command and a description of what cannot be commanded — sleep comes or it does not, and the will is irrelevant.

The **theological dimension** of Baudelaire's sleep (God creating sleep out of remorse) is unusual in his corpus and deserves attention. It suggests that even in Baudelaire's largely desacralized universe, sleep retains a trace of the sacred — it is the one experience that humbles the proudest consciousness, the one daily surrender that even the most defiant sinner must make. Sleep is, in this sense, Baudelaire's **last remaining ritual**: a compulsory act of trust that the self will be returned to itself in the morning.

The link between sleep and **the Abyss** is Baudelaire's most terrifying insight. If sleep is a "great hole leading one knows not where," then the distinction between sleep and death becomes merely quantitative — sleep is a shorter fall into the same void. This is why Baudelaire both craves and fears sleep: it is the closest available approximation to the death he desires (as escape from Spleen) and the death he fears (as annihilation of the self).
