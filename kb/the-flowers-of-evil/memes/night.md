# Night

## Definition

For Baudelaire, **Night** (*la Nuit*) is more than a time of day — it is a symbolic condition, a state of consciousness, and a privileged moment for poetic vision. When the sun sets and the mundane world of commerce and respectability withdraws, the poet's true life begins. Night is the hour of dreams, of desire, of the stars that offer a glimpse of the infinite, and of the urban spectacles — prostitutes, criminals, artists — that constitute Baudelaire's Paris.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

Night is the setting of many of the collection's most powerful poems. In **Le Crépuscule du Soir** (Evening Twilight), the coming of night is described as a release: the honest man goes to sleep, while "la Prostitution s'allume dans les rues" — prostitution lights up in the streets. Night liberates what day represses.

In **Recueillement**, night is personified as a consoler: "Sois sage, ô ma Douleur, et tiens-toi plus tranquille" — be calm, my Pain, night is descending. The night brings not terror but a strange peace, a suspension of the demands of daytime consciousness.

In **Les Ténèbres**, darkness becomes the medium of vision. Baudelaire, like the mystics, sees more clearly in the dark: when the external world dims, the internal world brightens. This nocturnal epistemology — knowing through darkness rather than light — is one of Baudelaire's most original contributions to the lyric tradition.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's night poetry draws on the Romantic nocturne tradition (Young, Novalis, the Gothic) while giving it a distinctively urban inflection. His nights are not the moonlit forests of Romantic convention but the gaslit streets of modern Paris. The city at night becomes a symbolic forest — "une forêt de symboles" — where every figure (the passing woman, the ragpicker, the assassin) carries allegorical weight.

The temporal structure of *Les Fleurs du Mal* follows a diurnal rhythm: the day belongs to spleen (boredom, duty, the weight of time), while the night opens toward the ideal. This rhythm structures the poet's emotional life and his creative practice.
