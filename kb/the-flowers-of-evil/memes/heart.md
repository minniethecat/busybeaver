# Heart

## Definition

In Baudelaire's poetic anatomy, the **Heart** (*le Cœur*) is the seat of emotion, the organ through which the poet experiences both the ecstasy of love and the agony of spleen. Unlike the soul, which aspires upward toward the ideal, the heart is bound to the body and its vulnerabilities. It is wounded, scarred, and exhausted, yet it is also the source of the poet's deepest sensitivity. The heart, for Baudelaire, is what makes the poet capable of being hurt — and thus capable of creating art.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

The heart appears throughout the collection as a symbol of vulnerability and depth. In **Bénédiction** (003), the poet's wife tears out his heart — a gesture of intimate violence that sets the tone for love in the collection. The heart is what can be taken, broken, or devoured by the beloved.

In **Causerie** (Conversation), the poet describes his heart as a "palais flétri" — a withered palace — suggesting emotional exhaustion. In **Le Guignon** (Bad Luck), the heart labors under the weight of artistic ambition: "Pour soulever un poids si lourd, / Sisyphe, il faudrait ton courage!" — to lift such a heavy weight, Sisyphus, your courage would be needed.

In **L'Irréparable**, the heart is the site of remorse: "Pouvons-nous étouffer le vieux, le long Remords?" — can we suffocate the old, long Remorse that gnaws the heart? For Baudelaire, the heart never forgets; it accumulates every wound and every guilt.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's treatment of the heart reflects both Romantic emotionalism and a more modern, physiological awareness. The heart is not a sentimental metaphor but a real organ, subject to the same decay and exhaustion as the rest of the body. This concreteness distinguishes Baudelaire from his Romantic predecessors: when he speaks of the heart, he means both the symbolic seat of emotion and the physical muscle that can fail.

The wounded heart is also the source of Baudelaire's characteristic tone — the blend of tenderness and bitterness that defines his love poetry. The poet loves, is hurt, and transforms the hurt into art. The heart is both the instrument of suffering and the instrument of creation.
