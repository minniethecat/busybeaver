# Skeleton

## Definition

**The skeleton** (*le squelette*) is Baudelaire's ultimate emblem of mortality — the human form reduced to its bare scaffolding, the body without flesh, the self without the illusion of permanence. In the *danse macabre* tradition inherited from the Middle Ages, the skeleton is an equalizer: king and peasant, beauty and hag, all are reduced to the same grinning framework. Baudelaire takes this medieval inheritance and makes it modern — his skeleton is not a lesson in humility but an encounter with the uncanny, at once repellent and perversely attractive, the object of a taste that dares not speak its name.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

The skeleton haunts the collection from its early poems to the final condemned pieces. Its appearances trace an arc from the grotesque to the erotic, revealing a fascination that deepens rather than resolves.

In **Une Charogne** (poem XXIX), the skeleton appears as the endpoint of decay. As the speaker and his beloved contemplate a rotting carcass, the poem imagines:

> Epiant le moment de reprendre au squelette (021-035)

Nature waits to reclaim from the skeleton the fragments she has lent — the flesh was always on loan, and the skeleton is the permanent residue, the debt that remains when all the soft tissue is returned. This is the biological skeleton: the truth of the body that decomposition reveals and that no amount of beauty can disguise.

In **Spleen** (poem LXXVI), the skeleton appears in a very different register — not as the residue of decay but as a figure of the macabre imagination:

> Pour tirer un souris de ce jeune squelette. (054-050)

The line belongs to the famous "Quand le ciel bas et lourd" Spleen. In the speaker's nightmare vision, a "peuple muet d'infâmes araignées" (silent people of infamous spiders) comes to weave its webs inside his skull — and among the horrors, a "jeune squelette" (young skeleton) is made to smile. The skeleton here is not a natural fact but a hallucination, the mind's own death projected outward in the grip of Spleen.

The skeleton achieves its fullest and most complex treatment in **Danse Macabre** (poem XCVII), dedicated to the sculptor Ernest Christophe. The poem addresses a statue of Death as a coquettish skeleton:

> Tu réponds, grand squelette, à mon goût le plus cher! (087-020)

"You answer, great skeleton, to my dearest taste!" The line is a confession: the speaker's deepest taste is for death itself, for the skeleton beneath the flesh. The poem elaborates this attraction in terms that fuse the aesthetic and the erotic — the skeleton is compared to a coquette at a ball, her bony structure adorned with elegance, her emptiness dressed in the trappings of fashion. Baudelaire inverts the *contemptus mundi* of the medieval dance of death: instead of teaching contempt for the world, the skeleton becomes an object of worldly desire.

The poem's most devastating line comes near the end:

> Pourtant, qui n'a serré dans ses bras un squelette, (087-041)

"Yet who has not pressed a skeleton in their arms?" The question is rhetorical and universal. Everyone who has loved a body has loved a skeleton, whether they knew it or not — the beloved's flesh is a temporary garment, and what we embrace is always, in the final analysis, the bones beneath. The skeleton is not an exception to erotic experience but its hidden truth.

The Danse Macabre section continues with a direct address to the reader:

> Vous sentez tous la mort! O squelettes musqués, (087-048)

"You all smell of death! O musky skeletons." The living are merely "musky skeletons" — skeletons drenched in perfume, attempting to disguise their mortality with scent. The musk is a metaphysical lie, and Baudelaire delights in stripping it away.

In the condemned poem **Les Métamorphoses du Vampire** (poem CXV), the skeleton appears in its most degraded form:

> Tremblaient confusément des débris de squelette, (115-025)

The vampire's metamorphosis reduces the seductive woman to trembling fragments of skeleton — the erotic spell broken, the truth of the body revealed as scattered bones. The skeleton is the reality principle that shatters the fantasy of eternal beauty.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's treatment of the skeleton represents a radical modernization of a medieval topos. The medieval *danse macabre* was didactic: it taught that worldly distinctions are vain because death levels all. Baudelaire's skeleton teaches the same lesson but with a crucial difference — it is not vain but *desirable*. The "grand squelette" answers to the poet's "goût le plus cher" (087-020); the skeleton is not what we should fear but what we secretly want.

This reversal is characteristic of Baudelaire's Satanism. Where Christianity sees the flesh as a veil that hides the horror of death, Baudelaire sees the flesh as a veil that hides the *beauty* of death. The skeleton is the truth, and truth, for Baudelaire, is always more beautiful than illusion — even when (especially when) it is horrible. This is the logic of *Les Fleurs du Mal* in its purest form: beauty extracted from evil, flowers cultivated from the soil of decay.

The notion that "qui n'a serré dans ses bras un squelette" (087-041) universalizes the insight: all love is necrophilia, all embrace is an embrace of the skeleton. The beloved's beauty is a temporary animation of bone, and to love a body is to love what will outlast it — the framework that decomposition cannot destroy.
