# Mystery

## Definition

**Mystery** (*le mystère*) is the ontological ground of Baudelaire's poetic world. It is not a temporary condition of ignorance that knowledge could eventually dispel but a permanent and essential quality of reality itself. In the famous opening of **Correspondances**, nature is a temple whose living pillars emit "confuses paroles" (confused words); the world is *full* of meaning, but that meaning is never fully legible. Mystery, in this sense, is the condition of there being meaning at all — if the world were transparent to reason, there would be nothing for the poet to do.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

The concept of mystery is established in the programmatic sonnet **Correspondances** (poem IV). The poem opens:

> La Nature est un temple où de vivants piliers
> Laissent parfois sortir de confuses paroles (004-001)

Nature is a temple, not a machine; its speech is *confused*, not clear. The poet's task is not to translate this confusion into clarity but to *hear* it — to be the one who can perceive the "forêt de symboles" (forest of symbols) and trace the "ténébreuse et profonde unité" (dark and profound unity) beneath the surface of things. Mystery is not the enemy of knowledge but its medium.

This vision of a world saturated with hidden meaning appears throughout the collection. In **Les Phares** (poem VI), the great artists of history are figured as lighthouses whose beams cross in the darkness:

> Tout chargé de mystère, apparaissent à l'ombre (006-007)

The lighthouses — Rubens, Leonardo, Rembrandt, Michelangelo, and others — are "charged with mystery." Each artist's vision is partial, a single beam cutting through the dark; their collective testimony is a chorus of cries, sobs, and *Te Deum* that echoes across the centuries. Mystery here is the shared condition of artistic vision — no artist sees the whole, but each artist testifies that the whole is there.

In **L'Ennemi** (poem X), Baudelaire images his creative life as a garden devastated by storms:

> Le mystique aliment qui ferait leur vigueur? (008-011)

The "mystic nourishment" that would give new poems their vigor is what the speaker no longer possesses. Mystery is thus not only a quality of the external world but a *resource* — something the poet draws upon and can be depleted of. The "mystique aliment" is at once spiritual and aesthetic: it feeds the soul and it feeds the poem, and when it is gone, both starve.

In **La Vie Antérieure** (poem XII), mystery is identified with the sensuous plenitude of an imagined past existence:

> Mêlaient d'une façon solennelle et mystique (009-006)

The colors and sounds of the anterior life are mingled "in a solemn and mystic fashion." Mystery here is not darkness but fullness — a richness of sensation so complete that it exceeds ordinary perception and passes over into the sacred.

The erotic dimension of mystery is explored in **Tout Entière** (poem XLI):

> O métamorphose mystique (033-021)

The beloved undergoes a "mystic metamorphosis" in which all her senses are fused into one — a synaesthetic union that is at once erotic and metaphysical. Mystery, in the erotic register, is the unknowability of the beloved: she is not a person to be understood but a presence to be contemplated.

In **Le Couvercle** (poem LXXVIII), mystery becomes a universal condition of terror:

> Partout l'homme subit la terreur du mystère, (058-007)

Everywhere man undergoes the terror of mystery. The sky itself is a lid (*couvercle*) that seals off the human world from the divine, and the mystery beneath which we live is not comforting but terrifying. This is the dark face of Baudelaire's mysticism — not the serene contemplation of the One but the anxious experience of being sealed off from it.

In **A Victor Hugo** (poem LXXXVI), dedicated to the exiled poet, mystery wounds:

> Blessé par le mystère et par l'absurdité! (080-048)

The speaker is "wounded by mystery and absurdity." The pairing is significant: mystery and absurdity are not opposites but allies. The world's hiddenness is not a promise of revelation but an assault — the poet is wounded by what he cannot understand.

The deepest register of mystery appears in **Lesbos** (poem CXIII):

> Et je fus dès l'enfance admis au noir mystère (113-043)

The speaker confesses to having been admitted since childhood to the "black mystery" of Lesbian love — a mystery at once sexual, sacred, and transgressive. "Noir mystère" condenses Baudelaire's entire theology of the forbidden: the sacred is not found in light but in darkness, not in permitted knowledge but in the knowledge that is itself a sin.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's concept of mystery is the philosophical foundation of his symbolism. Against the Enlightenment project of rendering the world transparent to reason, Baudelaire insists that the world is and must remain opaque. This is not an anti-intellectual position but an aesthetic one: if the world were fully intelligible, art would be mere description rather than revelation. The poet's task is to testify to the presence of meaning without claiming to exhaust it — to be, in effect, the priest of a religion whose god can never be named.

Mystery in Baudelaire operates on two levels. On the cosmic level, it names the fundamental hiddenness of reality — the "forest of symbols" in which we wander, the "temple" whose pillars speak in confused words. On the personal level, it names the poet's relationship to this hiddenness — at once drawn to it and wounded by it, nourished by the "mystique aliment" and terrified by the "couvercle" that seals the sky. The poet is the creature of mystery: he lives by it and suffers from it, and his art is the record of that double relationship.
