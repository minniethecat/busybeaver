## Definition

Blasphemy (*blasphème*) in Baudelaire's poetic universe is not simple irreverence but a complex act of sacred transgression. Rooted in the Romantic Satanism of the nineteenth century, Baudelairean blasphemy functions as an inverted form of religious engagement: to curse God is still to acknowledge His existence. The blasphemer occupies a liminal space between faith and revolt, and it is this tension that gives the poetry its spiritual intensity.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

Blasphemy appears at crucial moments throughout *Les Fleurs du Mal*, often marking the poet's relationship with divine authority. In "Bénédiction," the poet's mother reacts to his birth with horror, "épouvantée et pleine de blasphèmes" (003-003), cursing God for the gift of a poet-child. Here blasphemy is the world's first response to poetic genius — rejection clothed in sacred language.

In "Les Phares," Baudelaire catalogues the cries of great artists, listing "ces malédictions, ces blasphèmes, ces plaintes" alongside "ces extases, ces cris, ces pleurs, ces *Te Deum*" (006-033). Blasphemy and prayer become indistinguishable in the artist's cry — both are expressions of the same spiritual intensity that fuels all great art.

The poem "Le Masque" confronts the deceptive beauty of art itself: "O blasphème de l'art! ô surprise fatale!" (016-020). The statue that appears beautiful from afar reveals itself as a two-headed monster upon closer inspection. Art's promise of ideal beauty is itself a kind of blasphemy — a beautiful lie.

In "De Profundis Clamavi," the poet descends into a universe "où nagent dans la nuit l'horreur et le blasphème" (022-004), a sunless polar landscape where blasphemy is part of the atmosphere itself — the spiritual climate of despair.

The condemned poems intensify the theme. In "Lesbos," the island "mourut le jour de son blasphème" (113-066, 113-070), destroyed by divine judgment for its transgressive love. And in "Femmes Damnées," "la bière et l'alcôve en blasphèmes fécondes" (099-009) links blasphemy with erotic transgression, the bedchamber becoming a site of sacred violation.

## Analysis

Baudelairean blasphemy operates on two levels. On the surface, it is an act of Romantic rebellion — the poet shaking his fist at heaven, echoing the Miltonic Satan who declared it "better to reign in Hell than serve in Heaven." This is the blasphemy of the "Révolte" cycle, where the poet explicitly aligns himself with Satan against divine tyranny.

But a deeper reading reveals blasphemy as a paradoxical affirmation. To curse God is to remain in dialogue with the divine. The true opposite of faith is not blasphemy but indifference. Baudelaire's speaker, by continually invoking the sacred even in negation, betrays a desperate need for transcendence. This is the core tension of Baudelairean spirituality: the poet who says "I do not believe" is also the poet who cannot stop speaking of God.

The aesthetic dimension is equally important. Blasphemy in Baudelaire is often intertwined with beauty — the beautiful crime, the sacred violation that produces great art. "Le Masque" makes this explicit: art itself is a beautiful blasphemy, a seductive lie that reveals horrifying truth.
