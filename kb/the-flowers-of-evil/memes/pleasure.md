# Pleasure

## Definition

In Baudelaire's moral economy, **Pleasure** (*le Plaisir*) is both a goal and a trap. The poet pursues pleasure relentlessly — through love, intoxication, art, and urban spectacle — yet every pleasure achieved reveals its own insufficiency. Baudelaire's pleasure is never innocent: it carries the weight of guilt, the awareness of its own transience, and the knowledge that the pursuit of pleasure is a form of flight from the deeper demands of the soul.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

Pleasure appears throughout the collection as an object of pursuit and a source of disillusion. In **Le Vin** (The Wine) section, intoxication offers a temporary paradise — the drunken man forgets his misery, his poverty, his mortality. But the pleasure of wine is fugitive; the hangover returns the drinker to an even more acute awareness of his condition.

In **Les Bijoux**, the pleasure of the adorned female body is rendered with exquisite sensuality — and the poem was condemned for it. Baudelaire refuses to spiritualize or moralize pleasure; he presents it in its physical intensity and lets the reader feel both its attraction and its danger.

In **Le Voyage**, the ultimate pleasure is the journey itself: the travelers have seen everything, experienced everything, and found that "le monde est monotone et petit" — the world is monotonous and small. Pleasure exhausts its objects and leaves only the desire for something different, something new — which is death.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's treatment of pleasure is fundamentally modern in its rejection of both hedonism and asceticism. The hedonist believes pleasure is sufficient; the ascetic believes pleasure must be renounced. Baudelaire believes neither: pleasure is real but insufficient, worth pursuing but incapable of satisfying. This is the position of a post-Christian consciousness that has lost faith in transcendence but refuses the consolations of mere sensuality.

The legal condemnation of *Les Fleurs du Mal* was partly a response to Baudelaire's refusal to condemn pleasure. The poems depict pleasure without the moral framework that would make such depiction acceptable to 19th-century authorities. Baudelaire's modernity consists partly in this: the insistence that art can look at pleasure directly, without apology.
