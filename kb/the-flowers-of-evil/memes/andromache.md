# Andromache

## Identity

**Andromache** (*Andromaque*) is a figure from classical mythology, part of the vast repertoire of ancient stories that Baudelaire and his 19th-century readers knew intimately. In the densely allusive world of *Les Fleurs du Mal*, classical figures appear alongside biblical and modern references as coordinates in the poet's symbolic geography.

## Connection to Baudelaire

Baudelaire draws on classical mythology as a symbolic vocabulary for modern experience. The ancient figures are not remote antiquities but living presences — names and stories that give shape to emotions that modern language struggles to articulate. The mythological references in *Les Fleurs du Mal* function as a kind of shorthand: to name Andromache or the Muse is to invoke an entire world of meaning.

For Baudelaire, the classical tradition is both a resource and a burden. It provides the forms — the sonnet, the alexandrine, the mythological allusion — that make poetry possible. But it also represents a standard of formal perfection that the modern poet can only approach asymptotically.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

Andromache appears in Baudelaire's poetry as a figure of classical reference, woven into the dense fabric of literary and mythological allusion that characterizes the collection. The poet's engagement with classical figures is never merely decorative; each reference carries symbolic weight, connecting the modern urban experience to the ancient world of myth and epic.

## Literary Significance

Baudelaire's use of classical mythology exemplifies his broader strategy of literary appropriation. Ancient figures are not preserved in their original contexts but transformed — made to speak to modern concerns, invested with Baudelairean meanings. This practice influenced the Symbolists' free handling of myth and anticipates the modernist technique of mythological parallelism (Joyce's *Ulysses*, Eliot's *The Waste Land*).
