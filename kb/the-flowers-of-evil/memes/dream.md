# Dream

## Definition

In Baudelaire's poetic world, the **Dream** (*le Rêve*) is a privileged mode of consciousness — an escape from the weight of reality and a gateway to the ideal. Dreams are not mere fantasies but a form of creative perception, allowing the poet to see through the mundane surface of things to their symbolic essence. The dream is the natural habitat of the Baudelairean imagination, the space where correspondences between the visible and the invisible become tangible.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

Dreams permeate the collection as states of heightened vision. In **La Vie Antérieure**, the poet recalls a past life lived "au milieu de l'azur, des vagues, des splendeurs" — a dream of a tropical paradise that haunts his present existence. The dream here is memory, nostalgia, and ideal all at once.

In **Rêve Parisien**, the poet dreams a city more perfect than Paris — a mineral landscape of "métaux, marbres et flots" — only to wake to the horror of the real. The dream exposes the gap between what the imagination can conceive and what reality offers. This is the fundamental structure of the Baudelairean dream: not a simple escape but a painful oscillation between vision and disillusion.

In **Le Poison**, opium-induced reverie opens "tout l'infini" to the poet — a dream chemically induced but no less meaningful. Baudelaire treats artificial paradises as legitimate routes to the dream state, consistent with his broader refusal to privilege natural over artificial sources of the ideal.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's treatment of the dream reflects his broader aesthetics of transformation. The dream does not falsify reality; it reveals a deeper truth hidden beneath appearances. In this, Baudelaire draws on the Romantic tradition (especially Nerval and the German Romantics) while anticipating Surrealism's elevation of the dream to a primary mode of knowledge.

The dream in Baudelaire is always fragile. It shatters on waking, or decays into spleen. This fragility makes the dream precious — a glimpse of the ideal that cannot be sustained but must be continuously pursued. The poet's task is to capture these glimpses and render them in words that, however imperfectly, preserve something of the dream's power.
