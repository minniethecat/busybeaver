# The Poet

## Identity

**The Poet** (*le Poète*) in Baudelaire's work is more than a profession — it is a metaphysical condition. The poet is a figure set apart from ordinary humanity, endowed with extraordinary sensitivity and condemned to extraordinary suffering. This figure — who appears throughout *Les Fleurs du Mal* as the "je" (I) of the poems — is both Charles Baudelaire himself and an archetype: the *poète maudit* (accursed poet), the visionary who pays for his vision with exile, poverty, and despair.

## Connection to Baudelaire

The figure of the Poet is Baudelaire's most sustained self-portrait and his most influential creation. The accursed poet — rejected by society, misunderstood by women, haunted by spleen yet capable of glimpsing the ideal — became the model for generations of artists after Baudelaire. Verlaine, Rimbaud, and the Symbolists explicitly identified themselves with this figure; the myth of the poète maudit is one of Baudelaire's most enduring legacies.

Baudelaire's poet is distinct from the Romantic conception of the artist as inspired genius. The Romantic poet is in harmony with nature; Baudelaire's poet is in conflict with the world. His vision is not a gift but a wound — an awareness of beauty that makes the ugliness of modern life unbearable.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

The Poet is the speaker of nearly every poem in the collection. In **Bénédiction** (003), the poet is cursed from birth, rejected by his mother, and loved by a woman who tears out his heart (003-004, 003-012, 003-057). In **L'Albatros**, the poet is compared to the albatross — majestic in flight, clumsy and mocked on deck. The poet's grandeur is inseparable from his humiliation.

In **Élévation** (005), the poet soars above the world, his spirit "comme un bon nageur qui se pâme dans l'onde" (005-006); in **Le Vin des Chiffonniers**, the poet identifies with the ragpicker — the lowest of the urban poor — as a fellow collector of society's discards. The poet is both the highest and the lowest of beings.

## Literary Significance

Baudelaire's figure of the Poet represents a decisive break from both the Classical tradition (poet as craftsman) and the Romantic tradition (poet as inspired genius). The Baudelairean poet is a modern figure — urban, alienated, self-divided — whose art is not an escape from suffering but its transmutation into form.
