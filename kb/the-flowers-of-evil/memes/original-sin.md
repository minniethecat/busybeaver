# Original Sin

## Overview

**Original Sin** (*le Péché Originel*) is the foundational event of Christian theology: Adam and Eve's disobedience in Eden, which condemned all subsequent humanity to a fallen state. For Baudelaire, original sin is not a doctrine to be believed but a **structure of experience** — the Fall is not an event in the distant past but a condition felt in every present moment.

*Les Fleurs du Mal* opens not with beauty but with sin. The very first line of **Au Lecteur** (To the Reader, chapter 002), the collection's dedicatory poem, names sin as the universal human bond:

> La sottise, l'erreur, le péché, la lésine,
> Occupent nos esprits et travaillent nos corps (002-001)

Stupidity, error, sin, and miserliness — these are not aberrations but constants. The poem's famous final stanza declares that the reader shares this condition: "— Hypocrite lecteur, — mon semblable, — mon frère!" (002-040). Original sin is what makes the reader the poet's sibling.

## Significance for Baudelaire

Original sin provides Baudelaire with a **theological framework for his aesthetics of evil**. In orthodox Christianity, the Fall explains why the world contains suffering and why human nature is corrupted. Baudelaire repurposes this doctrine: the Fall explains why beauty is inseparable from evil, why pleasure shades into damnation, and why the poet's vision is necessarily transgressive.

Without original sin, Baudelaire's dualism would be mere moodiness. With it, the oscillation between Spleen and Idéal acquires cosmic weight. The poet's inability to sustain the Ideal is not a personal failure but the human condition — the consequence of a Fall that happened before he was born but that he relives in every poem.

Baudelaire's treatment of sin is characteristically ambivalent. Sin is not simply regretted; it is also celebrated. The "péchés" catalogued in **Au Lecteur** are not just vices but sources of poetic energy. Baudelaire's originality lies in refusing to choose between condemnation and celebration of the fallen condition — he does both, simultaneously, in poem after poem.

The doctrine of original sin also licenses Baudelaire's **democratic vision of damnation**. Everyone is fallen, not just the obviously wicked. The hypocritical reader, the respectable bourgeois, the pious churchgoer — all share the same corrupted nature. There is no moral hierarchy in Baudelaire's universe, only different styles of sinning.

## Background

In the Christian theological tradition, original sin entered the world through Adam and Eve's consumption of the forbidden fruit. Augustine of Hippo (354–430 CE) formulated the doctrine most influentially: Adam's sin is transmitted to all humanity through sexual generation, and only divine grace — dispensed through the Church's sacraments — can remedy it.

The nineteenth century saw the doctrine under sustained attack from Enlightenment rationalism and Romantic humanism. Rousseau's faith in natural human goodness was the anti-Augustinian position. Baudelaire, deeply hostile to Rousseau, reasserted original sin as a psychological truth against the progressive optimism of his age. He once wrote: "The theory of original sin is the only one that explains everything."

## Aftermath

In Baudelaire's poetic universe, the Fall has no redemption. There is no Christ who cancels sin, no grace that restores innocence. The poems that gesture toward salvation — **Bénédiction** (003), **Le Voyage** (108) — leave the question unresolved. This is Baudelaire's darkest revision of Christian theology: original sin without original grace, a Fall from which there is no getting up.
