# Voyage

## Definition

**The voyage** (*le voyage*) is the central figure of Baudelaire's longest and final great poem, "Le Voyage" (poem CXXVI). More than physical travel, the voyage in Baudelaire names a fundamental human restlessness — the conviction that somewhere, beyond the horizon, lies a world adequate to desire. Every actual journey is a failed attempt to reach what only the final voyage — death — can deliver: "Au fond de l'Inconnu pour trouver du *nouveau*!" (To the depths of the Unknown to find something *new*!, 108-144).

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

"Le Voyage" is the culminating poem of *Les Fleurs du Mal*, placed as the final poem in the "La Mort" (Death) section and thus the last word of the entire collection. It is a poem of monumental scale — eight sections tracing the arc of human restlessness from childhood to death.

The poem opens with the child's innate desire for the new:

> Pour l'enfant, amoureux de cartes et d'estampes,
> L'univers est égal à son vaste appétit. (108-001)

The child, in love with maps and prints, experiences the universe as equal to its appetite — a world vast enough to satisfy. But this equation fails with age, and the adult discovers that the world, however large, is never large enough.

The true voyagers are distinguished from mere tourists by a metaphysical quality:

> Mais les vrais voyageurs sont ceux-là seuls qui partent
> Pour partir; cœurs légers, semblables aux ballons (108-017)

True travelers are those who leave *for the sake of leaving* — hearts light as balloons, driven not by a destination but by the sheer necessity of departure. This is Baudelaire's radical definition: the voyage is not a means to an end but an end in itself. Departure is the substance, not the prelude.

What all voyagers report upon return is the same bitter wisdom:

> Amer savoir, celui qu'on tire du voyage! (108-112)

"Bitter knowledge, that which one draws from the voyage!" The world, seen and exhausted, is "monotone et petit" (monotonous and small, 108-112) — a repetition of the same boredom under different skies. The voyage that promised novelty delivers only the confirmation that novelty does not exist on earth.

The poem traces a typology of voyagers — the political exiles, the erotic adventurers, the spiritual seekers — all converging on the same disappointment. Even the paradisal islands are revealed as mirages. And yet, the poem does not end in resignation. Its final strophe is a summons:

> Ô Mort, vieux capitaine, il est temps! levons l'ancre!
> Ce pays nous ennuie, ô Mort! Appareillons! (108-137)

Death is addressed as an old captain — the final and only reliable navigator. The call to "raise anchor" transforms death from terminus to departure. The poem's last line is one of the most famous in French poetry:

> Au fond de l'Inconnu pour trouver du *nouveau*! (108-144)

The voyage ends where it began — with the hunger for the new — but now the hunger has learned that only death can feed it. The capital letter of "Inconnu" (Unknown) and the italicized *nouveau* (new) mark these words as charged beyond ordinary meaning: they are the coordinates of a destination that exists only in the act of dying.

Earlier in the collection, the voyage appears in a minor key. The albatross, that "voyageur ailé" (winged traveler, 004-009), is majestic in flight but "gauche et veule" (awkward and weak) on land — the voyager's natural element is the air, not the earth:

> Ce voyageur ailé, comme il est gauche et veule! (004-009)

The poet himself is such a voyager: sublime in the element of imagination, grotesque when forced to walk on the ground of the real.

And in **Moesta et Errabunda** (poem LXIV):

> Dis-moi, ton cœur parfois s'envole-t-il, Agathe,
> Loin du noir océan de l'immonde cité,
> Vers un autre océan où la splendeur éclate (064-001)

The longing to "voyager dans les rêves" (travel in dreams, 064-009) is the intimate form of the voyage — the escape not through space but through reverie, toward a lost paradise of childhood and purity.

## Analysis

"Le Voyage" is Baudelaire's answer to the epic tradition. Where Homer and Virgil made the voyage a narrative of return and foundation, Baudelaire makes it a narrative of exhaustion and departure. There is no homecoming — only the discovery that home was never home, and that the real voyage is the one from which there is no return.

The poem operates on a paradox: it is an encyclopedia of disappointment that ends in hope. Every stanza catalogues the failure of travel to deliver the new, yet the final stanza transforms failure into fuel. The "Inconnu" at the bottom of the voyage is not merely death — it is death grasped as an aesthetic opportunity, the one experience that cannot disappoint because it cannot be reported.

This is Baudelaire's modern sublime: not the awe before nature's grandeur but the vertigo before absolute departure. The voyage is the figure for a specifically modern desire — the desire to be elsewhere, to be otherwise, to escape the prison of the given. That this desire can never be satisfied on earth is the lesson of the poem; that it must be pursued anyway is its faith.
