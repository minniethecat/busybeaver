# Ennui

## Definition

In Baudelaire's lexicon, **Ennui** is not the ordinary boredom of a rainy afternoon — it is an **existential condition**, a paralysis of the will more devastating than any active vice. It is the spiritual state in which the self, drained of desire and disgusted by the world's repetitions, withdraws into a catatonic stupor that Baudelaire calls "the ugliest, meanest, most filthy" of all monsters. Ennui is Spleen at its most absolute: the point at which even suffering ceases to be interesting.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

Ennui receives its definitive portrait in the dedicatory poem **Au Lecteur** (To the Reader), where Baudelaire catalogs humanity's vices — stupidity, error, sin, stinginess — only to reveal that these are merely the supporting cast for a greater horror:

> Mais parmi les chacals, les panthères, les lices,
> Les singes, les scorpions, les vautours, les serpents,
> Les monstres glapissants, hurlants, grognants, rampants,
> Dans la ménagerie infâme de nos vices,
>
> Il en est un plus laid, plus méchant, plus immonde!
> Quoiqu'il ne pousse ni grands gestes ni grands cris,
> Il ferait volontiers de la terre un débris
> Et dans un bâillement avalerait le monde;
>
> C'est l'Ennui! — L'œil chargé d'un pleur involontaire,
> Il rêve d'échafauds en fumant son houka.
> Tu le connais, lecteur, ce monstre délicat,
> — Hypocrite lecteur, — mon semblable, — mon frère! (002-029)

The escalation is deliberate: after the howling, shrieking menagerie of active sins, Ennui does nothing — no grand gestures, no loud cries — yet it would "gladly make the earth a ruin / And in a yawn swallow the world." The yawn (*bâillement*) is Ennui's signature gesture: it consumes without appetite, destroys without passion. And in the poem's most famous lines, Baudelaire implicates the reader directly — "Hypocrite lecteur, — mon semblable, — mon frère!" — making Ennui a diagnosis not of the exceptional sinner but of modern humanity as such.

In **L'Irrémédiable** (027), Ennui is identified with the abyss itself. The poem traces a descent through successive images of irreversible fall — an angel tumbling from heaven, a ship sinking into the polar sea — and arrives at this conclusion:

> Et plonge tout entière au gouffre de l'Ennui; (027-004)

Ennui is not just a mood but a *gouffre* — a swallowing depth. The shift from external abyss to internal condition is complete: the terrifying void that Pascal saw in the cosmos has become the terrifying void within the self.

Elsewhere, ennui pervades the atmosphere of many poems without being explicitly named. The "noirs ennuis" of **Le Crépuscule du Soir** (007-003) darken the snowy evenings; the "ennuis" that the poet rises above in **Élévation** (005-013) are the "vastes chagrins" of the earthbound. In **Femmes Damnées**, the poet addresses "Femme impure! L'ennui rend ton âme cruelle" (018-047) — linking ennui to cruelty, as if the paralysis of boredom produces a sadistic lashing-out.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's Ennui is a **modern invention** — it belongs to the nineteenth century as the humors belonged to the Middle Ages. Where medieval theology had acedia (spiritual sloth), and Romanticism had the mal du siècle (the sickness of the century), Baudelaire's Ennui is distinct: it is not a sin against God but a condition of consciousness, not a youthful melancholy but a structural feature of modern urban existence.

The key to Baudelaire's Ennui is its **passivity**. The other vices in the "ménagerie infâme" are active — they howl, they shriek, they do. Ennui yawns. It is the vice of the spectator, the consumer who has consumed everything and found it tasteless. This is why Baudelaire's diagnosis resonates so powerfully with modernity: Ennui is the default state of the overstimulated subject, for whom nothing any longer surprises or moves.

The therapeutic options Baudelaire explores — intoxication, erotic passion, travel, artistic creation — are all attempts to escape Ennui. Wine ([[Wine]]) offers "l'oubli" (oblivion); the voyage ([[Le Voyage]]) promises "du nouveau" (something new); beauty ([[Beauty]]) provides "un rêve de pierre" (a dream of stone). But each remedy is temporary, and Ennui always returns. In Baudelaire's implicit argument, **only art can metabolize Ennui into meaning** — the yawn becomes a poem, and the void becomes a subject.
