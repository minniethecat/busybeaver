# Infinite

## Definition

**The infinite** (*l'infini*) is the gravitational center of Baudelaire's poetics of desire. It is what the soul longs for — a quality of experience that the finite world perpetually promises and perpetually withholds. Baudelaire's infinite is not a mathematical abstraction but a visceral presence: it is felt in the unrolling of the sea's wave, in the multiplication of erotic sensation, in the vertigo before death, and most of all in the insatiability of desire itself. The poet is defined by his "goût de l'infini" (taste for the infinite) — a hunger that can neither be satisfied nor renounced.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

The infinite appears with remarkable consistency across the collection, threading through the marine, erotic, spiritual, and mortal dimensions of Baudelaire's world.

In **L'Homme et la Mer**, the infinite is made visible through the wave:

> La mer est ton miroir; tu contemples ton âme
> Dans le déroulement infini de sa lame (011-002)

The "déroulement infini" — the infinite unrolling of the wave — is the visual form of the soul's own boundlessness. The sea does not *represent* the infinite; it *demonstrates* it. To watch the sea is to see infinity in motion.

In **La Chevelure** (poem XXIII), the infinite migrates from the cosmic to the intimate. The poet declares his love for a particular quality of infinity accessible only through the lover:

> D'un infini que j'aime et n'ai jamais connu (017-024)

"An infinite that I love and have never known" — the phrase captures the paradox of Baudelaire's desire. The infinite is loved before it is known, desired before it is experienced. It is not a memory of something had and lost but a premonition of something never yet possessed.

In the erotic poems, the infinite becomes a property of sensual multiplication. The lover's kisses proliferate beyond counting:

> Ô serments! ô parfums! ô baisers infinis! (026-030)

"O vows! O perfumes! O infinite kisses!" — the infinite here is not transcendent but immanent, located in the multiplication of bodily pleasure. Yet this erotic infinity is haunted by its own impossibility: kisses cannot be literally infinite, and the attempt to make them so reveals the gap between desire and fulfillment.

In **Le Tombeau** (poem LXXI), the infinite acquires a funereal intimacy:

> Tombeau, confident de mon rêve infini (024-009)

The tomb is the "confidant of my infinite dream" — the only entity that can receive and keep the dream's boundlessness. Death and infinity are linked here not as opposites but as cooperators: the tomb houses what the infinite dream cannot contain within a finite life.

The spiritual dimension emerges in **Les Phares** (poem VI), where artists and visionaries are "chercheuses d'infini" (seekers of the infinite, 098-023). Delacroix, in particular, is characterized by an infinity of aspiration:

> Delacroix, lac de sang hanté des mauvais anges (006-029)

The "lake of blood" is itself a figure of the infinite — a contained infinity, an abyss within a frame — and this is Baudelaire's definition of art: the infinite captured in finite form.

The most compressed expression appears in **Le Gouffre** (poem LXXXII):

> Je ne vois qu'infini par toutes les fenêtres (069-011)

"I see only infinity through all the windows" — this is the vertigo of Pascal transposed into the key of Baudelaire. Where Pascal feared the infinite silence of cosmic space, Baudelaire sees infinity everywhere, in every direction, through every window. It is not the absence of God that terrifies but the presence of the infinite in the absence — the boundless filling of a void.

In **Le Voyage**, the infinite is the cargo the voyagers carry with them:

> Berçant notre infini sur le fini des mers (108-008)

"Rocking our infinite on the finite of the seas" — the finite sea cradles the infinite desire, as though the finite and infinite were not opposites but a cradle and its child. And the poem's closing warning:

> Et fuyez l'infini que vous portez en vous! (114-104)

"Flee the infinite that you carry within you!" — a paradox that encapsulates Baudelaire's entire spiritual drama. The infinite is within, but it is dangerous; it must be fled, but it cannot be escaped. The poet carries his own abyss.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's infinite owes a complex debt to Pascal, whose famous phrase "le silence éternel de ces espaces infinis m'effraie" (the eternal silence of these infinite spaces terrifies me) is the unspoken subtext of many passages. But Baudelaire inverts Pascal's terror: where Pascal feared the infinite as the sign of God's absence, Baudelaire desires the infinite as the sign of the soul's depth. The infinite in Baudelaire is not above or beyond — it is *within*, and it is the very quality that makes the soul worth attending to.

This interiorization of the infinite is Baudelaire's major contribution to the Romantic tradition. The Romantics sought the infinite in nature (mountains, oceans, storms); Baudelaire finds it in the bedroom, the tomb, the mirror of the sea that reflects the soul rather than the sky. The infinite is not a property of space but a quality of desire — it is the shape of a hunger that no finite object can satisfy.

This also explains why the infinite in Baudelaire is always linked to melancholy. To desire the infinite is to be constitutionally unsatisfiable, and to be unsatisfiable is to be sad. The "goût de l'infini" is a taste for what cannot be tasted — a bittersweet pleasure in the very impossibility of fulfillment.
