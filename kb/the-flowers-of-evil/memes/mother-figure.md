# Mother Figure

## Identity

The **Mother Figure** (*la Mère*) in Baudelaire's poetry is a symbol of primal attachment and primal wound. Drawing on the poet's own biography — his father died when Baudelaire was six, and his mother's remarriage to Jacques Aupick was experienced as a betrayal — the mother appears throughout *Les Fleurs du Mal* as a figure of lost love, resentment, and impossible longing. More broadly, the mother represents the origin the poet can neither return to nor escape.

## Connection to Baudelaire

Baudelaire's relationship with his mother, Caroline, was the most emotionally charged relationship of his life. After his father's death, the young Charles and his mother lived together in an intense bond that was severed by her remarriage. Baudelaire never forgave this betrayal, and his letters to his mother — written throughout his life — alternate between desperate declarations of love and bitter accusations of abandonment.

This biographical wound informs the poetry but is not reducible to it. The mother in *Les Fleurs du Mal* is also a symbolic figure: the lost paradise of childhood, the unconditional love that adult sexuality can never replicate, the origin that art attempts to recover.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

In **Bénédiction** (003), the mother appears as the poet's first enemy — she "crispe ses poings vers Dieu" (003-004), cursing the child she has brought into the world. The poet is rejected from the moment of birth. Yet the mother is also the source of whatever capacity for love the poet retains. The ambivalence is total: the mother is both the first wound and the first love.

Throughout the collection, maternal imagery recurs in unexpected places — the beloved as mother, death as mother, the earth as mother. Baudelaire's characteristic move is to reveal the maternal dimension of figures that seem, on the surface, to be purely erotic or aesthetic.

## Literary Significance

Baudelaire's treatment of the mother figure influenced psychoanalytic readings of literature (Freud cited Baudelaire) and established the pattern of the artist whose creativity is fueled by unresolved childhood trauma. The mother as both wound and muse is a Baudelairean legacy that runs through Proust, Genet, and beyond.
