# Sin

## Definition

In Baudelaire's poetry, **Sin** (*le Péché*) is not primarily a theological category — a violation of divine commandment awaiting punishment — but an **existential condition**: the permanent stain of fallenness that marks every human thought, act, and aspiration. Baudelaire inherits the Christian doctrine of original sin but transforms it into a psychological and aesthetic principle: sin is what gives beauty its edge, pleasure its intensity, and art its depth. Without sin, Baudelaire seems to suggest, there would be no poetry — only the boring innocence of an unhaunted world.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

Sin appears in the very first line of the collection's opening poem, **Au Lecteur**:

> La sottise, l'erreur, le péché, la lésine,
> Occupent nos esprits et travaillent nos corps… (002-001)

It leads the catalogue, setting the tone for everything that follows. The verb *travaillent* (work upon, labor) is significant: sin is not an occasional lapse but a constant, grinding labor performed on the body and mind. We are not sinners because we sin; we sin because we are sinners — it is our natural state.

The same poem continues:

> Nos péchés sont têtus, nos repentirs sont lâches;
> Nous nous faisons payer grassement nos aveux… (002-005)

Sins are "stubborn" (*têtus*); repentances are "cowardly" (*lâches*). The symmetry is devastating: sin persists, and repentance fails, because we are fundamentally unwilling to give up our transgressions. Confession itself is a self-dealing transaction — "we make them pay us richly for our confessions."

In **Femmes Damnées**, the theme reaches an erotic crescendo. The poet addresses the damned woman:

> Femme impure! L'ennui rend ton âme cruelle… (018-047)

And elsewhere in the same sequence, the Delphine character is apostrophized as "ô femme, ô reine des péchés" (018-061) — "queen of sins." Here sin is explicitly gendered and eroticized: the woman's sin is her sexual transgression, but Baudelaire elevates (or damns) her to royalty within it.

In the condemned poems, sin becomes explicitly sexual and sapphic. **Femmes Damnées (Delphine et Hippolyte)** presents a meditation on the impossibility of innocent passion:

> Dévoilent pour nos péchés
> Des mystères de ciel… (077-022)

The heavens unveil mysteries — for their sins. The syntax is ambiguous: are the mysteries unveiled *because* of their sins, or *for the purpose* of revealing their sins? The ambiguity is the point: sin and revelation are inseparable.

The collection's final poem, **Le Voyage**, offers the most comprehensive formulation:

> Le spectacle ennuyeux de l'immortel péché… (108-091)

Sin is "immortal" (*immortel*) — not because it cannot be forgiven, but because it is coextensive with human existence. It is also "boring" (*ennuyeux*): the eternal sameness of transgression, repeated across all cultures and all ages. This is Baudelaire's bleakest insight — sin is not dramatic but tedious, a spectacle one has seen too many times.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's treatment of sin is fundamentally **anti-Augustinian**. For Augustine, sin is a privation of the good — a turning away from God toward the creature. For Baudelaire, sin is a **positive aesthetic category**: it is not the absence of good but the presence of a dark sublimity. When he writes "La Beauté sera BYRONIQUE ou ne sera pas" (paraphrased), he is asserting that modern beauty requires sin — specifically, the Byronic fusion of beauty and transgression.

This aestheticization of sin is Baudelaire's most controversial legacy. It is not, however, a simple celebration of evil. Baudelaire remains a Catholic in his insistence on the reality of sin and its consequences; what he rejects is the possibility of easy redemption. The sinner in Baudelaire's world does not repent and find grace — she is consumed, but the spectacle of her consumption is beautiful. This is the logic of the *fleurs du mal*: the flowers of evil, beauty grown from corrupted soil.

The relationship between sin and **Ennui** is crucial. In "Au Lecteur," the catalogue of active sins yields to the passive, yawning monster of Ennui — and Ennui is worse. Why? Because active sin at least implies desire, energy, engagement with the world. Ennui sins without even the dignity of wanting to. In Baudelaire's inverted moral hierarchy, it is better to sin passionately than to be too bored to sin at all.
