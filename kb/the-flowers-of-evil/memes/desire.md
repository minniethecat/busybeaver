# Desire

## Definition

In Baudelaire's psychology, **Desire** (*le Désir*) is the restless engine of human experience — a force that propels the poet toward beauty, love, and the ideal, yet never allows him to arrive. Desire in Baudelaire is fundamentally tragic: it is infinite in its aspirations but bound to finite, mortal objects. The beloved can never fully satisfy the desire she arouses; the ideal can never be fully attained. Desire is thus both the poet's vitality and his torment.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

Desire runs through the collection as an undercurrent of longing that is never quenched. In **À une Passante** (To a Passing Woman), desire crystallizes around a fleeting glimpse of a woman in the street: "Un éclair... puis la nuit! — Fugitive beauté" — a flash of lightning, then night. Desire is born in an instant and survives only as memory and regret.

In **Le Désir de Peindre** (The Desire to Paint), desire is redirected into art: the poet desires not to possess the beloved but to render her in paint. This sublimation of desire into creation is Baudelaire's characteristic solution — not satisfaction but transformation.

In **Les Bijoux** (The Jewels), desire is explicitly erotic, focused on the adorned body of the beloved. The poem was among those condemned in the 1857 trial for its frank sensuality. Baudelaire's insistence on depicting desire in its physical reality — rather than spiritualizing it — was central to his modernity.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's treatment of desire distinguishes him from both the Platonic tradition (desire as ladder to the divine) and the Romantic tradition (desire as path to union with nature). For Baudelaire, desire is an end in itself — not because it is satisfied but because it generates consciousness. To desire is to be alive, however painfully.

This is the Baudelairean paradox: satisfaction would be death. The poet needs desire to remain unsatisfied because desire is what fuels creativity. The beloved must remain partly mysterious, beauty must remain partly unattainable, the ideal must remain on the horizon. Desire is the poet's wound and his gift.
