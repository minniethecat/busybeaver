# Passion

## Definition

**Passion** (*la passion*) in Baudelaire's poetic vocabulary carries the full weight of its Latin root (*patior*, to suffer). It is never merely a synonym for enthusiastic feeling — it is an overwhelming force that possesses the subject, a fire that illuminates and consumes in the same movement. In the world of *Les Fleurs du Mal*, passion is always ambivalent: the lover's passion is indistinguishable from the torturer's, and the artist's creative passion bleeds into self-destruction.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

The word "passion" occurs at moments of extreme emotional intensity in the collection, and each occurrence marks a threshold where feeling passes over into something closer to possession or madness.

In **Une Charogne** (poem XXIX), the speaker addresses the beloved beside a rotting carcass, insisting that even her decomposition will not erase their bond:

> Vous, mon ange et ma passion! (021-040)

The juxtaposition is deliberate and shocking — the beloved is both "ange" (angel) and "passion" in the same breath, and this declaration is made while contemplating a corpse crawling with maggots. The passage insists that passion survives physical corruption: the beloved's form may decay but the "forme et l'essence divine" of the poet's love persist. Passion here is what outlasts the body — the spiritual residue of desire that decomposition cannot touch.

The darker face of passion emerges in **Chanson d'Après-Midi** (poem LVIII):

> Ma terrible passion! (042-006)

The adjective *terrible* is not hyperbole. In this poem, the speaker describes the beloved as a force that ravishes and devours him — her gaze, her movements, her very presence produce a state of derangement. Passion is "terrible" because it annihilates the self: the lover is no longer an agent but a patient, someone to whom passion *happens*.

This destructive dimension is explicit in **Sonnet d'Automne** (poem LXIV):

> Je hais la passion et l'esprit me fait mal! (046-008)

The speaker *hates* passion — and yet cannot escape it. The line is followed immediately by an evocation of a tender, autumnal love that would be the opposite of passion: gentle, seasonal, without the violence of summer. But this very wish reveals that passion, for Baudelaire, is the default mode of love — the attempt to replace it with something milder is a fantasy contradicted by experience.

In **La Musique** (poem LXIX), passion is generalized beyond the erotic to the aesthetic:

> Je sens vibrer en moi toutes les passions (050-009)

The speaker feels all passions vibrate within him as music transports him — a "vaste vaisseau" (vast vessel) taking him over a "grande mer" (great sea). Music unlocks the totality of human passion, and the speaker's soul becomes a theatre where all passions play at once. This is the aesthetic transfiguration of passion: what in love is destructive becomes, in art, a source of visionary transport.

Finally, in **Le Jeu** (poem XCVI), passion appears in its most degraded form — the gambler's obsession:

> Enviant de ces gens la passion tenace, (086-017)

The speaker envies the gamblers' tenacious passion — even this debased form of commitment is preferable, the poem suggests, to the speaker's own state of satiety and indifference. Passion, even when directed at the most sordid object, is still a form of *being alive*; its absence is the living death of Spleen.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's treatment of passion is structured by a fundamental ambivalence that he inherits from the Christian tradition but radicalizes. In Christian theology, the passions are disturbances of the soul that must be governed by reason; in Baudelaire, they are disturbances that *are* the soul — to extinguish them would be to extinguish life itself. Hence the paradox of **Sonnet d'Automne**: the speaker hates passion, yet the alternative is a lukewarm tenderness that would be a kind of death.

This ambivalence is connected to Baudelaire's theology of original sin. Passion — particularly erotic passion — is the mark of the Fall, the sign that humanity is separated from God. But in Baudelaire's aesthetic economy, the mark of the Fall is also the source of art. The "terrible passion" of **Chanson d'Après-Midi** and the "passion tenace" of the gamblers in **Le Jeu** are both forms of intensity that, however destructive, are preferable to the nullity of Ennui. The worst sin is not passion but indifference — the "Ennui" of **Au Lecteur** that "rêve d'échafauds en fumant son houka" (002-038).

Baudelaire thus arrives at a position that is neither the Romantic celebration of passion as liberation nor the classical condemnation of passion as disorder. Passion is suffering (*passio*), and suffering is the condition of vision. This is the argument that links the erotic passion of **Une Charogne** to the aesthetic passion of **La Musique**: both are modes of being-overwhelmed, and being-overwhelmed is the prerequisite for seeing what ordinary perception cannot.
