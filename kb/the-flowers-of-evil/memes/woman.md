# Woman

## Definition

In Baudelaire's poetics, **Woman** (*la Femme*) is not a person but a **metaphysical category** — a site where the most extreme contradictions of his universe converge. She is simultaneously the closest earthly approximation of the Ideal and the surest path to damnation; the poet's inspiration and his destruction; the Madonna whose purity redeems and the witch whose sensuality corrupts. This is not misogyny in the ordinary sense (though Baudelaire's language can be brutal) but a **metaphysical ambivalence** that uses the figure of Woman to explore the fundamental structure of desire, beauty, and evil.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

Woman appears in nearly every poem of *Les Fleurs du Mal*, but she is never the same figure twice. Baudelaire deliberately fragments her into incompatible archetypes, refusing to synthesize them into a coherent whole.

**The Madonna.** In poems like **À une Madone** (048), the beloved is elevated to the status of a religious icon — the poet builds a "subterranean altar" to his mistress-Madonna in the depth of his distress. The beloved as Virgin Mary is the poet's private cult object (001-078).

**The Witch.** In **Sed Non Satiata** and related poems, the woman is a sorceress whose beauty is indistinguishable from evil:

> Femme impure! L'ennui rend ton âme cruelle… (018-047)

Impure woman! Boredom makes your soul cruel. The witch figure is sexually voracious, morally indifferent, and irresistibly attractive. She is Baudelaire's revision of the Romantic femme fatale, but her cruelty is mechanical, a product of boredom rather than passion.

**The Lover.** In **Le Balcon** and **L'Invitation au Voyage**, the woman is the domestic partner, the companion of remembered evenings and imagined journeys. Here tenderness breaks through the usual harshness, and the woman is a collaborator in the creation of a shared aesthetic life.

**The Corpse.** In **Une Charogne** (023), Baudelaire addresses his beloved while describing a rotting animal carcass — and then turns the description toward her (001-080). The beloved woman, the source of all beauty and desire, will one day be carrion. This is a **memento mori** that uses the woman's body as its vehicle: beauty is inseparable from its future corruption.

**The Queen of Sin.** In **Femmes Damnées**, the woman is apostrophized as:

> De toi se sert, ô femme, ô reine des péchés… (018-061)

"Of you it makes use, O woman, O queen of sins" — the woman as royal sovereign of transgression. Baudelaire's language here is simultaneously condemnatory and reverent: she is a queen even in damnation.

In **Hymne à la Beauté** (017), the question that structures the entire collection is asked about Beauty — but the feminine grammatical gender makes Woman the implicit referent. Beauty — and by extension Woman — may be divine or demonic (003-009), and Baudelaire refuses to decide.

The condemned poems sharpen the sexual dimension. In **Femmes Damnées (Delphine et Hippolyte)** (103), the love between two women is presented with a mixture of fascination and horror. The poems were condemned for obscenity, but their real transgression is philosophical: they refuse to moralize about what they depict.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's Woman is best understood not as a portrait of actual women but as a **projection screen** for the male poetic consciousness. She is what the poet needs her to be — Madonna, witch, lover, corpse — and her instability across the collection is not inconsistency but method. Each poem activates a different archetype because each poem explores a different region of desire, and desire itself is inconsistent.

This projection model has been extensively critiqued by feminist scholarship, and the critique is valid: Baudelaire's Woman is not a subject but an object, not a speaker but a spoken-about. However, Baudelaire's own poems contain the seeds of this critique. The woman in **Le Balcon** is not a passive projection — she is the "mère des souvenirs," the active creator of the memories the poet cherishes. And the woman in **Femmes Damnées** speaks for herself, however briefly, in a voice that challenges the poet's clinical gaze.

Baudelaire's deepest insight about Woman is that she embodies the **modern condition** as he understands it. Woman is split between incompatible roles (virgin/whore, mother/lover, redeemer/destroyer) in the same way that the modern self is split between incompatible desires (for transcendence and for oblivion, for purity and for sin). Woman is not the exception to Baudelaire's universe but its **paradigmatic case** — the figure in whom all the contradictions of modern consciousness are written on the body.
