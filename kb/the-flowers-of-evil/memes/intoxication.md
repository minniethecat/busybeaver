## Definition

Intoxication (*ivresse*) in Baudelaire's poetics extends far beyond the effects of alcohol. It encompasses any state of heightened consciousness that liberates the self from the constraints of rational thought and the oppression of Time. Wine, opium, poetry, love, and even virtue can all produce *ivresse* — a temporary ecstasy that serves as the primary antidote to Spleen. The prose poem "Enivrez-vous" ("Get Drunk") articulates this philosophy most explicitly: one must be perpetually intoxicated to avoid feeling "the horrible burden of Time."

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

The motif of intoxication threads through *Les Fleurs du Mal* in multiple forms. In "Sed Non Satiata," the poet declares his intent to "plongerai ma tête amoureuse d'ivresse / Dans ce noir océan où l'autre est enfermé" (018-021), seeking intoxication in the beloved's dark hair — a sensuous drowning that promises both annihilation and ecstasy.

In "Le Parfum," Baudelaire asks the reader whether they have ever breathed in incense "avec ivresse et lente gourmandise" (028-017, echoed at 029-002), capturing intoxication as a slow, deliberate savoring. The doubled occurrence of this line across two poems signals the centrality of the experience: intoxication is not mere drunkenness but a cultivated aesthetic practice.

"Le Vin des Chiffonniers" and the wine cycle more broadly celebrate the democratizing power of intoxication. In "La Mort des Artistes," wine fuels the artist's struggle against death. But intoxication also has a darker face: "L'ivresse des choses funèbres" (060-029) in "Le Vertige" reveals that one can become intoxicated on death itself, on the morbid and the funereal.

In "Lesbos," the condemned poem, we encounter "des costumes qui sont pour les yeux une ivresse" (108-082) — intoxication mediated through visual spectacle, clothing that intoxicates the gaze. And in "Comme au beau temps de notre ivresse" (093-020) from "Un Fantôme," intoxication is cast into the past tense, a remembered paradise now lost, anticipating the elegiac tone of "Le Vin des Amants."

## Analysis

Baudelaire's concept of intoxication must be understood as a strategy of resistance. Against the two great enemies of the Baudelairean soul — Spleen (the paralysis of despair) and Time (the devourer of all things) — intoxication offers temporary liberation. But it is always temporary. The intoxication passes, and the return to sobriety brings an intensified awareness of what was momentarily escaped.

This cycle of intoxication and withdrawal mirrors the broader structure of Baudelairean experience: the oscillation between Idéal (the ecstatic elevation) and Spleen (the crushing fall). Intoxication is thus both remedy and symptom — it proves, by its very necessity, the depth of the wound it seeks to heal.

The aesthetic dimension is crucial. Baudelaire treats intoxication not as a vice to be condemned but as a technique to be refined. The "lente gourmandise" of "Le Parfum" suggests a connoisseurship of altered states, an art of intoxication that parallels the art of poetry itself. The poet is, in this sense, a professional intoxicant — one who produces, through language, the same ecstatic transport that others seek through wine.
