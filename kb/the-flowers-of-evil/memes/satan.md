# Satan

## Definition

In Baudelaire's dualistic cosmos, **Satan** is not merely a villain but a tragic figure — the rebel who chose knowledge over submission, freedom over obedience. Baudelaire inherits the Romantic rehabilitation of Satan begun by Blake, Shelley, and Hugo, but gives it a distinctively personal, psychological dimension. Satan is not just a mythological figure; he is a presence within the poet's own soul, the voice that whispers against the ideal and toward the abyss.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

Satan appears throughout the collection as a personified force of temptation and rebellion. In **Hymne à la Beauté** (017), the poet explicitly equates God and Satan as co-equal sources of beauty: "De Satan ou de Dieu, qu'importe?" (017-025). This is perhaps Baudelaire's most radical theological statement — the origin of beauty is irrelevant; what matters is its effect.

In **Les Litanies de Satan**, the collection's most scandalous poem, Satan is addressed in the language of Catholic liturgy. Each stanza begins with an invocation and ends with the refrain: "O Satan, prends pitié de ma longue misère!" — O Satan, take pity on my long misery. The poem inverts the structure of prayer, directing toward Satan the devotion traditionally reserved for God and the Virgin Mary. For this, Baudelaire was prosecuted in the 1857 obscenity trial.

In **La Destruction**, Satan is described as an internal tempter who "dans mes poumons altérés / Verse un parfum chargé de vapeurs sulfureuses" — pours sulfurous vapor into the poet's lungs. Satan is not external but intimate, a permanent resident of the poet's psyche.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's Satanism is literary and symbolic rather than cultic. He is not a believing Satanist but a poet who finds in Satan a powerful symbol of the human condition: the creature who defies authority, embraces knowledge at great cost, and suffers eternally for his choice. This Satan is a figure of the artist himself — the rebel who is condemned by society but whose rebellion is the source of his creative power.

The 1857 trial that condemned six poems from *Les Fleurs du Mal* focused partly on the Satanic content. Baudelaire's defense — that he was depicting evil to make it repellent — was only partially accepted. The poems remained banned in France until 1949.
