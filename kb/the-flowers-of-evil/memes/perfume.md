# Perfume

## Definition

In Baudelaire's sensory hierarchy, **perfume** (*parfum*) occupies the apex. More than mere fragrance, it is the sense capable of piercing the veil between the material and the spiritual — the sensory channel through which *correspondances* (the hidden analogies uniting all realms of experience) are most vividly perceived. Baudelaire's theory, articulated most famously in the sonnet **Correspondances**, posits perfume as uniquely equipped to traverse boundaries: between past and present, body and soul, the earthly and the divine. Unlike sight, which merely observes surfaces, or sound, which unfolds in time, perfume penetrates — it enters the body, triggers involuntary memory, and opens a door to what Baudelaire calls "l'expansion des choses infinies" (the expansion of infinite things).

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

Perfume appears throughout *Les Fleurs du Mal* not as decorative imagery but as a structural principle. In the dedicatory preface, the poet defines his unrealized ambition in olfactory terms — he sought "des fleurs rares et vénéneuses d'un parfum encore ignoré" (rare and poisonous flowers of a still-unknown perfume), establishing fragrance as the metaphor for the new poetic beauty he aimed to extract from evil (001-015). The unknown perfume is the unrealized Ideal itself.

The poem **La Chevelure** (The Head of Hair) is the collection's most sustained meditation on the power of perfume. The beloved's hair becomes a vessel of sensory transport:

> Tu répands des parfums comme un soir orageux… (017-006)

Through the perfume of her hair, the poet accesses distant worlds — tropical shores, sun-drenched harbors, the memory of a lost oceanic paradise. The hair is simultaneously a physical object and a portal; its perfume is the mechanism of transport. In a crescendo of synesthetic fusion, Baudelaire collapses all sensory channels into one:

> A grands flots le parfum, le son et la couleur… (018-017)

Perfume, sound, and color flow together in a single wave — the *correspondance* made experiential rather than theoretical.

The spiritual dimension of perfume reaches its fullest expression in **Tout Entière** (All of Her), where the beloved's essence transcends physical description:

> Sa chair spirituelle a le parfum des Anges… (033-031)

The body has become "spiritual flesh," and its perfume is that of Angels — a fragrance without material origin, pointing toward a purely transcendent beauty.

Baudelaire's theoretical statement on perfume arrives in **Correspondances** (035), the sonnet that serves as the collection's aesthetic manifesto:

> Il est de forts parfums pour qui toute matière
> Est poreuse. On dirait qu'ils pénètrent le verre. (035-001)

These "strong perfumes" — amber, musk, benzoin, incense — are distinguished from the weak perfumes of flowers by their moral and spiritual potency. They "sing the transports of the mind and senses" and possess the power of "l'expansion des choses infinies." This is Baudelaire's perfume theory in miniature: certain fragrances are *active agents* that dissolve the boundary between self and world, matter and spirit.

The darker register of perfume is not absent. In **Un Voyage à Cythère** (A Voyage to Cythera), the island of love reveals its underside through "sales parfums" (foul perfumes, 054-011) — the corruption that lurks beneath idealized beauty. In the condemned poem **Les Métamorphoses du Vampire**, the seducer exhales "parfums affreux" (horrible perfumes, 114-096) once the erotic illusion dissolves. Perfume can be a weapon of deception, the aesthetic gloss over moral decay.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's elevation of perfume over the other senses is a deliberate inversion of Enlightenment hierarchy. The Enlightenment privileged sight — the sense of distance, objectivity, and rational observation — as the royal sense. Baudelaire, by contrast, privileges smell: the sense of proximity, intimacy, and involuntary response. Perfume cannot be held at arm's length; it invades. This is precisely its value for a poet seeking to dissolve the boundary between subject and object.

The synesthetic fusion of perfume with sound and color in **La Chevelure** (018-017) prefigures the Wagnerian *Gesamtkunstwerk* (total work of art) and, further, the Symbolist ambition to create a poetry that operates on all senses simultaneously. Baudelaire understood that the most powerful aesthetic experiences are those in which sensory channels blur — and perfume, being the most diffuse and penetrating sense, is the natural vehicle for this blurring.

The distinction in **Correspondances** between "weak" floral perfumes and "strong" animal/mineral perfumes (035-001) is not merely aesthetic but philosophical. Weak perfumes are those of nature left to itself — pretty, transient, morally neutral. Strong perfumes — amber, musk, benzoin — are products of transformation: resins harvested from wounded trees, animal secretions processed by human art. They represent nature transfigured, which for Baudelaire is the definition of art itself. The strong perfume *corrupts* nature in order to elevate it — exactly as Baudelaire's poetry corrupts conventional beauty to extract a higher, more dangerous beauty.

In the broader architecture of Baudelaire's thought, perfume functions as a bridge between Spleen and Ideal. Spleen is the condition of being trapped in the material — the body as prison, time as weight. Perfume offers a temporary escape: it is material (it comes from a substance, a body, a flower) yet immaterial (it cannot be held, only experienced). It is the most spiritual of the senses and the most sensual of the spirits. This duality makes it the perfect Baudelairean medium.
