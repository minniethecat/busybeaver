# Ecstasy

## Definition

**Ecstasy** (*l'extase*) in Baudelaire's vocabulary names the experience of going outside the self — a temporary dissolution of the boundaries that normally confine consciousness to its individual prison. It is the opposite of Spleen: where Spleen is the unbearable weight of being trapped in one's own mind, ecstasy is the lightness of escape. Baudelaire does not discriminate sharply between its varieties — the ecstasy of the mystic, the lover, the drunkard, and the artist are different paths to the same destination. What matters is not the vehicle but the result: the self, for a moment, ceases to be a problem.

## In Les Fleurs du Mal

Ecstasy appears across the full spectrum of Baudelaire's registers. In its most exalted form, it is the divine rapture toward which the religious impulse strains: "A la gloire de Dieu rallume ton extase" (To the glory of God rekindle your ecstasy, 066-010). The verb *rallume* (rekindle) is telling — ecstasy is not a permanent state but a fire that must be reignited, always fading, always requiring renewal.

The erotic form of ecstasy is embodied in the beloved. Her body "contient toutes les extases" (contains all ecstasies, 037-014) — she is the living vessel of the state the poet seeks. In the condemned poem **Les Bijoux** (The Jewels), the beloved's presence "Me ravit en extase, et j'aime avec fureur" (Ravishes me in ecstasy, and I love with fury, 110-007). Ecstasy and fury are fused — the transport is not gentle but violent, a ravishment rather than a gentle ascent.

The aesthetic register of ecstasy is explored through the figure of the poet as transmitter. The poet's work "s'infiltre comme une extase dans tous ceux" (infiltrates like an ecstasy into all those, 059-051) who receive it — art itself is an ecstatic agent, dissolving the boundaries between creator and audience, self and other. The poem does not describe ecstasy; it *produces* it.

The condition of ecstasy's absence is given equal weight. A heart that is "que jamais ne visite l'extase" (never visited by ecstasy, 039-048) is in the state Baudelaire calls Spleen — the flat, gray, airless prison of a self that cannot escape itself. Ecstasy is not a luxury but a necessity; without it, existence becomes unbearable. This is why Baudelaire will pursue it through any means — prayer, sex, art, wine, opium — and why he refuses to condemn any path that leads there.

The mystical dimension of ecstasy appears in the image of one "Auprès d'un tombeau vide en extase courbée" (Near an empty tomb, bowed in ecstasy, 078-039). The empty tomb evokes the Resurrection — but it is *empty*. The ecstasy here hovers between faith and its absence, as though the posture of adoration persists even when the object of adoration has vanished. This is Baudelaire's mystical minimalism: ecstasy remains possible even without a God to receive it.

In its comic-ironic mode, the collection shows "la Fatuité" (Fatuity) promenading its ecstasy (016-012) — the self-satisfied complacency of the vain, for whom ecstasy is merely an inflated form of self-regard. Baudelaire's satire here draws a line: true ecstasy dissolves the self; false ecstasy inflates it.

## Analysis

Baudelaire's treatment of ecstasy belongs to a long tradition — Platonic *mania*, Christian mysticism, Romantic transport — but it is distinguished by its radical ecumenism regarding the means of access. The mystic, the lover, and the drunkard are not competitors but fellow travelers. Baudelaire's famous prose poem "Enivrez-vous!" (Get Drunk!) makes this explicit: "Il faut être toujours ivre. Tout est là: c'est l'unique question. Pour ne pas sentir l'horrible fardeau du Temps qui brise vos épaules et vous penche vers la terre, il faut vous enivrer sans trêve. Mais de quoi? De vin, de poésie, ou de vertu, à votre guise. Mais enivrez-vous."

The crucial word is "à votre guise" (as you wish). Baudelaire does not prescribe a single path to ecstasy because what matters is the escape itself, not the vehicle. This is profoundly anti-moralistic and places him at odds with both Catholic orthodoxy (which restricts ecstasy to the mystical) and bourgeois respectability (which restricts intoxication to moderate wine at dinner). Baudelaire's ecstasy is democratic in its availability and aristocratic in its intensity.

The dialectical relationship between ecstasy and Spleen is the deep structure of *Les Fleurs du Mal*. Ecstasy is not the solution to Spleen — it is Spleen's intermittent interruption, the lightning flash that briefly illuminates the darkness before the darkness returns. Baudelaire never claims that ecstasy can be made permanent. Its value lies precisely in its transience: the intensity of the escape is proportional to the weight of the prison.
