# Poitiers

## Location Overview

French territory claimed by [[england|England]], part of the territorial disputes that fuel the Anglo-French [[war|conflict]]. Poitiers (spelled "Poyctiers" in the Folio text) appears in the play as one of the French provinces (001-087) offered by [[king-john|King John]] as dowry (004-093) for his niece [[blanche-of-castile|Blanche]] when she marries [[lewis-the-dauphin|Lewis the Dauphin]], sealing the brief peace between the two crowns.

> For Angiers, and faire Toraine Maine, Poyctiers,
> And all that we vpon this side the Sea,
> (Except this Cittie now by vs besiedg'd)
> Finde liable to our Crowne and Dignitie,
> Shall gild her bridall bed and make her rich
> In titles, honors, and promotions
>
> (004-093–098)

## Role in the Play

### Strategic Significance

Poitiers serves as a key location in the play's geography of [[war|conflict]]. Its position within the french sphere of influence shapes the military and political dynamics that drive the action. The province's transfer to [[lewis-the-dauphin|Lewis]] as part of [[blanche-of-castile|Blanche]]'s dowry represents the territorial concessions [[king-john|John]] is willing to make to secure peace.

### Dramatic Function

The naming of Poitiers among the dowry provinces provides specificity to the play's territorial politics. Along with Anjou, Touraine, and Maine, Poitiers forms part of the Angevin patrimony that both kings claim. The willingness of [[king-john|John]] to give these territories away — territories that rightfully belong to [[arthur|Arthur]] — underscores the play's recurring concern with how legitimacy is traded away for political expediency.

### Associated Characters

Poitiers is most closely associated with [[king-john|King John]], who offers it as a bargaining chip, and with [[blanche-of-castile|Blanche]], whose marriage settlement includes the province. Its fate is inseparable from the larger contest between England and France for control of the Angevin inheritance.
