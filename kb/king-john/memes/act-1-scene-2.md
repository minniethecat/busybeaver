## Section 2

[002-001] K.Iohn. From henceforth beare his name

[002-002] Whose forme thou bearest:

[002-003] Kneele thou downe Philip, but rise more great,

[002-004] Arise Sir Richard, and Plantagenet

[002-005] Bast. Brother by th' mothers side, giue me your hand,

[002-006] My father gaue me [[honour|honor]], yours gaue land:

[002-007] Now blessed be the houre by night or day

[002-008] When I was got, Sir Robert was away

[002-009] Ele. The very spirit of Plantaginet:

[002-010] I am thy grandame Richard, call me so

[002-011] Bast. Madam by chance, but not by truth, what tho;

[002-012] Something about a little from the right,

[002-013] In at the window, or else ore the hatch:

[002-014] Who dares not stirre by day, must walke by night,

[002-015] And haue is haue, how euer men doe catch:

[002-016] Neere or farre off, well wonne is still well shot,

[002-017] And I am I, how ere I was begot

[002-018] K.Iohn. Goe, Faulconbridge, now hast thou thy desire,

[002-019] A landlesse Knight, makes thee a landed Squire:

[002-020] Come Madam, and come Richard, we must speed

[002-021] For [[france|France]], for France, for it is more then need

[002-022] Bast. Brother adieu, good fortune come to thee,

[002-023] For thou wast got i'th way of [[truth|honesty]].

[002-024] Exeunt. all but bastard.

[002-025] Bast. A foot of Honor better then I was,

[002-026] But many a many foot of Land the worse.

[002-027] Well, now can I make any Ioane a Lady,

[002-028] Good den Sir Richard, Godamercy fellow,

[002-029] And if his name be George, Ile call him Peter;

[002-030] For new made honor doth forget mens names:

[002-031] 'Tis two respectiue, and too sociable

[002-032] For your conuersion, now your traueller,

[002-033] Hee and his tooth-picke at my worships messe,

[002-034] And when my knightly stomacke is suffis'd,

[002-035] Why then I sucke my teeth, and catechize

[002-036] My picked man of Countries: my deare sir,

[002-037] Thus leaning on mine elbow I begin,

[002-038] I shall beseech you; that is question now,

[002-039] And then comes answer like an Absey booke:

[002-040] O sir, sayes answer, at your best command,

[002-041] At your employment, at your seruice sir:

[002-042] No sir, saies question, I sweet sir at yours,

[002-043] And so ere answer knowes what question would,

[002-044] Sauing in Dialogue of Complement,

[002-045] And talking of the Alpes and Appenines,

[002-046] The Perennean and the riuer Poe,

[002-047] It drawes toward supper in conclusion so.

[002-048] But this is worshipfull society,

[002-049] And fits the mounting spirit like my selfe;

[002-050] For he is but a bastard to the time

[002-051] That doth not smoake of obseruation,

[002-052] And so am I whether I smacke or no:

[002-053] And not alone in habit and deuice,

[002-054] Exterior forme, outward accoutrement;

[002-055] But from the inward motion to deliuer

[002-056] Sweet, sweet, sweet poyson for the ages tooth,

[002-057] Which though I will not practice to deceiue,

[002-058] Yet to auoid deceit I meane to learne;

[002-059] For it shall strew the footsteps of my rising:

[002-060] But who comes in such haste in riding robes?

[002-061] What woman post is this? hath she no husband

[002-062] That will take paines to blow a horne before her?

[002-063] O me, 'tis my mother: how now good Lady,

[002-064] What brings you heere to Court so hastily?

[002-065] Enter [[lady-faulconbridge|Lady Faulconbridge]] and Iames Gurney.

[002-066] Lady. Where is that slaue thy brother? where is he?

[002-067] That holds in chase mine honour vp and downe

[002-068] Bast. My brother Robert, old Sir Roberts sonne:

[002-069] Colbrand the Gyant, that same mighty man,

[002-070] Is it Sir Roberts sonne that you seeke so?

[002-071] Lady. Sir Roberts sonne, I thou vnreuerend boy,

[002-072] Sir Roberts sonne? why scorn'st thou at sir Robert?

[002-073] He is Sir Roberts sonne, and so art thou

[002-074] Bast. [[james-gurney|Iames Gournie]], wilt thou giue vs leaue a while?

[002-075] Gour. Good leaue good Philip

[002-076] Bast. Philip, sparrow, Iames,

[002-077] There's toyes abroad, anon Ile tell thee more.

[002-078] Exit Iames.

[002-079] Madam, I was not old Sir Roberts sonne,

[002-080] Sir Robert [[power|might]] haue eat his part in me

[002-081] Vpon good Friday, and nere broke his fast:

[002-082] Sir Robert could doe well, marrie to confesse

[002-083] Could get me sir Robert could not doe it;

[002-084] We know his handy-worke, therefore good mother

[002-085] To whom am I beholding for these limmes?

[002-086] Sir Robert neuer holpe to make this legge

[002-087] Lady. Hast thou conspired with thy brother too,

[002-088] That for thine owne gaine shouldst defend mine honor?

[002-089] What meanes this scorne, thou most vntoward knaue?

[002-090] Bast. Knight, knight good mother, Basilisco-like:

[002-091] What, I am dub'd, I haue it on my shoulder:

[002-092] But mother, I am not Sir Roberts sonne,

[002-093] I haue disclaim'd Sir Robert and my land,

[002-094] Legitimation, name, and all is gone;

[002-095] Then good my mother, let me know my father,

[002-096] Some proper man I hope, who was it mother?

[002-097] Lady. Hast thou denied thy selfe a Faulconbridge?

[002-098] Bast. As faithfully as I denie the deuill

[002-099] Lady. King Richard [[king-richard-i|Cordelion]] was thy father,

[002-100] By long and vehement suit I was seduc'd

[002-101] To make roome for him in my husbands bed:

[002-102] Heauen lay not my [[sin|transgression]] to my charge,

[002-103] That art the issue of my deere offence

[002-104] Which was so strongly vrg'd past my defence

[002-105] Bast. Now by this light were I to get againe,

[002-106] Madam I would not wish a better father:

[002-107] Some sinnes doe beare their priuiledge on earth,

[002-108] And so doth yours: your fault, was not your follie,

[002-109] Needs must you lay your heart at his dispose,

[002-110] Subiected tribute to commanding loue,

[002-111] Against whose furie and vnmatched force,

[002-112] The awlesse Lion could not wage the fight,

[002-113] Nor keepe his Princely heart from Richards hand:

[002-114] He that perforce robs Lions of their hearts,

[002-115] May easily winne a womans: aye my mother,

[002-116] With all my heart I thanke thee for my father:

[002-117] Who liues and dares but say, thou didst not well

[002-118] When I was got, Ile send his soule to hell.

[002-119] Come Lady I will shew thee to my kinne,

[002-120] And they shall say, when Richard me begot,

[002-121] If thou hadst sayd him nay, it had beene sinne;

[002-122] Who sayes it was, he lyes, I say twas not.

[002-123] Exeunt.

[002-124] Scaena Secunda.

[002-125] Enter before [[angiers|Angiers]], Philip King of France, Lewis, Daulphin,

[002-126] [[austria|Austria]],

[002-127] [[constance|Constance]], [[arthur|Arthur]].

[002-128] Lewis. Before Angiers well met braue Austria,

[002-129] Arthur that great fore-runner of thy bloud,

[002-130] Richard that rob'd the Lion of his heart,

[002-131] And fought the holy Warres in Palestine,

[002-132] By this braue Duke came early to his graue:

[002-133] And for amends to his posteritie,

[002-134] At our importance hether is he come,

[002-135] To spread his colours boy, in thy behalfe,

[002-136] And to rebuke the vsurpation

[002-137] Of thy vnnaturall Vncle, English Iohn,

[002-138] Embrace him, loue him, giue him welcome hether

[002-139] Arth. God shall forgiue you Cordelions death

[002-140] The rather, that you giue his off-spring life,

[002-141] Shadowing their right vnder your wings of warre:

[002-142] I giue you welcome with a powerlesse hand,

[002-143] But with a heart full of vnstained loue,

[002-144] Welcome before the gates Angiers Duke

[002-145] Lewis. A noble boy, who would not doe thee right?

[002-146] Aust. Vpon thy cheeke lay I this zelous kisse,

[002-147] As seale to this indenture of my loue:

[002-148] That to my home I will no more returne

[002-149] Till Angiers, and the right thou hast in France,

[002-150] Together with that pale, that white-fac'd shore,

[002-151] Whose foot spurnes backe the Oceans roaring tides,

[002-152] And coopes from other lands her Ilanders,

[002-153] Euen till that [[england|England]] hedg'd in with the maine,

[002-154] That Water-walled Bulwarke, still secure

[002-155] And confident from forreine purposes,

[002-156] Euen till that vtmost corner of the West

[002-157] Salute thee for her King, till then faire boy

[002-158] Will I not thinke of home, but follow Armes

[002-159] Const. O take his mothers thanks, a widdows thanks,

[002-160] Till your strong hand shall helpe to giue him strength,

[002-161] To make a more requitall to your loue

[002-162] Aust. The peace of heauen is theirs y lift their swords

[002-163] In such a iust and charitable warre

[002-164] King. Well, then to worke our Cannon shall be bent

[002-165] Against the browes of this resisting towne,

[002-166] Call for our cheefest men of discipline,

[002-167] To cull the plots of best aduantages:

[002-168] Wee'll lay before this towne our Royal bones,

[002-169] Wade to the market-place in French-mens bloud,

[002-170] But we will make it subiect to this boy

[002-171] Con. Stay for an answer to your Embassie,

[002-172] Lest vnaduis'd you staine your swords with bloud,

[002-173] My Lord Chattilion may from England bring

[002-174] That right in peace which heere we vrge in warre,

[002-175] And then we shall repent each drop of bloud,

[002-176] That hot rash haste so indirectly shedde.

[002-177] Enter Chattilion.

[002-178] King. A wonder Lady: lo vpon thy wish

[002-179] Our Messenger Chattilion is arriu'd,

[002-180] What England saies, say breefely gentle Lord,

[002-181] We coldly pause for thee, Chatilion speake,

[002-182] Chat. Then turne your forces from this paltry siege,

[002-183] And stirre them vp against a mightier taske:

[002-184] England impatient of your iust demands,

[002-185] Hath put himselfe in Armes, the aduerse windes

[002-186] Whose leisure I haue staid, haue giuen him time

[002-187] To land his Legions all as soone as I:

[002-188] His marches are expedient to this towne,

[002-189] His forces strong, his Souldiers confident:

[002-190] With him along is come the Mother Queene,

[002-191] An Ace stirring him to bloud and strife,

[002-192] With her her Neece, the Lady Blanch of Spaine,

[002-193] With them a Bastard of the Kings deceast,

[002-194] And all th' vnsetled humors of the Land,

[002-195] Rash, inconsiderate, fiery voluntaries,

[002-196] With Ladies faces, and fierce Dragons spleenes,

[002-197] Haue sold their fortunes at their natiue homes,

[002-198] Bearing their birth-rights proudly on their backs,

[002-199] To make a hazard of new fortunes heere:

[002-200] In briefe, a brauer choyse of dauntlesse spirits

[002-201] Then now the English bottomes haue waft o're,

[002-202] Did neuer flote vpon the swelling tide,

[002-203] To doe offence and scathe in Christendome:

[002-204] The interruption of their churlish drums

[002-205] Cuts off more circumstance, they are at hand,

[002-206] Drum beats.

[002-207] To parlie or to fight, therefore prepare

[002-208] Kin. How much vnlook'd for, is this expedition

[002-209] Aust. By how much vnexpected, by so much

[002-210] We must awake indeuor for defence,

[002-211] For courage mounteth with occasion,

[002-212] Let them be welcome then, we are prepar'd.

[002-220] Fran. Peace be to England, if that warre returne

[002-221] From France to England, there to liue in peace:

[002-222] England we loue, and for that Englands sake,

[002-223] With burden of our armor heere we sweat:

[002-224] This toyle of ours should be a worke of thine;

[002-225] But thou from louing England art so farre,

[002-229] Vpon the maiden vertue of the Crowne:

[002-253] Queen. Out insolent, thy bastard shall be King,

[002-255] Con. My bed was euer to thy sonne as true
