# Revolt of the English Lords

## Event Overview

[[salisbury|Salisbury]], [[pembroke|Pembroke]], and [[bigot|Bigot]] defect to [[lewis-the-dauphin|Lewis]] after [[arthur|Arthur]]'s death, fracturing the English kingdom from within (009-012). The lords' rebellion is the culmination of [[king-john|John]]'s accumulated betrayals: his seizure of the throne from [[arthur|Arthur]], his defiance of [[the-papacy|the Papacy]], and most fatally, the suspected murder of Arthur. The three lords appear together at key turning points throughout the play — from their initial loyalty to John (007-228) to their defection (009-012), their alliance with the French Dauphin (009-260), and their ultimate return to the English cause (010-202).

## Role in the Play

### Narrative Function

Revolt of the English Lords serves as the play's climactic internal crisis. Having survived threats from [[france|France]] and [[the-papacy|Rome]], [[king-john|John]] now faces defection from his own nobility. The lords justify their rebellion as a moral response to Arthur's murder, but the play exposes their self-justification: they switch sides not for justice but for self-preservation. The internal nature of their betrayal is underscored by Salisbury's recognition that rebellion is fundamentally self-destructive — "Is in thy selfe rebellion to thy selfe" (005-257).

### Key Participants

The principal figures in the revolt — [[salisbury|Salisbury]], [[pembroke|Pembroke]], and [[bigot|Bigot]] — represent the English nobility's power to make or break a king. Their unity is dramatised through shared entrances and exits: they appear as a bloc before John (007-228), enter together at the French camp (009-012), and take the field in arms alongside Lewis (009-260). Their defection to [[lewis-the-dauphin|Lewis]] demonstrates that even a legitimate king cannot rule without noble support. [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]] remains John's most loyal defender, and his rebuke of Austria's cry of "Rebellion, flat rebellion" (006-001) voices the play's ambivalence about the lords' cause.

Within the lords' faction, [[salisbury|Salisbury]] is the moral centre — his grief at Arthur's death drives the decision to defect. [[pembroke|Pembroke]] initially hesitates, urging Salisbury to "Stay yet (Lord Salisbury) Ile go with thee" (008-070), but ultimately follows. [[bigot|Bigot]], the third member, encounters both ally and adversary: he meets Salisbury and Pembroke on the road (008-141) and later confronts [[hubert|Hubert]] over Arthur's death.

### Consequences

The revolt drives the play's final act. Once allied with [[lewis-the-dauphin|Lewis]], the lords discover they have exchanged one master for another. Lewis's secret plan to execute them after victory — revealed by the dying [[melun|Melun]] — forces them to return to John's heir, [[prince-henry|Prince Henry]], restoring the kingdom's unity at the cost of the king's life. Their return is marked by a final shared entrance before the English forces (010-202), completing their arc from loyalty through rebellion to reconciliation.
