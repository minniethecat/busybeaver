# Ambition (King John)

**Ambition** is the motor of political action in *[[king-john|King John]]*, driving
the [[war|conflict]]s that structure the entire play. From dynastic ambition to
personal advancement, the pursuit of power is presented as both the
engine of action and the source of tragedy — a [[power|force]] that creates the
conditions for national crisis even as it animates the play's dramatic
energy.

> Commoditie, the byas of the world,
> The world, who of it selfe is peysed well,
> Made to run euen, vpon euen ground;
> Till this aduantage, this vile drawing byas,
> This sway of motion, this commoditie,
> Makes it take head from all indifferency.
>
> (004-185--190)

## Forms of Ambition

The play presents ambition in several interconnected forms:

- **Dynastic ambition**: [[constance]] tirelessly pursues [[arthur]]'s
  claim to the English [[crown|throne]], driven by maternal and political ambition.
  [[queen-elinor|Queen Elinor]] describes how "ambitious [[constance|Constance]] would
  not cease / Till she had kindled [[france|France]] and all the world" (001-109).
  Ambition here is both maternal and territorial.

- **Royal ambition**: [[king-john]] seeks to consolidate power despite
  a weak legal claim, his ambition manifested in the decision to
  imprison and ultimately order the death of [[arthur]], the rival
  claimant. The Bastard observes how [[king-john|John]] "Hath willingly departed with
  a part" to "stop [[arthur|Arthur]]s Title in the whole" (004-174), sacrificing
  territory to secure his crown.

- **Imperial ambition**: [[king-philip-ii|King Philip II]] and
  [[lewis-the-dauphin|Lewis the Dauphin]] seek to expand French power
  through the [[french-invasion-of-england]], their stated concern for
  [[arthur]]'s rights serving as a pretext for territorial ambition.
  Philip himself concedes the war was meant for "our owne vantage"
  (004-160).

- **Ecclesiastical ambition**: [[pandulph|Cardinal Pandulph]] and
  [[the-papacy]] seek supremacy over [[crown|royal authority]] through
  [[johns-excommunication]], their spiritual claims masking
  institutional ambition.

## The Bastard's Critique

[[philip-the-bastard]] provides the play's most acute commentary on
ambition. His famous soliloquy identifies "commodity" — [[commodity|self-interest]] —
as the true [[power|force]] governing all political actors: "That smooth-fac'd
Gentleman, tickling commoditie, / Commoditie, the byas of the world"
(004-185–186). Kings who "Do when they will, and when they say they
will, / The word is not their bond" are driven not by principle but by
the calculation of advantage. The Bastard's own trajectory — from
landless knight to the moral center of the play — offers an alternative
to ambition's logic: he serves the crown rather than seeking to wear
it, and his [[faith|loyalty]] to [[england|England]] transcends the [[commodity|self-interest]] that
motivates those around him.

## Ambition's Paradox

Ambition in *[[king-john|King John]]* is a paradox: it is necessary for action yet
corrosive to [[legitimacy]]. Every character who acts on ambition
achieves power at the cost of moral [[power|authority]]. [[king-john|John]] gains
the crown but loses the [[faith|loyalty]] of his nobles. [[king-philip-ii|Philip]]
gains territory but violates freshly sworn oaths. [[constance]]'s
ambition for her son drives the plot forward but ultimately achieves
nothing — [[arthur]] dies, and the crown passes to [[prince-henry]],
the late-emerging figure who has not sought power but inherits it by
right.

[[king-john|John]]'s territorial concessions to [[france|France]] — "I doe giue [[volquessen|Volquessen]],
Toraine, [[maine|Maine]], / Poyctiers and Aniow, these fiue Prouinces" (004-137–138)
— reveal ambition's cost: the king trades ancestral lands for political
security, demonstrating that unchecked ambition leads to national
diminishment.

The play thus presents ambition as a [[power|force]] that is simultaneously
indispensable and destructive — the energy that produces political
life and the poison that corrupts it. Resolution comes not through the
triumph of any ambitious actor but through the restoration of
legitimate succession and [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]]'s pledge of "true subiection
euerlastingly" (011-197) to a king who has not fought for his crown.
