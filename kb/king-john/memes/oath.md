# Oath

## Definition

The oath in *[[king-john|King John]]* is the primary mechanism by which feudal and personal [[faith|loyalty]] is formalized — and therefore the primary site at which [[faith|loyalty]] fails.

The play presents oaths as instruments whose value depends entirely on the character of those who swear them.

## Manifestations in the Play

### The Lords' Oaths and the Revolt

The rebellion of [[salisbury|Salisbury]], [[pembroke|Pembroke]], and [[bigot|Bigot]] is a crisis of oaths. [[pandulph|Pandulph]]'s manipulation sets "oath to oath" — the lords' feudal oaths to [[king-john|John]] against their new oaths to the French (005-232). The oath becomes not a bond but a weapon: "thou mak'st an oath the surety for thy truth, against an oath the truth" (005-250), exposing how oaths can be wielded to justify any position.

### The Marriage Oath

When King Philip swears to uphold the Anglo-French alliance, he makes a "King's oath" (004-223), yet the oath dissolves as soon as [[pandulph|Pandulph]]'s arrival changes the political calculus. The play exposes the hollowness of oaths sworn for reasons of state.

### Sacred Oaths

[[pandulph|Pandulph]] invokes "the religious strength of sacred vows" (005-197) as the foundation of his [[power|authority]], yet his own use of oaths is thoroughly political. The play contrasts sacred language with secular behavior.
