# Power

## Definition

Power in *[[king-john|King John]]* is never fixed. It cannot be taken for granted, inherited without contest, or held without cost. Shakespeare presents power not as a static possession but as a dynamic, transactional force: it ebbs from [[king-john|John]] as the play progresses, rises in [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]] through moral clarity and patriotic resolve, and is wielded by [[pandulph|Pandulph]] through spiritual coercion alone. The play's political crisis originates in a crisis of legitimacy -- [[king-john|John]] has the [[crown|throne]] but [[arthur|Arthur]] has the better claim -- and from this opening fissure all subsequent action flows. Power in the play operates across four registers: military (armies and swords), institutional (the crown and the Papacy), rhetorical ([[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]]'s wit, [[constance|Constance]]'s grief, [[pandulph|Pandulph]]'s curses), and moral (the authority that accrues to those who serve something larger than themselves).

> The world, who of it selfe is peysed well,
> Made to run euen, vpon euen ground;
> Till this aduantage, this vile drawing byas,
> This sway of motion, this commoditie,
> Makes it take head from all indifferency
> (004-187)

The Bastard's analysis enacts the play's diagnosis: power is not inherently stable; it requires an external force -- "commoditie," or [[commodity|self-interest]] -- to be activated and directed. The play's drama lies in watching different characters discover, wield, and lose that activating force.

## Manifestations in the Play

### Military Power: Force and Its Limits

The bluntest form of power in the play is military force. The opening scenes establish this register immediately: [[king-john|John]] and [[king-philip-ii|Philip]] arrive at [[angiers|Angiers]] with armies, and [[citizen-of-angiers|the Citizen]]'s response defines the stalemate that results when force meets equal force:

> Strength matcht with strength, and power confronted power,
> Both are alike, and both alike we like
> (003-214)

The Citizen's calculus is pragmatic: when two armed powers are evenly matched, neither commands [[faith|allegiance]]. The walls of [[angiers|Angiers]] literally hold out against both claimants until the [[philip-the-bastard|Bastard]] proposes the expedient alliance -- a [[commodity|commodity]]-driven [[marriage|union]] that temporarily merges the two forces against the town. Military power, the play suggests, is only decisive when it is asymmetric. Parity produces paralysis, and paralysis invites the intervention of other forms of power.

[[philip-the-bastard|The Bastard]] himself is first greeted with a physical emblem of diminished force: [[austria|Austria]] offers "a powerlesse hand" (002-142), yet [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]]'s verbal power instantly outmatches [[austria|Austria]]'s martial pretensions. The word "powerlesse" here is pointed: the play consistently demonstrates that the hand unbacked by legitimacy or moral conviction is, in [[truth|fact]], powerless, no matter how many soldiers stand behind it. As [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]] later observes to [[king-john|John]]'s beleaguered forces, "Till your strong hand shall helpe to giue him strength" (002-160) -- power must be actively bestowed by those who wield it, not passively expected.

### Institutional Power: Crown, Papacy, and the Illusion of Permanence

[[king-john|John]]'s power is institutional: he sits on the [[crown|throne]], wears the crown, and issues commands as king. Yet the play systematically dismantles the equation of title with authority. [[king-john|John]]'s double coronation -- "Heere once againe we sit: once againe crown'd" (007-229) -- is the clearest sign of his insecurity: "Was once superfluous: you were [[crown|Crown]]'d before" (007-232). The repetition betrays the emptiness; a crown worn twice does not double one's power, it advertises one's doubt. Power that must be publicly re-staged has already begun to drain away.

Against [[king-john|John]]'s institutional claim stands [[pandulph|Pandulph]]'s ecclesiastical authority -- power that operates through moral and social coercion rather than visible force. "Then by the lawfull power that I haue" (005-136), [[pandulph|Pandulph]] declares, and the political consequences (French invasion, domestic rebellion) follow as if by chain reaction. [[pandulph|Pandulph]]'s power is the power of the curse made actionable: "And raise the power of [[france|France]] vpon his head, / Vnlesse he doe submit himselfe to [[rome|Rome]]" (005-158). The word "power" here shifts meaning from spiritual authority to military force in the space of two lines, demonstrating how one register converts into another in the play's political economy.

Yet the play also exposes the limits of ecclesiastical power. When the target of the curse is already politically isolated, "There is no tongue hath power to curse him right" (005-148). [[constance|Constance]]'s insight is devastating: cursing requires a certain threshold of vulnerability in the accursed. Spiritual power, like military power, is contextual -- it depends on the readiness of others to act upon it. [[pandulph|Pandulph]] can excommunicate [[king-john|John]], but the excommunication only works because [[king-philip-ii|Philip]] of [[france|France]] is willing to be the instrument of its enforcement (and later, because the English lords are willing to [[rebellion|revolt]]). When [[pandulph|Pandulph]] arrives in Act 5 urging the Dauphin to "vse all your power / And from his holinesse" (009-181), his authority has been hollowed out; the Dauphin no longer needs the papal warrant to pursue his own ambition.

### De Jure vs. De Facto Power: John's Decline and the Bastard's Rise

The play's deepest structural insight is the separation of *de jure* power (power by right) from *de [[truth|fact]]o* power (power by effective control). [[arthur|Arthur]] has the better legal claim; [[king-john|John]] has the crown. This gap is the engine of the plot. [[king-john|John]]'s tragic trajectory is a lesson in the insufficiency of mere possession: he imprisons [[arthur|Arthur]], but the imprisonment only confirms his fear; he arranges the boy's death by proxy, but the act costs him his nobles' [[faith|loyalty]]; he submits to [[rome|Rome]], but the submission comes too late to recover his authority.

[[king-john|John]]'s assertion that "Where we doe reigne, we will alone vphold" (005-121) rings hollow precisely because it must be asserted. The true sovereign does not need to declare [[crown|sovereignty]]. His rival claim -- "He that holds his [[kingdom|Kingdom]]e, holds the Law" (005-153) -- is the play's most concentrated statement of de [[truth|fact]]o power theory, but the play's action refutes it: [[king-john|John]] holds the kingdom, yet the law bends against him, through the rebels, through [[pandulph|Pandulph]], through the mere existence of [[arthur|Arthur]]'s claim.

Against [[king-john|John]]'s trajectory stands the [[philip-the-bastard|Bastard]]'s arc. He begins with no power at all -- a bastard, a landless younger son, a figure of comic subversion -- and ends as the voice of national unity. His power is not inherited, not seized, but earned through the consistent exercise of moral clarity. He sees what others refuse to see, and his great soliloquy on [[commodity]] diagnoses the mechanism by which all other characters lose their grip on principle:

> Commoditie, the byas of the world
> (004-186)

"[[sin|Sin]]ce Kings breake faith vpon commoditie" (004-209), [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]] concludes, "Gaine be my Lord, for I will worship thee." The cynicism is tactical: by naming the game, he positions himself above it. The Bastard's journey from this knowing cynicism to genuine patriotic commitment is the play's counter-narrative to [[king-john|John]]'s decline. Where [[king-john|John]]'s power diminishes with every decision, [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]]'s grows -- not because he seeks it, but because he attracts it through demonstrated worth.

### Power and the Body: John's Physical Dissolution

The final acts literalize the loss of power in [[king-john|John]]'s physical body. Poisoned by a monk, [[king-john|John]] experiences powerlessness as fever, as the inability to command even his own temperature -- "Oh vanity of sicknesse" (011-100). The king who once commanded armies cannot command his own limbs. This corporeal dissolution mirrors the political: as [[king-john|John]]'s body fails, the [[kingdom|realm]] fractures, and the lords who abandoned him return not to him but to the idea of [[england|England]] that [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]] has come to embody.

[[prince-henry|Prince Henry]]'s succession (011-187) restores institutional continuity, but the play's final word belongs to [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]]:

> This England neuer did, nor neuer shall
> Lye at the proud foote of a Conqueror,
> But when it first did helpe to wound it selfe.
> (011-204)

> If England to it selfe, do rest but true.
> (011-210)

These closing lines reframe power entirely. It is no longer about kings or Popes, armies or crowns. Power, in [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]]'s final vision, is national self-integrity -- the capacity of a people to remain undivided against external threat. The tragic irony is that this vision is articulated at the very moment it is most precarious: the French dauphin occupies English soil, the king is dead, and the succession is uncertain. Yet the speech does the work of power even as it describes its absence. The Bastard's words do what [[king-john|John]]'s crown could not: they create the possibility of unity out of fragmentation.

## Historical Context

Shakespeare's *[[king-john|King John]]* (c. 1595-1596) was written in the aftermath of the Spanish Armada (1588) and during a period of renewed anxiety about foreign invasion and the Tudor succession. Elizabeth I, like [[king-john|John]], faced excommunication by a Pope (Pius V, 1570) and governed a [[kingdom|realm]] threatened by Catholic powers. The play's exploration of ecclesiastical versus [[crown|royal authority]], and its concluding insistence on national unity against foreign incursion, would have resonated powerfully with an Elizabethan audience that had recently faced the threat of Spanish conquest.

[[king-john|John]] himself was a contested figure in Tudor historiography. Catholic chroniclers portrayed him as a tyrant whose defiance of [[rome|Rome]] brought ruin; Protestant apologists, notably [[king-john|John]] Bale in *King Johan* (c. 1538), recast him as a proto-Reformation hero. Shakespeare follows neither tradition fully: his [[king-john|John]] is neither martyr nor monster, but a study in the erosion of power -- a king who has the title but never the moral authority that genuine [[crown|sovereignty]] requires. The Bastard, a character Shakespeare invented and expanded far beyond his chronicle sources, serves as the audience's surrogate, watching the machinery of power with amused detachment before being drawn into its operation.

## Critical Perspectives

The play has often been read as a transition point in Shakespeare's political thinking, midway between the providential history of the first tetralogy and the Machiavellian world of *Hamlet* and the great tragedies. E.M.W. Tillyard placed *[[king-john|King John]]* within the "Tudor myth" of divine order, but more recent critics have emphasized the play's skepticism about all institutional claims to authority. The Bastard's soliloquy on commodity is the pivot: it exposes power as a market, where [[faith|loyalty]] is bought and sold, and where the only stable currency is [[commodity|self-interest]].

The play's interest in *de [[truth|fact]]o* versus *de jure* power anticipates Hobbesian political philosophy. [[king-john|John]]'s "He that holds his [[kingdom|Kingdom]]e, holds the Law" is a proto-positivist argument about the relationship between sovereign power and legal legitimacy. The play itself, however, seems to side with a third position: that power is neither inherent in bloodlines nor reducible to force, but is a quality of character -- [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]]'s trajectory making the case that legitimate authority is earned through service to the body politic.

## Related Concepts

- **[[legitimacy]]**: The distinction between legal and natural legitimacy, embodied in [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]]'s renunciation of his Faulconbridge inheritance and his claim to Plantagenet blood. Power and legitimacy are inseparable in the play: power without legitimacy is force, legitimacy without power is impotence.
- **[[commodity]]**: The force that bends all human action away from principle. The Bastard's soliloquy on commodity is the play's definitive analysis of how power is acquired, brokered, and betrayed.
- **[[ambition]]**: The motive force that drives characters from [[king-john|John]] to the Dauphin Lewis. [[ambition|Ambition]] is the engine of power-seeking, but the play distinguishes between ambition that consumes the commonwealth ([[king-john|John]]'s) and ambition that serves it ([[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]]'s).
- **[[mercy]]**: The power that [[king-john|John]] conspicuously lacks. His refusal to show [[grace|mercy]] to [[arthur|Arthur]] is both a moral failing and a strategic error: it costs him the [[faith|allegiance]] of his nobles and accelerates his political isolation.
- **[[nature]]**: The Bastard's power is consistently associated with the natural -- his lionhearted bloodline, his physical courage, his untutored [[truth|honesty]] -- as against the artificial, institutional power of courts and churches.
- **[[fortune]]**: Power in the play is subject to fortune's wheel; characters rise ([[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]]) and fall ([[king-john|John]], [[arthur|Arthur]], [[constance|Constance]]) in patterns that suggest the operation of forces beyond individual control.
