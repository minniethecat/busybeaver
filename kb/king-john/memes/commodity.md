## Overview

Commodity — self-interest, expediency, the profit motive — is the central concept of [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard's]] most famous soliloquy in Act 3. After witnessing the sudden alliance between [[king-john|King John]] and [[king-philip-ii|King Philip II]] at the [[the-league-of-angiers|League of Angiers]], [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]] delivers a sardonic meditation on the universal [[power|force]] that drives political action.

The soliloquy opens with the Bastard's disbelieving exclamation:

> Mad world, mad kings, mad composition:
> (004-173)

The speech proceeds to its core diagnosis:

> Commoditie, the byas of the world
> (004-186)

The speech transforms "commodity" from an economic term into a philosophical diagnosis of human motivation. Kings break faith, alliances shift, and principles dissolve — all because of the "smooth-fac'd Gentleman, tickling commoditie."

## The Bastard's Soliloquy

The soliloquy (Act 3, following the League of [[angiers|Angiers]]) is structured as an anatomy of self-interest:

| Passage | Focus |
|---------|-------|
| (004-185)–(004-187) | Commodity as "the bias of the world" — the weight that tilts the balanced globe |
| (004-190)–(004-193) | "This sway of motion" — commodity as the engine of all human action |
| (004-193)–(004-197) | "This Bawd, this Broker, this all-changing-word" — commodity as pander and mediator |
| (004-199) | The Bastard catches himself: "And why rayle I on this Commoditie?" |
| (004-209) | The cynical conclusion: "[[sin|Sin]]ce Kings breake faith vpon commoditie, / Gaine be my Lord, for I will worship thee" |

The rhetorical arc is crucial: [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]] begins by mocking others for their hypocrisy, then realizes he himself is subject to the same [[power|force]]. The soliloquy's genius lies in this self-implicating turn — the satirist is also the satirized.

## Thematic Significance

Commodity is the play's dark answer to the question of why characters act as they do. The French king who swore to champion [[arthur|Arthur]]'s claim accepts a marriage alliance instead. The Pope's legate who thundered excommunication accepts [[king-john|John]]'s submission. [[the-rebel-lords|The English lords]] who rebelled against [[king-john|John]] return to his son. In each case, principle yields to advantage — and [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]]'s soliloquy provides the philosophical framework for understanding this pattern.
