# John's Poisoning

**John's Poisoning** is the climactic death event in *[[king-john|King John]]*, occurring at [[swinstead-abbey]] in Act 5. After retreating to the abbey in the midst of the [[french-invasion-of-england]] and civil war, [[king-john|King John]] is poisoned by a monk — an act that completes the play's arc of royal decline and retribution.

## The Event

The poisoning occurs off-stage. A monk of [[swinstead-abbey|Swinstead Abbey]], reportedly angered by [[king-john|John]]'s desecration of Church property and his long [[war|conflict]] with [[the-papacy]], administers poison to the King. The poison is described as "that fell poison which assayleth him" (011-095), a slow-acting venom that produces intense internal burning.

> That being brought into the open ayre,
> It would allay the burning qualitie
> Of that fell poison which assayleth him.
>
> Oh vanity of sicknesse: fierce extreames
> In their continuance, will not feele themselues.
>
> (011-094) News of the king's decline spreads rapidly through the English camp: "They say King Iohn sore sick, hath left the field" (010-208), a report that signals the collapse of royal authority even before death arrives.

## John's Final Hours

The poisoned King's suffering is described in vivid detail:

- **Physical agony**: [[king-john|John]] experiences burning fever and consuming thirst. [[prince-henry|Prince Henry]]'s lament — "Oh vanity of sicknesse: fierce extreames" (011-100) — captures both the physical reality and the philosophical shock of seeing a king reduced to a suffering body.
- **Mental torment**: He is haunted by the consequences of his reign — [[arthurs-death]], the [[revolt-of-the-english-lords]], and the devastation of his kingdom. The Bastard, too, feels the weight: "oh, my heart is sicke" (010-185), as the loyal soldier confronts the collapse of the cause he has served.
- **Spiritual reckoning**: Having been excommunicated ([[johns-excommunication]]) and later reconciled to [[rome|Rome]] through [[johns-submission-to-pandulph]], [[king-john|John]]'s spiritual state at death is ambiguous. The play's imagery of sickness extends well beyond John himself — "the present time's so sicke" (009-189) — suggesting that the king's bodily decay mirrors the kingdom's political disease.

> The poisoning at Swinstead Abbey represents the final convergence of the play's major themes: royal authority, ecclesiastical power, and the inescapable consequences of political violence (011-095).

## Consequences

[[king-john|John]]'s poisoning precipitates the play's rapid denouement:

1. **[[prince-henry|Prince Henry]]'s succession**: [[prince-henry]] assumes command and negotiates peace
2. **[[peace-settlement]]**: [[the-rebel-lords|The English lords]], learning of [[king-john|John]]'s death, abandon [[lewis-the-dauphin]] and return to their [[faith|allegiance]]
3. **Resolution**: The play ends with the kingdom united under a new, legitimate king

## Historical Basis

The historical [[king-john|King John]] died at Newark Castle in October 1216, possibly of dysentery, after losing his baggage train (including the [[crown|Crown]] Jewels) in the Wash. The poisoning-by-monk narrative is a later embellishment that Shakespeare adopts for dramatic effect, transforming a bureaucratic death into a morally resonant assassination.
