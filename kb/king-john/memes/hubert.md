# Hubert

## Character Overview

A citizen of [[angiers|Angiers]] turned [[king-john|John]]'s agent, whose moral crisis over the order to kill [[arthur|Arthur]] produces the play's most intense scene of conscience. Hubert first appears as part of John's retinue — "Enter Iohn, Arthur, Hubert" (006-059) — an entry that already ties him to the young prince whose fate will define his dramatic arc.

The moment that seals Hubert's dramatic fate is brief but consequential. Returning from battle, [[king-john|John]] entrusts the young [[arthur|Arthur]] to Hubert's custody with a terse command:

> Hubert, keepe this boy: Philip make vp,
> My Mother is assayled in our Tent,
> And tane I feare
>
> (006-061)

This handover, delivered in the urgency of combat, sets in motion the play's central moral crisis — the order to blind and kill a child, and Hubert's eventual refusal to carry it out. The order itself is delivered with brutal directness: "Hubert, away with him: imprison him" (008-134), and the imprisonment of Arthur becomes the play's most charged dramatic situation. The battlefield report — "Arthur tane prisoner? diuers deere friends slaine?" (006-162) — confirms the prince's capture and foreshadows the tragedy to come.

## Role in the Play

### The Blinding Scene

Hubert's dramatic centre is the scene in which he prepares to carry out John's order to blind Arthur. The scene is Shakespeare's most sustained exploration of the conflict between obedience and conscience. Hubert is not a villain — he is a subordinate caught between his duty to the king and his revulsion at the act demanded of him. The scene's power lies in the gradual shift of Hubert's resolve: he begins as John's instrument and ends as Arthur's protector, moved by the boy's eloquence and his own latent humanity.

### Political Position

As a member of the english [[truth|fact]]ion, Hubert navigates the shifting alliances of *[[king-john|King John]]* with consequences that ripple through the play's political architecture. His decision to spare Arthur has profound political ramifications: it delays but does not prevent Arthur's death, and when Arthur dies in the attempt to escape, Hubert is immediately suspected. His confrontation with [[salisbury|Salisbury]], [[pembroke|Pembroke]], and [[bigot|Bigot]] — who demand to know whether he killed Arthur — places him at the centre of the play's crisis of trust. The lords' accusation cuts to the heart of his character: "(If thou didst this deed of death) art y damn'd Hubert" (009-128).

### Dramatic Function

Hubert serves as a key node in the play's network of [[faith|loyalty]] and betrayal. His choice to spare Arthur is the one act of mercy in a play otherwise dominated by political calculation. Yet that mercy is tragically incomplete: Arthur dies anyway, and Hubert's moral victory proves politically meaningless. The character illuminates the play's central theme — that individual conscience, however admirable, cannot withstand the machinery of power.
