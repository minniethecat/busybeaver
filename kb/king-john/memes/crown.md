# Crown

## Definition

The crown in *[[king-john|King John]]* is both an object and an idea. As an object, it can be worn, seized, or surrendered. As an idea, it represents the legitimacy that transforms a person into a sovereign. The play's central tension is that [[king-john|John]] wears the crown while [[arthur|Arthur]] has the better claim to it (002-240).

## Manifestations in the Play

### Crown and Legitimacy

The crown's power derives from the [[faith|belief]] that its wearer rules by right. [[king-john|John]]'s occupancy of the throne is a [[truth|fact]]; [[arthur|Arthur]]'s claim is a right. The play asks whether the crown can confer legitimacy on an illegitimate occupant, or whether the illegitimacy of the occupant contaminates the crown itself. The question is posed directly: "Doth not the Crowne of England, prooue the King?" (003-150).

### The Hollow Crown

[[king-john|John]]'s eventual surrender of his crown to [[pandulph|Pandulph]] — receiving it back as a papal vassal — dramatizes the crown's ultimate emptiness (008-131). John had already been crowned once; as Pandulph observes, "you were Crown'd before" (007-232), making the second coronation a ritual of submission rather than elevation. A crown that can be given and taken back by a foreign prelate is not a crown at all but a piece of metal. The ceremony exposes the fiction at the heart of royal authority.

> Take againe
> From this my hand, as holding of the Pope
> Your Soueraigne greatnesse and authoritie
>
> (009-178)

When [[king-john|John]] submits to a second coronation, [[pembroke|Pembroke]] quietly registers the absurdity of a king being crowned twice. The rebuke is measured — "but that your Highnes pleas'd" — yet unmistakable: the ritual adds nothing because the crown, once placed, cannot be taken off by ceremony alone:

> This once again (but that your Highnes pleas'd)
> Was once superfluous: you were Crown'd before,
> And that high Royalty was nere pluck'd off:
>
> (007-232)

### The Bastard and the Crown

The Bastard, who has no claim to any crown, becomes the play's most articulate defender of the crown's symbolic value. His final speech reclaims the crown not as [[king-john|John]]'s possession but as [[england|England]]'s — a symbol of national unity that transcends the individuals who wear it.
