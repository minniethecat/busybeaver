## Section 7

[007-001] Dol. There's nothing in this world can make me ioy,

[007-002] Life is as tedious as a twice-told tale,

[007-003] Vexing the dull eare of a drowsie man;

[007-004] And bitter shame hath spoyl'd the sweet words taste,

[007-005] That it yeelds nought but shame and bitternesse

[007-006] Pand. Before the curing of a strong disease,

[007-007] Euen in the instant of repaire and health,

[007-008] The fit is strongest: Euils that take leaue

[007-009] On their departure, most of all shew euill:

[007-010] What haue you lost by losing of this day?

[007-011] Dol. All daies of glory, ioy, and happinesse

[007-012] Pan. If you had won it, certainely you had.

[007-013] No, no: when [[fortune|Fortune]] meanes to men most good,

[007-014] Shee lookes vpon them with a threatning eye:

[007-015] 'Tis strange to thinke how much King Iohn hath lost

[007-016] In this which he accounts so clearely wonne:

[007-017] Are not you grieu'd that [[arthur|Arthur]] is his prisoner?

[007-018] Dol. As heartily as he is glad he hath him

[007-019] Pan. Your minde is all as youthfull as your blood.

[007-020] Now heare me speake with a propheticke spirit:

[007-021] For euen the breath of what I meane to speake,

[007-022] Shall blow each dust, each straw, each little rub

[007-023] Out of the path which shall directly lead

[007-024] Thy foote to Englands Throne. And therefore marke:

[007-025] Iohn hath seiz'd Arthur, and it cannot be,

[007-026] That whiles warme life playes in that infants veines,

[007-027] The mis-plac'dIohn should entertaine an houre,

[007-028] One minute, nay one quiet breath of rest.

[007-029] A Scepter snatch'd with an vnruly hand,

[007-030] Must be as boysterously maintain'd as gain'd.

[007-031] And he that stands vpon a slipp'ry place,

[007-032] Makes nice of no vilde hold to stay him vp:

[007-033] That Iohn may stand, then Arthur needs must fall,

[007-034] So be it, for it cannot be but so

[007-035] Dol. But what shall I gaine by yong Arthurs fall?

[007-036] Pan. You, in the right of Lady Blanch your wife,

[007-037] May then make all the claime that Arthur did

[007-038] Dol. And loose it, life and all, as Arthur did

[007-039] Pan. How green you are, and fresh in this old world?

[007-040] Iohn layes you plots: the times conspire with you,

[007-041] For he that steepes his safetie in true blood,

[007-042] Shall finde but bloodie safety, and vntrue.

[007-043] This Act so euilly borne shall coole the hearts

[007-044] Of all his people, and freeze vp their zeale,

[007-045] That none so small aduantage shall step forth

[007-046] To checke his reigne, but they will cherish it.

[007-047] No naturall exhalation in the skie,

[007-048] No scope of Nature, no distemper'd day,

[007-049] No common winde, no customed euent,

[007-050] But they will plucke away his naturall cause,

[007-051] And call them Meteors, prodigies, and signes,

[007-052] Abbortiues, presages, and tongues of heauen,

[007-053] Plainly denouncing vengeance vpon Iohn

[007-054] Dol. May be he will not touch yong Arthurs life,

[007-055] But hold himselfe safe in his prisonment

[007-056] Pan. O Sir, when he shall heare of your approach,

[007-057] If that yong Arthur be not gone alreadie,

[007-058] Euen at that newes he dies: and then the hearts

[007-059] Of all his people shall reuolt from him,

[007-060] And kisse the lippes of vnacquainted change,

[007-061] And picke strong matter of reuolt, and wrath

[007-062] Out of the bloody fingers ends of Iohn.

[007-063] Me thinkes I see this hurley all on foot;

[007-064] And O, what better matter breeds for you,

[007-065] Then I haue nam'd. The Bastard Falconbridge

[007-066] Is now in [[england|England]] ransacking the Church,

[007-067] Offending Charity: If but a dozen French

[007-068] Were there in Armes, they would be as a Call

[007-069] To traine ten thousand English to their side;

[007-070] Or, as a little snow, tumbled about,

[007-071] Anon becomes a Mountaine. O noble Dolphine,

[007-072] Go with me to the King, 'tis wonderfull,

[007-073] What may be wrought out of their discontent,

[007-074] Now that their soules are topfull of [[sin|offence]],

[007-075] For England go; I will whet on the King

[007-076] Dol. Strong reasons makes strange actions: let vs go,

[007-077] If you say I, the King will not say no.

[007-078] Exeunt.

[007-079] Actus Quartus, Scaena prima.

[007-080] Enter [[hubert|Hubert]] and Executioners.

[007-081] Hub. Heate me these Irons hot, and looke thou stand

[007-082] Within the Arras: when I strike my foot

[007-083] Vpon the bosome of the ground, rush forth

[007-084] And binde the boy, which you shall finde with me

[007-085] Fast to the chaire: be heedfull: hence, and watch

[007-086] Exec. I hope your warrant will beare out the deed

[007-087] Hub. Vncleanly scruples feare not you: looke too't.

[007-088] Yong Lad come forth; I haue to say with you.

[007-089] Enter Arthur.

[007-090] Ar. Good morrow Hubert

[007-091] Hub. Good morrow, little Prince

[007-092] Ar. As little Prince, hauing so great a Title

[007-093] To be more Prince, as may be: you are sad

[007-094] Hub. Indeed I haue beene merrier

[007-095] Art. 'Mercie on me:

[007-096] Me thinkes no body should be sad but I:

[007-097] Yet I remember, when I was in [[france|France]],

[007-098] Yong Gentlemen would be as sad as night

[007-099] Onely for wantonnesse: by my Christendome,

[007-100] So I were out of prison, and kept Sheepe

[007-101] I should be as merry as the day is long:

[007-102] And so I would be heere, but that I doubt

[007-103] My Vnckle practises more harme to me:

[007-104] He is affraid of me, and I of him:

[007-105] Is it my fault, that I was Geffreyes sonne?

[007-106] No in deede is't not: and I would to heauen

[007-107] I were your sonne, so you would loue me, Hubert:

[007-108] Hub. If I talke to him, with his innocent prate

[007-109] He will awake my mercie, which lies dead:

[007-110] Therefore I will be sodaine, and dispatch

[007-111] Ar. Are you sicke Hubert? you looke pale to day,

[007-112] Insooth I would you were a little sicke,

[007-113] That I [[power|might]] sit all night, and watch with you.

[007-114] I warrant I loue you more then you do me

[007-115] Hub. His words do take possession of my bosome.

[007-116] Reade heere yong Arthur. How now foolish rheume?

[007-117] Turning dispitious torture out of doore?

[007-118] I must be breefe, least resolution drop

[007-119] Out at mine eyes, in tender womanish teares.

[007-120] Can you not reade it? Is it not faire writ?

[007-121] Ar. Too fairely Hubert, for so foule effect,

[007-122] Must you with hot Irons, burne out both mine eyes?

[007-123] Hub. Yong Boy, I must

[007-124] Art. And will you?

[007-125] Hub. And I will

[007-126] Art. Haue you the heart? When your head did but

[007-127] ake,

[007-128] I knit my hand-kercher about your browes

[007-129] (The best I had, a Princesse wrought it me)

[007-130] And I did neuer aske it you againe:

[007-131] And with my hand, at midnight held your head;

[007-132] And like the watchfull minutes, to the houre,

[007-133] Still and anon cheer'd vp the heauy time;

[007-134] Saying, what lacke you? and where lies your greefe?

[007-135] Or what good loue may I performe for you?

[007-136] Many a poore mans sonne would haue lyen still,

[007-137] And nere haue spoke a louing word to you:

[007-138] But you, at your sicke seruice had a Prince:

[007-139] Nay, you may thinke my loue was craftie loue,

[007-140] And call it cunning. Do, and if you will,

[007-141] If heauen be pleas'd that you must vse me ill,

[007-142] Why then you must. Will you put out mine eyes?

[007-143] These eyes, that neuer did, nor neuer shall

[007-144] So much as frowne on you

[007-145] Hub. I haue sworne to do it:

[007-146] And with hot Irons must I burne them out

[007-147] Ar. Ah, none but in this Iron Age, would do it:

[007-148] The Iron of it selfe, though heate red hot,

[007-149] Approaching neere these eyes, would drinke my teares,

[007-150] And quench this fierie indignation,

[007-151] Euen in the matter of mine innocence:

[007-152] Nay, after that, consume away in rust,

[007-153] But for containing fire to harme mine eye:

[007-154] Are you more stubborne hard, then hammer'd Iron?

[007-155] And if an Angell should haue come to me,

[007-156] And told me Hubert should put out mine eyes,

[007-157] I would not haue beleeu'd him: no tongue but Huberts

[007-158] Hub. Come forth: Do as I bid you do

[007-159] Art. O saue me Hubert, saue me: my eyes are out

[007-160] Euen with the fierce lookes of these bloody men

[007-161] Hub. Giue me the Iron I say, and binde him heere

[007-162] Art. Alas, what neede you be so boistrous rough?

[007-163] I will not struggle, I will stand stone still:

[007-164] For heauen sake Hubert let me not be bound:

[007-165] Nay heare me Hubert, driue these men away,

[007-166] And I will sit as quiet as a Lambe.

[007-167] I will not stirre, nor winch, nor speake a word,

[007-168] Nor looke vpon the Iron angerly:

[007-169] Thrust but these men away, and Ile forgiue you,

[007-170] What euer torment you do put me too

[007-171] Hub. Go stand within: let me alone with him

[007-172] Exec. I am best pleas'd to be from such a deede

[007-173] Art. Alas, I then haue chid away my friend,

[007-174] He hath a sterne looke, but a gentle heart:

[007-175] Let him come backe, that his compassion may

[007-176] Giue life to yours

[007-177] Hub. Come (Boy) prepare your selfe

[007-178] Art. Is there no remedie?

[007-179] Hub. None, but to lose your eyes

[007-180] Art. O heauen: that there were but a moth in yours,

[007-181] A graine, a dust, a gnat, a wandering haire,

[007-182] Any annoyance in that precious sense:

[007-183] Then feeling what small things are boysterous there,

[007-184] Your vilde intent must needs seeme horrible

[007-185] Hub. Is this your promise? Go too, hold your toong

[007-186] Art. Hubert, the vtterance of a brace of tongues,

[007-187] Must needes want pleading for a paire of eyes:

[007-188] Let me not hold my tongue: let me not Hubert,

[007-189] Or Hubert, if you will cut out my tongue,

[007-190] So I may keepe mine eyes. O spare mine eyes,

[007-191] Though to no vse, but still to looke on you.

[007-192] Loe, by my troth, the Instrument is cold,

[007-193] And would not harme me

[007-194] Hub. I can heate it, Boy

[007-195] Art. No, in good sooth: the fire is dead with griefe,

[007-196] Being create for comfort, to be vs'd

[007-197] In vndeserued extreames: See else your selfe,

[007-198] There is no malice in this burning cole,

[007-199] The breath of heauen, hath blowne his spirit out,

[007-200] And strew'd repentant ashes on his head

[007-201] Hub. But with my breath I can reuiue it Boy

[007-202] Art. And if you do, you will but make it blush,

[007-203] And glow with shame of your proceedings, Hubert:

[007-204] Nay, it perchance will sparkle in your eyes:

[007-205] And, like a dogge that is compell'd to fight,

[007-206] Snatch at his Master that doth tarre him on.

[007-207] All things that you should vse to do me wrong

[007-208] Deny their office: onely you do lacke

[007-209] That mercie, which fierce fire, and Iron extends,

[007-210] Creatures of note for [[grace|mercy]], lacking vses

[007-211] Hub. Well, see to liue: I will not touch thine eye,

[007-212] For all the Treasure that thine Vnckle owes,

[007-213] Yet am I sworne, and I did purpose, Boy,

[007-214] With this same very Iron, to burne them out

[007-215] Art. O now you looke like Hubert. All this while

[007-216] You were disguis'd

[007-217] Hub. [[peace|Peace]]: no more. Adieu,

[007-218] Your Vnckle must not know but you are dead.

[007-219] Ile fill these dogged Spies with false reports:

[007-220] And, pretty childe, sleepe doubtlesse, and secure,

[007-221] That Hubert for the wealth of all the world,

[007-222] Will not offend thee

[007-223] Art. O heauen! I thanke you Hubert

[007-224] Hub. Silence, no more; go closely in with mee,

[007-225] Much danger do I vndergo for thee.

[007-226] Exeunt.

[007-227] Scena Secunda.

[007-228] Enter Iohn, [[pembroke|Pembroke]], [[salisbury|Salisbury]], and other Lordes.

[007-229] Iohn. Heere once againe we sit: once against crown'd

[007-230] And look'd vpon, I hope, with chearefull eyes

[007-231] Pem. This once again (but that your Highnes pleas'd)

[007-232] Was once superfluous: you were [[crown|Crown]]'d before,

[007-233] And that high Royalty was nere pluck'd off:

[007-234] The faiths of men, nere stained with reuolt:

[007-235] Fresh expectation troubled not the Land

[007-236] With any long'd-for-change, or better State

[007-237] Sal. Therefore, to be possess'd with double pompe,

[007-238] To guard a Title, that was rich before;

[007-239] To gilde refined Gold, to paint the Lilly;

[007-240] To throw a perfume on the Violet,

[007-241] To smooth the yce, or adde another hew

[007-242] Vnto the Raine-bow; or with Taper-light

[007-243] To seeke the beauteous eye of heauen to garnish,

[007-244] Is wastefull, and ridiculous excesse

[007-245] Pem. But that your Royall pleasure must be done,

[007-246] This acte, is as an ancient tale new told,

[007-247] And, in the last repeating, troublesome,

[007-248] Being vrged at a time vnseasonable

[007-249] Sal. In this the Anticke, and well noted face

[007-250] Of plaine old forme, is much disfigured,

[007-251] And like a shifted winde vnto a saile,

[007-252] It makes the course of thoughts to fetch about,

[007-253] Startles, and frights consideration:

[007-254] Makes sound opinion sicke, and truth suspected,

[007-255] For putting on so new a fashion'd robe
