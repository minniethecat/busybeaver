# Kingdom

## Definition

The kingdom in *[[king-john|King John]]* is more than territory — it is the prize over which all political action is contested. [[england|England]] and [[france|France]] are rivals, but the kingdom as an idea transcends the individuals who claim it.

## Manifestations in the Play

### Kingdom as Inheritance

[[arthur|Arthur]]'s claim to the English kingdom is the play's originating [[war|conflict]]. [[king-john|John]] holds the kingdom by possession; [[arthur|Arthur]] claims it by right. The kingdom is thus figured as a possession that can be bequeathed, inherited, or seized — a commodity whose ownership is always contestable.

### Kingdom Divided

The French invasion and English lords' defection fracture the kingdom from within. When [[salisbury|Salisbury]] and [[pembroke|Pembroke]] join Lewis, the kingdom ceases to be a unified political entity. The play's darkest moments are those in which the kingdom is literally at war with itself.

### The Bastard and the Kingdom's Survival

The Bastard's final speech reclaims the kingdom as an ideal worth defending. His patriotism is not [[faith|loyalty]] to [[king-john|John]] personally but to the kingdom as a continuing entity — "this [[england|England]]." The play ends not with the king's death but with an assertion of the kingdom's endurance beyond any individual ruler.
