# Vow

## Definition

In *[[king-john|King John]]*, the vow carries a specifically religious and personal gravity that distinguishes it from the political oath. Whereas oaths bind in the feudal and diplomatic [[kingdom|realm]], vows invoke divine witness and personal conscience.

## Manifestations in the Play

### Pandulph and Sacred Vows

[[pandulph|Pandulph]] derives his [[power|authority]] from "the religious strength of sacred vows" (005-197). His excommunication of [[king-john|John]] is framed as an act of ecclesiastical duty — a vow to defend the Church — yet the play exposes how easily sacred language serves political ends.

> With all religous strength of sacred vowes,
> The latest breath that gaue the sound of words
> Was deepe-sworne faith, peace, amity, true loue
> Betweene our kingdomes and our royall selues,
>
> (005-197)

### Vows and Conscience

When [[constance|Constance]] invokes divine justice for [[arthur|Arthur]]'s cause, she frames it as a vow "most divinely vowed upon the right" (003-114). Her vow is maternal and moral, not political, and its frustration drives her toward madness. The play suggests that vows grounded in personal conviction, rather than political calculation, carry a psychological weight that cannot be simply discarded.

### Vows Broken

The Bastard's soliloquy names the central problem: "he that wins of all, that daily break-vow" (004-181) — the successful political actor is precisely the one who treats vows as disposable. Against this, the play asks whether any vow can survive in a world governed by commodity.
