## Overview

Peter of Pomfret is a prophet who appears in Act 4 of *[[king-john|King John]]*. Brought before the king by [[philip-the-bastard|Philip the Bastard]], he delivers a prophecy that [[king-john|King John]] will give up his crown before Ascension Day at noon. [[king-john|John]] dismisses him as an "idle dreamer," but the prophecy comes true when [[king-john|John]] surrenders his crown to the papal legate [[pandulph|Pandulph]] — an act [[king-john|John]] later claims was voluntary rather than compelled.

## The Prophecy

Peter of Pomfret is introduced when [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]] brings him before [[king-john|King John]], presenting him as a prophet who has attracted a large following:

> And here's a Prophet that I brought with me
> From forth the streets of Pomfret, whom I found
> With many hundreds treading on his heeles:
> To whom he sung in rude harsh sounding rimes,
> That ere the next Ascension day at noone,
> Your Highnes should deliuer vp your Crowne
> (008-126)

The prophecy is specific in its terms — the king must surrender the crown *before* noon on Ascension Day, a major feast in the Christian calendar. The detail that Peter sang his prophecy "in rude harsh sounding rimes" suggests a folk-prophet figure whose crude verse nonetheless attracted a substantial popular audience.

## John's Response

[[king-john|King John]] reacts with contempt to the prophecy, dismissing Peter with a single line:

> Thou idle Dreamer, wherefore didst thou so?
> (008-132)

[[king-john|John]]'s dismissal of Peter as an "idle dreamer" reveals the king's characteristic impatience with those who challenge his [[power|authority]]. At this point in the play, [[king-john|John]] has already defied the Pope and is at war with [[france|France]]; a folk prophet from Pomfret is beneath his notice.

## The Prophecy Fulfilled

The prophecy's dramatic [[power|force]] is felt in Act 5, when [[king-john|John]] himself recalls it on Ascension Day:

> Iohn. Is this Ascension day? did not the Prophet
> Say, that before Ascension day at noone,
> My Crowne I should giue off? euen so I haue:
> I did suppose it should be on constraint,
> But (heau'n be thank'd) it is but voluntary.
> (009-201)

The dramatic irony is layered: [[king-john|John]] has indeed given up his crown — not to a rival monarch but to [[pandulph|Pandulph]], the Pope's representative. By accepting his crown back from [[pandulph|Pandulph]], [[king-john|John]] becomes the Pope's vassal. His insistence that the surrender was "voluntary" (009-205) underscores how thoroughly his political position has collapsed.

## Dramatic Function

Peter of Pomfret serves several dramatic purposes in the play:

| Function | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Prophetic Foreshadowing** | The prophecy creates anticipation that [[king-john|John]] will lose his crown, building tension toward the play's climax |
| **Popular Unrest** | Peter's "many hundreds" of followers signal widespread discontent with [[king-john|John]]'s rule |
| **Dramatic Irony** | [[king-john|John]]'s mockery of Peter contrasts with the prophecy's accurate fulfillment |
| **Thematic Resonance** | The prophecy reinforces the play's preoccupation with legitimacy, usurpation, and divine versus earthly [[power|authority]] |

Though Peter of Pomfret appears only briefly, his prophecy provides a narrative thread that connects the play's middle acts to its resolution. His presence also illustrates how Shakespeare uses minor characters to voice popular sentiment and create a sense of [[power|force]]s beyond any one character's control.
