## Section 11

[011-001] Exeunt.

[011-002] Scena Quinta.

[011-003] Enter Dolphin, and his Traine.

[011-004] Dol. The Sun of heauen (me thought) was loth to set;

[011-005] But staid, and made the Westerne Welkin blush,

[011-006] When English measure backward their owne ground

[011-007] In faint Retire: Oh brauely came we off,

[011-008] When with a volley of our needlesse shot,

[011-009] After such bloody toile, we bid good night,

[011-010] And woon'd our tott'ring colours clearly vp,

[011-011] Last in the field, and almost Lords of it.

[011-012] Enter a Messenger.

[011-013] Mes. Where is my Prince, the Dolphin?

[011-014] Dol. Heere: what newes?

[011-015] Mes. The Count Meloone is slaine: The English Lords

[011-016] By his perswasion, are againe falne off,

[011-017] And your supply, which you haue wish'd so long,

[011-018] Are cast away, and sunke on [[goodwin-sands|Goodwin sands]]

[011-019] Dol. Ah fowle, shrew'd newes. Beshrew thy very hart:

[011-020] I did not thinke to be so sad to night

[011-021] As this hath made me. Who was he that said

[011-022] King Iohn did flie an houre or two before

[011-023] The stumbling night did part our wearie powres?

[011-024] Mes. Who euer spoke it, it is true my Lord

[011-025] Dol. Well: keepe good quarter, & good care to night,

[011-026] The day shall not be vp so soone as I,

[011-027] To try the faire aduenture of to morrow.

[011-028] Exeunt.

[011-029] Scena Sexta.

[011-030] Enter Bastard and [[hubert|Hubert]], seuerally.

[011-031] Hub. Whose there? Speake hoa, speake quickely, or

[011-032] I shoote

[011-033] Bast. A Friend. What art thou?

[011-034] Hub. Of the part of [[england|England]]

[011-035] Bast. Whether doest thou go?

[011-036] Hub. What's that to thee?

[011-037] Why may not I demand of thine affaires,

[011-038] As well as thou of mine?

[011-039] Bast. Hubert, I thinke

[011-040] Hub. Thou hast a perfect thought:

[011-041] I will vpon all hazards well beleeue

[011-042] Thou art my friend, that know'st my tongue so well:

[011-043] Who art thou?

[011-044] Bast. Who thou wilt: and if thou please

[011-045] Thou maist be-friend me so much, as to thinke

[011-046] I come one way of the Plantagenets

[011-047] Hub. Vnkinde remembrance: thou, & endles night,

[011-048] Haue done me shame: Braue Soldier, pardon me,

[011-049] That any accent breaking from thy tongue,

[011-050] Should scape the true acquaintance of mine eare

[011-051] Bast. Come, come: sans complement, What newes

[011-052] abroad?

[011-053] Hub. Why heere walke I in the black brow of night

[011-054] To finde you out

[011-055] Bast. Breefe then: and what's the newes?

[011-056] Hub. O my sweet sir, newes fitting to the night,

[011-057] Blacke, fearefull, comfortlesse, and horrible

[011-058] Bast. Shew me the very wound of this ill newes,

[011-059] I am no woman, Ile not swound at it

[011-060] Hub. The King I feare is poyson'd by a Monke,

[011-061] I left him almost speechlesse, and broke out

[011-062] To acquaint you with this euill, that you [[power|might]]

[011-063] The better arme you to the sodaine time,

[011-064] Then if you had at leisure knowne of this

[011-065] Bast. How did he take it? Who did taste to him?

[011-066] Hub. A Monke I tell you, a resolued villaine

[011-067] Whose Bowels sodainly burst out: The King

[011-068] Yet speakes, and peraduenture may recouer

[011-069] Bast. Who didst thou leaue to tend his Maiesty?

[011-070] Hub. Why know you not? The Lords are all come

[011-071] backe,

[011-072] And brought [[prince-henry|Prince Henry]] in their companie,

[011-073] At whose request the king hath pardon'd them,

[011-074] And they are all about his Maiestie

[011-075] Bast. With-hold thine indignation, mighty heauen,

[011-076] And tempt vs not to beare aboue our power.

[011-077] Ile tell thee Hubert, halfe my power this night

[011-078] Passing these Flats, are taken by the Tide,

[011-079] These Lincolne-Washes haue deuoured them,

[011-080] My selfe, well mounted, hardly haue escap'd.

[011-081] Away before: Conduct me to the king,

[011-082] I doubt he will be dead, or ere I come.

[011-083] Exeunt.

[011-084] Scena Septima.

[011-085] Enter Prince Henry, Salisburie, and [[bigot|Bigot]].

[011-086] Hen. It is too late, the life of all his blood

[011-087] Is touch'd, corruptibly: and his pure braine

[011-088] (Which some suppose the soules fraile dwelling house)

[011-089] Doth by the idle Comments that it makes,

[011-090] Fore-tell the ending of [[death|mortality]].

[011-091] Enter [[pembroke|Pembroke]].

[011-092] Pem. His Highnesse yet doth speak, & holds beleefe,

[011-093] That being brought into the open ayre,

[011-094] It would allay the burning qualitie

[011-095] Of that fell poison which assayleth him

[011-096] Hen. Let him be brought into the Orchard heere:

[011-097] Doth he still rage?

[011-098] Pem. He is more patient

[011-099] Then when you left him; euen now he sung

[011-100] Hen. Oh vanity of sicknesse: fierce extreames

[011-101] In their continuance, will not feele themselues.

[011-102] Death hauing praide vpon the outward parts

[011-103] Leaues them inuisible, and his seige is now

[011-104] Against the winde, the which he prickes and wounds

[011-105] With many legions of strange fantasies,

[011-106] Which in their throng, and presse to that last hold,

[011-107] Counfound themselues. 'Tis strange y death shold sing:

[011-108] I am the Symet to this pale faint Swan,

[011-109] Who chaunts a dolefull hymne to his owne death,

[011-110] And from the organ-pipe of frailety sings

[011-111] His soule and body to their lasting rest

[011-112] Sal. Be of good comfort (Prince) for you are borne

[011-113] To set a forme vpon that indigest

[011-114] Which he hath left so shapelesse, and so rude.

[011-115] Iohn brought in.

[011-116] Iohn. I marrie, now my soule hath elbow roome,

[011-117] It would not out at windowes, nor at doores,

[011-118] There is so hot a summer in my bosome,

[011-119] That all my bowels crumble vp to dust:

[011-120] I am a scribled forme drawne with a pen

[011-121] Vpon a Parchment, and against this fire

[011-122] Do I shrinke vp

[011-123] Hen. How fares your Maiesty?

[011-124] Ioh. Poyson'd, ill fare: dead, forsooke, cast off,

[011-125] And none of you will bid the winter come

[011-126] To thrust his ycie fingers in my maw;

[011-127] Nor let my kingdomes Riuers take their course

[011-128] Through my burn'd bosome: nor intreat the North

[011-129] To make his bleake windes kisse my parched lips,

[011-130] And comfort me with cold. I do not aske you much,

[011-131] I begge cold comfort: and you are so straight

[011-132] And so ingratefull, you deny me that

[011-133] Hen. Oh that there were some vertue in my teares,

[011-134] That might releeue you

[011-135] Iohn. The salt in them is hot.

[011-136] Within me is a hell, and there the poyson

[011-137] Is, as a fiend, confin'd to tyrannize,

[011-138] On vnrepreeuable condemned blood.

[011-139] Enter Bastard.

[011-140] Bast. Oh, I am scalded with my violent motion

[011-141] And spleene of speede, to see your Maiesty

[011-142] Iohn. Oh Cozen, thou art come to set mine eye:

[011-143] The tackle of my heart, is crack'd and burnt,

[011-144] And all the shrowds wherewith my life should saile,

[011-145] Are turned to one thred, one little haire:

[011-146] My heart hath one poore string to stay it by,

[011-147] Which holds but till thy newes be vttered,

[011-148] And then all this thou seest, is but a clod,

[011-149] And module of confounded royalty

[011-150] Bast. The Dolphin is preparing hither-ward,

[011-151] Where heauen he knowes how we shall answer him.

[011-152] For in a night the best part of my powre,

[011-153] As I vpon aduantage did remoue,

[011-154] Were in [[the-wash|the Washes]] all vnwarily,

[011-155] Deuoured by the vnexpected flood

[011-156] Sal. You breath these dead newes in as dead an eare

[011-157] My Liege, my Lord: but now a King, now thus

[011-158] Hen. Euen so must I run on, and euen so stop.

[011-159] What surety of the world, what hope, what stay,

[011-160] When this was now a King, and now is clay?

[011-161] Bast. Art thou gone so? I do but stay behinde,

[011-162] To do the office for thee, of reuenge,

[011-163] And then my soule shall waite on thee to heauen,

[011-164] As it on earth hath bene thy seruant still.

[011-165] Now, now you Starres, that moue in your right spheres,

[011-166] Where be your powres? Shew now your mended faiths,

[011-167] And instantly returne with me againe.

[011-168] To push destruction, and perpetuall shame

[011-169] Out of the weake doore of our fainting Land:

[011-170] Straight let vs seeke, or straight we shall be sought,

[011-171] The Dolphine rages at our verie heeles

[011-172] Sal. It seemes you know not then so much as we,

[011-173] The Cardinall [[pandulph|Pandulph]] is within at rest,

[011-174] Who halfe an houre since came from the Dolphin,

[011-175] And brings from him such offers of our peace,

[011-176] As we with [[honour|honor]] and respect may take,

[011-177] With purpose presently to leaue this warre

[011-178] Bast. He will the rather do it, when he sees

[011-179] Our selues well sinew'd to our defence

[011-180] Sal. Nay, 'tis in a manner done already,

[011-181] For many carriages hee hath dispatch'd

[011-182] To the sea side, and put his cause and quarrell

[011-183] To the disposing of the Cardinall,

[011-184] With whom your selfe, my selfe, and other Lords,

[011-185] If you thinke meete, this afternoone will poast

[011-186] To consummate this businesse happily

[011-187] Bast. Let it be so, and you my noble Prince,

[011-188] With other Princes that may best be spar'd,

[011-189] Shall waite vpon your Fathers Funerall

[011-190] Hen. At [[worcester|Worster]] must his bodie be interr'd,

[011-191] For so he will'd it

[011-192] Bast. Thither shall it then,

[011-193] And happily may your sweet selfe put on

[011-194] The lineall state, and glorie of the Land,

[011-195] To whom with all submission on my knee,

[011-196] I do bequeath my faithfull seruices

[011-197] And true subiection euerlastingly

[011-198] Sal. And the like tender of our loue wee make

[011-199] To rest without a spot for euermore

[011-200] Hen. I haue a kinde soule, that would giue thankes,

[011-201] And knowes not how to do it, but with teares

[011-202] Bast. Oh let vs pay the time: but needfull woe,

[011-203] Since it hath beene before hand with our greefes.

[011-204] This England neuer did, nor neuer shall

[011-205] Lye at the proud foote of a Conqueror,

[011-206] But when it first did helpe to wound it selfe.

[011-207] Now, these her Princes are come home againe,

[011-208] Come the three corners of the world in Armes,

[011-209] And we shall shocke them: Naught shall make vs rue,

[011-210] If England to it selfe, do rest but true.

[011-211] Exeunt.
