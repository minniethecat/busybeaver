[006-001] Aust. [[rebellion|Rebellion]], flat rebellion

[006-002] Bast. Wil't not be?

[006-003] Will not a Calues-skin stop that mouth of thine?

[006-004] Daul. Father, to Armes

[006-005] Blanch. Vpon thy wedding day?

[006-006] Against the blood that thou hast married?

[006-007] What, shall our feast be kept with slaughtered men?

[006-008] Shall braying trumpets, and loud churlish drums

[006-010] O husband heare me: aye, alacke, how new

[006-011] Is husband in my mouth? euen for that name

[006-012] Which till this time my tongue did nere pronounce;

[006-013] Vpon my knee I beg, goe not to Armes

[006-014] Against mine Vncle

[006-015] Const. O, vpon my knee made hard with kneeling,

[006-016] I doe pray to thee, thou vertuous Daulphin,

[006-017] Alter not the doome fore-thought by heauen

[006-018] Blan. Now shall I see thy loue, what motiue may

[006-019] Be stronger with thee, then the name of wife?

[006-020] Con. That which vpholdeth him, that thee vpholds,

[006-022] Dolph. I muse your Maiesty doth seeme so cold,

[006-024] Pand. I will denounce a curse vpon his head

[006-025] Fra. Thou shalt not need. [[england|England]], I will fall fro[m] thee

[006-026] Const. O faire returne of banish'd Maiestie

[006-027] Elea. O foule reuolt of French inconstancy

[006-028] Eng. [[france|France]], y shalt rue this houre within this houre

[006-030] Is it as he will? well then, France shall rue

[006-031] Bla. The Sun's orecast with bloud: faire day adieu,

[006-032] Which is the side that I must goe withall?

[006-033] I am with both, each Army hath a hand,

[006-034] And in their rage, I hauing hold of both,

[006-035] They whurle a-sunder, and dismember mee.

[006-036] Husband, I cannot pray that thou maist winne:

[006-037] Vncle, I needs must pray that thou maist lose:

[006-038] Father, I may not wish the fortune thine:

[006-039] Grandam, I will not wish thy wishes thriue:

[006-040] Who-euer wins, on that side shall I lose:

[006-041] Assured losse, before the match be plaid

[006-042] Dolph. Lady, with me, with me thy fortune lies

[006-043] Bla. There where my fortune liues, there my life dies

[006-044] Iohn. Cosen, goe draw our puisance together,

[006-046] A rage, whose heat hath this condition;

[006-047] That nothing can allay, nothing but blood,

[006-048] The blood and deerest valued bloud of France

[006-049] Fra. Thy rage shall burne thee vp, & thou shalt turne

[006-050] To ashes, ere our blood shall quench that fire:

[006-051] Looke to thy selfe, thou art in ieopardie

[006-052] Iohn. No more then he that threats. To Arms let's hie.

[006-053] Exeunt.

[006-059] Enter Iohn, [[arthur|Arthur]], [[hubert|Hubert]].

[006-060] While Philip breathes

[006-063] And tane I feare

[006-065] Her Highnesse is in safety, feare you not:

[006-070] Hubert,

[006-081] Must by the hungry now be fed vpon:

[006-082] Vse our Commission in his vtmost [[power|force]]

[006-083] Bast. Bell, Booke, & Candle, shall not driue me back,

[006-084] When gold and siluer becks me to come on.

[006-085] I leaue your highnesse: Grandame, I will pray

[006-086] (If euer I remember to be holy)

[006-088] Ele. Farewell gentle Cosen

[006-089] Iohn. Coz, farewell

[006-090] Ele. Come hether little kinsman, harke, a worde

[006-091] Iohn. Come hether Hubert. O my gentle Hubert,

[006-092] We owe thee much: within this wall of flesh

[006-093] There is a soule counts thee her Creditor,

[006-094] And with aduantage meanes to pay thy loue:

[006-095] And my good friend, thy voluntary oath

[006-096] Liues in this bosome, deerely cherished.

[006-097] Giue me thy hand, I had a thing to say,

[006-098] But I will fit it with some better tune.

[006-099] By heauen Hubert, I am almost asham'd

[006-100] To say what good respect I haue of thee

[006-101] Hub. I am much bounden to your Maiesty

[006-102] Iohn. Good friend, thou hast no cause to say so yet,

[006-103] But thou shalt haue: and creepe time nere so slow,

[006-104] Yet it shall come, for me to doe thee good.

[006-105] I had a thing to say, but let it goe:

[006-106] The Sunne is in the heauen, and the proud day,

[006-107] Attended with the pleasures of the world,

[006-108] Is all too wanton, and too full of gawdes

[006-109] To giue me audience: If the mid-night bell

[006-110] Did with his yron tongue, and brazen mouth

[006-111] Sound on into the drowzie race of night:

[006-112] If this same were a Church-yard where we stand,

[006-113] And thou possessed with a thousand wrongs:

[006-114] Or if that surly spirit melancholy

[006-115] Had bak'd thy bloud, and made it heauy, thicke,

[006-116] Which else runnes tickling vp and downe the veines,

[006-117] Making that idiot laughter keepe mens eyes,

[006-118] And straine their cheekes to idle merriment,

[006-119] A passion hatefull to my purposes:

[006-120] Or if that thou couldst see me without eyes,

[006-121] Heare me without thine eares, and make reply

[006-122] Without a tongue, vsing conceit alone,

[006-123] Without eyes, eares, and harmefull sound of words:

[006-124] Then, in despight of brooded watchfull day,

[006-125] I would into thy bosome poure my thoughts:

[006-126] But (ah) I will not, yet I loue thee well,

[006-127] And by my troth I thinke thou lou'st me well

[006-128] Hub. So well, that what you bid me vndertake,

[006-129] Though that my death were adiunct to my Act,

[006-130] By heauen I would doe it

[006-132] Good Hubert, Hubert, Hubert throw thine eye

[006-137] Thou art his keeper

[006-138] Hub. And Ile keepe him so,

[006-139] That he shall not offend your Maiesty

[006-142] Iohn. A Graue

[006-143] Hub. He shall not liue

[006-186] And put my eye-balls in thy vaultie browes,

[006-187] And ring these fingers with thy houshold wormes,

[006-188] And stop this gap of breath with fulsome dust,

[006-189] And be a Carrion Monster like thy selfe;

[006-191] And busse thee as thy wife: Miseries Loue,

[006-192] O come to me

[006-193] Fra. O faire affliction, peace

[006-194] Con. No, no, I will not, hauing breath to cry:

[006-195] O that my tongue were in the thunders mouth,

[006-196] Then with a passion would I shake the world,

[006-197] And rowze from sleepe that fell Anatomy

[006-198] Which cannot heare a Ladies feeble voyce,

[006-199] Which scornes a moderne Inuocation

[006-201] Con. Thou art holy to belye me so,

[006-215] Or madly thinke a babe of clowts were he;

[006-216] I am not mad: too well, too well I feele

[006-220] Where but by chance a siluer drop hath falne,

[006-221] Euen to that drop ten thousand wiery fiends

[006-222] Doe glew themselues in sociable griefe,

[006-224] Sticking together in calamitie

[006-225] Con. To England, if you will
