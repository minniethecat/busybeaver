# Rhetoric (King John)

**Rhetoric** — the art of persuasion through speech — is a central
concern of *King John*. In a play notably lacking in extended battle
scenes, verbal contest and political persuasion drive the plot more
than military action. Shakespeare presents rhetoric as a double-edged
tool: capable of revealing truth or manufacturing falsehood, of
binding oaths or dissolving them.

## Rhetoric as Political Weapon

The play's major turning points are achieved through language rather
than force. The [[citizen-of-angiers|citizens of Angiers]] are
persuaded by rhetoric rather than conquered by siege. [[pandulph|Cardinal Pandulph]]'s arrival demonstrates the power of ecclesiastical rhetoric
to undo secular alliances — his arguments persuade [[king-philip-ii|King Philip]] to break his freshly sworn peace with [[king-john|John]],
demonstrating that words can dissolve what words have only just
created. [[constance]]'s laments deploy grief as a form of political
rhetoric, turning personal loss into public accusation.

The siege of Angiers itself is resolved not by arms but by the
[[citizen-of-angiers|citizen]]'s rhetorical intervention — "Stay for
an answer to your Embassie" (002-171) — which redirects both armies
from mutual destruction toward a negotiated settlement. The city's
gates, which both kings had vowed to breach with "Cannon" and "French-mens
bloud" [002-164, 169], are opened by words rather than by force.

[[arthurs-plea-to-hubert|Arthur's plea to Hubert]] represents
rhetoric's most intimate manifestation in the play. The young prince's
appeal — "Must you with hot Irons, burne out both mine eyes?"
(007-122) — deploys pathos, memory, and moral reasoning with a
sophistication that belies his age. His persuasion succeeds where
political argument fails: "let me not hold my tongue: let me not
Hubert" (007-188), and his words, not his station, move Hubert to
mercy. The prince's rhetoric is so effective that Hubert concedes
"His words do take possession of my bosome" (007-115).

> Must you with hot Irons, burne out both mine eyes?
> let me not hold my tongue: let me not Hubert,
> His words do take possession of my bosome.
>
> (007-122)

## The Bastard's Voice

[[philip-the-bastard]] serves as the play's rhetorical conscience. His
plain-speaking honesty — most famously in his soliloquy on "commodity"
— contrasts with the diplomatic flattery and casuistry of kings and
cardinals. When [[austria]] boasts of his martial achievements, the
Bastard's retort "And hang a Calues-skin on those recreant limbs"
(005-164) deploys ridicule as a rhetorical weapon that deflates
pomposity without needing to engage its arguments. His closing speech
— "This England neuer did, nor neuer shall / Lye at the proud foote
of a Conqueror" (011-204–205) — transforms the play's cynical
exploration of political language into a moment of apparently
unironic national affirmation, a rhetorical performance that
retrospectively organizes the chaos of the preceding acts into
coherent meaning.

## The Tongue as Instrument

Shakespeare repeatedly figures the tongue as the agent of political
persuasion. [[queen-elinor|Elinor]] identifies the Bastard by "The
accent of his tongue" (001-165). [[constance|Constance]] weaponizes
hers: "There is no tongue hath power to curse him right" (005-148).
[[pandulph|Pandulph]] warns France that holding John's hand is more
dangerous than holding "a serpent by the tongue" (005-226). The
Bastard mocks Austria — "He giues the bastinado with his tongue"
(004-069) — and Pembroke serves as "the tongue of these / To sound
the purposes of all their hearts" (008-020–021). The tongue is the
play's dominant symbol for rhetoric itself: an organ equally capable
of truth and poison.

## Commodity and Cynicism

The play ultimately presents a skeptical view of political rhetoric.
Words, in *King John*, are instruments of "commodity" — self-interest
disguised as principle. The [[the-league-of-angiers]] — sworn with
elaborate oaths and immediately broken under papal pressure —
exemplifies this vision of language as a tool of expediency. The
Bastard's observation that kings and nobles "Do when they will, and
when they say they will, / The word is not their bond" exposes
rhetoric's subordination to interest.

Yet the play does not entirely abandon faith in language.
[[arthur|Arthur]]'s words move [[hubert|Hubert]] to mercy — "I will
not stirre, nor winch, nor speake a word" (007-167). The Bastard's
patriotic peroration closes the play on a note of resolution that,
however fragile, represents rhetoric's capacity to create the reality
it describes. In *King John*, the most persuasive speaker may be the
most self-interested — but the most effective speech can transcend
self-interest to speak for a nation.
