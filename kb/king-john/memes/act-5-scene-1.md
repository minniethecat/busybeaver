## Section 9

[009-001] Ar. The Wall is high, and yet will I leape downe.

[009-002] Good ground be pittifull, and hurt me not:

[009-003] There's few or none do know me, if they did,

[009-004] This Ship-boyes semblance hath disguis'd me quite.

[009-005] I am afraide, and yet Ile venture it.

[009-006] If I get downe, and do not breake my limbes,

[009-007] Ile finde a thousand shifts to get away;

[009-008] As good to dye, and go; as dye, and stay.

[009-009] Oh me, my Vnckles spirit is in these stones,

[009-010] Heauen take my soule, and [[england|England]] keep my bones.

[009-011] Dies

[009-012] Enter [[pembroke|Pembroke]], [[salisbury|Salisbury]], & [[bigot|Bigot]].

[009-013] Sal. Lords, I will meet him at S[aint]. Edmondsbury,

[009-014] It is our safetie, and we must embrace

[009-015] This gentle offer of the perillous time

[009-016] Pem. Who brought that Letter from the Cardinall?

[009-017] Sal. The Count Meloone, a Noble Lord of [[france|France]],

[009-018] Whose priuate with me of the Dolphines loue,

[009-019] Is much more generall, then these lines import

[009-020] Big. To morrow morning let vs meete him then

[009-021] Sal. Or rather then set forward, for 'twill be

[009-022] Two long dayes iourney (Lords) or ere we meete.

[009-023] Enter Bastard.

[009-024] Bast. Once more to day well met, distemper'd Lords,

[009-025] The King by me requests your presence straight

[009-026] Sal. The king hath dispossest himselfe of vs,

[009-027] We will not lyne his thin-bestained cloake

[009-028] With our pure Honors: nor attend the foote

[009-029] That leaues the print of blood where ere it walkes.

[009-030] Returne, and tell him so: we know the worst

[009-031] Bast. What ere you thinke, good words I thinke

[009-032] were best

[009-033] Sal. Our greefes, and not our manners reason now

[009-034] Bast. But there is little reason in your greefe.

[009-035] Therefore 'twere reason you had manners now

[009-036] Pem. Sir, sir, impatience hath his priuiledge

[009-037] Bast. 'Tis true, to hurt his master, no mans else

[009-038] Sal. This is the prison: What is he lyes heere?

[009-039] P. Oh death, made proud with pure & princely beuty,

[009-040] The earth had not a hole to hide this deede

[009-041] Sal. Murther, as hating what himselfe hath done,

[009-042] Doth lay it open to vrge on reuenge

[009-043] Big. Or when he doom'd this Beautie to a graue,

[009-044] Found it too precious Princely, for a graue

[009-045] Sal. Sir Richard, what thinke you? you haue beheld,

[009-046] Or haue you read, or heard, or could you thinke?

[009-047] Or do you almost thinke, although you see,

[009-048] That you do see? Could thought, without this obiect

[009-049] Forme such another? This is the very top,

[009-050] The heighth, the Crest: or Crest vnto the Crest

[009-051] Of murthers Armes: This is the bloodiest shame,

[009-052] The wildest Sauagery, the vildest stroke

[009-053] That euer wall-ey'd wrath, or staring rage

[009-054] Presented to the teares of soft remorse

[009-055] Pem. All murthers past, do stand excus'd in this:

[009-056] And this so sole, and so vnmatcheable,

[009-057] Shall giue a holinesse, a puritie,

[009-058] To the yet vnbegotten sinne of times;

[009-059] And proue a deadly bloodshed, but a iest,

[009-060] Exampled by this heynous spectacle

[009-061] Bast. It is a damned, and a bloody worke,

[009-062] The gracelesse action of a heauy hand,

[009-063] If that it be the worke of any hand

[009-064] Sal. If that it be the worke of any hand?

[009-065] We had a kinde of light, what would ensue:

[009-066] It is the shamefull worke of Huberts hand,

[009-067] The practice, and the purpose of the king:

[009-068] From whose obedience I forbid my soule,

[009-069] Kneeling before this ruine of sweete life,

[009-070] And breathing to his breathlesse Excellence

[009-071] The Incense of a [[vow|Vow]], a holy Vow:

[009-072] Neuer to taste the pleasures of the world,

[009-073] Neuer to be infected with delight,

[009-074] Nor conuersant with Ease, and Idlenesse,

[009-075] Till I haue set a glory to this hand,

[009-076] By giuing it the worship of Reuenge

[009-077] Pem. Big. Our soules religiously confirme thy words.

[009-078] Enter [[hubert|Hubert]].

[009-079] Hub. Lords, I am hot with haste, in seeking you,

[009-080] [[arthur|Arthur]] doth liue, the king hath sent for you

[009-081] Sal. Oh he is bold, and blushes not at death,

[009-082] Auant thou hatefull villain, get thee gone

[009-083] Hu. I am no villaine

[009-084] Sal. Must I rob the Law?

[009-085] Bast. Your sword is bright sir, put it vp againe

[009-086] Sal. Not till I sheath it in a murtherers skin

[009-087] Hub. Stand backe Lord Salsbury, stand backe I say

[009-088] By heauen, I thinke my sword's as sharpe as yours.

[009-089] I would not haue you (Lord) forget your selfe,

[009-090] Nor tempt the danger of my true defence;

[009-091] Least I, by marking of your rage, forget

[009-092] Your Worth, your Greatnesse, and Nobility

[009-093] Big. Out dunghill: dar'st thou braue a Nobleman?

[009-094] Hub. Not for my life: But yet I dare defend

[009-095] My innocent life against an Emperor

[009-096] Sal. Thou art a Murtherer

[009-097] Hub. Do not proue me so:

[009-098] Yet I am none. Whose tongue so ere speakes false,

[009-099] Not truely speakes: who speakes not truly, Lies

[009-100] Pem. Cut him to peeces

[009-101] Bast. Keepe the peace, I say

[009-102] Sal. Stand by, or I shall gaul you Faulconbridge

[009-103] Bast. Thou wer't better gaul the diuell Salsbury.

[009-104] If thou but frowne on me, or stirre thy foote,

[009-105] Or teach thy hastie spleene to do me shame,

[009-106] Ile strike thee dead. Put vp thy sword betime,

[009-107] Or Ile so maule you, and your tosting-Iron,

[009-108] That you shall thinke the diuell is come from hell

[009-109] Big. What wilt thou do, renowned Faulconbridge?

[009-110] Second a Villaine, and a Murtherer?

[009-111] Hub. Lord Bigot, I am none

[009-112] Big. Who kill'd this Prince?

[009-113] Hub. 'Tis not an houre since I left him well:

[009-114] I honour'd him, I lou'd him, and will weepe

[009-115] My date of life out, for his sweete liues losse

[009-116] Sal. Trust not those cunning waters of his eyes,

[009-117] For villanie is not without such rheume,

[009-118] And he, long traded in it, makes it seeme

[009-119] Like Riuers of remorse and innocencie.

[009-120] Away with me, all you whose soules abhorre

[009-121] Th' vncleanly sauours of a Slaughter-house,

[009-122] For I am stifled with this smell of sinne

[009-123] Big. Away, toward Burie, to the Dolphin there

[009-124] P. There tel the king, he may inquire vs out.

[009-125] Ex. Lords.

[009-126] Ba. Here's a good world: knew you of this faire work?

[009-127] Beyond the infinite and boundlesse reach of mercie,

[009-128] (If thou didst this deed of death) art y damn'd Hubert

[009-129] Hub. Do but heare me sir

[009-130] Bast. Ha? Ile tell thee what.

[009-131] Thou'rt damn'd as blacke, nay nothing is so blacke,

[009-132] Thou art more deepe damn'd then Prince Lucifer:

[009-133] There is not yet so vgly a fiend of hell

[009-134] As thou shalt be, if thou didst kill this childe

[009-135] Hub. Vpon my soule

[009-136] Bast. If thou didst but consent

[009-137] To this most cruell Act: do but dispaire,

[009-138] And if thou want'st a Cord, the smallest thred

[009-139] That euer Spider twisted from her wombe

[009-140] Will serue to strangle thee: A rush will be a beame

[009-141] To hang thee on. Or wouldst thou drowne thy selfe,

[009-142] Put but a little water in a spoone,

[009-143] And it shall be as all the Ocean,

[009-144] Enough to stifle such a villaine vp.

[009-145] I do suspect thee very greeuously

[009-146] Hub. If I in act, consent, or sinne of thought,

[009-147] Be guiltie of the stealing that sweete breath

[009-148] Which was embounded in this beauteous clay,

[009-149] Let hell want paines enough to torture me:

[009-150] I left him well

[009-151] Bast. Go, beare him in thine armes:

[009-152] I am amaz'd me thinkes, and loose my way

[009-153] Among the thornes, and dangers of this world.

[009-154] How easie dost thou take all England vp,

[009-155] From forth this morcell of dead Royaltie?

[009-156] The life, the right, and truth of all this Realme

[009-157] Is fled to heauen: and England now is left

[009-158] To tug and scamble, and to part by th' teeth

[009-159] The vn-owed interest of proud swelling State:

[009-160] Now for the bare-pickt bone of Maiesty,

[009-161] Doth dogged warre bristle his angry crest,

[009-162] And snarleth in the gentle eyes of peace:

[009-163] Now Powers from home, and discontents at home

[009-164] Meet in one line: and vast confusion waites

[009-165] As doth a Rauen on a sicke-falne beast,

[009-166] The iminent decay of wrested pompe.

[009-167] Now happy he, whose cloake and center can

[009-168] Hold out this tempest. Beare away that childe,

[009-169] And follow me with speed: Ile to the King:

[009-170] A thousand businesses are briefe in hand,

[009-171] And heauen it selfe doth frowne vpon the Land.

[009-172] Enter.

[009-173] Actus Quartus, Scaena prima.

[009-174] Enter King Iohn and Pandolph, attendants.

[009-175] K.Iohn. Thus haue I yeelded vp into your hand

[009-176] The Circle of my glory

[009-177] Pan. Take againe

[009-178] From this my hand, as holding of the Pope

[009-179] Your Soueraigne greatnesse and authoritie

[009-180] Iohn. Now keep your holy word, go meet the French,

[009-181] And from his holinesse vse all your power

[009-182] To stop their marches 'fore we are enflam'd:

[009-183] Our discontented Counties doe reuolt:

[009-184] Our people quarrell with obedience,

[009-185] Swearing Allegiance, and the loue of soule

[009-186] To stranger-bloud, to forren Royalty;

[009-187] This inundation of mistempred humor,

[009-188] Rests by you onely to be qualified.

[009-189] Then pause not: for the present time's so sicke,

[009-190] That present medcine must be ministred,

[009-191] Or ouerthrow incureable ensues

[009-192] Pand. It was my breath that blew this Tempest vp,

[009-193] Vpon your stubborne vsage of the Pope:

[009-194] But since you are a gentle conuertite,

[009-195] My tongue shall hush againe this storme of warre,

[009-196] And make faire weather in your blustring land:

[009-197] On this Ascention day, remember well,

[009-198] Vpon your oath of seruice to the Pope,

[009-199] Goe I to make the French lay downe their Armes.

[009-200] Enter.

[009-201] Iohn. Is this Ascension day? did not [[peter-of-pomfret|the Prophet]]

[009-202] Say, that before Ascension day at noone,

[009-203] My Crowne I should giue off? euen so I haue:

[009-204] I did suppose it should be on constraint,

[009-205] But (heau'n be thank'd) it is but voluntary.

[009-206] Enter Bastard.

[009-207] Bast. All Kent hath yeelded: nothing there holds out

[009-208] But [[dover-castle|Douer Castle]]: [[london|London]] hath receiu'd

[009-209] Like a kinde Host, the Dolphin and his powers.

[009-210] Your Nobles will not heare you, but are gone

[009-211] To offer seruice to your enemy:

[009-212] And wilde amazement hurries vp and downe

[009-213] The little number of your doubtfull friends

[009-214] Iohn. Would not my Lords returne to me againe

[009-215] After they heard yong Arthur was aliue?

[009-216] Bast. They found him dead, and cast into the streets,

[009-217] An empty Casket, where the Iewell of life

[009-218] By some damn'd hand was rob'd, and tane away

[009-219] Iohn. That villaine Hubert told me he did liue

[009-220] Bast. So on my soule he did, for ought he knew:

[009-221] But wherefore doe you droope? why looke you sad?

[009-222] Be great in act, as you haue beene in thought:

[009-223] Let not the world see feare and sad distrust

[009-224] Gouerne the motion of a kinglye eye:

[009-225] Be stirring as the time, be fire with fire,

[009-226] Threaten the threatner, and out-face the brow

[009-227] Of bragging horror: So shall inferior eyes

[009-228] That borrow their behauiours from the great,

[009-229] Grow great by your example, and put on

[009-230] The dauntlesse spirit of resolution.

[009-231] Away, and glister like the god of warre

[009-232] When he intendeth to become the field:

[009-233] Shew boldnesse and aspiring confidence:

[009-234] What, shall they seeke the Lion in his denne,

[009-235] And fright him there? and make him tremble there?

[009-236] Oh let it not be said: forrage, and runne

[009-237] To meet displeasure farther from the dores,

[009-238] And grapple with him ere he come so nye

[009-239] Iohn. The Legat of the Pope hath beene with mee,

[009-240] And I haue made a happy peace with him,

[009-241] And he hath promis'd to dismisse the Powers

[009-242] Led by the Dolphin

[009-243] Bast. Oh inglorious league:

[009-244] Shall we vpon the footing of our land,

[009-245] Send fayre-play-orders, and make comprimise,

[009-246] Insinuation, parley, and base [[peace|truce]]

[009-247] To Armes Inuasiue? Shall a beardlesse boy,

[009-248] A cockred-silken wanton braue our fields,

[009-249] And flesh his spirit in a warre-like soyle,

[009-250] Mocking the ayre with colours idlely spred,

[009-251] And finde no checke? Let vs my Liege to Armes:

[009-252] Perchance the Cardinall cannot make your peace;

[009-253] Or if he doe, let it at least be said

[009-254] They saw we had a purpose of defence

[009-255] Iohn. Haue thou the ordering of this present time

[009-256] Bast. Away then with good courage: yet I know

[009-257] Our Partie may well meet a prowder foe.

[009-258] Exeunt.

[009-259] Scoena Secunda.

[009-261] Souldiers.

[009-262] Dol. My Lord Melloone, let this be coppied out,

[009-263] And keepe it safe for our remembrance:

[009-264] Returne the president to these Lords againe,

[009-265] That hauing our faire order written downe,

[009-266] Both they and we, perusing ore these notes

[009-267] May know wherefore we tooke the Sacrament,

[009-268] And keepe our faithes firme and inuiolable
