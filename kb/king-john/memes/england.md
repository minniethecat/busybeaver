# England

England is the central territorial entity of *[[king-john|King John]]* — at once the seat of the
[[crown|throne]] contested by [[king-john|John]] and [[arthur|Arthur]], the prize that draws French invasion,
and the embodiment of national identity in [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]]'s great patriotic speeches.
The kingdom appears in every act of the play, shifting in dramatic function from
royal court to besieged homeland.

## Role in the Play

### The Crown Contested

The play opens with England's [[crown|sovereignty]] in question. [[chatillon|Chatillon]], ambassador of
[[king-philip-ii|Philip II of France]], delivers the French claim: [[arthur|Arthur]] Plantagenet, son of [[king-john|John]]'s
elder brother Geoffrey, is the rightful heir. The challenge is framed as a matter
of territorial possession as much as royal title:

> Philip of France, in right and true behalfe
> Of thy deceased brother, Geffreyes sonne,
> Arthur Plantaginet, laies most lawfull claime
> To this faire Iland, and the Territories:
> To Ireland, Poyctiers, Aniowe, Torayne, Maine... (001-083)

[[king-john|John]]'s response — "The borrowed Maiesty of England heere" (001-080) — sets the
terms of the [[war|conflict]]: legitimacy is not inherent but asserted through power.
The Dauphin [[lewis-the-dauphin|Lewis]] later formalizes the territorial claim in precise terms:

> England and Ireland, Angiers, Toraine, Maine,
> In right of Arthur doe I claime of thee... (003-024)

### The Fortress Island

England's insular geography is repeatedly invoked as a source of military
strength. [[austria|Austria]], pledging to fight for [[arthur|Arthur]]'s cause, describes the kingdom
in terms that evoke natural defences and providential protection:

> Till Angiers, and the right thou hast in France,
> Together with that pale, that white-fac'd shore,
> Whose foot spurnes backe the Oceans roaring tides,
> And coopes from other lands her Ilanders,
> Euen till that England hedg'd in with the maine,
> That Water-walled Bulwarke, still secure
> And confident from forreine purposes... (002-149)

The image of England as a "Water-walled Bulwarke" — a sea-girt fortress —
is central to the play's conception of national security. Yet this same fortress
is breached in Act IV when a French army lands on English soil, an invasion
whose scale is described as unprecedented:

> From France to England, neuer such a powre
> For any forraigne preparation,
> Was leuied in the body of a land. (008-086)

### The Bastard's Patriotism

The most sustained expression of English national identity comes from
[[philip-the-bastard|Philip the Bastard]], whose [[faith|loyalty]] to England transcends his personal
obligations to [[king-john|John]]. In Act V, confronting the rebel English lords who have
joined the Dauphin's invasion, he delivers a speech that fuses nation and
mother in one of Shakespeare's most visceral images of patriotism:

> And you degenerate, you ingrate Reuolts,
> You bloudy Nero's, ripping vp the wombe
> Of your deere Mother-England: blush for shame... (010-147)

This image — the rebel lords as matricides disembowelling their own mother-land —
invests the political [[war|conflict]] with the [[power|force]] of a natural bond. The Bastard's
language transforms England from a geographical entity into a moral and
biological imperative.

[[salisbury|Salisbury]], too, though a rebel, speaks movingly of England's violated integrity.
Addressing his fellow English lords, he laments the civil war that has [[power|force]]d
them to march alongside a foreign invader:

> And is't not pitty, (oh my grieued friends)
> That we, the sonnes and children of this Isle,
> Was borne to see so sad an houre as this,
> Wherein we step after a stranger, march
> Vpon her gentle bosom, and fill vp
> Her Enemies rankes? (010-018)

### "England to Itself Be True"

The play's final speech, delivered by [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]] over [[king-john|John]]'s dead body, distills
the play's political philosophy into a single couplet. England's vulnerability
comes not from foreign powers but from internal division:

> This England neuer did, nor neuer shall
> Lye at the proud foote of a Conqueror,
> But when it first did helpe to wound it selfe.
> Now, these her Princes are come home againe,
> Come the three corners of the world in Armes,
> And we shall shocke them: Naught shall make vs rue,
> If England to it selfe, do rest but true. (011-204)

The speech recasts the entire play's action as a lesson in the cost of
disunity. England fell not because [[france|France]] was stronger but because "her Princes"
— [[salisbury|Salisbury]], [[pembroke|Pembroke]], [[bigot|Bigot]] — chose rebellion. With the rebel lords now
reconciled to the crown, [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]] asserts that England can defy "the three
corners of the world."

This patriotic finale has often been read in light of Shakespeare's later
history plays, where [[king-john|John]]'s reign appears as a prelude to later crises. The
sentiment — that England's greatness depends on internal unity — would
reverberate through the Henriad.

## Historical Context

### John's England vs. Shakespeare's

Shakespeare's *[[king-john|King John]]* compresses and rearranges the historical reign of [[king-john|John]]
(1199–1216) for dramatic effect. The real [[king-john|John]] did lose Normandy to Philip II
(1204), was excommunicated by [[the-papacy|Pope Innocent III]] (1209), and faced baronial
rebellion resulting in Magna Carta (1215). Shakespeare, however, never mentions
Magna Carta — a striking omission that shifts the play's focus from constitutional
questions to questions of legitimacy and national unity.

### England at the Death of John

[[king-john|John]]'s [[death|dying]] words locate him firmly within the kingdom he has contested:

> Heauen take my soule, and England keep my bones. (009-010)

The line is poignant in its division: the soul belongs to heaven, but [[king-john|John]]'s
mortal remains — and with them his claim to England's crown — remain in the
earth he ruled. The poisoned king dies in the orchard at [[swinstead-abbey|Swinstead Abbey]],
far from the French invasion and the rebel lords, but his body and his legacy
are England's to keep.

## Symbolic Significance

England functions in the play as both a physical kingdom and a moral
abstraction. Contrasted with [[france|France]] — associated in the play's rhetoric with
inconstancy and foreign ambition — England becomes the site of authentic
national feeling. The Bastard's speeches turn England into a wounded mother,
a body whose violation is at once political and sacrilegious. The play's
resolution, in which the reconciled English lords return to their rightful
[[faith|allegiance]], asserts that England's security lies not in its "Water-walled"
barriers but in the [[faith|loyalty]] of its people.
