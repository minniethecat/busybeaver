# Nature (King John)

**Nature** in *[[king-john|King John]]* functions as a complex and contested concept, encompassing natural law, biological inheritance, and the proper order of the world. Characters appeal to nature to justify claims, condemn actions, and lament disorder — yet the play ultimately suggests that "nature" is less a fixed standard than a rhetorical resource deployed by whoever holds power.

## Nature and Legitimacy

The question of legitimacy — who is the "natural" heir — drives the play's central [[war|conflict]]. [[constance]] argues that [[arthur]] is the natural successor by primogeniture; [[king-john]] rules by possession and de [[truth|fact]]o power. The language of nature is weaponised early: John is denounced as an "vnnaturall Vncle" (002-137) for usurping his nephew's throne, a charge that frames his kingship as a violation of the natural order. The accusation carries particular force because it comes from [[king-philip-ii|King Philip of France]], who styles himself the defender of natural right even as he pursues his own territorial ambitions. The [[siege-of-angiers]] dramatises the failure of natural-law arguments to resolve political disputes — both claimants appeal to nature, and neither prevails. When each side can invoke nature to justify its position, the concept loses all binding force.

> And to rebuke the vsurpation
> Of thy vnnaturall Vncle, English Iohn,
> Embrace him, loue him, giue him welcome hether.
>
> (002-135)

## The Bastard and Natural Identity

[[philip-the-bastard]] embodies the play's ambivalent treatment of nature. He abandons his social identity (as a Faulconbridge) for his biological identity (as [[king-richard-i|Richard the Lionheart]]'s son), claiming that "nature" — his innate qualities inherited from his royal father — matters more than legal legitimacy. His self-fashioning suggests that nature is not a constraint to be accepted but a resource to be claimed: he makes himself noble by acting as if he were noble, and the play rewards him for it. The Bastard's trajectory thus offers the play's most radical suggestion about nature — that it may be not a birthright but a performance, something created rather than inherited.

## Nature and Commodity

The Bastard's famous soliloquy on "commodity" — the self-interest that governs all political action — provides the play's darkest counterpoint to its rhetoric of natural order. Where characters invoke nature as a moral anchor, the Bastard exposes the material calculations beneath: "Mad world, mad kings, mad composition" (004-173). The pun on "composition" — meaning both treaty and the act of composing or fabricating — suggests that the natural order itself is a convenient fiction, composed by the powerful to serve their interests. Commodity, "the byas of the world" (004-186), tilts all human action away from nature and toward self-interest.

## Nature Disordered

The play repeatedly images political crisis as a disorder in nature. Civil war is figured as the collapse of ordered hierarchy: "Such temperate order in so fierce a cause" (006-167) gives way to "When there is such disorder in my wit" (006-260), as the external chaos of rebellion mirrors the internal unravelling of the characters. [[constance|Constance]]'s grief is framed as a natural weakness — "A woman naturally borne to feares" (004-228) — yet the play refuses to let this reduction stand, giving her some of its most powerful language. [[pandulph|Pandulph]]'s theological rhetoric reaches its unsettling climax when he declares that "All forme is formelesse, Order orderlesse" (005-221) in the service of papal policy, suggesting that appeals to natural order are themselves a form of political manipulation. The papal legate, supposedly the guarantor of divine and natural order on earth, becomes the character who most explicitly unmakes that order with his words.

> In *King John*, "nature" is the most powerful argument anyone can make — and the least reliable standard anyone can rely on.
