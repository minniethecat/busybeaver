# Constance

## Role in the Play

Constance is the play's **tragic mother** -- a woman whose entire dramatic identity is bound up in her son [[arthur|Arthur]]'s claim to the English [[crown|throne]]. As the widow of Geoffrey Plantagenet ([[king-john|John]]'s elder brother), she possesses the strongest legal claim in the play: [[arthur|Arthur]] is the rightful heir by primogeniture, and Constance knows it. Her relentless, furious advocacy for her son's cause drives the first half of the play's political action.

Shakespeare gives Constance some of the most emotionally raw and rhetorically sophisticated verse in the entire canon. She moves from fierce political [[war|combat]]ant to grief-stricken mourner, and finally to a figure hovering at the edge of madness. Her arc is contained within a compressed dramatic span -- she enters the action, fights, loses everything, and dies, all within the space of two acts -- yet her presence haunts the remainder of the play.

### Political Position

Constance is aligned with the **French [[truth|fact]]ion** -- not out of [[faith|loyalty]] to [[france|France]], but because [[king-philip-ii|King Philip II]] champions [[arthur|Arthur]]'s claim against [[king-john|King John]]. Her alliance is purely instrumental: she needs French arms to unseat [[king-john|John]] and place [[arthur|Arthur]] on the [[crown|throne]]. When [[king-philip-ii|Philip]] abandons [[arthur|Arthur]]'s cause in the [[the-league-of-angiers|League of Angiers]] to make peace with [[king-john|John]], Constance's political infrastructure collapses entirely.

### Key Relationships

Constance's most intense relationship is with [[queen-elinor|Queen Elinor]], [[king-john|John]]'s mother. Their verbal duels crackle with mutual contempt. [[queen-elinor|Elinor]] calls [[arthur|Arthur]] a bastard (002-253); Constance retorts that her bed was as true to Geoffrey as [[queen-elinor|Elinor]]'s was to her husband [002-255--256]. These exchanges are not mere insults -- they are competing visions of legitimacy, [[blood|lineage]], and maternal [[honour|honor]], fought in public before the assembled courts of [[england|England]] and [[france|France]].

Her relationship with [[king-philip-ii|King Philip II]] is one of dependence turned to bitter accusation. When Philip breaks faith with [[arthur|Arthur]] to ally with [[king-john|John]], Constance denounces him as a counterfeit, a man who has "beguil'd me with a counterfeit / Resembling Majesty" (005-061). Her relationship with [[pandulph|Cardinal Pandulph]] is similarly adversarial: she seizes on his excommunication of [[king-john|John]] with savage glee (005-146), only to find that the Cardinal's political machinations offer her no real relief.

## Historical Background

The historical Constance (c. 1161--1201) was the daughter of Conan IV, Duke of [[brittany|Brittany]], and Margaret of Huntingdon. She married Geoffrey Plantagenet, the third son of Henry II and [[queen-elinor|Eleanor of Aquitaine]], in 1181. Geoffrey died in 1186, leaving Constance pregnant with [[arthur|Arthur]]. When Richard I died in 1199, Constance pressed [[arthur|Arthur]]'s claim against [[king-john|John]] -- a claim that enjoyed the support of the French king Philip Augustus.

Shakespeare compresses the historical chronology significantly. The real Constance died in 1201, well before [[king-john|John]]'s excommunication (1209) and the events of the play's later acts. Her death in the play -- "The Lady Constance in a frenzie di'de" (008-099) -- is Shakespeare's invention, collapsing years of political struggle into a concentrated dramatic arc. More importantly, Shakespeare transforms the historical Constance -- a capable political operator who governed [[brittany|Brittany]] in her son's name -- into a figure defined almost entirely by maternal emotion, first as fierce advocate and then as grief-maddened mourner.

## Key Speeches and Scenes

### The Advocate: Act 2 Scene 1

When Constance first appears before the French court alongside [[arthur|Arthur]], she is no supplicant but a formidable advocate. She demands that [[king-philip-ii|Philip]] honour his commitment to [[arthur|Arthur]]'s claim and reminds him of the obligation owed to "the Lady Constance" (002-127). Her rhetoric blends legal argument with emotional appeal, positioning [[arthur|Arthur]] as the rightful heir whose inheritance has been "Cut off the sequence of posterity" (002-227). She fights as a mother, but with the intellectual armour of a seasoned diplomat.

### The Grief: Act 3 Scene 3

Constance's great scene of lamentation follows [[arthur|Arthur]]'s capture. When [[king-philip-ii|Philip]] offers comfort, she rejects it with extraordinary vehemence. Her speech to [[death|Death]] [006-179--192] is one of Shakespeare's most startling passages:

> Death, death, O amiable, louely death,
> Thou odoriferous stench: sound rottennesse,
> Arise forth from the couch of lasting night,
> Thou hate and terror to prosperitie,
> And I will kisse thy detestable bones,
> And put my eye-balls in thy vaultie browes,
> And ring these fingers with thy houshold wormes [006-181--187]

The passage is remarkable for its oxymoronic intensity -- death is simultaneously lovely and detestable, a bridegroom and a carrion monster. Constance embraces death with an erotic fervour that transforms conventional [[grief|mourning]] rhetoric into something raw and unsettling.

### "I Am Not Mad": Act 3 Scene 3

When [[pandulph|Pandulph]] dismisses her as mad, Constance delivers her most famous retort:

> My name is Constance, I was Geffreyes wife,
> Yong Arthur is my sonne, and he is lost:
> I am not mad, I would to heauen I were,
> For then 'tis like I should forget my selfe [006-203--206]

She challenges [[pandulph|Pandulph]] to be "Canoniz'd" if he can preach philosophy enough to drive her truly mad -- for madness would be a [[grace|mercy]] compared to the sanity that [[power|force]]s her to feel every particle of her grief [006-208--209]. Her claim that "He talkes to me, that neuer had a sonne" (006-249) is the sonorous final word: [[pandulph|Pandulph]]'s ecclesiastical [[power|authority]] cannot comprehend what he has never experienced.

Her closing image -- "Greefe fils the roome vp of my absent childe" (006-251) -- transforms grief from absence into presence. [[grief|Grief]] becomes the child's surrogate, filling the vacant garments, repeating his words, occupying the physical space he has left behind. It is one of the most psychologically acute depictions of [[grief|mourning]] in early modern drama.

### Death and Legacy

Constance's death is reported in a single line: "The Lady Constance in a frenzie di'de" (008-099). The brevity is devastating. The woman who spoke such torrents of grief dies offstage, her death reduced to a messenger's passing report. Yet her influence persists: [[arthur|Arthur]]'s ghost, the civil war, and [[king-john|John]]'s collapse all trace their [[blood|lineage]] back to the disinheritance she fought against. Her lament resonates through the latter half of the play, as the consequences of [[arthur|Arthur]]'s dispossession unfold in blood and political chaos (005-177).

## Character Analysis

Constance occupies a unique position in Shakespeare's early histories. She is neither a queen regnant nor a comic shrew nor a passive victim -- she is a mother whose identity is inseparable from her son's cause. Her tragedy is that her eloquence, intelligence, and ferocity are ultimately powerless against the political machinations of men. The play grants her magnificent poetry but no political efficacy.

Critics have noted the proto-tragic quality of Constance's scenes. Her grief anticipates the more celebrated laments of Shakespeare's mature tragedies -- the "willow" scene of Desdemona, the distracted [[grief|sorrow]] of Ophelia, the rage of Constance's own literary descendant, Volumnia in *Coriolanus*. In a play dominated by political calculation, commodity, and oath-breaking, Constance represents the human cost that political [[blood|violence]] exacts upon the innocent and the powerless.

## Performance History

Constance is a coveted role for actresses despite her limited stage time. The part demands a performer capable of moving from trenchant political advocacy to operatic grief to fragile, self-aware near-madness, all within the space of roughly two hundred lines. Notable Constances have included Sarah Siddons in the 18th century, whose "I am not mad" speech was considered a landmark of tragic acting, and more recent performers who have brought psychological nuance to what could be read as a simply hysterical role. The challenge of Constance is to make the audience feel that her "madness" is, in [[truth|fact]], the only sane response to her situation.
