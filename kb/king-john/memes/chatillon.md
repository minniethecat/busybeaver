# Chatillon

**Chatillon** (also spelled *Chatillion*) is the French ambassador in Shakespeare's *[[king-john|King John]]*, serving as [[king-philip-ii|King Philip II]]'s diplomatic envoy to the English court. He appears in two scenes, delivering [[france|France]]'s ultimatum in Act 1 and reporting [[england|England]]'s military response in Act 2. Though a minor role, Chatillon frames the play's central [[war|conflict]] between [[king-john|King John]] and the French-backed claim of [[arthur|Arthur Plantagenet]].

## Role in the Play

Chatillon's dramatic function is to deliver formal diplomatic messages that escalate the [[war|conflict]] between [[england|England]] and [[france|France]]. His speeches are consistently formal and ceremonial, contrasting with the personal invective exchanged by the principal characters.

### The Embassy to England (Act 1, Scene 1)

Chatillon's first appearance opens the political action of the play. He arrives at [[king-john|King John]]'s court to deliver [[king-philip-ii|Philip of France]]'s demand: that [[king-john|John]] abdicate in favor of [[arthur|Arthur]], the son of [[king-john|John]]'s deceased elder brother Geoffrey.

> Thus (after greeting) speakes the King of France,
> In my behauiour to the Maiesty,
> The borrowed Maiesty of England heere
> (001-077)

[[queen-elinor|Queen Elinor]] immediately objects to the phrase "borrowed Majesty," (001-081) but [[king-john|John]] silences her to hear the full message. Chatillon proceeds to lay out Philip's formal claim:

> Philip of France, in right and true behalfe
> Of thy deceased brother, Geffreyes sonne,
> Arthur Plantaginet, laies most lawfull claime
> To this faire Iland, and the Territories
> (001-083)

### The Return to France (Act 2, Scene 1)

Chatillon's second and final appearance occurs before the walls of [[angiers|Angiers]], where he reports to Philip that [[king-john|John]] has not only rejected the ultimatum but has already crossed the Channel with an army:

> Then turne your forces from this paltry siege,
> And stirre them vp against a mightier taske:
> England impatient of your iust demands,
> Hath put himselfe in Armes
> (002-182)

## Dramatic Significance

Chatillon serves several key dramatic functions:

| Function | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Exposition** | Delivers the backstory of [[arthur|Arthur]]'s claim and the succession dispute |
| **Conflict Catalyst** | His embassy transforms latent tension into active war |
| **Structural Frame** | His two appearances bracket [[king-john|John]]'s military preparation |
| **Tone Setting** | His formal style establishes the play's concern with ceremonial speech |

## Relationship to Major Characters

- **[[king-john|King John]]**: Chatillon addresses [[king-john|John]] with formal deference but delivers an uncompromising ultimatum. [[king-john|John]] responds with defiant threats, calling himself "the thunder of my Cannon" (001-102).
- **[[king-philip-ii|King Philip II]]**: As Philip's ambassador, Chatillon speaks with his master's [[power|authority]]. The ambassador's role highlights Philip's preference for formal diplomatic channels before resorting to war.
- **[[queen-elinor|Queen Elinor]]**: [[queen-elinor|Elinor]] interrupts Chatillon's embassy, reacting sharply to the phrase "borrowed Majesty." Her interjection reveals her protective nature toward [[king-john|John]]'s legitimacy.
- **[[pembroke|Pembroke]]**: Chatillon is escorted by [[pembroke|Pembroke]] upon his departure (001-106), linking the ambassador to the English nobility.

## Language and Style

Chatillon's language is characterized by formal, legalistic diction appropriate to diplomatic speech. He uses terms such as "lawfull claime," "right and true behalfe," and "Embassie," reinforcing the play's concern with legal legitimacy. His final speech uses martial imagery — "[[power|might]]ier taske," "Armes," "Legions," "marches" — to convey the scale of [[king-john|John]]'s military response.

## Historical Context

The historical Chatillon served as a French diplomat during the reign of Philip II Augustus (r. 1180–1223). Shakespeare follows his chronicle sources — primarily Holinshed — in presenting the ambassador's role as a formal trigger for the Anglo-French war of 1202–1204, though the play compresses the chronology considerably.
