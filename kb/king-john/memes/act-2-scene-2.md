## Section 4

[004-001] Iohn. Now by the sky that hangs aboue our heads,

[004-002] I like it well. [[france|France]], shall we knit our powres,

[004-003] And lay this [[angiers|Angiers]] euen with the ground,

[004-004] Then after fight who shall be king of it?

[004-005] Bast. And if thou hast the mettle of a king,

[004-006] Being wrong'd as we are by this peeuish Towne:

[004-007] Turne thou the mouth of thy Artillerie,

[004-008] As we will ours, against these sawcie walles,

[004-009] And when that we haue dash'd them to the ground,

[004-010] Why then defie each other, and pell-mell,

[004-011] Make worke vpon our selues, for heauen or hell

[004-012] Fra. Let it be so: say, where will you assault?

[004-013] Iohn. We from the West will send destruction

[004-014] Into this Cities bosome

[004-015] Aust. I from the North

[004-016] Fran. Our Thunder from the South,

[004-017] Shall raine their drift of bullets on this Towne

[004-018] Bast. O prudent discipline! From North to South:

[004-019] [[austria|Austria]] and France shoot in each others mouth.

[004-020] Ile stirre them to it: Come, away, away

[004-021] Hub. Heare vs great kings, vouchsafe awhile to stay

[004-022] And I shall shew you peace, and faire-fac'd league:

[004-023] Win you this Citie without stroke, or wound,

[004-024] Rescue those breathing liues to dye in beds,

[004-025] That heere come sacrifices for the field.

[004-026] Perseuer not, but heare me mighty kings

[004-027] Iohn. Speake on with fauour, we are bent to heare

[004-028] Hub. That daughter there of Spaine, the Lady Blanch

[004-029] Is neere to [[england|England]], looke vpon the yeeres

[004-030] Of Lewes the Dolphin, and that louely maid.

[004-031] If lustie loue should go in quest of beautie,

[004-032] Where should he finde it fairer, then in Blanch:

[004-033] If zealous loue should go in search of vertue,

[004-034] Where should he finde it purer then in Blanch?

[004-035] If loue ambitious, sought a match of birth,

[004-036] Whose veines bound richer blood then Lady Blanch?

[004-037] Such as she is, in beautie, vertue, birth,

[004-038] Is the yong Dolphin euery way compleat,

[004-039] If not compleat of, say he is not shee,

[004-040] And she againe wants nothing, to name want,

[004-041] If want it be not, that she is not hee.

[004-042] He is the halfe part of a blessed man,

[004-043] Left to be finished by such as shee,

[004-044] And she a faire diuided excellence,

[004-045] Whose fulnesse of perfection lyes in him.

[004-046] O two such siluer currents when they ioyne

[004-047] Do glorifie the bankes that bound them in:

[004-048] And two such shores, to two such streames made one,

[004-049] Two such controlling bounds shall you be, kings,

[004-050] To these two Princes, if you marrie them:

[004-051] This Vnion shall do more then batterie can

[004-052] To our fast closed gates: for at this match,

[004-053] With swifter spleene then powder can enforce

[004-054] The mouth of passage shall we fling wide ope,

[004-055] And giue you entrance: but without this match,

[004-056] The sea enraged is not halfe so deafe,

[004-057] Lyons more confident, Mountaines and rockes

[004-058] More free from motion, no not death himselfe

[004-059] In mortall furie halfe so peremptorie,

[004-060] As we to keepe this Citie

[004-061] Bast. Heeres a stay,

[004-062] That shakes the rotten carkasse of old death

[004-063] Out of his ragges. Here's a large mouth indeede,

[004-064] That spits forth death, and mountaines, rockes, and seas,

[004-065] Talkes as familiarly of roaring Lyons,

[004-066] As maids of thirteene do of puppi-dogges.

[004-067] What Cannoneere begot this lustie blood,

[004-068] He speakes plaine Cannon fire, and smoake, and bounce,

[004-069] He giues the bastinado with his tongue:

[004-070] Our eares are cudgel'd, not a word of his

[004-071] But buffets better then a fist of France:

[004-072] Zounds, I was neuer so bethumpt with words,

[004-073] Since I first cal'd my brothers father Dad

[004-074] Old Qu. Son, list to this coniunction, make this match

[004-075] Giue with our Neece a dowrie large enough,

[004-076] For by this knot, thou shalt so surely tye

[004-077] Thy now vnsur'd assurance to the Crowne,

[004-078] That yon greene boy shall haue no Sunne to ripe

[004-079] The bloome that promiseth a mightie fruite.

[004-080] I see a yeelding in the lookes of France:

[004-081] Marke how they whisper, vrge them while their soules

[004-082] Are capeable of this ambition,

[004-083] Least zeale now melted by the windie breath

[004-084] Of soft petitions, pittie and remorse,

[004-085] Coole and congeale againe to what it was

[004-086] Hub. Why answer not the double Maiesties,

[004-087] This friendly treatie of our threatned Towne

[004-088] Fra. Speake England first, that hath bin forward first

[004-089] To speake vnto this Cittie: what say you?

[004-090] Iohn. If that the Dolphin there thy Princely sonne,

[004-091] Can in this booke of beautie read, I loue:

[004-092] Her Dowrie shall weigh equall with a Queene:

[004-093] For Angiers, and faire Toraine [[maine|Maine]], Poyctiers,

[004-094] And all that we vpon this side the Sea,

[004-095] (Except this Cittie now by vs besiedg'd)

[004-096] Finde liable to our Crowne and Dignitie,

[004-097] Shall gild her bridall bed and make her rich

[004-098] In titles, honors, and promotions,

[004-099] As she in beautie, education, blood,

[004-100] Holdes hand with any Princesse of the world

[004-101] Fra. What sai'st thou boy? looke in the Ladies face

[004-102] Dol. I do my Lord, and in her eie I find

[004-103] A wonder, or a wondrous miracle,

[004-104] The shadow of my selfe form'd in her eye,

[004-105] Which being but the shadow of your sonne,

[004-106] Becomes a sonne and makes your sonne a shadow:

[004-107] I do protest I neuer lou'd my selfe

[004-108] Till now, infixed I beheld my selfe,

[004-109] Drawne in the flattering table of her eie.

[004-110] Whispers with Blanch.

[004-111] Bast. Drawne in the flattering table of her eie,

[004-112] Hang'd in the frowning wrinkle of her brow,

[004-113] And quarter'd in her heart, hee doth espie

[004-114] Himselfe loues traytor, this is pittie now;

[004-115] That hang'd, and drawne, and quarter'd there should be

[004-116] In such a loue, so vile a Lout as he

[004-117] Blan. My vnckles will in this respect is mine,

[004-118] If he see ought in you that makes him like,

[004-119] That any thing he see's which moues his liking,

[004-120] I can with ease translate it to my will:

[004-121] Or if you will, to speake more properly,

[004-122] I will enforce it easlie to my loue.

[004-123] Further I will not flatter you, my Lord,

[004-124] That all I see in you is worthie loue,

[004-125] Then this, that nothing do I see in you,

[004-126] Though churlish thoughts themselues should bee your

[004-127] Iudge,

[004-128] That I can finde, should merit any hate

[004-129] Iohn. What saie these yong-ones? What say you my

[004-130] Neece?

[004-131] Blan. That she is bound in [[honour|honor]] still to do

[004-132] What you in wisedome still vouchsafe to say

[004-133] Iohn. Speake then Prince Dolphin, can you loue this

[004-134] Ladie?

[004-135] Dol. Nay aske me if I can refraine from loue,

[004-136] For I doe loue her most vnfainedly

[004-137] Iohn. Then I doe giue [[volquessen|Volquessen]], Toraine, Maine,

[004-138] Poyctiers and Aniow, these fiue Prouinces

[004-139] With her to thee, and this addition more,

[004-140] Full thirty thousand Markes of English coyne:

[004-141] Phillip of France, if thou be pleas'd withall,

[004-142] Command thy sonne and daughter to ioyne hands

[004-143] Fra. It likes vs well young Princes: close your hands

[004-144] Aust. And your lippes too, for I am well assur'd,

[004-145] That I did so when I was first assur'd

[004-146] Fra. Now Cittizens of Angires ope your gates,

[004-147] Let in that amitie which you haue made,

[004-148] For at Saint Maries Chappell presently,

[004-149] The rights of marriage shallbe solemniz'd.

[004-150] Is not the Ladie [[constance|Constance]] in this troope?

[004-151] I know she is not for this match made vp,

[004-152] Her presence would haue interrupted much.

[004-153] Where is she and her sonne, tell me, who knowes?

[004-154] Dol. She is sad and passionate at your highnes Tent

[004-155] Fra. And by my faith, this league that we haue made

[004-156] Will giue her sadnesse very little cure:

[004-157] Brother of England, how may we content

[004-158] This widdow Lady? In her right we came,

[004-159] Which we God knowes, haue turn'd another way,

[004-160] To our owne vantage

[004-161] Iohn. We will heale vp all,

[004-162] For wee'l create yong [[arthur|Arthur]] Duke of Britaine

[004-163] And Earle of Richmond, and this rich faire Towne

[004-164] We make him Lord of. Call the Lady Constance,

[004-165] Some speedy Messenger bid her repaire

[004-166] To our solemnity: I [[faith|trust]] we shall,

[004-167] (If not fill vp the measure of her will)

[004-168] Yet in some measure satisfie her so,

[004-169] That we shall stop her exclamation,

[004-170] Go we as well as hast will suffer vs,

[004-171] To this vnlook'd for vnprepared pompe.

[004-172] Exeunt.

[004-173] Bast. Mad world, mad kings, mad composition:

[004-174] Iohn to stop Arthurs Title in the whole,

[004-175] Hath willingly departed with a part,

[004-176] And France, whose armour Conscience buckled on,

[004-177] Whom zeale and charitie brought to the field,

[004-178] As Gods owne souldier, rounded in the eare,

[004-179] With that same purpose-changer, that slye diuel,

[004-180] That Broker, that still breakes the pate of faith,

[004-181] That dayly breake-vow, he that winnes of all,

[004-182] Of kings, of beggers, old men, yong men, maids,

[004-183] Who hauing no externall thing to loose,

[004-184] But the word Maid, cheats the poore Maide of that.

[004-185] That smooth-fac'd Gentleman, tickling commoditie,

[004-186] Commoditie, the byas of the world,

[004-187] The world, who of it selfe is peysed well,

[004-188] Made to run euen, vpon euen ground;

[004-189] Till this aduantage, this vile drawing byas,

[004-190] This sway of motion, this commoditie,

[004-191] Makes it take head from all indifferency,

[004-192] From all direction, purpose, course, intent.

[004-193] And this same byas, this Commoditie,

[004-194] This Bawd, this Broker, this all-changing-word,

[004-195] Clap'd on the outward eye of fickle France,

[004-196] Hath drawne him from his owne determin'd ayd,

[004-197] From a resolu'd and honourable warre,

[004-198] To a most base and vile-concluded peace.

[004-199] And why rayle I on this Commoditie?

[004-200] But for because he hath not wooed me yet:

[004-201] Not that I haue the power to clutch my hand,

[004-202] When his faire Angels would salute my palme,

[004-203] But for my hand, as vnattempted yet,

[004-204] Like a poore begger, raileth on the rich.

[004-205] Well, whiles I am a begger, I will raile,

[004-206] And say there is no sin but to be rich:

[004-207] And being rich, my vertue then shall be,

[004-208] To say there is no vice, but beggerie:

[004-209] Since Kings breake faith vpon commoditie,

[004-210] Gaine be my Lord, for I will worship thee.

[004-211] Enter.

[004-212] Actus Secundus

[004-213] Enter Constance, Arthur, and [[salisbury|Salisbury]].

[004-214] Con. Gone to be married? Gone to sweare a peace?

[004-215] False blood to false blood ioyn'd. Gone to be freinds?

[004-216] Shall Lewis haue Blaunch, and Blaunch those Prouinces?

[004-217] It is not so, thou hast mispoke, misheard,

[004-218] Be well aduis'd, tell ore thy tale againe.

[004-219] It cannot be, thou do'st but say 'tis so.

[004-220] I trust I may not trust thee, for thy word

[004-221] Is but the vaine breath of a common man:

[004-222] Beleeue me, I doe not beleeue thee man,

[004-223] I haue a Kings oath to the contrarie.

[004-224] Thou shalt be punish'd for thus frighting me,

[004-225] For I am sicke, and capeable of feares,

[004-226] Opprest with wrongs, and therefore full of feares,

[004-227] A widdow, husbandles, subiect to feares,

[004-228] A woman naturally borne to feares;

[004-229] And though thou now confesse thou didst but iest

[004-230] With my vext spirits, I cannot take a Truce,

[004-231] But they will quake and tremble all this day.

[004-232] What dost thou meane by shaking of thy head?

[004-233] Why dost thou looke so sadly on my sonne?

[004-234] What meanes that hand vpon that breast of thine?

[004-235] Why holdes thine eie that lamentable rhewme,

[004-236] Like a proud riuer peering ore his bounds?

[004-237] Be these sad signes confirmers of thy words?

[004-238] Then speake againe, not all thy former tale,

[004-239] But this one word, whether thy tale be true

[004-240] Sal. As true as I beleeue you thinke them false,

[004-241] That giue you cause to proue my saying true

[004-242] Con. Oh if thou teach me to beleeue this [[grief|sorrow]],

[004-243] Teach thou this sorrow, how to make me dye,

[004-244] And let beleefe, and life encounter so,

[004-245] As doth the furie of two desperate men,

[004-246] Which in the very meeting fall, and dye.

[004-247] Lewes marry Blaunch? O boy, then where art thou?

[004-248] France friend with England, what becomes of me?

[004-249] Fellow be gone: I cannot brooke thy sight,

[004-250] This newes hath made thee a most vgly man

[004-251] Sal. What other harme haue I good Lady done,

[004-252] But spoke the harme, that is by others done?

[004-253] Con. Which harme within it selfe so heynous is,

[004-254] As it makes harmefull all that speake of it

[004-255] Ar. I do beseech you Madam be content
