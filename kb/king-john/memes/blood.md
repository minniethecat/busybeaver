# Blood

## Definition

Blood in *[[king-john|King John]]* operates on two registers simultaneously: as the carrier of dynastic legitimacy ("blood right") and as the marker of violence ("bloodshed"). The play's action moves between these two meanings, tracing how the claim of blood becomes the cause of blood.

## Manifestations in the Play

### Blood as Lineage

[[king-john|King John]]'s claim to the [[crown|throne]] is a claim of blood — his inheritance from Richard I. Yet [[arthur|Arthur]]'s blood claim is stronger, making blood a source of [[war|conflict]] rather than resolution. The Bastard complicates this further: his "blood" is royal (Richard I is his father), but his social position is illegitimate. Blood, the play suggests, is never a simple guarantee of identity or right.

### Blood as Violence

The siege of [[angiers|Angiers]] transforms blood from a claim into a reality: "All preparation for a bloody siege" (003-090) and "bloody power" (003-098) mark the shift from political argument to military action. When [[constance|Constance]] imagines returning home with "that lusty blood again" (003-132), blood signifies both [[arthur|Arthur]]'s living presence and the violence that threatens it.

### Blood and the Living Body

The Bastard's observation that "living blood doth in these temples beat" (002-239) grounds the play's abstractions in physical reality. Blood is not merely a metaphor — it is what flows through living bodies, and when it is spilled, the political order is not merely threatened but physically wounded.
