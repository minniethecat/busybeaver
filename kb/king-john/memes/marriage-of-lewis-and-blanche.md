# Marriage of Lewis and Blanche

## Event Overview

The political marriage between [[lewis-the-dauphin|Lewis the Dauphin]] and [[blanche-of-castile|Blanche of Castile]], designed to seal peace between [[england|England]] and [[france|France]]. The match is proposed by the citizens of Angiers as a way to end the siege of their city: [[king-john|King John]] offers his niece Blanche along with five French provinces as her dowry (002-192), and [[king-philip-ii|King Philip II]] accepts on behalf of his son Lewis. The marriage is solemnized at Saint Mary's Chapel, temporarily uniting the warring kings.

> If lustie loue should go in quest of beautie,
> Where should he finde it fairer, then in Blanch:
> If zealous loue should go in search of vertue,
> Where should he finde it purer then in Blanch?
> If loue ambitious, sought a match of birth,
> Whose veines bound richer blood then Lady Blanch?
>
> (004-031–036)

## Role in the Play

### Narrative Function

Marriage of Lewis and Blanche serves as a crucial turning point in the play's action. The event reshapes the political landscape and [[power|force]]s characters to make choices that define the remainder of the dramatic arc. What appears to be a diplomatic triumph — uniting the houses of England and France — is immediately undercut by [[constance|Constance]]'s grief and rage at the betrayal of her son [[arthur|Arthur]]'s claim.

### Key Participants

The principal figures involved in Marriage of Lewis and Blanche represent the intersecting political [[power|force]]s at work in the play. [[king-john|King John]] offers the bride and the dowry; [[king-philip-ii|King Philip II]] accepts for his son; [[lewis-the-dauphin|Lewis]] and [[blanche-of-castile|Blanche]] are the matched couple; and [[constance|Constance]], excluded from the negotiations, denounces the marriage as a betrayal.

### Consequences

The aftermath of Marriage of Lewis and Blanche reverberates through subsequent acts, affecting alliances, loyalties, and the fates of all major characters. The peace it creates is fragile and short-lived. [[constance|Constance]]'s curse — that the marriage bed will breed "a plague" (005-213) — proves prophetic when the alliance collapses and war resumes.
