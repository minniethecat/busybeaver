## Overview

The peace settlement of Act 5 brings the play's tangled [[war|conflict]]s to resolution. After [[king-john|King John]] dies of poison (011-095), his son [[prince-henry|Prince Henry]] assumes leadership. The rebel English lords, who had joined [[lewis-the-dauphin|Lewis the Dauphin]]'s [[french-invasion-of-england|invasion]], return to their [[faith|allegiance]] (011-085). The French, weakened by the [[wreck-of-the-french-fleet|loss of their reinforcements]] and facing a unified English front, negotiate a withdrawal. The settlement restores the political order that [[king-john|John]]'s reign had placed under such severe strain.

## The Bastard's Closing Speech

The settlement is framed by [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard's]] famous closing speech, which shifts the register from diplomatic detail to patriotic assertion:

> This England neuer did, nor neuer shall
> Lye at the proud foote of a Conqueror...
>
> (011-204–205)

The speech transforms the ad-hoc peace into a declaration of national resilience. Notably, [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]] speaks not as a legitimate [[power|authority]] but as the play's moral center — the character who has earned, through his journey from bastard adventurer to loyal commander, the right to pronounce the play's closing verdict on [[england|England]].

## Dramatic Significance

The peace settlement is achieved not through military victory but through convergence: [[king-john|John]]'s death removes the source of [[war|conflict]], the French fleet's destruction removes Lewis's capability, and [[prince-henry|Prince Henry]]'s succession provides continuity. It is a resolution that feels contingent rather than triumphant — appropriate for a play that has persistently questioned the legitimacy of political power.
