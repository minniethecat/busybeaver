# The Crown of England

**The Crown of England** is the central political institution in Shakespeare's *[[king-john|King John]]*, embodied by [[king-john|King John]]'s royal government. It is the source of legitimacy, the object of contention, and the prize for which [[france|France]], the [[the-papacy|Papacy]], and rebel English lords compete throughout the play.

> Iohn. Doth not the Crowne of England, prooue the
> King?
> And if not that, I bring you Witnesses
> Twice fifteene thousand hearts of Englands breed
>
> Bast. Bastards and else
>
> [003-150–153]

## Role in the Play

The English Crown serves as the gravitational center around which all political action orbits. The play opens with [[king-john|John]] enthroned at [[northampton|Northampton]], receiving [[chatillon|Chatillon]]'s embassy (001-083). From this point, the [[crown|Crown]]'s [[power|authority]] is tested by three [[power|force]]s:

- **Dynastic challenge**: [[arthur|Arthur Plantagenet]]'s claim (001-085), backed by [[king-philip-ii|King Philip II]]
- **Ecclesiastical challenge**: [[pandulph|Pandulph]]'s papal [[curse]] (005-102) and [[johns-excommunication|excommunication]]
- **Internal rebellion**: The [[revolt-of-the-english-lords|revolt]] of [[salisbury|Salisbury]], [[pembroke|Pembroke]], and [[bigot|Bigot]]

## Authority and Legitimacy

The [[crown|Crown]]'s [[power|authority]] derives from multiple sources that the play systematically interrogates:

- **Hereditary right**: [[king-john|John]]'s claim rests on being Henry II's son, but [[arthur|Arthur]]'s superior hereditary claim (as son of [[king-john|John]]'s elder brother Geoffrey) destabilizes this foundation (002-240)
- **Possession**: The Bastard's "commodity" speech argues that de [[truth|fact]]o possession matters more than de jure right
- **Papal recognition**: [[king-john|John]]'s initial defiance of the Pope and later submission demonstrate the [[crown|Crown]]'s dependence on ecclesiastical sanction

## Historical Context

The historical reign of [[king-john|John]] (1199–1216) was marked by the loss of Normandy (1204), the papal interdict (1208–1213), and the baronial rebellion that produced Magna Carta (1215). Shakespeare's play compresses these events and omits Magna Carta entirely, focusing instead on the personal and political [[war|conflict]]s that characterized [[king-john|John]]'s rule.
