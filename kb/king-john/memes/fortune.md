# Fortune (King John)

**Fortune** is a recurring concept in *[[king-john|King John]]*, functioning as both a rhetorical device and a thematic preoccupation. Characters across the play invoke fortune to explain reversals, justify risks, and lament their circumstances — reflecting an early modern worldview in which Fate and Fortune were understood as quasi-divine [[power|force]]s governing human affairs (001-227) (006-042) (006-043).

## The Bastard's Fortune

[[philip-the-bastard]] is the character most intimately associated with fortune. He abandons his inherited identity as the son of [[sir-robert-faulconbridge|Sir Robert Faulconbridge]] to claim his "fortune" as the bastard son of [[king-richard-i|Richard the Lionheart]] (001-227). Throughout the play, [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]] serves as fortune's agent and commentator — seizing opportunities, adapting to reversals, and embodying the principle that fortune favours the bold (002-022) (002-197) (002-199).

> That smooth-fac'd Gentleman, tickling commoditie,
> Commoditie, the byas of the world,
> The world, who of it selfe is peysed well,
> Made to run euen, vpon euen ground;
> Till this aduantage, this vile drawing byas,
> This sway of motion, this commoditie,
> Makes it take head from all indifferency,
> From all direction, purpose, course, intent.
>
> (004-185)

## Fortune and Political Power

The play repeatedly frames royal power as subject to fortune's caprice:

- Kings stake their fortunes on military ventures, as at the [[siege-of-angiers]] (002-199)
- Political alliances rise and fall with fortune's wheel, as in the [[the-league-of-angiers]] and its collapse
- The [[wreck-of-the-french-fleet]] demonstrates fortune's power to undo even the [[power|might]]iest military expedition

> Fortune in King John is not mere chance — it is a moralised force, rewarding the bold and punishing the overreaching, yet ultimately beyond human control or prediction (005-089).

## Relation to Providence

The play balances a classical notion of Fortune against a Christian framework of divine providence. [[pandulph]] and the [[the-papacy]] represent providential order, while [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]] personifies fortune's secular energy. The tension between these two frameworks — fortune vs. providence, chance vs. divine plan — is one of the play's unresolved philosophical questions.
