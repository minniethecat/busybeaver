# France

France is the primary antagonistic power in *[[king-john|King John]]*: first as the sponsor of
[[arthur|Arthur]]'s claim to the English [[crown|throne]], then as the invading army that lands
on English soil under the Dauphin's command. The kingdom appears throughout the
play, its relationship to [[england|England]] shifting from diplomatic challenge to open war.

## Role in the Play

### The Embassy and the Claim

France enters the play in the person of [[chatillon|Chatillon]], ambassador to the English
court. The opening scene establishes France's role as challenger to [[king-john|John]]'s
legitimacy:

> Now say Chatillion, what would France with vs? (001-076)

[[chatillon|Chatillon]]'s reply articulates France's position as [[arthur|Arthur]]'s protector. [[king-philip-ii|Philip II]]
claims [[england|England]]'s [[crown|throne]] — and its continental territories — not for himself but
"in right and true behalfe / Of thy deceased brother, Geffreyes sonne" (001-083).
[[king-john|John]]'s response is defiance grounded in military confidence:

> Be thou as lightning in the eies of France;
> For ere thou canst report, I will be there:
> The thunder of my Cannon shall be heard. (001-100)

### Before Angiers: France and England Face to Face

The armies meet at [[angiers|Angiers]] in Act II, where France's military presence is
announced with full pageantry:

> Enter before Angiers, Philip King of France, Lewis, Daulphin... (002-125)

At [[angiers|Angiers]], Philip II's claims to moral [[power|authority]] are tested when [[king-john|John]] challenges
the French king's right to intervene in English succession:

> From whom hast thou this great commission France,
> To draw thy answer from their articles? (002-241)

The Dauphin [[lewis-the-dauphin]] formalizes the territorial claim later in the scene:

> England and Ireland, Angiers, Toraine, Maine,
> In right of Arthur doe I claime of thee... (003-024)

[[king-john|John]]'s response — "My life as soone: I doe defie thee France" (003-027) — signals
that the [[war|conflict]] will be settled by war, not diplomacy. The [[war|battle]] outside
[[angiers|Angiers]] produces no clear victor, and [[citizen-of-angiers|the Citizen]] proposes the marriage of
[[blanche-of-castile|Blanch]] of Spain to the Dauphin as a peace settlement.

### "Fickle France": Alliance and Betrayal

The peace brokered at [[angiers|Angiers]] proves fragile. When [[pandulph|Cardinal Pandulph]] arrives as
papal legate and excommunicates [[king-john|John]], he demands that Philip II abandon the
English alliance. The French king's dilemma captures the play's concern with
political inconstancy. [[pandulph|Pandulph]]'s ultimatum is absolute:

> Philip of France, on perill of a curse,
> Let goe the hand of that Arch-heretique,
> And raise the power of France vpon his head,
> Vnlesse he doe submit himselfe to Rome (005-156)

[[queen-elinor|Elinor]] taunts the wavering monarch: "Look'st thou pale France? do not let go
thy hand" (005-160). Philip breaks with [[king-john|John]], choosing papal obedience over his
new alliance. The Bastard's commentary on this turn brands France with the
epithet that echoes through the rest of the play:

> O foule reuolt of French inconstancy (006-027)

Even [[constance|Constance]], who has most to gain from France's renewed hostility to [[king-john|John]],
is appalled by the moral cost:

> France friend with England, what becomes of me? (004-248)

The alliance with [[england|England]] has been sacrificed to the papacy, and [[constance|Constance]]'s
son [[arthur|Arthur]] is left without the protection France once promised.

### The Dauphin's Invasion

In the play's final movement, the Dauphin Lewis leads a French invasion of
[[england|England]] — an enterprise justified by [[pandulph|Pandulph]]'s earlier promise of papal
support but now driven by Lewis's own ambition for conquest. [[king-john|John]], learning
of the invasion's scale, is stunned:

> Poure downe thy weather: how goes all in France? (008-085)

The messenger's reply describes an army of unprecedented magnitude:

> From France to England, neuer such a powre
> For any forraigne preparation,
> Was leuied in the body of a land. (008-086)

When [[king-john|John]] asks who commands these [[power|force]]s, the answer is chilling: "Vnder the
Dolphin" (008-108). The French crown prince — once the bridegroom in a peace
alliance — now leads the invasion.

Lewis's ambition, however, proves self-destructive. [[melun|Count Melune]], a French
nobleman mortally wounded at the [[war|battle]], reveals to the rebel English lords
that Lewis intends to betray them once victory is secured:

> The Count Meloone, a Noble Lord of France,
> Whose priuate with me of the Dolphines loue,
> Is much more generall, then these lines import (009-017)

This revelation triggers the rebel lords' return to their English [[faith|allegiance]],
fulfilling the play's central thesis that [[england|England]]'s vulnerability lies in
internal division — and that French ambition, once exposed, unites even
estranged Englishmen against the foreign invader.

### France in the Play's Rhetoric

Throughout the drama, "France" functions as more than a geographical term.
It is used as a personified antagonist, directly addressed and rebuked.
[[king-john|John]] defies "thee France" (003-027); [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]] taunts "France, I am burn'd
vp with inflaming wrath" (006-045); and Philip II is repeatedly warned that
"France, y shalt rue this houre" (006-028). This personification gives the
political [[war|conflict]] an almost personal intensity, as if the nation itself
were a character capable of being shamed, defied, or warned.

France is also associated with the play's vocabulary of inconstancy. The
Bastard's "O foule reuolt of French inconstancy" articulates a prejudice
already present in the drama's structure: France shifts [[faith|allegiance]] from
conquest to alliance, from alliance to excommunication, and from
excommunication to invasion. Each turn serves immediate advantage but
erodes the moral credibility that Philip II claimed when first championing
[[arthur|Arthur]]'s cause.

## Historical Context

### Philip II Augustus

The historical Philip II of France (r. 1180–1223), known as Philip Augustus,
was one of medieval Europe's most successful monarchs. His [[war|conflict]] with the
Plantagenet kings — Henry II, Richard I, and [[king-john|John]] — defined the geopolitics
of western Europe in the late twelfth and early thirteenth centuries. Philip
did indeed support [[arthur|Arthur]] of [[brittany|Brittany]]'s claim to the English [[crown|throne]], though
[[arthur|Arthur]]'s mysterious disappearance in 1203 (while in [[king-john|John]]'s custody) shifts
the historical focus from succession to conquest.

### The Real Invasion of 1216

The Dauphin Louis (the future Louis VIII) did invade [[england|England]] in 1216 at the
invitation of rebel barons, landing at Sandwich in May. The historical Louis
controlled much of southeast [[england|England]] and was proclaimed king in [[london|London]] before
[[king-john|John]]'s death in October 1216 and the subsequent rallying of support for the
young [[prince-henry|Henry III]] [[power|force]]d Louis to abandon his claim in the Treaty of Lambeth
(1217). Shakespeare compresses this multi-year [[war|conflict]] into the play's final
two acts.

## Symbolic Significance

France in *[[king-john|King John]]* serves as the necessary external antagonist against which
English identity is forged. The play's rhetoric of patriotism — [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]]'s
"[[england|Mother-England]]," [[salisbury|Salisbury]]'s "children of [[england|this Isle]]," the final insistence
that [[england|England]] must be "true" to itself — all require France as the foreign
other. France's shift from diplomatic ally to invading enemy mirrors the
play's moral structure: what begins as a legitimate claim on behalf of [[arthur|Arthur]]
becomes naked ambition under the Dauphin, whose intended betrayal of his
English allies reveals French [[honour|honor]] as a political instrument rather than
a binding principle.

The contrast between France and [[england|England]] is not merely political but dramatic.
French inconstancy — the "foule reuolt" that destroys the [[angiers|Angiers]] peace —
validates English [[faith|loyalty]] by opposition. When [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]] speaks of
"[[england|Mother-England]]," he invokes a bond of nature that France, a land "remote"
fighting under "vnacquainted colours" (010-018), cannot claim.
