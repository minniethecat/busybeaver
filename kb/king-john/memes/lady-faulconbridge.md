# Lady Faulconbridge

Lady Faulconbridge is the widow of [[sir-robert-faulconbridge]] and the mother
of [[philip-the-bastard]]. Her confession — that Philip was fathered not by her
husband but by [[king-richard-i]] ([[king-richard-i|Richard the Lionheart]]) — is one of the play's
pivotal revelations, transforming Philip from a mere gentleman's son into a
Plantagenet.

## Entrance and Confrontation

Lady Faulconbridge enters in Act I, Scene 2, summoned to clarify the disputed
parentage of her son Philip. The stage direction marks her entrance alongside
her servant:

> Enter Lady Faulconbridge and Iames Gurney (002-065)

She immediately demands to know the whereabouts of her legitimate son Robert,
showing a mother's concern that cuts across the legal dispute:

> Where is that slaue thy brother? where is he? (002-066)

Her language — calling Robert a "slaue" (knave) — suggests favoritism toward
Philip, whose wit and spirit she may prefer over Robert's legalistic claim.

## The Interrogation

Philip, now knighted as Sir [[king-richard-i|Richard Plantagenet]] by [[king-john]], turns his
characteristic wit upon his mother. He demands to know the truth of his
parentage with a mixture of jest and genuine need:

> But mother, I am not Sir Roberts sonne,
> I haue disclaim'd Sir Robert and my land... (002-092)

> Then good my mother, let me know my father,
> Some proper man I hope, who was it mother? (002-095)

Lady Faulconbridge's initial resistance gives way under her son's persistent
questioning:

> Hast thou denied thy selfe a Faulconbridge? (002-097)

## The Confession

The crucial revelation comes when Lady Faulconbridge finally admits the truth:

> King Richard Cordelion was thy father (002-099)

This confession transforms the scene — and Philip's identity. He is no longer
the illegitimate son of a country knight but the bastard of a king, a
Plantagenet by blood if not by law. Lady Faulconbridge's admission, made under
pressure but without coercion, grants Philip the [[blood|lineage]] he requires to claim a
place in [[england|England]]'s ruling dynasty.

## Character Significance

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Dramatic function | Provides the crucial revelation of Philip's royal parentage |
| Moral complexity | Her adultery with King Richard is a sin, but her [[truth|honesty]] redeems her |
| Relationship to theme | Embodies the play's concern with legitimacy, truth, and social identity |
| Stage presence | Brief (one scene) but decisive — her confession changes the plot's direction |

Lady Faulconbridge's confession also reveals something about the late King
Richard I: the Lionheart's private conduct was as bold as his public reputation.
The play thus uses her testimony to build a portrait of Richard without ever
bringing him on stage.
