# Arthur's Plea to Hubert

**Arthur's Plea to Hubert** is the central scene of Act 4, Scene 1 in *[[king-john|King John]]*. Having secretly ordered [[hubert]] to put out the young [[arthur]]'s eyes — thereby rendering the boy incapable of ruling — [[king-john]] has placed [[arthur|Arthur]] in [[hubert|Hubert]]'s custody (006-059) (006-061). The scene in which [[arthur|Arthur]] pleads with his would-be executioner is widely regarded as one of Shakespeare's most affecting depictions of innocence confronting cruelty.

## The Scene

[[hubert|Hubert]], armed with a hot iron and attended by executioners, prepares to carry out the King's command (007-122). The young [[arthur|Arthur]], unaware at first, speaks with gentle affection to [[hubert|Hubert]] — calling him "kind [[hubert|Hubert]]" and noting how much he cares for him. When [[hubert|Hubert]] reveals the warrant ordering the blinding, [[arthur|Arthur]]'s response unfolds in stages:

1. **Shock and dis[[faith|belief]]**: [[arthur|Arthur]] cannot reconcile the affectionate [[hubert|Hubert]] with the man holding the iron
2. **[[rhetoric|Rhetoric]]al persuasion**: [[arthur|Arthur]] reminds [[hubert|Hubert]] of past kindnesses and the bonds of humanity (007-142)
3. **Pathos**: [[arthur|Arthur]] describes the physical pain of the iron and the darkness of blindness with childlike vividness
4. **Moral appeal**: [[arthur|Arthur]] invokes divine justice and [[hubert|Hubert]]'s own conscience (007-190)

> Hubert, the vtterance of a brace of tongues,
> Must needes want pleading for a paire of eyes:
> Let me not hold my tongue: let me not Hubert,
> Or Hubert, if you will cut out my tongue,
> So I may keepe mine eyes. O spare mine eyes,
> Though to no vse, but still to looke on you.
>
> (007-186)

## Dramatic Achievement

The scene's power derives from its intimate focus — two characters alone, one an innocent child, the other a reluctant agent of state [[blood|violence]]. Shakespeare strips away the political framework to expose the human cost of royal power, a technique he would develop further in the tragedies.

## Outcome

[[hubert|Hubert]] relents. Moved by [[arthur|Arthur]]'s eloquence and innocence, he dismisses the executioners and spares the boy — though he tells [[king-john]] that [[arthur|Arthur]] is dead, setting in motion the chain of events leading to [[arthurs-death]] and the [[revolt-of-the-english-lords]].

> The scene demonstrates Shakespeare's mastery of pathos: the child's plea achieves what no political argument could — it breaks the machinery of royal command through sheer moral force.

## Critical Reception

Critics have long noted that this scene — with its combination of tenderness, terror, and redemption — anticipates the great set-pieces of Shakespeare's mature work, particularly the blinding of Gloucester in *King Lear*.
