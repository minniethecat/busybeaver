# Pembroke

## Character Overview

The Earl of Pembroke is one of the English lords who joins [[salisbury|Salisbury]]'s rebellion against [[king-john|King John]]. More than a silent follower, Pembroke emerges as a significant voice in his own right — serving as the spokesman for the nobles' grievances, demanding the release of [[arthur|Arthur]], and later discovering the body of the murdered prince. His trajectory from loyal courtier to rebel to reconciled subject tracks the play's larger movement from crisis to resolution.

## Role in the Play

### Courtier and Critic

Pembroke first appears as part of [[king-john|John]]'s retinue, present at the confrontation with [[king-philip-ii|King Philip II]] before [[angiers|Angiers]] (002-213). He is part of John's military expedition to France and witnesses the [[the-league-of-angiers|league of Angiers]] — the diplomatic marriage that briefly unites England and France.

### Voice of the Lords

Pembroke's most significant role emerges in Act 4, following John's second coronation. When the king invites his nobles to speak freely, Pembroke steps forward as their designated spokesman. "Then I, as one that am the tongue of these / To sound the purposes of all their hearts" (008-020–021), he declares, requesting the release of Arthur from imprisonment. His language is carefully diplomatic — "Both for my selfe, and them: but chiefe of all / Your safety" (008-022–023) — yet the substance is a direct challenge to John's policy of holding the rival claimant captive.

> Then I, as one that am the tongue of these
> To sound the purposes of all their hearts,
> Both for my selfe, and them: but chiefe of all
> Your safety
>
> (008-020)

Pembroke argues that John's fears of Arthur are self-defeating. If John's title is just, Arthur poses no threat: "If what in rest you haue, in right you hold, / Why then your feares ... should moue you to mew vp / Your tender kinsman?" (008-028–030). The logic is impeccable, and it exposes the contradiction at the heart of John's position.

### The Discovery of Arthur's Body

When [[hubert|Hubert]] arrives with the news of Arthur's death, Pembroke is the first to voice suspicion: "This is the man should do the bloody deed" (008-043). As the lords search for Arthur's body in Act 4, Pembroke is present at its discovery. Confronted with the corpse of the young prince, he erupts in horror: "All murthers past, do stand excus'd in this" (009-055). His reaction — that this crime is so monstrous it renders all previous murders merely excusable by comparison — underscores the depth of his revulsion at John's tyranny.

Pembroke joins [[salisbury|Salisbury]] in vowing revenge: "Our soules religiously confirme thy words" (009-077), binding himself to the oath that leads the lords into alliance with [[lewis-the-dauphin|Lewis the Dauphin]].

### Return to Loyalty

Though Pembroke's arc carries him into rebellion, his ultimate destination is reconciliation. When the French plan to execute the English lords after victory is revealed by [[melun|Count Meloone]], Pembroke and the other lords return to their natural allegiance. His presence at the final peace settlement signals the restoration of order that [[prince-henrys-succession|Prince Henry's succession]] represents.

## Dramatic Significance

Pembroke serves as a barometer of aristocratic sentiment in the play. Unlike [[salisbury|Salisbury]], whose rebellion is driven by moral outrage and elaborate grief, Pembroke represents the more pragmatic voice of the English nobility — loyal when loyalty serves the realm, critical when the crown endangers it, and ultimately reconcilable when legitimate authority is restored. His function as "the tongue" (008-020) of the lords makes him the play's embodiment of collective aristocratic conscience.

## Key Relationships

- **[[salisbury|Salisbury]]**: Pembroke's closest ally among the rebel lords, though Salisbury's rhetoric is more passionate and Pembroke's more measured
- **[[king-john|King John]]**: Pembroke's sovereign, whose tyranny drives him to rebellion; ultimately reconciled through Prince Henry
- **[[arthur|Arthur]]**: The young prince whose imprisonment and death catalyze Pembroke's break with John
- **[[hubert|Hubert]]**: The agent of John's will whom Pembroke identifies as "the man should do the bloody deed" (008-043)
