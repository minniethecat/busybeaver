# The League of Angiers

**The League of Angiers** is a diplomatic settlement in Act 3 of Shakespeare's *[[king-john|King John]]*, negotiated between [[king-john|King John]] of [[england|England]] and [[king-philip-ii|King Philip II]] of [[france|France]] following the inconclusive [[siege-of-angiers|Siege of Angiers]]. The league is sealed through the marriage of [[blanche-of-castile|Blanche of Castile]] ([[king-john|John]]'s niece) to [[lewis-the-dauphin|Lewis the Dauphin]] and [[king-john|John]]'s cession of five French territories. Though celebrated as a triumph of diplomacy, the league collapses within a single scene when [[pandulph|Pandulph]] arrives with the papal [[curse]].

## Background

The league is proposed by [[citizen-of-angiers|the Citizen]] of [[angiers|Angiers]] as a solution to the military stalemate between the English and French armies outside the city walls. Both kings have claimed [[angiers|Angiers]], and neither can dislodge the other. The Citizen's proposal — a marriage alliance — transforms military [[war|conflict]] into diplomatic negotiation.

## The Terms

The league involves two principal components:

| Component | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **[[marriage|Marriage]]** | [[lewis-the-dauphin|Lewis the Dauphin]] weds [[blanche-of-castile|Blanche of Castile]], [[king-john|John]]'s niece |
| **Territorial Cession** | [[king-john|John]] grants [[volquessen|Volquessen]], [[touraine|Touraine]], [[maine|Maine]], [[anjou|Anjou]], and [[poitiers|Poitiers]] to [[france|France]] |

The marriage is celebrated with remarkable speed. Lewis and Blanche exchange vows of love within moments of their first meeting, their political marriage cloaked in the language of romantic passion:

> And by my faith, this league that we haue made
> Will giue vs peace and plenty
> (004-155)

## Consequences for Arthur

The league's most immediate victim is [[arthur|Arthur Plantagenet]], whose claim to the English [[crown|throne]] is effectively abandoned by Philip in exchange for territorial gain. [[constance|Constance]], [[arthur|Arthur]]'s mother, responds with a torrent of grief and rage:

> This day of shame, oppression, periury
> (005-050)

Her denunciation frames the league as fundamentally immoral — a betrayal dressed in diplomatic ceremony. The [[philip-the-bastard|Bastard]]'s commentary on "commodity" (political expediency) offers a cynical analysis of the same transaction:

> Mad world, mad kings, mad composition
> (004-173)

## The League's Collapse

[[pandulph|Pandulph]]'s arrival immediately destabilizes the settlement. As papal legate, he demands that Philip break the league — and the [[oath|oath]] that seals it — or face excommunication:

> Philip of France, on perill of a curse,
> Let goe the hand of that Arch-heretique
> (005-156)

Philip's agonized choice between keeping his oath to [[king-john|John]] and obeying the Pope dramatizes the play's central [[war|conflict]] between secular and religious [[power|authority]]. His decision to break the league and resume war against [[king-john|John]] marks the play's pivot from diplomacy back to [[war|conflict]].

## Thematic Significance

The league embodies several of the play's central concerns:

- **The fragility of oaths**: The league's collapse illustrates how easily sacred vows are broken when political or religious pressure intervenes
- **[[marriage|Marriage]] as politics**: Blanche and Lewis's [[marriage|union]] is the play's most explicit representation of marriage as a political instrument
- **Commodity**: The Bastard's famous soliloquy on "commodity" is a direct response to the league, analyzing how self-interest governs all political action — "That smooth-fac'd Gentleman, tickling commoditie" (004-185)
