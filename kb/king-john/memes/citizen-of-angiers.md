# Citizen of Angiers

The Citizen of Angiers is an unnamed but pivotal figure who speaks from the walls
of the besieged city in Shakespeare's *[[king-john|King John]]*. Acting as the voice of
[[angiers]]'s populace, this character shifts the play's trajectory from military
confrontation to diplomatic resolution by proposing the marriage alliance between
[[lewis-the-dauphin]] and [[blanche-of-castile]].

## Appearance at the Walls

The Citizen first appears in Act II, Scene 1, summoned by the trumpets of the
opposing armies that surround [[angiers|Angiers]]. From atop the city walls, the Citizen
speaks with an [[power|authority]] that belies their common status — addressing two kings
as an equal and demanding to know their purpose:

> Who is it that hath warn'd vs to the walles? (003-077)

This opening line establishes the Citizen's role as a figure who stands outside
the feudal hierarchy, answerable not to any king but to the city's collective
interest.

## The Stalemate Assessment

Speaking through [[hubert|Hubert]], the Citizen delivers a frank assessment of the military
situation. Both the [[england|english]] army under [[king-john]] and the [[france|french]]
[[power|force]]s under [[king-philip-ii]] have fought to a standstill before the
city gates. The Citizen's report cuts through royal rhetoric with plain
observation:

> Blood hath bought blood, and blowes haue answerd blowes:
> Strength matcht with strength, and power confronted power,
> Both are alike, and both alike we like:
> One must proue greatest. While they weigh so euen,
> We hold our Towne for neither: yet for both. (003-213)

This declaration of neutrality — "for neither: yet for both" — captures the
Citizen's political acumen. [[angiers|Angiers]] refuses to commit until a clear victor
emerges, a prudent strategy that preserves the city's independence.

## Defiance of Royal Authority

When pressed by the [[king-philip-ii|King of France]] to declare [[faith|allegiance]], the Citizen
refuses to be intimidated. The gates remain barred until "our feares resolu'd /
Be by some certaine king, purg'd and depos'd" (003-257). This defiance draws a
sharp response from [[philip-the-bastard]], who calls the citizens "scroyles of
[[angiers|Angiers]]" that "flout you kings" (003-259), and proposes a joint assault on the
city.

The Citizen's steadfastness transforms the scene. Rather than choosing between
two kings, the citizens of [[angiers|Angiers]] [[power|force]] both monarchs to negotiate — a
remarkable inversion of the expected power dynamic.

## The Marriage Proposal

The Citizen's most consequential act is the proposal of a marriage alliance.
Recognizing that continued war benefits neither side, the Citizen suggests uniting
the houses of [[england|England]] and [[france|France]] through the marriage of [[lewis-the-dauphin|Lewis the Dauphin]] to
[[blanche-of-castile]], [[king-john|King John]]'s niece:

> That daughter there of Spaine, the Lady Blanch,
> Is neere to England, looke vpon the yeeres
> Of Lewes the Dolphin, and that louely maid.
> If lustie loue should go in quest of beautie,
> Where should he finde it fairer, then in Blanch?
> If zealous loue should go in search of vertue,
> Where should he finde it purer then in Blanch? (004-033)
> If loue ambitious, sought a match of birth,
> Whose veines bound richer blood then Lady Blanch? (004-028)

The proposal is audacious in its pragmatism. A common citizen dictates terms to
royalty, offering the city's [[faith|allegiance]] as the prize for peace:

> For at this match,
> With swifter spleene then powder can enforce
> The mouth of passage shall we fling wide ope,
> And giue you entrance: but without this match,
> The sea enraged is not halfe so deafe... (004-052)

## Dramatic Significance

The Citizen of Angiers serves several interconnected dramatic functions:

| Function | Effect |
|----------|--------|
| Voice of the commons | Challenges the assumption that royal [[war|conflict]]s concern only nobility |
| Catalyst for peace | The marriage proposal redirects the plot from war to alliance |
| Mirror to kings | The Citizen's clear-eyed pragmatism exposes royal posturing |
| Structural pivot | Shifts Act II from military confrontation to diplomatic negotiation |

The marriage arranged at the Citizen's suggestion does not secure lasting peace —
the alliance fractures in Act III under ecclesiastical pressure. Yet the
Citizen's intervention demonstrates that even in a world of kings and conquest,
the voice of an ordinary citizen can reshape the course of events.
