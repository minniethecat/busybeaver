# Arthur

Arthur Plantagenet, Duke of [[brittany|Brittany]], is the son of Geoffrey Plantagenet ([[king-john|King John]]'s elder brother) and [[constance|Constance]]. As the son of the elder brother, Arthur's hereditary claim to the English [[crown|throne]] is legally superior to [[king-john|John]]'s — a [[truth|fact]] that drives the central political [[war|conflict]] of *[[king-john|King John]]*.

## Role in the Play

### The Contested Heir

Arthur is the play's most important character who never commits a political act. He is, instead, the object around which political action revolves. The French ambassador announces Arthur's claim in Act 1, declaring that "Arthur Plantaginet, laies most lawfull claime" to the English [[crown|throne]] (001-085). At the gates of [[angiers|Angiers]] in Act 2, [[king-philip-ii|King Philip of France]] presents Arthur as the rightful king, stating that "In right of Arthur doe I claime of thee" the territories of [[england|England]], [[ireland|Ireland]], and [[france|France]] (003-025).

Yet Arthur himself seems to want no part of the [[war|conflict]]. His first spoken words are a wish for death rather than contention:

> I would that I were low laid in my graue,
> I am not worth this coyle that's made for me
> (003-037-038)

This self-effacement defines Arthur's dramatic function: he is the innocent center around which a vortex of adult ambition and [[blood|violence]] spins. The [[citizen-of-angiers|Citizen of Angiers]]'s neutrality — "Whose title they admit, Arthurs or Iohns" (003-075) — captures the play's central indeterminacy, with Arthur as the pivot point of contested legitimacy.

### Capture and Imprisonment

Arthur's fate is sealed in Act 3, Scene 3. After the [[war|battle]] at [[angiers|Angiers]], [[king-john|John]] places Arthur in [[hubert|Hubert]]'s custody with the command "[[hubert|Hubert]], keepe this boy" (006-061). The capture is marked by [[constance|Constance]]'s grief — "Arthur tane prisoner?" (006-162) — which erupts into the play's most celebrated expression of maternal despair.

[[king-john|John]]'s subsequent private conversation with [[hubert|Hubert]] is the play's most sinister moment of political euphemism. The king's whispered order — "I had a thing to say, but let it goe" (006-105) — communicates the command for Arthur's death without ever speaking it. The scene reveals [[king-john|John]]'s moral cowardice: he cannot name the act he requires, yet he ensures his servant understands it perfectly.

### Key Relationships

Arthur's dramatic significance emerges through his relationships with the adults who control his fate:

- **[[constance|Constance]]**: His mother is his fiercest advocate, whose grief transforms from political rhetoric into the play's most powerful language of loss. Her lament — "Must I behold my pretty Arthur more" (006-247) — marks the transition from political argument to human tragedy.

- **[[king-john|John]]**: His uncle and captor. [[king-john|John]]'s relationship with Arthur is defined by the paradox of political necessity: Arthur must die for [[king-john|John]] to stand, yet the act of killing him destroys the legitimacy it was meant to secure.

- **[[hubert|Hubert]]**: The most important relationship in the play's second half. [[hubert|Hubert]] is the instrument of [[king-john|John]]'s will, but Arthur's innocence transforms him from executioner to protector.

- **[[queen-elinor|Queen Elinor]]**: Arthur's grandmother, who supports [[king-john|John]]'s claim over her grandson's, embodying the play's inversion of natural family bonds.

## Key Speeches or Scenes

### The Plea to Hubert (Act 4, Scene 1)

The scene in which Arthur pleads with [[hubert|Hubert]] not to blind him is the dramatic and emotional heart of *[[king-john|King John]]*. [[hubert|Hubert]] enters the prison with hot irons and executioners, prepared to carry out [[king-john|John]]'s order (007-080-089). Arthur, initially unaware of the threat, speaks with gentle affection:

> Are you sicke Hubert? you looke pale to day,
> Insooth I would you were a little sicke,
> That I might sit all night, and watch with you
> (007-111-113)

When [[hubert|Hubert]] shows him the warrant, Arthur's response unfolds as one of Shakespeare's most remarkable displays of childlike rhetoric. His argument operates on multiple registers simultaneously: personal affection, physical vulnerability, Christian [[grace|mercy]], and sheer eloquence. He reminds [[hubert|Hubert]] of the care he gave during [[hubert|Hubert]]'s own illness — "I knit my hand-kercher about your browes" (007-128) — and asks whether [[hubert|Hubert]] can now burn out the eyes that never even frowned upon him (007-143-144).

The speech that defeats [[hubert|Hubert]]'s resolve is Arthur's argument that even the iron itself would refuse the deed:

> The Iron of it selfe, though heate red hot,
> Approaching neere these eyes, would drinke my teares,
> And quench this fierie indignation,
> Euen in the matter of mine innocence
> (007-148-151)

Arthur personifies the iron as possessing more [[grace|mercy]] than the man who wields it — a rhetorical inversion of extraordinary sophistication for a child speaker. When [[hubert|Hubert]] resists, Arthur deploys his final weapon: the threat of shame. If [[hubert|Hubert]] breathes life into the dead coal, "you will but make it blush, / And glow with shame of your proceedings, [[hubert|Hubert]]" (007-202-203).

[[hubert|Hubert]] capitulates in one of the play's most consequential lines: "Well, see to liue: I will not touch thine eye, / For all the Treasure that thine Vnckle owes" (007-211-212). The scene achieves what no political argument in the play can — it breaks the machinery of royal command through sheer moral [[power|force]].

### The Escape and Death

[[hubert|Hubert]] spares Arthur but conceals the truth, telling [[king-john|John]] the boy is dead. When the deception is revealed — "Yong Arthur is aliue" (008-235) — the political pressure for Arthur's elimination intensifies. Arthur, now disguised as a ship-boy, attempts to escape from the castle walls:

> The Wall is high, and yet will I leape downe.
> Good ground be pittifull, and hurt me not
> (009-001-002)

His fall is fatal. The discovery of his body by [[salisbury|Salisbury]], [[pembroke|Pembroke]], and [[bigot|Bigot]] produces the play's most searing language of outrage. [[salisbury|Salisbury]] calls it "the bloodiest shame, / The wildest Sauagery, the vildest stroke" (009-051-052). Though Arthur's death is an accident — or, in the play's characteristic ambiguity, perhaps suicide — the lords interpret it as murder, and their defection to the French follows immediately.

## Historical Background

The historical Arthur of [[brittany|Brittany]] (1187-1203) was the posthumous son of Geoffrey II, Duke of [[brittany|Brittany]] ([[king-john|John]]'s elder brother), making him a genuine rival to the English [[crown|throne]]. Arthur was captured by [[king-john|John]]'s [[power|force]]s in 1202 at the siege of Mirebeau and imprisoned. He died in 1203 at the age of sixteen, almost certainly murdered on [[king-john|John]]'s orders, though the exact circumstances remain uncertain.

Shakespeare compresses and reshapes these events dramatically. The historical Arthur was a teenager, not the young child of the play. Shakespeare's decision to present Arthur as a boy of perhaps ten or twelve is a deliberate choice that maximizes the pathos of his situation. The blinding-with-hot-irons plot is a Shakespearean invention with no historical basis — the chronicles mention only that [[king-john|John]] ordered Arthur blinded and castrated, and that [[hubert|Hubert]] refused, but Shakespeare transforms this into the play's most extended scene of moral crisis.

## Character Analysis

Arthur is the play's embodiment of innocence destroyed by political necessity. He is, in structural terms, a sacrificial figure: his death is the price the world of *[[king-john|King John]]* demands for its political settlements. Unlike the Machiavellian adults who surround him, Arthur possesses no political agency. He never plots, never schemes, never makes a strategic choice. His single act of will — the leap from the castle wall — is an act of desperation, not calculation.

Yet Arthur is far from passive in rhetorical terms. His plea to [[hubert|Hubert]] is the play's longest uninterrupted argument and its most persuasive speech. The paradox of Arthur is that he is simultaneously the play's most powerless character and the owner of its most powerful language. His words accomplish what armies cannot: they change a man's heart.

Critics have noted the anticipation of later Shakespearean innocents in Arthur — particularly the young princes in *Richard III* and Mamillius in *The Winter's Tale*. The plea scene's combination of tenderness, terror, and moral awakening prefigures the blinding of Gloucester in *King Lear*, where the language of eyes and seeing returns with tragic [[power|force]].

## Thematic Significance

Arthur's role connects the play's major thematic concerns:

- **[[legitimacy|Legitimacy]]**: Arthur possesses hereditary right without political power; [[king-john|John]] possesses power without right. The play refuses to resolve which matters more.
- **[[mercy|Mercy]] and conscience**: Arthur's plea to [[hubert|Hubert]] is the play's most sustained exploration of individual conscience against political command.
- **Innocence and sacrifice**: Arthur's death — whether accident, suicide, or murder — is the moral wound from which [[king-john|John]]'s reign never recovers.
- **Language and power**: Arthur, the most powerless character, deploys the play's most effective rhetoric, suggesting that eloquence can triumph where armies fail.
