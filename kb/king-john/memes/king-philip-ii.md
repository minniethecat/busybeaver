# King Philip II

## Character Overview

King Philip II of [[france|France]] is the principal antagonist to [[king-john|King John]] and the play's most politically fluid monarch. He enters the drama as the champion of [[arthur|Arthur]]'s claim to the English throne, waging war on behalf of "thy elder brother borne" (002-235). He then abandons that claim in exchange for territorial concessions and the [[marriage-of-lewis-and-blanche|marriage of his son Lewis to Blanche of Castile]]. When [[pandulph|Cardinal Pandulph]] arrives with the papal [[curse]], Philip reverses himself again — breaking his freshly sworn oath to John and resuming hostilities. His political trajectory embodies the play's cynicism about the relationship between principle and self-interest.

## Role in the Play

> Philip of France, in right and true behalfe
> Of thy deceased brother, Geffreyes sonne,
> Arthur Plantaginet, laies most lawfull claime
> To this faire Iland, and the Territories.
>
> (001-083--086)

### Champion of Arthur

Philip's opening position is presented as a moral crusade. Through his ambassador [[chatillon|Chatillon]], he demands that John surrender the crown to Arthur: "Philip of France, in right and true behalfe / Of thy deceased brother, Geffreyes sonne" (001-083). He presents himself as the instrument of divine justice: "Fro[m] that supernal Iudge that stirs good thoughts / In any breast of strong authoritie, / To looke into the blots and staines of right" (002-243–245). This self-presentation as the guardian of legitimacy is the moral platform from which all his subsequent actions will fall.

### The Peace of Angiers

When the [[siege-of-angiers|siege of Angiers]] stalls, Philip proves himself a pragmatist. The [[citizen-of-angiers|citizen]]'s proposal — marriage between [[lewis-the-dauphin|Lewis]] and [[blanche-of-castile|Blanche]] — offers Philip a face-saving exit from an unwinnable siege. He accepts, abandoning [[constance|Constance]] and [[arthur|Arthur]] in exchange for territorial gain: "Brother of England, how may we content / This widdow Lady? In her right we came, / Which we God knowes, haue turn'd another way, / To our owne vantage" (004-158–160). The concession that the war was turned "To our owne vantage" is Philip's most honest moment — an admission that principle was always subordinate to interest.

### Breaking the Oath

The arrival of [[pandulph|Pandulph]] presents Philip with his most dramatic reversal. Having just sworn "deepe-sworne faith, peace, amity, true loue" (005-199) with John, Philip is ordered by the papal legate to break his oath on pain of excommunication:

> Philip of France, on perill of a curse,
> Let goe the hand of that Arch-heretique,
> And raise the power of France vpon his head,
> Vnlesse he doe submit himselfe to Rome.
>
> (005-156--159)

Philip's anguished response — "I am perplext, and know not what to say" (005-189) — is the closest the play comes to genuine moral conflict. His elaborate speech pleading with Pandulph to find "Some gentle order" (005-219) reveals a man caught between incompatible obligations: faith to the Church and faith to a fellow king.

Yet the outcome is never in doubt. Faced with the threat of being "excommunicate, and curst" (005-191), Philip chooses Rome over John: "England, I will fall fro[m] thee" (006-025). The speed of his reversal — from sworn ally to enemy in a single scene — is the play's most brutal demonstration that oaths, in the world of *King John*, are only as binding as the power that enforces them.

### The Revolt of the Lords and Aftermath

Philip's final contribution is military. He leads the French forces in renewed war, and though his appearances in the later acts are less frequent than [[lewis-the-dauphin|Lewis]]'s, his political authority underpins the French campaign. The French withdrawal at the play's end — with Lewis putting "his cause and quarrell / To the disposing of the Cardinall" (011-182–183) — suggests that the French adventure has run its course, leaving Philip with no permanent gains and a trail of broken oaths.

## Thematic Significance

King Philip II is the play's embodiment of political fluidity. He champions Arthur's right, then sells it. He swears eternal peace with John, then breaks it. He invokes heaven as his authority, then defers to Rome. In each case, the pivot is dictated not by principle but by "commodity" — the self-interest that the Bastard identifies as the true ruler of human affairs. Philip is not a villain in the conventional sense; he is something more unsettling: a competent monarch who treats oaths as instruments rather than obligations, a figure who reveals that "The word is not their bond" when power shifts.

## Key Relationships

- **[[king-john|King John]]**: Philip's rival, ally, and rival again — their relationship tracks the play's shifting political landscape
- **[[lewis-the-dauphin|Lewis the Dauphin]]**: Philip's son and heir, who shares his father's ambition but exceeds him in ruthlessness
- **[[pandulph|Cardinal Pandulph]]**: The papal legate whose authority Philip cannot defy, even when it means breaking his oath
- **[[constance|Constance]]**: The widow whose cause Philip champions and then abandons — her curses pursue him through the play
- **[[arthur|Arthur]]**: The young claimant whose rights Philip invokes as a pretext for war, and whose cause he discards for territory
