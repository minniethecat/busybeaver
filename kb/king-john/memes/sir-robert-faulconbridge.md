# Sir Robert Faulconbridge

Sir Robert Faulconbridge is a deceased English knight whose disputed inheritance
sets the opening action of Shakespeare's *[[king-john|King John]]* in motion. Though he never
appears on stage, his legacy — embodied in his legitimate son Robert and his
illegitimate son [[philip-the-bastard]] (Philip) — drives the play's first major
[[war|conflict]] and establishes its thematic preoccupation with [[legitimacy]] and
[[inheritance]].

## Family and Estate

Sir Robert was married to [[lady-faulconbridge]] (002-065) and left behind an estate that
becomes the subject of a legal dispute between his two sons. The elder (and
legitimate) son, also named Robert, claims the inheritance by right of
primogeniture. The younger, Philip — later known as [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]] — was
acknowledged by Sir Robert as his heir in his will, despite widespread suspicion
that Philip was actually fathered by [[king-richard-i]]:

> Your father, on his death-bed, gaue me all his lands,
> And said, I was the heire and onely sonne
> Vnto sir Robert Faulconbridge. (001-133)

## The Inheritance Dispute

The dispute over Sir Robert's lands occupies the opening scene of the play.
Before [[king-john]]'s [[crown|throne]], the two brothers present their competing claims (001-128).
Robert the legitimate son argues for his legal right, while Philip ([[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]])
initially defends his position based on Sir Robert's will. The legal question of
inheritance quickly becomes entangled with the question of Philip's true
parentage, as [[queen-elinor]] observes Philip's physical resemblance to her
late son, [[king-richard-i]] ([[king-richard-i|Richard the Lionheart]]) (001-213).

The dispute over Sir Robert's estate functions as more than a legal prologue.
It introduces the play's central concern with the tension between legal
legitimacy and natural merit — a theme that resonates throughout the subsequent
struggle for the English crown.

## Dramatic Function

As a dramatic device, Sir Robert serves three key purposes:

| Function | Description |
|----------|------------|
| Catalyst | His death triggers the inheritance dispute that opens Act I |
| Thematic anchor | His estate embodies the play's concern with legitimacy versus worth |
| Character revelation | The circumstances of his marriage reveal [[lady-faulconbridge|Lady Faulconbridge]]'s character and Philip's origins |

Sir Robert's absence from the stage is itself meaningful. Like [[king-richard-i]]
(also deceased before the play begins), he exerts influence through memory,
legal instruments (his will), and the social relations he left behind. The play
thus establishes a world in which the dead continue to shape the actions of the
living.

## Name and Identity

The name "Faulconbridge" carries symbolic weight. [[philip-the-bastard|Philip the Bastard]]'s choice
to abandon his Faulconbridge identity and adopt the Plantagenet name of his
biological father represents his elevation from mere gentry to royal blood. Sir
Robert's final question to his son, relayed through other characters, haunts
this transformation:

> Hast thou denied thy selfe a Faulconbridge? (002-097)

The question probes whether Philip's renunciation of the Faulconbridge name
constitutes a denial of the man who raised him — a tension between biological
and social fatherhood that the play never fully resolves.
