# Lewis the Dauphin

## Character Overview

Son of Philip II of [[france|France]], who leads the French invasion of [[england|England]] and whose political ambition drives the play's final act. Lewis first appears alongside his father before the walls of Angiers (002-125), where his opening words — "Before Angiers well met braue Austria" (002-128) — establish him as a courteous prince in the French chivalric tradition. But the play gradually reveals a darker political intelligence beneath this polished surface.

Lewis's most significant early action is his marriage to [[blanche-of-castile|Blanche of Castile]], [[king-john|John]]'s niece — a diplomatic union engineered by the [[citizen-of-angiers|Citizen of Angiers]] to unite the warring crowns. Lewis accepts the match with courtly grace, declaring Blanche "a noble boy" in a line that conflates political calculation with the language of chivalric admiration (002-145). When [[constance|Constance]] learns that Lewis will marry Blanche and receive the Angevin provinces of [[anjou|Anjou]], [[maine|Maine]], [[touraine|Touraine]], and [[poitiers|Poitiers]], she reacts with fury at the betrayal of her son [[arthur|Arthur]]'s cause:

> Shall Lewis haue Blaunch, and Blaunch those Prouinces?
> It is not so, thou hast mispoke, misheard,
> Be well aduis'd, tell ore thy tale againe.
>
> (004-216)

## Role in the Play

### Political Ambition

Lewis's political arc traces a trajectory from obedient son to independent actor. At the French court, he stands alongside his father [[king-philip-ii|Philip II]] and [[blanche-of-castile|Blanche]] (005-035), participating in the diplomatic theatre that surrounds [[king-john|John]]'s excommunication. But after [[pandulph|Pandulph]] absolves Philip of his oath to John, Lewis emerges as the driving force behind the French war effort. His decision to invade [[england|England]] in Act 5 transforms what was a continental dynastic dispute into an existential threat to the English crown.

### The Secret Plan

Lewis's most revealing moment comes not in public speech but in private council. Having secured the allegiance of the rebel English lords — [[salisbury|Salisbury]], [[pembroke|Pembroke]], and [[bigot|Bigot]] — he confides a secret plan to execute them after victory, as [[melun|Melun]] reveals on his deathbed. This revelation exposes the gap between Lewis's courtly manner and his ruthless political calculation: he values the lords only as instruments, to be discarded once they have served their purpose. The plan's exposure drives the lords back to [[king-john|John]]'s camp and ultimately to his heir [[prince-henry|Prince Henry]].

### Defeat and Withdrawal

When the French reinforcement fleet is [[wreck-of-the-french-fleet|wrecked on Goodwin Sands]] and the rebel lords defect, Lewis's invasion collapses. He enters the final scenes alongside [[pandulph|Pandulph]] and the French forces (006-155), but the military momentum has shifted decisively. The Dauphin who began the play as a courteous prince ends it as a defeated claimant, his ambitions wrecked by the same combination of chance and treachery that he himself had hoped to exploit.
