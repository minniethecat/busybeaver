# The Papacy

**The Papacy**, under Pope Innocent III, is the most powerful supranational institution in Shakespeare's *[[king-john|King John]]*. Represented by the legate [[pandulph|Pandulph]], the Papacy intervenes directly in the Anglo-French [[war|conflict]], dissolving the [[the-league-of-angiers|League of Angiers]], pronouncing a [[curse]] upon [[king-john|King John]], and authorizing [[king-philip-ii|King Philip II]] to resume war against an excommunicated monarch.

## Role in the Play

The Papacy enters the play abruptly at the moment of diplomatic resolution. The [[the-league-of-angiers|League of Angiers]] has been concluded, the [[marriage-of-lewis-and-blanche|marriage of Lewis and Blanche]] celebrated — and [[pandulph|Pandulph]] arrives to dismantle it all. His demand is simple: Philip must break his oath to [[king-john|John]] and resume war, or face excommunication himself.

The Papacy's power is demonstrated through its effects: a peace treaty dissolves, a king is abandoned by his ally, and war resumes — all because one man speaks with the Pope's [[power|authority]].

> I Pandulph, of faire Millane Cardinall,
> And from Pope Innocent the Legate heere,
> Doe in his name religiously demand
> Why thou against the Church, our holy Mother,
> So wilfully dost spurne
>
> (005-099)

## Pandulph as Instrument

[[pandulph|Pandulph]] embodies the Papacy's institutional power. His language is that of uncompromising [[power|authority]]:

- **"I will denounce a curse vpon his head"** (006-024) — the formal pronouncement of anathema
- **"Philip of [[france|France]], on perill of a curse"** (005-156) — the ultimatum that [[power|force]]s Philip's choice
- **"There's Law and [[war|War]]rant (Lady) for my curse"** (005-149) — the assertion of institutional legitimacy

[[pandulph|Pandulph]]'s success reveals the Papacy's unique position: it is the only [[power|authority]] in the play that can compel obedience from kings. Neither [[king-john|John]] nor [[king-philip-ii|Philip]] can resist its claims without risking damnation.

## Ecclesiastical vs. Secular Power

The Papacy represents the play's most sustained exploration of the tension between ecclesiastical and secular [[power|authority]]:

| Dimension | Ecclesiastical (Papacy) | Secular ([[crown|Crown]]s) |
|-----------|------------------------|-------------------|
| **Source of Authority** | Divine appointment through apostolic succession | Hereditary right and military power |
| **Sanction** | Excommunication and anathema | [[war|War]] and execution |
| **Scope** | Universal (all Christendom) | Territorial (national borders) |
| **Ultimate Weapon** | The [[curse]] | The sword |

The play's treatment of this tension is notably ambivalent. [[pandulph|Pandulph]] is not portrayed as villainous — he is simply effective. The Papacy's power is presented as a political [[truth|fact]], as real and consequential as any army.

## Historical Context

Pope Innocent III (r. 1198–1216) was a dominant figure of the medieval papacy. His dispute with [[king-john|John]] over the appointment of Stephen Langton as Archbishop of [[canterbury|Canterbury]] led to [[england|England]] being placed under interdict (1208) and [[king-john|John]]'s excommunication (1209). [[king-john|John]] ultimately submitted to the Pope in 1213, making [[england|England]] a papal fief. Shakespeare's play dramatizes this [[war|conflict]] but compresses the chronology significantly.
