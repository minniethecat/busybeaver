# The Plot Against Arthur

**The Plot Against Arthur** is the central crisis of Act 4 in Shakespeare's *[[king-john|King John]]*. Having secured his [[crown|throne]] through diplomatic maneuvering and war, [[king-john|King John]] attempts to eliminate the rival claim of his nephew [[arthur|Arthur Plantagenet]] by ordering [[hubert|Hubert]] to blind the boy with hot irons. [[hubert|Hubert]]'s refusal — and [[arthur|Arthur]]'s subsequent death attempting escape — triggers the [[revolt-of-the-english-lords|revolt of the English lords]] and plunges [[king-john|John]]'s reign into crisis.

## John's Order

The plot originates in [[king-john|John]]'s secret command to [[hubert|Hubert]], his [[faith|trust]]ed servant. [[king-john|John]]'s language is indirect but unmistakable:

> Death
> [...]
> I had a thing to say, but let it goe
> (006-105)

[[hubert|Hubert]] understands the implication without it being spoken aloud, and [[king-john|John]]'s subsequent "warrant" makes the order explicit: [[arthur|Arthur]] is to be blinded with hot irons, a punishment traditionally reserved for traitors and rebels.

## The Blinding Scene

The play's most intense scene occurs in [[arthur|Arthur]]'s prison, where [[hubert|Hubert]] prepares to carry out [[king-john|John]]'s order. [[arthur|Arthur]]'s pleading — using the language of [[grace]], [[mercy]], and the physical vulnerability of his eyes — dissuades [[hubert|Hubert]]:

> The iron of it selfe, though heate red hot,
> Approaching neere these eyes, would drinke them vp
> (007-148)

[[arthur|Arthur]]'s rhetorical power, combined with [[hubert|Hubert]]'s innate decency, produces the play's most significant act of [[grace|mercy]]. [[hubert|Hubert]] chooses conscience over obedience, declaring: "I will not touch thine eye" (007-211), sparing [[arthur|Arthur]] but lying to [[king-john|John]] about the deed.

## The Escape and Death

[[hubert|Hubert]]'s deception cannot hold. When [[king-john|John]]'s court learns that [[arthur|Arthur]] lives, the political pressure for his elimination intensifies. [[hubert|Hubert]]'s report that [[arthur|Arthur]] is dead — intended to protect the boy — paradoxically places [[arthur|Arthur]] in greater danger.

[[arthur|Arthur]]'s attempt to escape by leaping from the castle walls results in his death:

> The wall is high, and yet will I leape downe
> (009-001)

The ambiguity surrounding [[arthur|Arthur]]'s death — accident, suicide, or murder — becomes a central point of contention that drives [[salisbury|Salisbury]], [[pembroke|Pembroke]], and [[bigot|Bigot]] to defect.

## Conscience and Mercy

The plot against [[arthur|Arthur]] is the play's most sustained exploration of individual conscience against political command. [[hubert|Hubert]]'s choice — to defy a king's order in favor of moral instinct — makes him the ethical center of the play's second half. His line "I will not touch thy eye" (007-211) is one of the few moments in the play where compassion defeats policy:

> Within the scorched veines of one new burn'd
> (005-246)

[[hubert|Hubert]]'s [[grace|mercy]] contrasts sharply with [[pandulph|Pandulph]]'s political manipulation and [[king-philip-ii|King Philip]]'s oath-breaking, suggesting that moral agency resides not in sovereigns but in their servants.

## Thematic Resonances

The blinding plot engages with several of the play's key thematic concerns:

- **[[power]] and its abuse**: [[king-john|John]]'s willingness to murder a child to secure his [[crown|throne]]
- **[[mercy]]**: [[hubert|Hubert]]'s refusal to carry out the blinding
- **[[sin]]**: The moral stain that follows [[king-john|John]] throughout the remainder of the play
- **Eyes and sight**: The play's recurring imagery of eyes, blindness, and moral perception — "And told me Hubert should put out mine eyes" (007-156)
