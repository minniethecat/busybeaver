## Overview

The Rebel Lords are the [[truth|fact]]ion of English nobles who break with [[king-john|King John]] after the death of [[arthur|Arthur]] and throw their support behind [[lewis-the-dauphin|Lewis the Dauphin]]'s [[french-invasion-of-england|invasion of England]]. Led by [[salisbury|Salisbury]], [[pembroke|Pembroke]], and [[bigot|Bigot]] (009-012), they represent the collapse of [[king-john|John]]'s domestic political support and the play's most sustained exploration of the tensions between [[faith|loyalty]] to a king and [[faith|loyalty]] to conscience.

## Formation and Motivation

The rebel lords' defection is motivated by [[arthur|Arthur]]'s death. They hold [[king-john|John]] responsible and see the killing of a royal nephew — and a rival claimant — as an act that legitimizes their own rebellion. [[salisbury|Salisbury]] speaks for the group when he declares they can no longer serve a king who has committed such a crime.

> Your Nobles will not heare you, but are gone
> (009-210)

The Bastard's report captures the completeness of [[king-john|John]]'s political isolation: the nobles who should be his strongest supporters have become his enemies.

## Alliance with France

The rebel lords make the morally complicated decision to ally with the French invader:

> Enter (in Armes) Dolphin, Salisbury, Meloone, Pembroke, Bigot
> (009-260)

This alliance places them in the awkward position of fighting against [[england|England]] alongside a foreign prince. Their moral discomfort with this position is a recurring note, and it is French [[betrayal|treachery]] — Lewis's plan to execute the English lords after victory, revealed by the [[death|dying]] [[melun|Melun]] (010-212) — that ultimately drives them back to [[king-john|John]]'s side.

## Return to Allegiance

After [[prince-henry|Prince Henry]] brokers a [[peace|reconciliation]], the rebel lords return to their English [[faith|allegiance]] (011-015). Their arc — [[faith|loyalty]], rebellion, guilt, [[peace|reconciliation]] — constitutes one of the play's most developed political narratives, dramatizing the costs and complexities of principled opposition to a legitimate but criminal king.

| Phase | Action | Moral Position |
|-------|--------|----------------|
| **Loyalty** | Serve [[king-john|King John]] | Traditional fealty |
| **Revolt** | Join French invasion after [[arthur|Arthur]]'s death | Principled opposition, but compromised by alliance with foreign power |
| **Return** | Reconcile with [[death|dying]] [[king-john|John]] and [[prince-henry|Prince Henry]] | Restoration of legitimate order |
