# Arthur's Death

## Event Overview

[[arthur|Arthur]] dies attempting to escape imprisonment, an event that catalyzes the English lords' defection to the French.

## Role in the Play

### Narrative Function

Arthur's Death serves as a crucial turning point in the play's action. The event reshapes the political landscape and [[power|force]]s characters to make choices that define the remainder of the dramatic arc.

### Key Participants

The principal figures involved in Arthur's Death represent the intersecting political [[power|force]]s at work in the play. Their actions and reactions to this event reveal character and advance the play's thematic concerns.

### Consequences

The aftermath of Arthur's Death reverberates through subsequent acts, affecting alliances, loyalties, and the fates of all major characters. The event is both a culmination of previous tensions and the catalyst for what follows.

## Arthur's Leap

Arthur's death is one of Shakespeare's most poignant scenes. The boy prince,
disguised as a ship-boy, leaps from the castle wall in a desperate attempt to
escape his imprisonment, speaking his final words with a childlike trust in the
world's mercy — a trust the play immediately betrays.

> The Wall is high, and yet will I leape downe.
> Good ground be pittifull, and hurt me not:
> Heauen take my soule, and England keep my bones.
>
> (009-001)

## Discovery of the Body

The discovery of Arthur's body by [[salisbury|Salisbury]], [[pembroke|Pembroke]],
and [[bigot|Bigot]] transforms the political landscape of the play. Salisbury's
horror at the sight — ''the very top, the heighth, the Crest of murthers Armes''
(009-050) — drives the English lords to [[rebellion|rebel]] against [[king-john|John]] and
join [[lewis-the-dauphin|Lewis the Dauphin]].

> Oh death, made proud with pure & princely beuty,
> The earth had not a hole to hide this deede.
> Murther, as hating what himselfe hath done,
> Doth lay it open to vrge on reuenge.
>
> (009-039)
