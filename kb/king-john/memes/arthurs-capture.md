# Arthur's Capture

## Event Overview

[[king-john|King John]] orders [[arthur|Arthur]] imprisoned, setting in motion the chain of events that leads to the boy's death and the lords' rebellion. After the battle at Angiers, John entrusts Arthur to [[hubert|Hubert]]'s custody — a decision that proves fateful for all parties.

> Fra. What can goe well, when we haue runne so ill?
> Are we not beaten? Is not Angiers lost?
> Arthur tane prisoner? diuers deere friends slaine?
> And bloudy England into England gone,
> Ore-bearing interruption spight of France?
>
> (006-160–164)

## Role in the Play

### Narrative Function

Arthur's Capture serves as a crucial turning point in the play's action. The event reshapes the political landscape and [[power|force]]s characters to make choices that define the remainder of the dramatic arc. John's decision to imprison his rival — conveyed in the terse command to Hubert, "Hubert, keepe this boy" (006-061) — initiates the chain of events that culminates in Arthur's death and the lords' defection. The grief over Arthur's imprisonment echoes through the French camp, where the question is asked: "Are not you grieu'd that Arthur is his prisoner?" (007-017).

### Key Participants

The principal figures involved in Arthur's Capture represent the intersecting political [[power|force]]s at work in the play. [[king-john|King John]] orders the imprisonment; [[hubert|Hubert]] is charged with keeping the boy; [[arthur|Arthur]] himself is the captive prince; and [[constance|Constance]], Arthur's mother, responds with grief and fury at the loss of her son.

### Consequences

The aftermath of Arthur's Capture reverberates through subsequent acts, affecting alliances, loyalties, and the fates of all major characters. The event is both a culmination of previous tensions — the disputed succession, the war with France — and the catalyst for what follows: John's secret order for Arthur's death, Hubert's crisis of conscience, and the rebellion of the English lords.
