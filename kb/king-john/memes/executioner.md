## Overview

The Executioner appears at the opening of Act 4, Scene 1, entering with
[[hubert|Hubert]] who commands that irons be heated for the blinding of
[[arthur|Arthur]]. The figure represents the imminent threat of [[blood|violence]]
against the young prince, though the blinding is never actually carried
out — [[hubert|Hubert]]'s conscience intervenes before the executioners
are called upon to perform the deed.

> Enter Hubert and Executioners.
>
> Hub. Heate me these Irons hot, and looke thou stand
> Within the Arras: when I strike my foot
> Vpon the bosome of the ground, rush forth
> And binde the boy, which you shall finde with me
> Fast to the chaire: be heedfull: hence, and watch
>
> Exec. I hope your warrant will beare out the deed
>
> Hub. Vncleanly scruples feare not you: looke too't.
>
> (007-080–087)

## Dramatic Entry

The stage direction is stark: "Enter [[hubert|Hubert]] and Executioners" (007-080).
[[hubert|Hubert]]'s first words are chillingly practical: "Heate me these
Irons hot, and looke thou stand / Within the Arras" (007-081–082). The
Executioner is to be hidden behind the arras (tapestry), poised to emerge
at [[hubert|Hubert]]'s signal: "when I strike my foot / Vpon the bosome of the
ground, rush forth / And binde the boy, which you shall finde with me /
Fast to the chaire" (007-082–085).

## A Brief Voice of Caution

The Executioner speaks exactly one line in the play: "I hope your warrant
will beare out the deed" (007-086). This single sentence is significant —
it reveals that even the unnamed agent of [[blood|violence]] requires assurance that
the act has legitimate authorization. [[hubert|Hubert]] dismisses this
concern with "Vncleanly scruples feare not you: looke too't" (007-087),
but the Executioner's question anticipates [[hubert|Hubert]]'s own eventual crisis
of conscience.

## Dramatic Function

The Executioner's function is atmospheric and structural rather than
narrative. The heated irons and the concealed attendant establish the grim
stakes of the scene before [[hubert|Hubert]]'s change of heart. When
[[arthur|Arthur]] pleads for [[grace|mercy]] — "Alas, what neede you be so boistrous rough? /
I will not struggle, I will stand stone still" (007-162–163) — the silent
presence of the executioners sharpens the tension: the apparatus of
[[blood|violence]] is literally waiting in the wings.

## Hubert's Conscience

The turning point occurs when [[hubert|Hubert]], moved by [[arthur|Arthur]]'s
eloquence and innocence, resolves to spare the boy. Crucially, he dismisses
the Executioners before the blinding can occur: "Come forth: Do as I bid
you do" (007-158) — a command that is immediately followed by [[arthur|Arthur]]'s
cry "O saue me [[hubert|Hubert]], saue me: my eyes are out / Euen with the fierce
lookes of these bloody men" (007-159–160). [[hubert|Hubert]] seizes the iron himself
— "Giue me the Iron I say, and binde him heere" (007-161) — but cannot
bring himself to use it. The Executioners, dismissed from the stage, never
return.

## Thematic Significance

The Executioner represents the machinery of state [[blood|violence]] at its most
depersonalized. He has no name, no motivation, no independent will — he
exists only as an instrument of [[crown|royal authority]], asking only that the
"warrant" be sufficient to protect him from the consequences of his
actions. His single line and swift exit embody the play's recurring
concern with how legitimate [[power|authority]] can be perverted into instruments
of cruelty, and how the conscience of an individual — here, [[hubert|Hubert]]
— must ultimately bear the weight that institutions evade.
