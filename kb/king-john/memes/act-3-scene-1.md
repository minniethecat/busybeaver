## Section 5

[005-001] Con. If thou that bidst me be content, wert grim

[005-002] Vgly, and slandrous to thy Mothers wombe,

[005-003] Full of vnpleasing blots, and sightlesse staines,

[005-004] Lame, foolish, crooked, swart, prodigious,

[005-005] Patch'd with foule Moles, and eye-offending markes,

[005-006] I would not care, I then would be content,

[005-007] For then I should not loue thee: no, nor thou

[005-008] Become thy great birth, nor deserue a Crowne.

[005-009] But thou art faire, and at thy birth (deere boy)

[005-010] Nature and [[fortune|Fortune]] ioyn'd to make thee great.

[005-011] Of Natures guifts, thou mayst with Lillies boast,

[005-012] And with the halfe-blowne Rose. But Fortune, oh,

[005-013] She is corrupted, chang'd, and wonne from thee,

[005-014] Sh' adulterates hourely with thine Vnckle Iohn,

[005-015] And with her golden hand hath pluckt on [[france|France]]

[005-016] To tread downe faire respect of Soueraigntie,

[005-017] And made his Maiestie the bawd to theirs.

[005-018] France is a Bawd to Fortune, and king Iohn,

[005-019] That strumpet Fortune, that vsurping Iohn:

[005-020] Tell me thou fellow, is not France forsworne?

[005-021] Envenom him with words, or get thee gone,

[005-022] And leaue those woes alone, which I alone

[005-023] Am bound to vnder-beare

[005-024] Sal. Pardon me Madam,

[005-025] I may not goe without you to the kings

[005-026] Con. Thou maist, thou shalt, I will not go with thee,

[005-027] I will instruct my sorrowes to bee proud,

[005-028] For greefe is proud, and makes his owner stoope,

[005-029] To me and to the state of my great greefe,

[005-030] Lets kings assemble: for my greefe's so great,

[005-031] That no supporter but the huge firme earth

[005-032] Can hold it vp: here I and sorrowes sit,

[005-033] Heere is my Throne bid kings come bow to it.

[005-034] Actus Tertius, Scaena prima.

[005-035] Enter King Iohn, France, Dolphin, Blanch, Elianor, Philip, [[austria|Austria]],

[005-036] [[constance|Constance]].

[005-037] Fran. 'Tis true (faire daughter) and this blessed day,

[005-038] Euer in France shall be kept festiuall:

[005-039] To solemnize this day the glorious sunne

[005-040] Stayes in his course, and playes the Alchymist,

[005-041] Turning with splendor of his precious eye

[005-042] The meager cloddy earth to glittering gold:

[005-043] The yearely course that brings this day about,

[005-044] Shall neuer see it, but a holy day

[005-045] Const. A wicked day, and not a holy day.

[005-046] What hath this day deseru'd? what hath it done,

[005-047] That it in golden letters should be set

[005-048] Among the high tides in the Kalender?

[005-049] Nay, rather turne this day out of the weeke,

[005-050] This day of shame, oppression, periury.

[005-051] Or if it must stand still, let wiues with childe

[005-052] Pray that their burthens may not fall this day,

[005-053] Lest that their hopes prodigiously be crost:

[005-054] But (on this day) let Sea-men feare no wracke,

[005-055] No bargaines breake that are not this day made;

[005-056] This day all things begun, come to ill end,

[005-057] Yea, faith it selfe to hollow falshood change

[005-058] Fra. By heauen Lady, you shall haue no cause

[005-059] To curse the faire proceedings of this day:

[005-060] Haue I not pawn'd to you my Maiesty?

[005-061] Const. You haue beguil'd me with a counterfeit

[005-062] Resembling Maiesty, which being touch'd and tride,

[005-063] Proues valuelesse: you are forsworne, forsworne,

[005-064] You came in Armes to spill mine enemies bloud,

[005-065] But now in Armes, you strengthen it with yours.

[005-066] The grapling vigor, and rough frowne of Warre

[005-067] Is cold in amitie, and painted peace,

[005-068] And our oppression hath made vp this league:

[005-069] Arme, arme, you heauens, against these periur'd Kings,

[005-070] A widdow cries, be husband to me (heauens)

[005-071] Let not the howres of this vngodly day

[005-072] Weare out the daies in [[peace|Peace]]; but ere Sun-set,

[005-073] Set armed discord 'twixt these periur'd Kings,

[005-074] Heare me, Oh, heare me

[005-075] Aust. Lady Constance, peace

[005-076] Const. [[war|War]], war, no peace, peace is to me a warre:

[005-077] O Lymoges, O Austria, thou dost shame

[005-078] That bloudy spoyle: thou slaue, thou wretch, y coward,

[005-079] Thou little valiant, great in villanie,

[005-080] Thou euer strong vpon the stronger side;

[005-081] Thou Fortunes Champion, that do'st neuer fight

[005-082] But when her humourous Ladiship is by

[005-083] To teach thee safety: thou art periur'd too,

[005-084] And sooth'st vp greatnesse. What a foole art thou,

[005-085] A ramping foole, to brag, and stamp, and sweare,

[005-086] Vpon my partie: thou cold blooded slaue,

[005-087] Hast thou not spoke like thunder on my side?

[005-088] Beene sworne my Souldier, bidding me depend

[005-089] Vpon thy starres, thy fortune, and thy strength,

[005-090] And dost thou now fall ouer to my foes?

[005-091] Thou weare a Lyons hide, doff it for shame,

[005-092] And hang a Calues skin on those recreant limbes

[005-093] Aus. O that a man should speake those words to me

[005-094] Phil. And hang a Calues-skin on those recreant limbs

[005-095] Aus. Thou dar'st not say so villaine for thy life

[005-096] Phil. And hang a Calues-skin on those recreant limbs

[005-097] Iohn. We like not this, thou dost forget thy selfe.

[005-098] Enter [[pandulph|Pandulph]].

[005-099] Fra. Heere comes the holy Legat of the Pope

[005-100] Pan. Haile you annointed deputies of heauen;

[005-101] To thee King Iohn my holy errand is:

[005-102] I Pandulph, of faire Millane Cardinall,

[005-103] And from Pope Innocent the Legate heere,

[005-104] Doe in his name religiously demand

[005-105] Why thou against the Church, our holy Mother,

[005-106] So wilfully dost spurne; and [[power|force]] perforce

[005-107] Keepe Stephen Langton chosen Archbishop

[005-108] Of [[canterbury|Canterbury]] from that holy Sea:

[005-109] This in our foresaid holy Fathers name

[005-110] Pope Innocent, I doe demand of thee

[005-111] Iohn. What earthie name to Interrogatories

[005-112] Can tast the free breath of a sacred King?

[005-113] Thou canst not (Cardinall) deuise a name

[005-114] So slight, vnworthy, and ridiculous

[005-115] To charge me to an answere, as the Pope:

[005-116] Tell him this tale, and from the mouth of [[england|England]],

[005-117] Adde thus much more, that no Italian Priest

[005-118] Shall tythe or toll in our dominions:

[005-119] But as we, vnder heauen, are supreame head,

[005-120] So vnder him that great supremacy

[005-121] Where we doe reigne, we will alone vphold

[005-122] Without th' assistance of a mortall hand:

[005-123] So tell the Pope, all reuerence set apart

[005-124] To him and his vsurp'd authoritie

[005-125] Fra. Brother of England, you blaspheme in this

[005-126] Iohn. Though you, and all the Kings of Christendom

[005-127] Are led so grossely by this medling Priest,

[005-128] Dreading the curse that money may buy out,

[005-129] And by the merit of vilde gold, drosse, dust,

[005-130] Purchase corrupted pardon of a man,

[005-131] Who in that sale sels pardon from himselfe:

[005-132] Though you, and al the rest so grossely led,

[005-133] This iugling witchcraft with reuennue cherish,

[005-134] Yet I alone, alone doe me oppose

[005-135] Against the Pope, and count his friends my foes

[005-136] Pand. Then by the lawfull power that I haue,

[005-137] Thou shalt stand curst, and excommunicate,

[005-138] And blessed shall he be that doth reuolt

[005-139] From his Allegeance to an heretique,

[005-140] And meritorious shall that hand be call'd,

[005-141] Canonized and worship'd as a Saint,

[005-142] That takes away by any secret course

[005-143] Thy hatefull life

[005-144] Con. O lawfull let it be

[005-145] That I haue roome with [[rome|Rome]] to curse a while,

[005-146] Good Father Cardinall, cry thou Amen

[005-147] To my keene curses; for without my wrong

[005-148] There is no tongue hath power to curse him right

[005-149] Pan. There's Law and Warrant (Lady) for my curse

[005-150] Cons. And for mine too, when Law can do no right.

[005-151] Let it be lawfull, that Law barre no wrong:

[005-152] Law cannot giue my childe his kingdome heere;

[005-153] For he that holds his Kingdome, holds the Law:

[005-154] Therefore since Law it selfe is perfect wrong,

[005-155] How can the Law forbid my tongue to curse?

[005-156] Pand. Philip of France, on perill of a curse,

[005-157] Let goe the hand of that Arch-heretique,

[005-158] And raise the power of France vpon his head,

[005-159] Vnlesse he doe submit himselfe to Rome

[005-160] Elea. Look'st thou pale France? do not let go thy hand

[005-161] Con. Looke to that Deuill, lest that France repent,

[005-162] And by disioyning hands hell lose a soule

[005-163] Aust. King Philip, listen to the Cardinall

[005-164] Bast. And hang a Calues-skin on his recreant limbs

[005-165] Aust. Well ruffian, I must pocket vp these wrongs,

[005-166] Because,

[005-167] Bast. Your breeches best may carry them

[005-168] Iohn. Philip, what saist thou to the Cardinall?

[005-169] Con. What should he say, but as the Cardinall?

[005-170] Dolph. Bethinke you father, for the difference

[005-171] Is purchase of a heauy curse from Rome,

[005-172] Or the light losse of England, for a friend:

[005-173] Forgoe the easier

[005-174] Bla. That's the curse of Rome

[005-175] Con. O Lewis, stand fast, the deuill tempts thee heere

[005-176] In likenesse of a new vntrimmed Bride

[005-177] Bla. The Lady Constance speakes not from her faith,

[005-178] But from her need

[005-179] Con. Oh, if thou grant my need,

[005-180] Which onely liues but by the death of faith,

[005-181] That need, must needs inferre this principle,

[005-182] That faith would liue againe by death of need:

[005-183] O then tread downe my need, and faith mounts vp,

[005-184] Keepe my need vp, and faith is trodden downe

[005-185] Iohn. The king is moud, and answers not to this

[005-186] Con. O be remou'd from him, and answere well

[005-187] Aust. Doe so king Philip, hang no more in doubt

[005-188] Bast. Hang nothing but a Calues skin most sweet lout

[005-189] Fra. I am perplext, and know not what to say

[005-190] Pan. What canst thou say, but wil perplex thee more?

[005-191] If thou stand excommunicate, and curst?

[005-192] Fra. Good reuerend father, make my person yours,

[005-193] And tell me how you would bestow your selfe?

[005-194] This royall hand and mine are newly knit,

[005-195] And the coniunction of our inward soules

[005-196] Married in league, coupled, and link'd together

[005-197] With all religous strength of sacred vowes,

[005-198] The latest breath that gaue the sound of words

[005-199] Was deepe-sworne faith, peace, amity, true loue

[005-200] Betweene our kingdomes and our royall selues,

[005-201] And euen before this truce, but new before,

[005-202] No longer then we well could wash our hands,

[005-203] To clap this royall bargaine vp of peace,

[005-204] Heauen knowes they were besmear'd and ouer-staind

[005-205] With slaughters pencill; where reuenge did paint

[005-206] The fearefull difference of incensed kings:

[005-207] And shall these hands so lately purg'd of bloud?

[005-208] So newly ioyn'd in loue? so strong in both,

[005-209] Vnyoke this seysure, and this kinde regreete?

[005-210] Play fast and loose with faith? so iest with heauen,

[005-211] Make such vnconstant children of our selues

[005-212] As now againe to snatch our palme from palme:

[005-213] Vn-sweare faith sworne, and on the marriage bed

[005-214] Of smiling peace to march a bloody hoast,

[005-215] And make a ryot on the gentle brow

[005-216] Of true sincerity? O holy Sir

[005-217] My reuerend father, let it not be so;

[005-218] Out of your grace, deuise, ordaine, impose

[005-219] Some gentle order, and then we shall be blest

[005-220] To doe your pleasure, and continue friends

[005-221] Pand. All forme is formelesse, Order orderlesse,

[005-222] Saue what is opposite to Englands loue.

[005-223] Therefore to Armes, be Champion of our Church,

[005-224] Or let the Church our mother breathe her curse,

[005-225] A mothers curse, on her reuolting sonne:

[005-226] France, thou maist hold a serpent by the tongue,

[005-227] A cased Lion by the mortall paw,

[005-228] A fasting Tyger safer by the tooth,

[005-229] Then keepe in peace that hand which thou dost hold

[005-230] Fra. I may dis-ioyne my hand, but not my faith

[005-231] Pand. So mak'st thou faith an enemy to faith,

[005-232] And like a ciuill warre setst oath to oath,

[005-233] Thy tongue against thy tongue. O let thy vow

[005-234] First made to heauen, first be to heauen perform'd,

[005-235] That is, to be the Champion of our Church,

[005-236] What since thou sworst, is sworne against thy selfe,

[005-237] And may not be performed by thy selfe,

[005-238] For that which thou hast sworne to doe amisse,

[005-239] Is not amisse when it is truely done:

[005-240] And being not done, where doing tends to ill,

[005-241] The truth is then most done not doing it:

[005-242] The better Act of purposes mistooke,

[005-243] Is to mistake again, though indirect,

[005-244] Yet indirection thereby growes direct,

[005-245] And falshood, falshood cures, as fire cooles fire

[005-246] Within the scorched veines of one new burn'd:

[005-247] It is religion that doth make vowes kept,

[005-248] But thou hast sworne against religion:

[005-249] By what thou swear'st against the thing thou swear'st,

[005-250] And mak'st an oath the suretie for thy truth,

[005-251] Against an oath the truth, thou art vnsure

[005-252] To sweare, sweares onely not to be forsworne,

[005-253] Else what a mockerie should it be to sweare?

[005-254] But thou dost sweare, onely to be forsworne,

[005-255] And most forsworne, to keepe what thou dost sweare,

[005-256] Therefore thy later vowes, against thy first,

[005-257] Is in thy selfe rebellion to thy selfe:

[005-258] And better conquest neuer canst thou make,

[005-259] Then arme thy constant and thy nobler parts

[005-260] Against these giddy loose suggestions:

[005-261] Vpon which better part, our prayrs come in,

[005-262] If thou vouchsafe them. But if not, then know

[005-263] The perill of our curses light on thee

[005-264] So heauy, as thou shalt not shake them off

[005-265] But in despaire, dye vnder their blacke weight
