# Sin

## Definition

Sin in *[[king-john|King John]]* is simultaneously a theological category and a political weapon. The question the play poses is not "who has sinned?" but "who has the [[power|authority]] to declare sin?"

[[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]] gives the play's most provocative statement on sin early in Act 1, when he comforts his mother about the circumstances of his own bastard birth — arguing that some transgressions enjoy a kind of earthly immunity:

> Some sinnes doe beare their priuiledge on earth,
> And so doth yours: your fault, was not your follie,
> Needs must you lay your heart at his dispose,
> Subiected tribute to commanding loue
>
> (002-107)

The Bastard extends this logic moments later, when he leads his mother away and imagines the verdict of his new-found Plantagenet kin. If she had refused Richard the Lionheart, he declares, the refusal itself — not the adultery — would have been the true sin:

> And they shall say, when Richard me begot,
> If thou hadst sayd him nay, it had beene sinne;
> Who sayes it was, he lyes, I say twas not.
>
> (002-121)

## Manifestations in the Play

### Sin as Political Instrument

[[pandulph|Pandulph]]'s excommunication of [[king-john|John]] transforms sin from a spiritual condition into a political act (005-137). By declaring [[king-john|John]] outside the faithful, [[pandulph|Pandulph]] authorizes rebellion and invasion. The play exposes how easily the language of moral transgression can be weaponized by institutional power.

### The Sin of Kings

[[king-john|John]]'s political transgressions — usurpation, the attempted murder of [[arthur|Arthur]], the breaking of oaths — are the play's narrative engine (003-053). Yet the play refuses to treat [[king-john|John]] as simply villainous; his sins are contextualized within a political system that makes them almost inevitable. The Bastard notes that "some sinnes doe beare their priuiledge" (002-107), suggesting that power itself grants immunity from the moral consequences that would destroy lesser men.

### Sin and Conscience

[[hubert|Hubert]]'s crisis over [[arthur|Arthur]] is the play's most direct engagement with sin as a matter of individual conscience (008-051). His decision to spare the boy is a refusal to sin, even at the cost of disobeying his king. The tragedy is that his moral choice cannot prevent the outcome — renunciation of sin cannot halt the machinery of political [[blood|violence]].
