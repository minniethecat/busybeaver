# Rebellion

## Definition

Rebellion in *[[king-john|King John]]* is the political act that the play simultaneously condemns and makes inevitable. The lords' defection to Lewis is treason against their anointed king, yet the play has already established that [[king-john|John]]'s own claim to the [[crown|throne]] is itself a form of rebellion against the rightful heir.

[[austria|Austria]] names the act in its most naked form when the French camp fractures at Angiers. [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]]'s retort — dismissing Austria as nothing more than a "Calues-skin" — exposes rebellion's dependence on the credibility of those who cry it:

> Aust. Rebellion, flat rebellion
>
> Bast. Wil't not be?
> Will not a Calues-skin stop that mouth of thine?
>
> (006-001)

[[pandulph|Cardinal Pandulph]], however, offers the play's most philosophically sophisticated account of rebellion. Pressuring [[king-philip-ii|King Philip]] to break his newly sworn oath, Pandulph argues that keeping a wrongful oath is itself a form of self-betrayal — a rebellion against one's own better nature:

> But thou dost sweare, onely to be forsworne,
> And most forsworne, to keepe what thou dost sweare,
> Therefore thy later vowes, against thy first,
> Is in thy selfe rebellion to thy selfe:
> And better conquest neuer canst thou make,
> Then arme thy constant and thy nobler parts
> Against these giddy loose suggestions:
>
> (005-257)

## Manifestations in the Play

### The Lords' Defection

[[salisbury|Salisbury]], [[pembroke|Pembroke]], and [[bigot|Bigot]] justify their rebellion as a response to [[king-john|John]]'s moral failings — specifically, the suspected murder of [[arthur|Arthur]]. Austria's cry of "Rebellion, flat rebellion" (006-001) names the act even as the lords resist the label. Yet the play exposes their self-justification: they rebel not for justice but for self-preservation, aligning with whichever power seems most likely to prevail.

### Rebellion as Contagion

Once the lords rebel, rebellion becomes self-perpetuating. Lewis, who entered [[england|England]] as the lords' ally, becomes their occupier. The lords discover that rebellion against one [[power|authority]] does not produce freedom — it merely exchanges one master for another. The Bastard's final task is to "vnthred the rude eye of Rebellion" (010-215), restoring order through the assertion of national unity.

### The Bastard and Loyalty

Against the lords' rebellion, [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]]'s [[faith|loyalty]] stands as the play's counter-example. His [[faith|fidelity]] to [[king-john|John]] is not blind — he sees [[king-john|John]]'s flaws clearly — but he understands that rebellion, once begun, is harder to stop than to start. His final speech reasserts the principle of [[faith|loyalty]] not to a person but to the nation.
