## Overview

Pomfret (modern Pontefract) in Yorkshire appears in the play solely as
the origin of [[peter-of-pomfret|Peter of Pomfret]], the folk prophet
brought before [[king-john|King John]] by [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]]
in Act 4. Though mentioned only once, the town carries significant
political and geographical weight in the play's portrayal of a kingdom
in crisis.

> And here's a Prophet that I brought with me
> From forth the streets of Pomfret, whom I found
> With many hundreds treading on his heeles:
> To whom he sung in rude harsh sounding rimes,
> That ere the next Ascension day at noone,
> Your Highnes should deliuer vp your Crowne
>
> (008-126–131)

## The Prophet and His Following

[[philip-the-bastard|The Bastard]] reports finding Peter "From forth the
streets of Pomfret, whom I found / With many hundreds treading on his
heeles" (008-127–128). This image — a crowd of "many hundreds" following
a prophet through a northern town — is one of the play's few glimpses
of ordinary English people beyond the court and [[war|battle]]field. The
Bastard's wider report contextualizes Peter's popularity: "as I
trauail'd hither through the land, / I finde the people strangely
fantasied, / Possest with rumors, full of idle dreames, / Not knowing
what they feare, but full of feare" (008-122–125).

The connection between Pomfret's streets and this national mood of
anxiety is significant. Peter "sung in rude harsh sounding rimes"
(008-129) a prophecy that [[king-john|John]] would "deliuer vp your
[[crown|Crown]]e" (008-131) by the next Ascension Day — a prediction that, in
a play fascinated by [[legitimacy]] and the transfer of [[power|authority]],
carries dangerous political implications.

## Geography of Resistance

Pomfret's Yorkshire location matters. The north of [[england|England]]
had a long history of semi-autonomy and resistance to southern royal
[[power|authority]] in both Shakespeare's time and the medieval period of the
play's setting. By situating Peter's popular following in a Yorkshire
town, Shakespeare subtly codes dissent as a regional as well as a
political phenomenon. The detail adds geographical specificity to the
play's portrait of a kingdom whose fractures run deeper than the
quarrels of its nobility.

## John's Response

[[king-john|John]]'s reaction to the Pomfret prophet is swift and brutal:
"[[hubert|Hubert]], away with him: imprison him, / And on that day at noone, whereon
he sayes / I shall yeeld vp my [[crown|Crown]]e, let him be hang'd" (008-134–136).
The king's attempt to dispose of the prophet mirrors his earlier attempt
to dispose of [[arthur]] — in both cases, [[king-john|John]] believes that
eliminating the messenger eliminates the threat. The play, of course,
proves him wrong on both counts.

## Dramatic Significance

Pomfret serves as a representative provincial town in the play's
geographical imagination. Its streets, filled with common people drawn
to a folk prophet, remind the audience that [[king-john|John]]'s crisis
of [[legitimacy]] extends beyond the court and the [[war|battle]]field into the
daily life of English communities. In a play otherwise dominated by kings,
nobles, and papal legates, the brief glimpse of Pomfret's "many hundreds"
offers a rare window into popular political consciousness.
