## Overview

Prince Henry is the son and heir of [[king-john|King John]], appearing only in the final scene of the play (Act 5, Scene 7). His role is structurally significant: as the future King Henry III, he represents the restoration of legitimate succession after the turmoil of [[king-john|John]]'s reign. Brought back by the repentant rebel lords [[salisbury|Salisbury]] and [[bigot|Bigot]], Henry witnesses his father's death by poison and delivers the play's closing speech, promising peace and arranging [[king-john|John]]'s burial at Worcester.

## Arrival and Context

Prince Henry is introduced indirectly when [[hubert|Hubert]] reports to [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]] that the rebellious lords have returned to the king's side:

> And brought Prince Henry in their companie,
> At whose request the king hath pardon'd them,
> And they are all about his Maiestie
> (011-072)

The mention that the lords returned "at whose request" suggests Henry already exercises a mediating influence, even before becoming king. His appearance signals the healing of the political fractures that have dominated the play.

## The Deathbed Scene

Henry enters with [[salisbury|Salisbury]] and [[bigot|Bigot]] (011-085) to find his father [[death|dying]] of poison. His first speech diagnoses [[king-john|John]]'s condition with clinical precision:

> It is too late, the life of all his blood
> Is touch'd, corruptibly: and his pure braine
> (Which some suppose the soules fraile dwelling house)
> Doth by the idle Comments that it makes,
> Fore-tell the ending of mortality.
> (011-086)

Henry's language is strikingly philosophical for a young prince — the image of the brain as the soul's "frail dwelling house" and its "idle comments" foretelling death elevates the scene beyond simple pathos. When [[pembroke|Pembroke]] reports that [[king-john|John]], in his fever, "sung," Henry responds with one of the play's most haunting reflections on [[death|mortality]]:

> Oh vanity of sicknesse: fierce extreames
> In their continuance, will not feele themselues.
> Death hauing praide vpon the outward parts
> Leaues them inuisible...
> I am the Symet to this pale faint Swan,
> Who chaunts a dolefull hymne to his owne death
> (011-100)

The image of Henry as the "cygnet" (young swan) to his father's "pale faint swan" — a bird fabled to sing only at death — creates a poignant transition from [[king-john|John]]'s reign to Henry's.

## John's Final Words

When Henry asks "How fares your Majesty?", [[king-john|John]]'s reply is a broken list of calamities:

> Poyson'd, ill fare: dead, forsooke, cast off,
> (011-124)

Henry's weeping response ("Oh that there were some vertue in my teares, / That [[power|might]] releeue you" (011-133)) presents him as a dutiful, grieving son — the emotional counterweight to [[king-john|John]]'s politically ruthless character.

## The Closing Speech

After [[king-john|John]]'s death, [[salisbury|Salisbury]] addresses Henry directly, declaring him born "to set a forme vpon that indigest / Which he hath left so shapelesse, and so rude." Henry's final speech arranges the practical aftermath:

> At Worster must his bodie be interr'd,
> For so he will'd it
> (011-190)

He ends with a gesture toward national unity and gratitude:

> I haue a kinde soule, that would giue thankes,
> And knowes not how to do it but with teares
> (011-200)

## Dramatic Function

| Aspect | Significance |
|--------|-------------|
| **Structural Role** | The legitimate heir who closes the play's cycle of usurpation and [[war|conflict]] |
| **Emotional Counterweight** | Henry's filial grief contrasts with [[king-john|John]]'s political [[blood|violence]] |
| **Succession Theme** | His presence affirms the principle of hereditary monarchy that [[king-john|John]]'s reign threatened |
| **Tonal Resolution** | The "kind soul" who would give thanks "but with tears" provides the play's elegiac close |

Though Prince Henry speaks fewer than a dozen lines, his appearance transforms the play's final moments from a portrait of a poisoned king's agony into a narrative of continuity and legitimate succession — the Tudor political values Shakespeare consistently endorses.
