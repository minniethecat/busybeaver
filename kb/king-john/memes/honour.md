# Honour

## Definition

In *[[king-john|King John]]*, honour operates as a contested moral framework that different characters invoke, manipulate, and betray. Unlike the straightforward chivalric honour of Shakespeare's earlier history plays, honour here is fractured into competing claims: [[faith|loyalty]] to king versus [[faith|loyalty]] to kin, personal integrity versus political [[commodity|expediency]], and divine right versus de [[truth|fact]]o power. The play's most searching examinations of honour come through [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]]'s evolving conscience and [[hubert|Hubert]]'s moral crisis over [[arthur|Arthur]].

## Manifestations in the Play

### The Bastard's Honour: From Inherited to Earned

[[philip-the-bastard|Philip the Bastard]] begins the play trading his inherited honour for a more flexible, self-fashioned code. His father gave him "honor" while his brother received "land" (002-006), yet [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]] embraces the exchange gladly. His first soliloquy articulates the play's most cynical view: "For new made honor doth forget mens names" (002-030) — honour is not a fixed moral quality but a commodity that can be acquired and, crucially, wielded to serve one's purposes. The Bastard's mockery of the Duke of [[austria|Austria]] descends to an assault on chivalric dignity itself, as he threatens to "set an Oxe-head to your Lyons hide: / And make a monster of you" (003-170).

Yet [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]]'s arc traces a movement from this pragmatic, almost comic detachment toward genuine moral engagement. By Act 4, as the English lords defect after [[arthur|Arthur]]'s apparent murder, his language shifts: he speaks of the need for "honourable rescue, and defence" (010-011) as something worth fighting for, not merely performing. His closing speech closes the play on a note of patriotic honour that has been tested and, however provisionally, affirmed: [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]] calls on [[england|England]] to resist invasion "As we with honor and respect may take" (011-176).

### Women and Honour: Constance and Blanche

[[constance|Constance]]'s honour is maternal and dynastic — it is inseparable from her son [[arthur|Arthur]]'s claim to the [[crown|throne]]. When [[austria|Austria]] asks whether [[constance|Constance]] truly expects him to "defend mine honor" (002-088), the question exposes how [[constance|Constance]]'s honour depends entirely on male protectors. When her cause fails, so does her sense of moral order; her madness in Act 3 is a collapse of the honour-world that gave her identity meaning.

[[blanche-of-castile|Blanche]]'s honour, by contrast, is explicitly contractual. She is the instrument of the [[marriage-of-lewis-and-blanche|marriage alliance]] that ends the siege of [[angiers|Angiers]]. She admits "My vnckles will in this respect is mine" (004-117) and concedes that she will "enforce it easlie to my loue" (004-122). When the alliance fractures, Blanche is "bound in honor still to do" (004-131) what the political settlement demands, even as her loyalties are torn between husband and uncle. Her honour is not freely chosen but structurally imposed — a condition of her position rather than a reflection of her character.

### Hubert's Moral Crisis

The play's most intense examination of honour occurs in [[hubert|Hubert]]'s scenes with [[arthur|Arthur]] in Act 4 Scene 1. Tasked with blinding the boy prince, [[hubert|Hubert]] finds his professional honour — obedience to the king — in direct [[war|conflict]] with every other code he [[power|might]] recognize. [[arthur|Arthur]]'s appeal to their shared history is devastating in its intimacy: "I knit my hand-kercher about your browes" (007-128), and "with my hand, at midnight held your head" (007-131). The boy's goodness "will awake my mercie, which lies dead" (007-109), [[hubert|Hubert]] admits, and his decision to spare [[arthur|Arthur]] is the play's one unambiguous act of moral honour.

The consequences are tragic — [[arthur|Arthur]] dies anyway, attempting to escape — but [[hubert|Hubert]] describes his actions afterward as rooted in "honourable" feeling (009-114), and the tears he sheds over the discovery of [[arthur|Arthur]]'s body are "honourable dewe" (010-038). The language of honour is reclaimed here for genuine moral feeling rather than political posturing.

### Kingship and the Honour of the Crown

[[king-john|King John]]'s relationship to honour is fundamentally instrumental. He wounds his mother's "honor" with "this diffidence" (001-144), questioning [[queen-elinor|Elinor]]'s [[faith|fidelity]] to Henry II in a way that tarnishes his own legitimacy. He uses "titles, honors, and promotions" (004-098) to buy [[faith|loyalty]], as though honour were currency rather than virtue. [[king-john|John]] appeals to "the honour of my marriage bed" (010-088), but no value in the play suffers more from inflation than honour. The play suggests that honour divorced from legitimacy — the [[crown|Crown]]'s honour without the [[crown|Crown]]'s right — is unsustainable. [[king-john|John]]'s [[power|authority]] collapses when the lords conclude that his "pure Honors" (009-028) are stained beyond recovery by [[arthur|Arthur]]'s blood.

## See Also

Honour intersects with [[legitimacy]], [[oath]], [[faith]], and [[truth]] throughout the play. The concept most destructive to honour is [[commodity]] — [[commodity|self-interest]] as the [[power|force]] that corrupts every chivalric ideal. The [[revolt-of-the-english-lords|lords' rebellion]] is framed as a matter of honour reclaimed from a tainted king, yet the play never fully endorses their self-justification, suggesting that honour, in *[[king-john|King John]]*, is always a performance as much as a principle.
