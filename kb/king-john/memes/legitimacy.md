# Legitimacy

Legitimacy is a central thematic concern of Shakespeare's *[[king-john|King John]]*. The play
investigates the difference between legal legitimacy (conferred by law,
primogeniture, and social recognition) and natural legitimacy (conferred by
merit, character, and ability). This tension is embodied most vividly in
[[philip-the-bastard]], who renounces his legal inheritance as a Faulconbridge
to claim the Plantagenet bloodline of his biological father [[king-richard-i]].

## The Two Faces of Legitimacy

Shakespeare structures the play around competing claims to legitimacy.
[[arthur]], the son of John's elder brother Geoffrey, possesses the
strongest legal claim to the English throne: "this little abstract doth
containe that large, / Which died in Geffrey" (002-232–233). France's
King Philip supports Arthur precisely because he is "thy elder brother
borne" (002-235), making John's kingship a usurpation that has "Cut off
the sequence of posterity" (002-227).

Yet [[king-john|John]]'s rule, however legally dubious, enjoys the practical
legitimacy of possession and performance: "Our iust and lineall entrance to
our owne" (002-216). He commands armies, makes alliances, and governs. The
play refuses to resolve the question of whether blood or action confers the
truer right to rule — instead, it demonstrates how political legitimacy is
always negotiated, never given.

## Blood and Bastardy

The language of bastardy pervades the play's treatment of legitimacy.
When [[constance]] challenges [[queen-elinor]], Elinor retorts "Out insolent,
thy bastard shall be King" (002-253) — a charge Constance fiercely rebuts:
"My bed was euer to thy sonne as true / As thine was to thy husband"
(002-255–256). The accusation and its denial reveal that legitimacy
discourse in *King John* is weaponized as a political tool, not a neutral
legal standard.

> Out insolent, thy bastard shall be King,
> That thou maist be a Queen, and checke the world.
>
> (002-253)

[[philip-the-bastard]]'s unique position — legitimate by Plantagenet blood
but illegitimate by Faulconbridge law — makes him the play's most acute
commentator on the subject. His famous soliloquy on "commodity" (self-interest
as the true ruler of human affairs) suggests that all claims to legitimacy
are ultimately self-serving fictions.

## The Bastard's Paradox

The resolution of the [[inheritance]] dispute in Act 1 dramatizes the
paradox at the heart of legitimacy. Philip is visually identifiable as
[[king-richard-i|Richard the Lionheart]]'s son — "Mine eye hath well examined
his parts, / And findes them perfect Richard" (001-168–169) — yet his
legal status as a Faulconbridge bars him from the inheritance he grew up
expecting. He trades one form of legitimacy (local, economic, familial)
for another (royal, nominal, political), gaining a knighthood but losing
his land: "A foot of Honor better then I was, / But many a many foot of
Land the worse" (002-025–026).

This exchange encapsulates the play's central insight: legitimacy is not
a binary but a currency, convertible between domains at varying rates,
always contested and never finally settled. John himself acknowledges the
validity of Robert Faulconbridge's legal claim: "Sirra, your brother is
Legittimate" (001-195), yet simultaneously elevates the bastard Philip to
Plantagenet status.

## The Right of Possession

The Bastard's soliloquy on the league of Angiers exposes the hollowness
of legitimacy claims when they collide with self-interest. France, who
entered the war "Whom zeale and charitie brought to the field, / As Gods
owne souldier" (004-177–178), abandons Arthur's cause when territorial
concessions are offered. John, in turn, seeks "to stop Arthurs Title in
the whole" (004-174) by bribing France with the very lands he claims to
hold legitimately. The Bastard's verdict — "Mad world, mad kings, mad
composition" (004-173) — is the play's definitive judgment on the
legitimacy discourse of political actors.

> Mad world, mad kings, mad composition:
> Iohn to stop Arthurs Title in the whole,
> Hath willingly departed with a part.
>
> (004-173)

## Resolution Through Succession

The play's resolution — [[prince-henrys-succession|Prince Henry's succession]]
after [[king-john|John]]'s death — restores the discourse of legitimacy to
its conventional form. The Bastard pledges allegiance to "The lineall state,
and glorie of the Land" (011-194) and offers "true subiection euerlastingly"
(011-197). The English lords who defected to [[lewis-the-dauphin]] return to
their allegiance because their grievance was with John personally, not with
the crown. The hereditary principle reasserts itself, and the legitimacy
crisis that drove the action is resolved through the one mechanism the play
has shown to be fragile and contingent: dynastic succession. Shakespeare
leaves the audience to wonder whether this resolution is genuine or merely
another performance of legitimacy.
