# Siege of Angiers

## Event Overview

The military confrontation at [[angiers|Angiers]] where English and French armies converge, resolved by the citizens' proposal of the marriage alliance between [[lewis-the-dauphin|Lewis]] and [[blanche-of-castile|Blanche]]. The siege frames the play's central question: who has the right to rule — the possessor or the claimant?

> Enter before Angiers, Philip King of France, Lewis, Daulphin,
> Austria,
> Constance, Arthur.
>
> Lewis. Before Angiers well met braue Austria,
> Arthur that great fore-runner of thy bloud,
> Richard that rob'd the Lion of his heart,
> And fought the holy Warres in Palestine,
> By this braue Duke came early to his graue:
>
> (002-125–132)

## Role in the Play

### Narrative Function

Siege of Angiers serves as the play's first major set-piece. The rival armies of [[king-john|England]] and [[king-philip-ii|France]] meet before the city walls, each demanding entry in the name of their candidate for the English throne (002-125). The citizens of Angiers, led by [[hubert|Hubert]], refuse to admit either army until one proves victorious (003-080) — and when the battle proves inconclusive, they propose the marriage alliance that briefly unites the warring kings (003-183).

### Key Participants

The siege brings together nearly the entire cast: [[king-john|King John]] and [[queen-elinor|Queen Elinor]] represent England; [[king-philip-ii|King Philip II]] and [[lewis-the-dauphin|Lewis]] represent France; [[arthur|Arthur]] and [[constance|Constance]] are the claimants in whose name France fights; [[austria|Austria]] arrives as a French ally; and [[hubert|Hubert]] speaks for the citizens of Angiers, whose pragmatic neutrality forces the kings to negotiate.

### Consequences

The siege's resolution — the marriage of [[lewis-the-dauphin|Lewis]] and [[blanche-of-castile|Blanche]] — creates the fragile peace of the "League of Angiers." But [[constance|Constance]]'s fury at this betrayal of Arthur's cause and [[pandulph|Pandulph]]'s subsequent intervention ensure the peace is short-lived. The siege thus demonstrates the play's central dynamic: no political settlement endures when legitimacy remains contested.
