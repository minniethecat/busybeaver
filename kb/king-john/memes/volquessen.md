# Volquessen

**Volquessen** (modern **Vexin**, specifically *Vexin Français*) is a historical region in northern [[france|France]], mentioned in Shakespeare's *[[king-john|King John]]* as one of the territorial concessions made by [[king-john|King John]] to [[king-philip-ii|King Philip II]] of [[france|France]]. It appears in the peace settlement that seals the [[marriage-of-lewis-and-blanche|marriage of Lewis and Blanche]].

## In the Play

Volquessen appears in the peace negotiations when [[king-john|John]], seeking to secure peace with [[france|France]], offers significant territorial concessions. Lewis the Dauphin had already demanded "England and Ireland, Angiers, Toraine, Maine" (003-024) on Arthur's behalf. John's counter-offer concedes much of this territory:

> Then I doe giue Volquessen, Toraine, Maine,
> Poyctiers and Aniow, these fiue Prouinces
> (004-137–138)

This gift — alongside [[maine|Maine]], [[anjou|Anjou]], [[touraine|Touraine]], and [[poitiers|Poitiers]] — represents a substantial surrender of English continental holdings. The concession is politically motivated: [[king-john|John]] seeks to neutralize the French threat to his [[crown|throne]] by purchasing Philip's alliance through territorial bribery and the marriage of his niece [[blanche-of-castile|Blanche]] to the [[lewis-the-dauphin|Dauphin]].

## The Bastard's Commentary

[[philip-the-bastard]] immediately recognizes the political calculus behind the territorial concessions. His soliloquy frames the gift of Volquessen and the other provinces as a strategic bribe: "Iohn to stop Arthurs Title in the whole, / Hath willingly departed with a part" (004-174–175). The five provinces, including Volquessen, are the price John pays to purchase French neutrality — a transaction that the Bastard acidly summarizes as "Mad world, mad kings, mad composition" (004-173).

## The Vexin in History

The historical Vexin was a contested border region between Normandy and the French royal domain, divided into two parts:

| Division | Control | Description |
|----------|---------|-------------|
| **Vexin Français** | French crown | South of the Epte River, with its seat at Pontoise |
| **Vexin Normand** | Norman/English | North of the Epte, with its seat at Gisors |

The Vexin was strategically vital because it controlled the approaches to both Paris and Rouen. Its possession was contested throughout the 12th and early 13th centuries.

## Dramatic Significance

Volquessen's inclusion in [[king-john|John]]'s territorial concessions highlights several themes:

- **The cost of legitimacy**: [[king-john|John]] purchases French recognition of his [[crown|kingship]] with English land, trading territorial integrity for political security
- **[[arthur|Arthur]]'s dispossession**: The lands [[king-john|John]] gives away are, in part, the inheritance of [[arthur|Arthur Plantagenet]], deepening the play's concern with usurpation and rightful claim
- **The fragility of peace**: The [[the-league-of-angiers|peace league]] that Volquessen helps purchase collapses within a single scene when [[pandulph|Pandulph]] arrives with the papal [[curse]]

## Strategic Geography

The Vexin's strategic importance derived from its position on the frontier between the Duchy of Normandy and the French royal domain. The Epte River divided the region: the northern bank (Vexin Normand, with its fortress at Gisors) stood under Norman — and later English — control, while the southern bank (Vexin Français, with its seat at Pontoise) belonged to the French crown. Control of the Vexin meant control of the military approaches to both Rouen and Paris, making the region one of the most fiercely contested territories of the twelfth and early thirteenth centuries.

Shakespeare's rendering of the name as "Volquessen" (004-137) reflects an anglicization of the French *Vexin*, possibly filtered through an intermediate textual source. The spelling appears only once in the play, in John's enumeration of the five provinces, and the territory is not subsequently referenced — its function is purely as a diplomatic token in the peace negotiations (004-137–138).
