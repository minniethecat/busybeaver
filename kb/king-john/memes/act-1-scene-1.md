[001-001] Executive Director's Notes:

[001-002] In addition to the notes below, and so you will *NOT* think all

[001-003] the spelling errors introduced by the printers of the time have

[001-004] been corrected, here are the first few lines of Hamlet, as they

[001-005] are presented herein:

[001-006] Barnardo. Who's there?

[001-007] Fran. Nay answer me: Stand & vnfold

[001-008] your selfe

[001-009] Bar. Long liue the King

[001-010] * * * * *

[001-011] As I understand it, the printers often ran out of certain words

[001-012] or letters they had often packed into a "cliche". . .this is the

[001-013] original meaning of the term cliche. . .and thus, being unwilling

[001-014] to unpack the cliches, and thus you will see some substitutions

[001-015] that look very odd. . .such as the exchanges of u for v, v for u,

[001-016] above. . .and you may wonder why they did it this way, presuming

[001-017] Shakespeare did not actually write the play in this manner. . . .

[001-018] The answer is that they MAY have packed "liue" into a cliche at a

[001-019] time when they were out of "v"'s. . .possibly having used "vv" in

[001-020] place of some "w"'s, etc. This was a common practice of the day,

[001-021] as print was still quite expensive, and they didn't want to spend

[001-022] more on a wider selection of characters than they had to.

[001-023] You will find a lot of these kinds of "errors" in this text, as I

[001-024] have mentioned in other times and places, many "scholars" have an

[001-025] extreme attachment to these errors, and many have accorded them a

[001-026] very high place in the "canon" of Shakespeare. My father read an

[001-027] assortment of these made available to him by Cambridge University

[001-028] in [[england|England]] for several months in a glass room constructed for the

[001-029] purpose. To the best of my knowledge he read ALL those available

[001-030] . . .in great detail. . .and determined from the various changes,

[001-031] that Shakespeare most likely did not write in nearly as many of a

[001-032] variety of errors we credit him for, even though he was in/famous

[001-033] for signing his name with several different spellings.

[001-034] So, please take this into account when reading the comments below

[001-035] made by our volunteer who prepared this file: you may see errors

[001-036] that are "not" errors. . . .

[001-037] So. . .with this caveat. . .we have NOT changed the canon errors,

[001-038] here is the Project Gutenberg Etext of Shakespeare's The first

[001-039] Part of Henry the Sixt.

[001-048] The elongated S's have been changed to small s's and the

[001-049] conjoined ae have been changed to ae. I have left the spelling,

[001-050] punctuation, capitalization as close as possible to the printed

[001-051] text. I have corrected some spelling mistakes (I have put

[001-052] together a spelling dictionary devised from the spellings of

[001-053] the Geneva Bible and Shakespeare's First Folio and have unified

[001-054] spellings according to this template), typo's and expanded

[001-055] abbreviations as I have come across them. Everything within

[001-059] Another thing that you should be aware of is that there are

[001-060] textual differences between various copies of the first folio. So

[001-061] there may be differences (other than what I have mentioned above)

[001-062] between this and other first folio editions. This is due to the

[001-063] printer's habit of setting the type and running off a number of

[001-064] copies and then proofing the printed copy and correcting the type

[001-065] and then continuing the printing run. The proof run wasn't thrown

[001-066] away but incorporated into the printed copies. This is just the

[001-067] way it is. The text I have used was a composite of more than 30

[001-068] different First Folio editions' best pages.

[001-071] The life and death of [[king-john|King John]]

[001-072] Actus Primus, Scaena Prima.

[001-073] Enter King Iohn, Queene [[queen-elinor|Elinor]], [[pembroke|Pembroke]], [[essex|Essex]], and [[salisbury|Salisbury]],

[001-074] with the

[001-075] Chattylion of [[france|France]].

[001-076] King Iohn. Now say [[chatillon|Chatillion]], what would France with vs?

[001-077] Chat. Thus (after greeting) speakes the King

[001-078] of France,

[001-079] In my behauiour to the Maiesty,

[001-080] The borrowed Maiesty of England heere

[001-081] Elea. A strange beginning: borrowed Maiesty?

[001-082] K.Iohn. Silence (good mother) heare the Embassie

[001-083] Chat. Philip of France, in right and true behalfe

[001-084] Of thy deceased brother, Geffreyes sonne,

[001-085] [[arthur|Arthur]] Plantaginet, laies most lawfull claime

[001-086] To this faire Iland, and the Territories:

[001-087] To [[ireland|Ireland]], Poyctiers, Aniowe, Torayne, [[maine|Maine]],

[001-088] Desiring thee to lay aside the sword

[001-089] Which swaies vsurpingly these seuerall titles,

[001-090] And put the same into yong Arthurs hand,

[001-091] Thy Nephew, and right royall Soueraigne

[001-092] K.Iohn. What followes if we disallow of this?

[001-093] Chat. The proud controle of fierce and bloudy warre,

[001-094] To inforce these rights, so forcibly with-held,

[001-095] K.Io. Heere haue we war for war, & bloud for bloud,

[001-096] Controlement for controlement: so answer France

[001-097] Chat. Then take my Kings defiance from my mouth,

[001-098] The farthest limit of my Embassie

[001-099] K.Iohn. Beare mine to him, and so depart in peace,

[001-100] Be thou as lightning in the eies of France;

[001-101] For ere thou canst report, I will be there:

[001-102] The thunder of my Cannon shall be heard.

[001-103] So hence: be thou the trumpet of our wrath,

[001-104] And sullen presage of your owne decay:

[001-105] An [[honour|honourable]] conduct let him haue,

[001-106] Pembroke looke too't: farewell Chattillion.

[001-107] Exit Chat. and Pem.

[001-108] Ele. What now my sonne, haue I not euer said

[001-109] How that ambitious [[constance|Constance]] would not cease

[001-110] Till she had kindled France and all the world,

[001-111] Vpon the right and party of her sonne.

[001-112] This [[power|might]] haue beene preuented, and made whole

[001-113] With very easie arguments of loue,

[001-114] Which now the mannage of two kingdomes must

[001-115] With fearefull bloudy issue arbitrate

[001-116] K.Iohn. Our strong possession, and our right for vs

[001-117] Eli. Your strong possessio[n] much more then your right,

[001-118] Or else it must go wrong with you and me,

[001-119] So much my conscience whispers in your eare,

[001-120] Which none but heauen, and you, and I, shall heare.

[001-121] Enter a Sheriffe.

[001-122] Essex. My Liege, here is the strangest controuersie

[001-123] Come from the Country to be iudg'd by you

[001-124] That ere I heard: shall I produce the men?

[001-125] K.Iohn. Let them approach:

[001-126] Our Abbies and our Priories shall pay

[001-127] This expeditions charge: what men are you?

[001-128] Enter [[robert-faulconbridge|Robert Faulconbridge]], and Philip.

[001-129] Philip. Your faithfull subiect, I a gentleman,

[001-130] Borne in [[northampton|Northamptonshire]], and eldest sonne

[001-131] As I suppose, to [[sir-robert-faulconbridge|Robert Faulconbridge]],

[001-132] A Souldier by the Honor-giuing-hand

[001-133] Of [[king-richard-i|Cordelion]], Knighted in the field

[001-134] K.Iohn. What art thou?

[001-135] Robert. The son and heire to that same Faulconbridge

[001-136] K.Iohn. Is that the elder, and art thou the heyre?

[001-137] You came not of one mother then it seemes

[001-138] Philip. Most certain of one mother, mighty King,

[001-139] That is well knowne, and as I thinke one father:

[001-140] But for the certaine knowledge of that truth,

[001-141] I put you o're to heauen, and to my mother;

[001-142] Of that I doubt, as all mens children may

[001-143] Eli. Out on thee rude man, y dost shame thy mother,

[001-144] And wound her honor with this diffidence

[001-145] Phil. I Madame? No, I haue no reason for it,

[001-146] That is my brothers plea, and none of mine,

[001-147] The which if he can proue, a pops me out,

[001-148] At least from faire fiue hundred pound a yeere:

[001-149] Heauen guard my mothers honor, and my Land

[001-150] K.Iohn. A good blunt fellow: why being yonger born

[001-151] Doth he lay claime to thine inheritance?

[001-152] Phil. I know not why, except to get the land:

[001-153] But once he slanderd me with bastardy:

[001-154] But where I be as true begot or no,

[001-155] That still I lay vpon my mothers head,

[001-156] But that I am as well begot my Liege

[001-157] (Faire fall the bones that tooke the paines for me)

[001-158] Compare our faces, and be Iudge your selfe

[001-159] If old Sir Robert did beget vs both,

[001-160] And were our father, and this sonne like him:

[001-161] O old sir Robert Father, on my knee

[001-162] I giue heauen thankes I was not like to thee

[001-163] K.Iohn. Why what a mad-cap hath heauen lent vs here?

[001-164] Elen. He hath a tricke of Cordelions face,

[001-165] The accent of his tongue affecteth him:

[001-166] Doe you not read some tokens of my sonne

[001-167] In the large composition of this man?

[001-168] K.Iohn. Mine eye hath well examined his parts,

[001-169] And findes them perfect Richard: sirra speake,

[001-170] What doth moue you to claime your brothers land

[001-171] Philip. Because he hath a half-face like my father?

[001-172] With halfe that face would he haue all my land,

[001-173] A halfe-fac'd groat, fiue hundred pound a yeere?

[001-174] Rob. My gracious Liege, when that my father liu'd,

[001-175] Your brother did imploy my father much

[001-176] Phil. Well sir, by this you cannot get my land,

[001-177] Your tale must be how he employ'd my mother

[001-178] Rob. And once dispatch'd him in an Embassie

[001-179] To Germany, there with the Emperor

[001-180] To treat of high affaires touching that time:

[001-181] Th' aduantage of his absence tooke the King,

[001-182] And in the meane time soiourn'd at my fathers;

[001-183] Where how he did preuaile, I shame to speake:

[001-184] But truth is truth, large lengths of seas and shores

[001-185] Betweene my father, and my mother lay,

[001-186] As I haue heard my father speake himselfe

[001-187] When this same lusty gentleman was got:

[001-188] Vpon his death-bed he by will bequeath'd

[001-189] His lands to me, and tooke it on his death

[001-190] That this my mothers sonne was none of his;

[001-191] And if he were, he came into the world

[001-192] Full fourteene weekes before the course of time:

[001-193] Then good my Liedge let me haue what is mine,

[001-194] My fathers land, as was my fathers will

[001-195] K.Iohn. Sirra, your brother is Legittimate,

[001-196] Your fathers wife did after wedlocke beare him:

[001-197] And if she did play false, the fault was hers,

[001-198] Which fault lyes on the hazards of all husbands

[001-199] That marry wiues: tell me, how if my brother

[001-200] Who as you say, tooke paines to get this sonne,

[001-201] Had of your father claim'd this sonne for his,

[001-202] Insooth, good friend, your father might haue kept

[001-203] This Calfe, bred from his Cow from all the world:

[001-204] Insooth he might: then if he were my brothers,

[001-205] My brother might not claime him, nor your father

[001-206] Being none of his, refuse him: this concludes,

[001-207] My mothers sonne did get your fathers heyre,

[001-208] Your fathers heyre must haue your fathers land

[001-209] Rob. Shal then my fathers Will be of no force,

[001-210] To dispossesse that childe which is not his

[001-211] Phil. Of no more force to dispossesse me sir,

[001-212] Then was his will to get me, as I think

[001-213] Eli. Whether hadst thou rather be a Faulconbridge,

[001-214] And like thy brother to enioy thy land:

[001-215] Or the reputed sonne of Cordelion,

[001-216] Lord of thy presence, and no land beside

[001-217] Bast. Madam, and if my brother had my shape

[001-218] And I had his, sir Roberts his like him,

[001-219] And if my legs were two such riding rods,

[001-220] My armes, such eele skins stuft, my face so thin,

[001-221] That in mine eare I durst not sticke a rose,

[001-222] Lest men should say, looke where three farthings goes,

[001-223] And to his shape were heyre to all this land,

[001-224] Would I might neuer stirre from off this place,

[001-225] I would giue it euery foot to haue this face:

[001-226] It would not be sir nobbe in any case

[001-227] Elinor. I like thee well: wilt thou forsake thy fortune,

[001-228] Bequeath thy land to him, and follow me?

[001-229] I am a Souldier, and now bound to France

[001-230] Bast. Brother, take you my land, Ile take my chance;

[001-231] Your face hath got fiue hundred pound a yeere,

[001-232] Yet sell your face for fiue pence and 'tis deere:

[001-233] Madam, Ile follow you vnto the death

[001-234] Elinor. Nay, I would haue you go before me thither

[001-235] Bast. Our Country manners giue our betters way

[001-236] K.Iohn. What is thy name?

[001-237] Bast. Philip my Liege, so is my name begun,

[001-238] Philip, good old Sir Roberts wiues eldest sonne
