# Faith

## Definition

Faith in *[[king-john|King John]]* is a multivalent concept that bridges the religious and the political, the personal and the institutional. The word appears across every register of Elizabethan English: as religious belief ("holy faith"), as feudal loyalty ("faithful subject"), as marital fidelity ("deep-sworn faith"), as oath-keeping ("break faith"), and as simple trust ("in faith"). The play's central dramatic project is the systematic testing and breaking of faith at every one of these levels. *[[king-john|King John]]* asks whether any form of faith — in God, in kings, in spouses, in allies, in oaths, in words themselves — can survive in a world driven by what [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]] calls "commodity, the bias of the world" (004-185). The answer the play gives is unsettling: faith endures only where it is chosen rather than inherited, actively maintained rather than passively assumed, and grounded in personal integrity rather than institutional [[power|authority]].

## Manifestations in the Play

### Broken Faith Between Kings

The Bastard's soliloquy on commodity delivers the play's definitive diagnosis: "[[sin|Sin]]ce kings break faith upon commodity" (004-209). The line is both an observation and an accusation. When King Philip abandons his sworn alliance with [[king-john|John]] — "deep-sworn faith, peace, amity, true love" (005-199) — to obey [[pandulph|Pandulph]]'s papal directive, he breaks faith not only with [[king-john|John]] but with his own son Lewis and new daughter-in-law Blanche. The Bastard anatomizes the mechanism: "that broker, that still breaks the pate of faith" (004-180) is commodity, a "smooth-faced gentleman, tickling commodity" (004-185) who corrupts every bond it touches. This breach cascades through the play's social order: once a king breaks faith on grounds of [[commodity|self-interest]], all subordinate bonds — between lords and king, subjects and crown, husband and wife — become equally negotiable. The feudal chain of fidelity shatters from the top.

### Religious Faith and Its Instrumentalization

[[pandulph|Pandulph]] wields faith as both weapon and shield, and his excommunication of [[king-john|John]] is the play's most sustained examination of how religious [[power|authority]] can be instrumentalized for political ends. The papal legate arrives declaring "Here comes the holy Legate of the Pope" (005-099) and immediately demands why [[king-john|John]] acts "against the Church, our holy Mother" (005-105). The excommunication — "Thou shalt stand cursed, and excommunicate" (005-137) — is framed as a defense of the faith, yet the play exposes the political machinery beneath the theological language. [[king-john|John]]'s defiant response — "that I have room with [[rome|Rome]] to curse awhile" (005-145) — denies papal [[power|authority]] while acknowledging its power. The line "Yea, faith itself to hollow falsehood change" (005-057) captures the core corruption: faith, which should be the most stable of commitments, becomes the vehicle for the deepest betrayals when it is institutionalized.

The French king's dilemma dramatizes the impossible position faith creates: "If thou stand excommunicate, and cursed?" (005-191) asks Philip, torn between his oath to [[king-john|John]] and his obedience to [[rome|Rome]]. [[pandulph|Pandulph]] resolves this dilemma with breathtaking sophistry, arguing that breaking faith with a heretic is no sin: "So mak'st thou faith an enemy to faith" (005-231). The phrase is the play's most concise summary of faith's tragic structure: when faiths [[war|conflict]], one must break, and the act of choosing which faith to keep is itself a form of [[betrayal|faith-breaking]].

### John's Submission and Its Ironies

[[king-john|John]]'s eventual submission to [[rome|Rome]] — yielding his crown to [[pandulph|Pandulph]], who returns it "as holding of the Pope" (009-178) — appears to restore religious faith to the political order. [[pandulph|Pandulph]] demands the Dauphin "keep your holy word, go meet the French" (009-180), claiming that his "tongue shall hush again this storm of war" (009-195). But the restoration is hollow. The Dauphin refuses to disarm, asking bitterly, "Am I [[rome|Rome]]'s slave? What penny hath [[rome|Rome]] borne?" (010-092). The Bastard, watching [[king-john|John]]'s submission, remarks that "his spirit is come in, / That so stood out against the holy Church, / The great Metropolis and Sea of [[rome|Rome]]" (010-065 to 010-067). The irony is crushing: [[king-john|John]]'s return to faith is presented not as spiritual regeneration but as strategic surrender. The word "spirit" hovers ambiguously between the Holy Spirit and mere morale. When [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]] later says "And welcome home again discarded faith" (010-216), the line can be read as relief, sarcasm, or both. Faith has been restored to [[england|England]], but it is a faith that everyone knows was purchased, not believed.

### Personal Faith and the Bastard's Arc

Against the backdrop of institutional betrayal, [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]]'s personal fidelity stands as the play's one stable center of faith. His loyalty to [[king-john|John]] — flawed king though [[king-john|John]] is — represents a form of faith that is chosen rather than inherited, actively maintained rather than passively assumed. He declares himself [[king-john|John]]'s "faithful subject" (001-129) before he has any reason to be one, and this commitment is tested to the breaking point and beyond. He follows [[king-john|John]] through excommunication, military disaster, and moral collapse. His faith survives because it is grounded not in [[king-john|John]]'s worthiness but in his own integrity: "I may disjoin my hand, but not my faith" (005-230), says [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]], a line that redefines faith as something interior and individual rather than contractual and conditional. Unlike the French king, whose faith to [[king-john|John]] could be dissolved by papal decree, [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]]'s faith is not subject to external revocation.

### The Paradox of Conflicting Faiths

[[constance|Constance]] gives the play's most poignant articulation of faith's paradox: "Which only lives but by the death of faith" (005-180). She elaborates: "That faith would live again by death of need: / O then tread down my need, and faith mounts up, / Keep my need up, and faith is trodden down" (005-182 to 005-184). In [[constance|Constance]]'s formulation, faith and need are locked in a zero-sum relationship — one can only rise if the other falls. This is not cynicism but the play's structural insight: faith is always defined against something that threatens it, and the act of maintaining faith is therefore always an act of struggle.

### Faith as Performance

The play repeatedly stages faith as a public performance whose sincerity is impossible to verify. Blanche's marriage vows bind her "in faith" to a political arrangement, and when that arrangement collapses, her faith becomes impossible to maintain — or even to locate. The lords' oaths of loyalty to [[king-john|John]] prove equally fragile. [[pandulph|Pandulph]]'s instruction that the French break faith with [[king-john|John]] is itself performed as a holy act, "with holy breath" (010-062). *[[king-john|King John]]* thus poses an uncomfortable question: if faith must be performed to be recognized, how can we distinguish genuine faith from its imitation? The Bastard alone seems to answer this question, and his answer is not verbal but existential — faith is not what you say but what you do when saying it costs you everything.

## Historical Context

For Shakespeare's original audience, faith was not a philosophical abstraction but a matter of life, death, and damnation. The Elizabethan Religious Settlement (1559) had attempted to stabilize [[england|England]] after decades of oscillation between Catholicism and Protestantism, but the threat of papal [[power|authority]] remained vivid: Pope Pius V's bull *Regnans in Excelsis* (1570) had excommunicated Elizabeth and absolved her Catholic subjects of their loyalty — precisely the situation dramatized in *[[king-john|King John]]*. The play's depiction of [[pandulph|Pandulph]] wielding excommunication as a political weapon would have resonated powerfully. By setting the play in [[king-john|John]]'s reign — the only English king ever to submit to papal overlordship — Shakespeare could explore the church-state [[war|conflict]] at a safe historical distance while addressing contemporary anxieties.

The play's treatment of faith also engages with the late-Elizabethan crisis of oath-taking. The [[oath|Oath]] of Supremacy and [[oath|Oath]] of Allegiance required subjects to swear allegiance to the monarch as supreme governor of the Church, and refusal meant persecution or death. "It is religion that doth make vows kept" (005-247), says [[pandulph|Pandulph]], but the play has already demonstrated that religion can equally be used to break vows. The line "But thou hast sworn against religion" (005-248) captures the circular logic by which any oath could be declared invalid by the opposing religious [[power|authority]].

## Critical Perspectives

*[[king-john|King John]]* is unusual among Shakespeare's histories in its sustained focus on religious faith as a political [[power|force]]. The Henriad treats the church largely as a background institution; *[[king-john|King John]]* makes it a central dramatic agent. Critics have read the play as a proto-secular text that exposes the political functions beneath theological claims, while others see it as a genuinely religious work that laments the corruption of faith by power. The play's refusal to resolve this ambiguity is arguably its central achievement. Faith in *[[king-john|King John]]* is not dismissed as mere illusion — [[philip-the-bastard|the Bastard]]'s fidelity proves otherwise — but it is shown to be uniquely vulnerable to manipulation, impossible to institutionalize without corrupting, and sustainable only through acts of individual will that cost the faithful everything.
