# Marriage

## Definition

Marriage in *[[king-john|King John]]* is political theater. The union of Lewis and Blanche is proposed not as a personal bond but as a diplomatic tool — it is offered by the citizens of [[angiers|Angiers]] to end the siege and seal the peace between [[england|England]] and [[france|France]].

## Manifestations in the Play

### The Marriage of Lewis and Blanche

The ceremony is framed in explicitly political terms: "The rights of marriage shall be solemnized" (004-149) not to unite two people but to unite two kingdoms. Blanche becomes a human treaty, and her tragedy is that she alone takes the marriage seriously while everyone around her treats it as revocable.

> The rights of marriage shallbe solemniz'd.
>
> (004-149)

### Marriage Beds Broken

When [[pandulph|Pandulph]]'s arrival [[power|force]]s King Philip to choose between his new alliance and papal [[power|authority]], the play's language turns to the marriage bed as a site of violation. [[constance|Constance]] confronts the moral bankruptcy directly:

> Vn-sweare faith sworne, and on the marriage bed
> (005-213)

The oath sworn on the marriage bed is broken as casually as the diplomatic alliance it was meant to seal. Blanche's later reference to "the honour of my marriage bed" (010-088) is a bitter reminder of what was sacrificed to politics.

### Marriage and Commodity

The Bastard's "commodity" soliloquy names the mechanism: marriage, like every other bond in the play, is subject to the logic of [[commodity|self-interest]]. When Blanche demands her husband's [[faith|loyalty]], she appeals to a personal obligation that the political marriage was never designed to sustain.
