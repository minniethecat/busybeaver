# Prince Henry's Succession

**Prince Henry's Succession** occurs at the end of *King John*, as
[[prince-henry]] becomes King Henry III following [[johns-poisoning]] and
[[johns-death]].

## The Death of John

The transition of power unfolds against the backdrop of John's agonizing
death. [[hubert]] reports that "The King I feare is poyson'd by a
Monke" (011-060), and the lords have already "brought Prince Henry in
their companie" (011-072) to attend the dying king. John, in his final
moments, has pardoned the rebellious English nobles: "At whose request the
king hath pardon'd them, / And they are all about his Maiestie" (011-073–074).

When Prince Henry arrives, John is already beyond recovery. Henry observes
that "the life of all his blood / Is touch'd, corruptibly" (011-086–087),
and the king's "pure braine ... Doth by the idle Comments that it makes, /
Fore-tell the ending of mortality" (011-087–090). The death scene is
Shakespeare's most fully realized portrait of royal mortality in the play:
John, who has fought, schemed, and killed to hold the crown, now "shrinke[s]
up" like "a scribled forme drawne with a pen / Vpon a Parchment" (011-120–122)
against the fire of poison.

## The Return of the Lords

The succession is inseparable from the reconciliation of the English
nobility. [[salisbury]], who had defected to [[lewis-the-dauphin]] in
protest of John's abuses, now returns to the legitimate royal line.
He tells Prince Henry: "Be of good comfort (Prince) for you are borne /
To set a forme vpon that indigest / Which he hath left so shapelesse, and
so rude" (011-112–114). The image of Henry as the one who must give
"forme" to John's chaotic legacy captures the succession's symbolic weight:
it is not merely a transfer of power but a restoration of order.

## The Bastard's Fealty

The most politically significant moment of the succession is
[[philip-the-bastard]]'s pledge of allegiance. Having served [[king-john|John]]
throughout the play as his most loyal and effective supporter, the Bastard
now kneels before the new king:

"And happily may your sweet selfe put on / The lineall state, and glorie of
the Land, / To whom with all submission on my knee, / I do bequeath my
faithfull seruices / And true subiection euerlastingly" (011-193–197).

> And happily may your sweet selfe put on
> The lineall state, and glorie of the Land,
> To whom with all submission on my knee,
> I do bequeath my faithfull seruices
> And true subiection euerlastingly.
>
> (011-193)

This oath is crucial: the Bastard, who has been the play's most independent
and skeptical voice, willingly binds himself to the hereditary monarchy.
[[salisbury]] echoes the gesture: "And the like tender of our loue wee make
/ To rest without a spot for euermore" (011-198–199).

## The Peace Settlement

With Prince Henry's authority established, [[peace-settlement]] is quickly
negotiated. [[salisbury]] reports that [[lewis-the-dauphin|Lewis]] has already
"dispatch'd / To the sea side" many of his forces and "put his cause and
quarrell / To the disposing of the Cardinall" (011-181–183). The French
withdrawal, combined with the domestic reconciliation, allows the Bastard
to deliver the play's famous closing speech — a declaration of English
unity: "This England neuer did, nor neuer shall / Lye at the proud foote
of a Conqueror, / But when it first did helpe to wound it selfe"
(011-204–206). The succession has healed the self-inflicted wound, and
"these her Princes are come home againe" (011-207).

## Thematic Significance

Prince Henry's succession resolves the play's central tension between
[[legitimacy]] and usurpation. Unlike [[king-john|John]], who seized the
throne from the rightful heir [[arthur]], Henry inherits the crown through
uncontested lineal succession. His rule is "lineall" (011-194) — a word
that would have been impossible in reference to John. The play thus ends
not with the triumph of merit (the Bastard's domain) or the triumph of
force (John's domain), but with the triumph of legitimate succession, the
mechanism by which England might "to it selfe, do rest but true" (011-210).
