# Melun

**Melun** (or *Lord Melun*) is a French nobleman in Shakespeare's *[[king-john|King John]]* whose deathbed confession in Act 5 constitutes one of the play's most pivotal turning points. Mortally wounded in [[war|battle]], Melun reveals to the [[rebellion|revolt]]ed English lords that [[lewis-the-dauphin|Lewis the Dauphin]] has secretly sworn to execute them after victory, prompting [[salisbury|Salisbury]], [[pembroke|Pembroke]], and [[bigot|Bigot]] to abandon the French cause and return to [[king-john|King John]].

## Role in the Play

Melun appears only in Act 5, Scene 4 (PN range 010), but his brief role fundamentally alters the trajectory of the final act. His intervention transforms what appears to be certain French victory into an English restoration.

### The Deathbed Revelation

Found wounded on the [[war|battle]]field, Melun is approached by the English lords who have defected to the Dauphin's side. Recognizing them, Melun — motivated by conscience and the proximity of death — warns them of the Dauphin's treachery:

> Fly Noble English, you are bought and sold,
> Vnthread the rude eye of rebellion,
> And welcome home againe discarded faith
> (010-214)

He further reveals the specific nature of the threat: should the French win the day, the English lords who fought alongside them will be executed as traitors to both crowns:

> Haue I not hideous death within my view,
> Retaining but a quantity of life,
> Which bleeds away
> (010-226)

## Character Analysis

Melun is defined by his [[death|dying]] conscience. Unlike the play's more calculating characters — [[pandulph|Pandulph]], the Dauphin, even [[king-john|John]] himself — Melun acts from a simple impulse: a [[death|dying]] man ought to tell the truth. His choice of the word "hideous death" (010-226) underscores the physical and spiritual stakes of his confession.

The character serves as a mirror to [[hubert|Hubert]], who earlier in the play also faced a moral test concerning [[arthur|Arthur]]. Where [[hubert|Hubert]] chose [[grace|mercy]] despite orders, Melun chooses truth despite [[faith|allegiance]]. Both represent Shakespeare's interest in the moral agency of subordinate figures.

## Relationship to Major Characters

- **[[lewis-the-dauphin|Lewis the Dauphin]]**: Melun serves the Dauphin but ultimately betrays him, revealing the Dauphin's political calculation to execute erstwhile allies.
- **[[salisbury|Salisbury]]**: The primary recipient of Melun's warning. [[salisbury|Salisbury]]'s response — immediate return to [[king-john|John]] — demonstrates the persuasive power of Melun's [[death|dying]] testimony.
- **[[pembroke|Pembroke]]** and **[[bigot|Bigot]]**: Fellow recipients of the revelation, who join [[salisbury|Salisbury]] in abandoning the French.
- **[[king-john|King John]]**: Melun never meets [[king-john|John]], but his confession indirectly saves [[king-john|John]]'s reign.

## Language and Style

Melun's speech employs the heightened rhetoric of the deathbed confession. His language moves from the imperative ("Fly Noble English") to the self-descriptive ("Haue I not hideous death within my view"), creating a tone of urgent sincerity. The metaphor of "vnthreading" the "rude eye of rebellion" uses textile imagery to frame political [[faith|allegiance]] as something that can be unpicked and rewoven.

## Historical Context

The historical Melun (or Melun) appears in Holinshed's *Chronicles* as a French lord who revealed the Dauphin's intentions to the English. Shakespeare follows his sources closely in presenting Melun's deathbed confession, though he heightens the moral dimension by emphasizing the character's awareness of approaching death as the motive for truth-telling.
