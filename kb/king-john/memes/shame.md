# Shame

**Shame** appears over twenty times across Shakespeare's *[[king-john|King John]]*, making it among the play's most frequently invoked concepts. It functions as both a personal emotion and a public sanction, used to enforce social norms, express moral outrage, and articulate the consequences of dishonourable action. The concept is wielded most powerfully by [[constance|Constance]], but touches nearly every major character.

## Occurrences in the Play

Shame language clusters around several key moments:

| Scene | Speaker | Target | Context |
|-------|---------|--------|---------|
| Act 1 | [[queen-elinor|Queen Elinor]] | [[philip-the-bastard|Philip the Bastard]] | Accuses him of shaming his mother by claiming Faulconbridge [[blood|lineage]] — "Out on thee rude man, y dost shame thy mother" (001-143) |
| Act 2 | [[constance|Constance]] | [[austria|Austria]] | Denounces [[austria|Austria]] for dishonourable conduct — "O Lymoges, O Austria, thou dost shame" (005-077) |
| Act 3 | [[constance|Constance]] | [[arthur|Arthur]]'s cause | Lament for abandoned honour — "His mother shames him so, poore boy hee weepes" (003-040) |
| Act 4 | [[philip-the-bastard|Bastard]] | English lords | Comments on the shame of [[arthur|Arthur]]'s suspected murder — "It is apparant foule-play, and 'tis shame" (008-067) |
| Act 5 | [[salisbury|Salisbury]] | Self-reflection | Acknowledges the shame of rebellion against [[king-john|John]] — "Of your deere Mother-England: blush for shame" (010-149) |

## Constance and Shame

[[constance|Constance]] is the play's primary voice of shame discourse. Her rhetorical strategy consistently links shame to the abandonment of [[arthur|Arthur]]'s rightful claim:

> Looke heere vpon thy brother Geffreyes face,
> These eyes, these browes, were moulded out of his
> (002-230)

When she perceives [[king-philip-ii|King Philip]] wavering in his support, she deploys shame as the ultimate sanction:

> His mother shames him so, poore boy hee weepes
> (003-040)

[[constance|Constance]]'s shaming is distinctive for its ferocity and its willingness to target allies as well as enemies. For her, shame is not merely an emotion but a weapon — the last recourse of the politically powerless. Her condemnation of the league is distilled in the line "This day of shame, oppression, periury" (005-050).

## Shame and Bastardy

The play's preoccupation with [[philip-the-bastard|Philip the Bastard]]'s illegitimate birth makes shame a recurring motif in his characterization. [[queen-elinor|Queen Elinor]]'s early accusation that Philip shames his mother by claiming the Faulconbridge name introduces the theme:

> Out on thee rude man, y dost shame thy mother
> (001-143)

Philip's response — embracing his bastardy rather than being shamed by it — represents one of the play's most original dramatic moves. His famous "commodity" speech inverts the shame/bastardy equation, suggesting that political [[commodity|expediency]], not illegitimate birth, is the true source of dishonour.

## Shame as Political Sanction

Beyond personal emotion, shame functions in the play as a mechanism of political control. The threat of shame enforces the [[oath|oaths]] that bind the English lords to [[king-john|John]]. When [[salisbury|Salisbury]] and [[pembroke|Pembroke]] defect, they experience their own action as shameful:

> Making that idiot laughter keepe mens eyes
> (006-117)

The [[revolt-of-the-english-lords]] is presented as a shame-driven and shame-producing sequence: the lords are shamed by [[king-john|John]]'s apparent murder of [[arthur|Arthur]] into rebellion, but are themselves shamed by the act of rebellion against their anointed king. [[hubert|Hubert]] himself feels "Deepe shame" that "had struck me dumbe, made me break off" (008-219) when [[king-john|John]] first broached the murder.

## Theological Dimension

Shame in *[[king-john|King John]]* also carries theological weight. The play associates shame with the loss of [[grace|divine favour]] and the rupture of sacred bonds. The papal [[curse]], delivered by [[pandulph|Pandulph]], operates as a form of institutionalized shaming — making public what would otherwise be private moral failure:

> That I haue roome with Rome to curse a while
> (005-145)

The "bloodied shame" (009-051) of [[arthur|Arthur]]'s death is described as "the shamefull worke of Huberts hand" (009-066), linking individual shame to the divine judgment that pervades the play's final acts.
