# Touraine

## Location Overview

French territory forming part of the Angevin lands claimed by the English crown. Spelled "Toraine" in the Folio text, Touraine appears in the play as one of the provinces contested between [[king-john|King John]] and [[king-philip-ii|King Philip II]] (003-024), and is among the territories offered as dowry for [[blanche-of-castile|Blanche]] in the marriage alliance. (004-137)

> Then I doe giue Volquessen, Toraine, Maine,
> Poyctiers and Aniow, these fiue Prouinces
> With her to thee, and this addition more,
> Full thirty thousand Markes of English coyne:
> Phillip of France, if thou be pleas'd withall,
> Command thy Sonne and Daughter to ioyne hands
>
> (004-137–142)

## Role in the Play

### Strategic Significance

Touraine serves as a key location in the play's geography of [[war|conflict]]. Along with [[anjou|Anjou]], [[maine|Maine]], and [[poitiers|Poitiers]], it forms part of the Angevin patrimony whose ownership is contested between the English and French crowns. [[king-john|John]]'s willingness to give these provinces to [[lewis-the-dauphin|Lewis]] as [[blanche-of-castile|Blanche]]'s dowry represents both a diplomatic masterstroke and a betrayal of [[arthur|Arthur]]'s claim — for these lands rightfully belong to the young prince.

### Dramatic Function

Touraine is never visited or described; it exists in the play solely as a name in territorial inventories. This reduction of a rich historical province to a bargaining chip illustrates the play's political logic: land is not a home but a currency, and the kings who trade it are indifferent to the populations who inhabit it.

### Associated Characters

Touraine is most closely associated with [[king-john|King John]], who offers it as dowry, and [[blanche-of-castile|Blanche]], whose marriage settlement includes it. Its transfer from the Angevin inheritance to the French crown marks one of the play's many territorial realignments.
