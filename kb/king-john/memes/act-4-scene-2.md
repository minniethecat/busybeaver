## Section 8

[008-001] Pem. When Workemen striue to do better then wel,

[008-002] They do confound their skill in couetousnesse,

[008-003] And oftentimes excusing of a fault,

[008-004] Doth make the fault the worse by th' excuse:

[008-005] As patches set vpon a little breach,

[008-006] Discredite more in hiding of the fault,

[008-007] Then did the fault before it was so patch'd

[008-008] Sal. To this effect, before you were new crown'd

[008-009] We breath'd our Councell: but it pleas'd your Highnes

[008-010] To ouer-beare it, and we are all well pleas'd,

[008-011] Since all, and euery part of what we would

[008-012] Doth make a stand, at what your Highnesse will

[008-013] Ioh. Some reasons of this double Corronation

[008-014] I haue possest you with, and thinke them strong.

[008-015] And more, more strong, then lesser is my feare

[008-016] I shall indue you with: Meane time, but aske

[008-017] What you would haue reform'd, that is not well,

[008-018] And well shall you perceiue, how willingly

[008-019] I will both heare, and grant you your requests

[008-020] Pem. Then I, as one that am the tongue of these

[008-021] To sound the purposes of all their hearts,

[008-022] Both for my selfe, and them: but chiefe of all

[008-023] Your safety: for the which, my selfe and them

[008-024] Bend their best studies, heartily request

[008-025] Th' infranchisement of [[arthur|Arthur]], whose restraint

[008-026] Doth moue the murmuring lips of discontent

[008-027] To breake into this dangerous argument.

[008-028] If what in rest you haue, in right you hold,

[008-029] Why then your feares, which (as they say) attend

[008-030] The steppes of wrong, should moue you to mew vp

[008-031] Your tender kinsman, and to choake his dayes

[008-032] With barbarous ignorance, and deny his youth

[008-033] The rich aduantage of good exercise,

[008-034] That the times enemies may not haue this

[008-035] To grace occasions: let it be our suite,

[008-036] That you haue bid vs aske his libertie,

[008-037] Which for our goods, we do no further aske,

[008-038] Then, whereupon our weale on you depending,

[008-039] Counts it your weale: he haue his liberty.

[008-040] Enter [[hubert|Hubert]].

[008-041] Iohn. Let it be so: I do commit his youth

[008-042] To your direction: Hubert, what newes with you?

[008-043] Pem. This is the man should do the bloody deed:

[008-044] He shew'd his warrant to a friend of mine,

[008-045] The image of a wicked heynous fault

[008-046] Liues in his eye: that close aspect of his,

[008-047] Do shew the mood of a much troubled brest,

[008-048] And I do fearefully beleeue 'tis done,

[008-049] What we so fear'd he had a charge to do

[008-050] Sal. The colour of the King doth come, and go

[008-051] Betweene his purpose and his conscience,

[008-052] Like Heralds 'twixt two dreadfull battailes set:

[008-053] His passion is so ripe, it needs must breake

[008-054] Pem. And when it breakes, I feare will issue thence

[008-055] The foule corruption of a sweet childes death

[008-056] Iohn. We cannot hold mortalities strong hand.

[008-057] Good Lords, although my will to giue, is liuing,

[008-058] The suite which you demand is gone, and dead.

[008-059] He tels vs Arthur is deceas'd to night

[008-060] Sal. Indeed we fear'd his sicknesse was past cure

[008-061] Pem. Indeed we heard how neere his death he was,

[008-062] Before the childe himselfe felt he was sicke:

[008-063] This must be answer'd either heere, or hence

[008-064] Ioh. Why do you bend such solemne browes on me?

[008-065] Thinke you I beare the Sheeres of destiny?

[008-066] Haue I commandement on the pulse of life?

[008-067] Sal. It is apparant foule-play, and 'tis shame

[008-068] That Greatnesse should so grossely offer it;

[008-069] So thriue it in your game, and so farewell

[008-070] Pem. Stay yet (Lord [[salisbury|Salisbury]]) Ile go with thee,

[008-071] And finde th' inheritance of this poore childe,

[008-072] His little kingdome of a forced graue.

[008-073] That blood which ow'd the bredth of all this Ile,

[008-074] Three foot of it doth hold; bad world the while:

[008-075] This must not be thus borne, this will breake out

[008-076] To all our sorrowes, and ere long I doubt.

[008-077] Exeunt.

[008-078] Io. They burn in indignation: I repent:

[008-079] Enter Mes.

[008-080] There is no sure foundation set on blood:

[008-081] No certaine life atchieu'd by others death:

[008-082] A fearefull eye thou hast. Where is that blood,

[008-083] That I haue seene inhabite in those cheekes?

[008-084] So foule a skie, cleeres not without a storme,

[008-085] Poure downe thy weather: how goes all in [[france|France]]?

[008-086] Mes. From France to [[england|England]], neuer such a powre

[008-087] For any forraigne preparation,

[008-088] Was leuied in the body of a land.

[008-089] The Copie of your speede is learn'd by them:

[008-090] For when you should be told they do prepare,

[008-091] The tydings comes, that they are all arriu'd

[008-092] Ioh. Oh where hath our Intelligence bin drunke?

[008-093] Where hath it slept? Where is my Mothers care?

[008-094] That such an Army could be drawne in France,

[008-095] And she not heare of it?

[008-096] Mes. My Liege, her eare

[008-097] Is stopt with dust: the first of Aprill di'de

[008-098] Your noble mother; and as I heare, my Lord,

[008-099] The Lady [[constance|Constance]] in a frenzie di'de

[008-100] Three dayes before: but this from Rumors tongue

[008-101] I idely heard: if true, or false I know not

[008-102] Iohn. With-hold thy speed, dreadfull Occasion:

[008-103] O make a league with me, 'till I haue pleas'd

[008-104] My discontented Peeres. What? Mother dead?

[008-105] How wildely then walkes my Estate in France?

[008-106] Vnder whose conduct came those powres of France,

[008-107] That thou for truth giu'st out are landed heere?

[008-108] Mes. Vnder the Dolphin.

[008-109] Enter Bastard and [[peter-of-pomfret|Peter of Pomfret]].

[008-110] Ioh. Thou hast made me giddy

[008-111] With these ill tydings: Now? What sayes the world

[008-112] To your proceedings? Do not seeke to stuffe

[008-113] My head with more ill newes: for it is full

[008-114] Bast. But if you be a-feard to heare the worst,

[008-115] Then let the worst vn-heard, fall on your head

[008-116] Iohn. Beare with me Cosen, for I was amaz'd

[008-117] Vnder the tide; but now I breath againe

[008-118] Aloft the flood, and can giue audience

[008-119] To any tongue, speake it of what it will

[008-120] Bast. How I haue sped among the Clergy men,

[008-121] The summes I haue collected shall expresse:

[008-122] But as I trauail'd hither through the land,

[008-123] I finde the people strangely fantasied,

[008-124] Possest with rumors, full of idle dreames,

[008-125] Not knowing what they feare, but full of feare.

[008-126] And here's a Prophet that I brought with me

[008-127] From forth the streets of [[pomfret|Pomfret]], whom I found

[008-128] With many hundreds treading on his heeles:

[008-129] To whom he sung in rude harsh sounding rimes,

[008-130] That ere the next Ascension day at noone,

[008-131] Your Highnes should deliuer vp your Crowne

[008-132] Iohn. Thou idle Dreamer, wherefore didst thou so?

[008-133] Pet. Fore-knowing that the truth will fall out so

[008-134] Iohn. Hubert, away with him: imprison him,

[008-135] And on that day at noone, whereon he sayes

[008-136] I shall yeeld vp my Crowne, let him be hang'd

[008-137] Deliuer him to safety, and returne,

[008-138] For I must vse thee. O my gentle Cosen,

[008-139] Hear'st thou the newes abroad, who are arriu'd?

[008-140] Bast. The French (my Lord) mens mouths are ful of it:

[008-141] Besides I met Lord [[bigot|Bigot]], and Lord Salisburie

[008-142] With eyes as red as new enkindled fire,

[008-143] And others more, going to seeke the graue

[008-144] Of Arthur, whom they say is kill'd to night, on your suggestion

[008-145] Iohn. Gentle kinsman, go

[008-146] And thrust thy selfe into their Companies,

[008-147] I haue a way to winne their loues againe:

[008-148] Bring them before me

[008-149] Bast. I will seeke them out

[008-150] Iohn. Nay, but make haste: the better foote before.

[008-151] O, let me haue no subiect enemies,

[008-152] When aduerse Forreyners affright my Townes

[008-153] With dreadfull pompe of stout inuasion.

[008-154] Be Mercurie, set feathers to thy heeles,

[008-155] And flye (like thought) from them, to me againe

[008-156] Bast. The spirit of the time shall teach me speed.

[008-157] Exit

[008-158] Iohn. Spoke like a sprightfull Noble Gentleman.

[008-159] Go after him: for he perhaps shall neede

[008-160] Some Messenger betwixt me, and the Peeres,

[008-161] And be thou hee

[008-162] Mes. With all my heart, my Liege

[008-163] Iohn. My mother dead?

[008-164] Enter Hubert.

[008-165] Hub. My Lord, they say fiue Moones were seene to night:

[008-166] Foure fixed, and the fift did whirle about

[008-167] The other foure, in wondrous motion

[008-168] Ioh. Fiue Moones?

[008-169] Hub. Old men, and Beldames, in the streets

[008-170] Do prophesie vpon it dangerously:

[008-171] Yong Arthurs death is common in their mouths,

[008-172] And when they talke of him, they shake their heads,

[008-173] And whisper one another in the eare.

[008-174] And he that speakes, doth gripe the hearers wrist,

[008-175] Whilst he that heares, makes fearefull action

[008-176] With wrinkled browes, with nods, with rolling eyes.

[008-177] I saw a Smith stand with his hammer (thus)

[008-178] The whilst his Iron did on the Anuile coole,

[008-179] With open mouth swallowing a Taylors newes,

[008-180] Who with his Sheeres, and Measure in his hand,

[008-181] Standing on slippers, which his nimble haste

[008-182] Had falsely thrust vpon contrary feete,

[008-183] Told of a many thousand warlike French,

[008-184] That were embattailed, and rank'd in Kent.

[008-185] Another leane, vnwash'd Artificer,

[008-186] Cuts off his tale, and talkes of Arthurs death

[008-187] Io. Why seek'st thou to possesse me with these feares?

[008-188] Why vrgest thou so oft yong Arthurs death?

[008-189] Thy hand hath murdred him: I had a mighty cause

[008-190] To wish him dead, but thou hadst none to kill him

[008-191] H. No had (my Lord?) why, did you not prouoke me?

[008-192] Iohn. It is the curse of Kings, to be attended

[008-193] By slaues, that take their humors for a warrant,

[008-194] To breake within the bloody house of life,

[008-195] And on the winking of Authoritie

[008-196] To vnderstand a Law; to know the meaning

[008-197] Of dangerous Maiesty, when perchance it frownes

[008-198] More vpon humor, then aduis'd respect

[008-199] Hub. Heere is your hand and Seale for what I did

[008-200] Ioh. Oh, when the last accompt twixt heauen & earth

[008-201] Is to be made, then shall this hand and Seale

[008-202] Witnesse against vs to damnation.

[008-203] How oft the sight of meanes to do ill deeds,

[008-204] Make deeds ill done? Had'st not thou beene by,

[008-205] A fellow by the hand of Nature mark'd,

[008-206] Quoted, and sign'd to do a deede of shame,

[008-207] This murther had not come into my minde.

[008-208] But taking note of thy abhorr'd Aspect,

[008-209] Finding thee fit for bloody villanie:

[008-210] Apt, liable to be employ'd in danger,

[008-211] I faintly broke with thee of Arthurs death:

[008-212] And thou, to be endeered to a King,

[008-213] Made it no conscience to destroy a Prince

[008-214] Hub. My Lord

[008-215] Ioh. Had'st thou but shooke thy head, or made a pause

[008-216] When I spake darkely, what I purposed:

[008-217] Or turn'd an eye of doubt vpon my face;

[008-218] As bid me tell my tale in expresse words:

[008-219] Deepe shame had struck me dumbe, made me break off,

[008-220] And those thy feares, [[power|might]] haue wrought feares in me.

[008-221] But, thou didst vnderstand me by my signes,

[008-222] And didst in signes againe parley with sinne,

[008-223] Yea, without stop, didst let thy heart consent,

[008-224] And consequently, thy rude hand to acte

[008-225] The deed, which both our tongues held vilde to name.

[008-226] Out of my sight, and neuer see me more:

[008-227] My Nobles leaue me, and my State is braued,

[008-228] Euen at my gates, with rankes of forraigne powres;

[008-229] Nay, in the body of this fleshly Land,

[008-230] This kingdome, this Confine of blood, and breathe

[008-231] Hostilitie, and ciuill tumult reignes

[008-232] Betweene my conscience, and my Cosins death

[008-233] Hub. Arme you against your other enemies:

[008-234] Ile make a peace betweene your soule, and you.

[008-235] Yong Arthur is aliue: This hand of mine

[008-236] Is yet a maiden, and an innocent hand.

[008-237] Not painted with the Crimson spots of blood,

[008-238] Within this bosome, neuer entred yet

[008-239] The dreadfull motion of a murderous thought,

[008-240] And you haue slander'd Nature in my forme,

[008-241] Which howsoeuer rude exteriorly,

[008-242] Is yet the couer of a fayrer minde,

[008-243] Then to be butcher of an innocent childe

[008-244] Iohn. Doth Arthur liue? O hast thee to the Peeres,

[008-245] Throw this report on their incensed rage,

[008-246] And make them tame to their obedience.

[008-247] Forgiue the Comment that my passion made

[008-248] Vpon thy feature, for my rage was blinde,

[008-249] And foule immaginarie eyes of blood

[008-250] Presented thee more hideous then thou art.

[008-251] Oh, answer not; but to my Closset bring

[008-252] The angry Lords, with all expedient hast,

[008-253] I coniure thee but slowly: run more fast.

[008-254] Exeunt.

[008-255] Scoena Tertia.

[008-256] Enter Arthur on the walles.
