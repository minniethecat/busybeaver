# Philip the Bastard

Philip Faulconbridge -- known throughout the play as the Bastard -- is Shakespeare's most fully realized illegitimate son. He appears in every act of *[[king-john|King John]]*, speaks more lines than any character except the king, and ends the play with its most famous speech. His role as comic commentator, satiric chorus, and ultimately the moral centre of the play makes him arguably more compelling than the monarch whose name titles the work. He begins as a witty opportunist trading his [[inheritance]] for royal blood, and he ends as the voice of English patriotism -- a patriotism tested and chosen, not passively received.

## Role in the Play

### The Exchange: Land for Honour

The Bastard's first major action is to trade his inheritance -- the Faulconbridge lands -- for the recognition that he is Richard Coeur-de-lion's illegitimate son. When [[queen-elinor|Queen Elinor]] offers him a choice between his brother's modest estate and the identity of a Plantagenet, his response is swift and revealing:

> Madam, and if my brother had my shape
> And I had his, sir Roberts his like him,
> And if my legs were two such riding rods,
> My armes, such eele skins stuft, my face so thin,
> That in mine eare I durst not sticke a rose,
> Lest men should say, looke where three farthings goes,
> And to his shape were heyre to all this land,
> Would I might neuer stirre from off this place,
> I would giue it euery foot to haue this face: (001-217)

This exchange establishes his defining quality: he understands that identity itself is negotiable, that honour is constructed, that social position is a performance. [[king-john|King John]] promptly knights him:

> Kneele thou downe Philip, but rise more great,
> Arise Sir Richard, and Plantagenet (002-004)

The newly-minted Sir Richard Plantagenet responds with characteristic irreverence, punning on his own bastardy: "Madam by chance, but not by truth, what tho; / Something about a little from the right, / In at the window, or else ore the hatch" (002-012).

### The Soliloquy on Knighthood

Left alone after his knighting, the Bastard delivers his first extended soliloquy -- a meditation on social climbing, the superficiality of honour, and his own newly elevated status. He mocks the "worshipfull society" he has entered, with its travellers "talking of the Alpes and Appenines" (002-052), then turns the jest inward:

> For he is but a bastard to the time
> That doth not smoake of obseruation, (002-050)

This self-awareness is the Bastard's hallmark. He is never more legitimate than when he acknowledges his own illegitimacy -- never more honest than when he admits his own capacity for deceit.

### The Angiers Cynic

At the siege of [[angiers|Angiers]], the Bastard functions as the play's diagnostic instrument. While the kings of [[king-philip-ii|France]] and [[king-john|England]] posture diplomatically, he articulates what they cannot say. When the [[citizen-of-angiers|Citizen]] proposes the marriage of Lewis and Blanch, the Bastard's imagery of cannon-fire and bethumping words strips the rhetoric bare:

> He speakes plaine Cannon fire, and smoake, and bounce,
> He giues the bastinado with his tongue:
> Our eares are cudgel'd, not a word of his
> But buffets better then a fist of France: (004-069)

When the kings agree to a joint assault on the city, he notes with gleeful cynicism that their artillery will fire into one another's lines: "O prudent discipline! From North to South: / [[austria|Austria]] and [[france|France]] shoot in each others mouth" (004-018).

### The Commodity Soliloquy

The Bastard's greatest speech -- and the play's thematic keystone -- comes at the end of Act 2, after the French king's conscience has been bribed away by a marriage alliance. He stands alone on stage and anatomises the [[power|force]] he calls "commodity" -- [[commodity|self-interest]] disguised as principle:

> Mad world, mad kings, mad composition: (004-173)

> That smooth-fac'd Gentleman, tickling commoditie,
> Commoditie, the byas of the world,
> The world, who of it selfe is peysed well,
> Made to run euen, vpon euen ground;
> Till this aduantage, this vile drawing byas,
> This sway of motion, this commoditie,
> Makes it take head from all indifferency,
> From all direction, purpose, course, intent. (004-185)

The speech builds to a startling confession -- he rails against commodity only because it has not yet bribed him:

> And why rayle I on this Commoditie?
> But for because he hath not wooed me yet: (004-199)

And then the punch line, delivered with a self-awareness that distinguishes him from every other character in the play:

> Since Kings breake faith vpon commoditie,
> Gaine be my Lord, for I will worship thee. (004-209)

This soliloquy has attracted sustained critical attention. It is the play's diagnostic instrument: it reveals that every oath sworn, every treaty signed, every alliance formed in this play is built upon the shifting sand of [[commodity|self-interest]]. And yet the speaker himself is implicated in the very corruption he diagnoses. The Bastard's genius -- and his modernity -- is that he can see the disease in the body politic while acknowledging it runs in his own veins.

### Arthur's Death and Moral Engagement

As the play darkens, the Bastard's role shifts from witty commentator to moral actor. In Act 4, he discovers [[arthur|Arthur]]'s body and confronts [[hubert|Hubert]], whom he initially suspects of the murder:

> It is a damned, and a bloody worke,
> The gracelesse action of a heauy hand,
> If that it be the worke of any hand (009-061)

He defends [[hubert|Hubert]] from [[salisbury|Salisbury]]'s sword, interrogates him, and ultimately accepts his innocence. The moment marks a turning point: the Bastard who once joked about social climbing now stands as a defender of justice. His earlier detachment has given way to something like conviction.

### The Voice of England

In Act 5, with [[king-john|John]] ailing and the French advancing, the Bastard becomes the de [[truth|fact]]o commander of the English [[power|force]]s. His confrontation with the [[lewis-the-dauphin|Dauphin]] transforms his earlier wit into patriotic defiance:

> No: know the gallant Monarch is in Armes,
> And like an Eagle, o're his ayerie towres,
> To sowsse annoyance that comes neere his Nest (010-144)

He turns upon the rebellious English lords with searing contempt, calling them "bloudy Nero's, ripping vp the wombe / Of your deere [[england|Mother-England]]" (010-149) -- a metaphor of violated maternity that is among the play's most powerful images.

His army half-drowned in the treacherous tidal sands of the [[the-wash|Wash]], his [[king-john|king]] [[death|dying]] of poison, the Bastard nonetheless ends the play not in despair but in resolution. His closing speech is the play's most quoted passage, and for good reason:

> This England neuer did, nor neuer shall
> Lye at the proud foote of a Conqueror,
> But when it first did helpe to wound it selfe.
> Now, these her Princes are come home againe,
> Come the three corners of the world in Armes,
> And we shall shocke them: Naught shall make vs rue,
> If England to it selfe, do rest but true. (011-204)

## Character Analysis

### Legitimacy Inverted

The Bastard's great dramatic irony is that he -- the illegitimate son, the landless knight, the "bastard to the time" -- proves more legitimate in his loyalties than any of the play's legitimate rulers. [[king-john|King John]] murders his nephew to secure a [[crown]] he has no right to hold. [[king-philip-ii|Philip of France]] sells his conscience for territory. The English nobles defect to the invader when their king displeases them. Only the Bastard -- the man who freely chose his [[faith|allegiance]] rather than inheriting it -- remains steadfast.

### Chorus and Character

The Bastard belongs to a line of Shakespearean figures who stand partly inside and partly outside the dramatic action: he has something of Richard III's conspiratorial intimacy with the audience, something of Falstaff's comic irreverence, something of Iago's cynical intelligence. But unlike Iago, his cynicism does not curdle into nihilism. Unlike Falstaff, he is not left behind when the play turns serious. He grows with it.

### The Play's Conscience

Critics have described the Bastard as the play's "conscience" -- but that term does not quite capture his function. He is more accurately the play's intelligence: the character who sees through what others believe, who speaks what others suppress, who acts when others vacillate. His journey from detached irony to committed patriotism is the emotional and moral arc of the play itself.

## Historical Background

The historical Philip Faulconbridge is wholly Shakespeare's invention. The chronicle sources mention no illegitimate son of Richard I who served [[king-john|King John]]. Shakespeare created the character to solve a structural problem: *[[king-john|King John]]* lacks a hero. [[king-john|John]] is too compromised to carry the audience's sympathy; [[arthur|Arthur]] is too passive; the nobles are too fickle. The Bastard provides the play with a character the audience can invest in -- someone who comments on the action with wit, participates in it with energy, and ultimately transcends it with moral vision.

## Performance History

The role of the Bastard has attracted significant actors across centuries of production. In the Victorian era, it was often played as a swaggering, heroic figure -- a proto-patriotic Englishman in whom the audience could take national pride. Mid-twentieth-century productions, responding to two world wars, emphasised the character's cynicism and the ironic gap between his patriotic speeches and the corrupt political world around him. More recent productions have explored the Bastard's complexity as a self-made identity, drawing out the play's modern resonance with questions of [[legitimacy]], self-invention, and the performance of [[faith|loyalty]].
