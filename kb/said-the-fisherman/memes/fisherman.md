# Fisherman

## Overview

The fisherman is the foundational social identity of Saïd, the titular protagonist of Marmaduke Pickthall's *Saïd the Fisherman*. As a livelihood, fishing anchors Saïd's character in the working class of late Ottoman Damascus and the Levantine coast, defining his social status, his relationship to wealth and power, and his resilience through the narrative's reversals of fortune.

## Definition

In the context of the novel, the fisherman is a manual laborer who earns his living from the sea, using nets and boats along the coast. The term carries strong social connotations: a fisherman is "the son of low people" [PN=005-018], occupying the bottom rung of the urban social hierarchy. The profession requires practical knowledge of the sea and its ways, as reflected in the rhetorical question put to Saïd: "Thou that art a fisherman, and knowest the ways of the sea, tell me, What does a mariner when shipwrecked on the coast of his own country?" [PN=009-030]

In Arabic, a fisherman would be called *sayyād* (صياد), though Pickthall uses only the English term throughout the novel.

## In the Narrative

Fishing is Saïd's original trade before the reversals of fortune that propel the plot. The novel opens with Saïd having already abandoned fishing for trade, yet his former identity clings to him. Throughout the narrative, Saïd is repeatedly identified as "the fisherman" — a label used by others and by the narrator alike, and one that Saïd internalizes even as he rises in wealth and station.

When Saïd's fortunes collapse, he considers "returning back to his first trade of a fisherman" [PN=009-035], recognizing that the sea offers a reliable, if humble, livelihood. The fisherman identity recurs as a touchstone: Abdullah is introduced as "his sometime friend and partner, Abdullah the fisherman" [PN=010-010]; even in disguise, Saïd is addressed as "O fisherman" [PN=009-018]; and in his darkest moments, he is still "the sometime fisherman" hugging "himself at the prospect" of recovery [PN=006-002].

Key scenes involving fishing and fishermen include:
- Saïd's partnership with Abdullah the fisherman before the events of the novel [PN=010-010]
- The mariner's pantomime on the ship, trying to embrace the fisherman [PN=013-013]
- Saïd's confinement at Selìm's house, where Selìm "would glance ruefully at the fisherman where he sat cross-legged" [PN=017-002]
- The fat taverner Abu Khalìl's warm regard: "Another person who had conceived a warm liking for the fisherman" [PN=020-008]
- The revelation that Saïd is introduced as "a fisherman, whose name was Saïd" [PN=028-025]

## Cultural Context

In 19th-century Ottoman society, fishing was a low-status occupation associated with poverty and manual labor. The contrast between the fisherman and the merchant class — represented by figures like Abdullah the wealthy usurer — mirrors the broader social tensions of the late Ottoman period, where traditional trades were being disrupted by commercial capitalism and European economic penetration.

The fisherman's knowledge is practical and environmental: knowing the sea, the tides, the habits of fish. This stands in contrast to the book-learning and religious knowledge prized by the urban elite. Yet the novel treats this knowledge with respect, presenting the fisherman's wisdom as authentic and grounded.

The motif also recalls the *Thousand and One Nights* tradition, where fishermen appear as protagonists in tales of fortune and misfortune — a literary lineage that Pickthall consciously invokes.
