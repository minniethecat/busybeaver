# Selìm

## Overview

Selìm is [[Saïd]]'s most constant friend and protector in *Saïd the Fisherman*, a figure of unwavering loyalty in a world of deception. He shelters Saïd during his darkest hours and serves as a moral anchor, demonstrating that faithfulness can survive even the novel's harshest tests. His "faithfulness was suitably rewarded" [PN=007-019], marking him as one of the novel's few unambiguously virtuous characters.

## Biography

Selìm enters the narrative as a friend who takes Saïd into his home during the protagonist's period of destitution. "For months, for years, he led this peaceful kind of life" [PN=017-007] under Selìm's protection. Selìm's own "faithfulness was suitably rewarded" when he received money from Saïd's recovered [[wealth]] [PN=007-019].

The novel presents Selìm as a man of means — he has a household with children and servants — but also of genuine warmth, who "would tell his children and any chance guest the story of his acquaintance with Saïd" [PN=017-002].

## Relationships

Selìm's primary relationship is with [[Saïd]], whom he treats with protective care bordering on possessiveness: Selìm "would glance ruefully at the fisherman where he sat cross-legged" [PN=017-002], and his "loving care would not trust the fisherman out of his sight" [PN=017-007]. This intense guardianship, while born of affection, also borders on confinement — Saïd is effectively a prisoner of kindness.

Selìm is praised as "a good man and a faithful — none like him in all the world" [PN=009-042]. His role as organizer of the [[journey]] — securing donkeys, provisions, and documents — demonstrates practical as well as emotional loyalty.

## Character Traits

Selìm embodies the virtue of faithfulness. His generosity is practical: he provides shelter, food, and protection. His emotional openness — tears in his eyes as he tells Saïd's story [PN=017-002] — contrasts with the guarded, calculating characters who surround the protagonist.

He is also protective to a fault, "fearing a tumult" [PN=017-007] and keeping Saïd sheltered even when confinement chafes.

## In the Narrative

Selìm appears in both parts of the novel as Saïd's protector. His home serves as a refuge and a prison. Key scenes include his storytelling [PN=017-002], his protective vigilance [PN=017-007], his organization of the [[journey]] to the [[sea]] [PN=009-041], and the reward for his faithfulness [PN=007-019].

## Cultural Context

As a Muslim merchant of [[Damascus]], Selìm represents the ideal of Islamic hospitality (*ḍiyāfa*) and brotherhood. His willingness to shelter a destitute friend reflects both personal virtue and cultural expectation. The novel uses Selìm to demonstrate that the values of faithfulness and generosity — so often betrayed in the narrative — can still be found in the world.
