# Judge

## Overview

The judge stands at the intersection of law, power, and narrative in _Saïd the Fisherman_. When Saïd demands "judge between these men and me" [PN=012-014](#pn-012-014), he invokes the oldest function of judicial authority: to hear competing stories and decide which is true. The novel's judges — both the formal _qâdî_ and the informal judgment that characters pass on one another — embody the human need for impartial arbitration in a world of conflicting claims.

## Definition

In Islamic legal tradition, the _qâdî_ (قاضي) is a judge appointed by the ruler (_walî_) to adjudicate disputes according to the _sharî'a_. The qâdî's jurisdiction covered personal status (marriage, divorce, inheritance), commercial disputes, and certain criminal matters. The office required both legal knowledge (_fiqh_) and personal integrity (_'adâla_) — a corrupt judge was considered one of the gravest threats to social order.

The novel uses "judge" in both its formal legal sense and its broader meaning of moral discernment. Characters constantly judge one another — assessing motives, weighing evidence, reaching verdicts — in a world where formal justice is distant and expensive, and informal judgment is immediate and often brutal.

## In the Narrative

The most dramatic invocation of the judge occurs when Saïd, telling his story to a foreign audience, demands:

> "Hear now my story, O khawaja, and judge between these men and me." [PN=012-014](#pn-012-014)

This plea to "judge between" echoes the Quranic language of divine judgment (_yawm ad-dîn_ — the Day of Judgment). Saïd turns his audience into an informal court, his autobiography into legal testimony. The request implies that judgment is not only the prerogative of officials — anyone who hears a story fairly must decide where justice lies.

Ismaìl's judgment is described more clinically: "He (Ismaìl) had judged it false from the first." [PN=009-035](#pn-009-035) This private judgment — one character assessing another's claim — carries no legal force but shapes the course of events nonetheless. In the novel's world, social judgment often precedes and overwhelms legal judgment.

The act of judging extends to practical deliberation. Saïd "strove to judge of the distance" [PN=011-004](#pn-011-004) as he attempted to navigate the dark waters — a reminder that "to judge" originally meant to assess, to estimate, to weigh probabilities before acting. Before one judges others, one must judge the world.

The novel's treatment of judgment thus spans a spectrum: from the cosmic (divine judgment on the Day of Reckoning) through the legal (the qâdî's court) to the practical (judging distance in the dark) and the social (judging character). At every level, to judge is to impose order on uncertainty — and to accept the moral responsibility that comes with that act.

## Cultural Context

The qâdî was a central figure in Ottoman urban life. His court (_mahkama_) was typically located near the main mosque, and his jurisdiction covered both the city and its surrounding district. Qâdîs were appointed from the ranks of the _'ulamâ'_ (religious scholars) and served for limited terms to prevent the formation of local loyalties.

The qâdî's court was known for its accessibility and informality compared to European courts. Plaintiffs could bring cases directly, without lawyers, and the qâdî was expected to reconcile parties when possible, not merely to issue rulings. The novel reflects this accessibility: when Saïd needs justice, he demands a hearing, not a lawyer.

The corruption of judges was a perennial theme in Ottoman satire and complaint literature. Bribes, political pressure, and the complexity of overlapping legal systems (sharî'a courts, consular courts for foreigners, communal courts for religious minorities) made the ideal of impartial judgment elusive. The novel's insistence on "judging between" reflects both the aspiration to justice and the difficulty of achieving it.
