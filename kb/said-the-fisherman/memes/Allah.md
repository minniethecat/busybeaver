# Allah

## Overview

Allah is the unseen but ever-present character in *Saïd the Fisherman*. His name appears on nearly every page — in greetings ("the peace of Allah upon this house" [PN=002-009]), in exclamations ("a fortune, by Allah!" [PN=001-019]), in prayers ("Full of distress he prayed Allah" [PN=001-027]), and in oaths ("He called Allah to witness" [PN=001-034]). The novel's characters inhabit a world saturated with divine presence, where no significant action occurs without reference to the Almighty.

## Definition

In the novel's theological universe, Allah is the sole God of Islam — the Creator, Sustainer, and Judge. His attributes invoked in the text include greatness ("Allah is great and bountiful" [PN=001-004]), mercy ("the mercy of Allah, and His blessings" [PN=002-009]), generosity ("by the grace of Allah" [PN=002-016]), and forgiveness ("Allah pardon!" [PN=002-018]).

Allah's name is woven into the fabric of everyday speech through formulaic phrases. The greeting "the peace of Allah upon this house" [PN=002-009] sacralizes social encounters; "praise be to Allah" [PN=001-016] frames even casual exchanges in devotional language; "Allah pardon thee" [PN=001-032] functions as both rebuke and blessing. This linguistic saturation is not presented as piety but as the natural texture of speech in the novel's world.

## In the Narrative

Allah functions in the narrative as the ultimate guarantor of justice and the ultimate audience for human action. Saïd prays "Allah, if it might be, to take away this torment" [PN=001-027], treating Allah as an intimate interlocutor who can be petitioned directly. When he wants to emphasize his sincerity, he "called Allah to witness" [PN=001-034] — an act that would be meaningless in a world where divine witness was not taken seriously.

The novel uses Allah's name to mark the stakes of moral choices. Hasneh rebukes Saïd with religious language: "Allah is great and bountiful, and I am established upon a sure foundation" [PN=001-004], contrasting her faith-rooted stability with his restless ambition. When characters lie or deceive, the implicit violation is not merely social but theological — a sin against the God whose name they constantly invoke.

Allah's name is also physically present in the world of the novel. Saïd's shop door bears an inscription: "At all points the name of Allah met the eye, cunningly obscured among the arabesques" [PN=002-004]. The divine name is literally written into the architecture of daily life.

## Cultural Context

The centrality of Allah in the novel reflects the Islamic concept of *tawḥīd* — the absolute oneness of God, which is the foundational doctrine of the faith. Every aspect of a Muslim's life, from business transactions to family relations, is ideally conducted with awareness of divine presence (*taqwā*).

The formulaic phrases that punctuate the novel — *as-salāmu ʿalaykum*, *al-ḥamdu lillāh*, *in shāʾ Allāh* — are not merely conventions but performative acts that weave religious consciousness into the texture of speech. In 19th-century Damascus, as in much of the Islamic world today, these phrases were used by Muslims, Christians, and Jews alike, forming a shared linguistic heritage.

The inscription of Allah's name in architectural decoration reflects the Islamic prohibition on figural representation in sacred contexts. Calligraphy — particularly the writing of divine names — became the dominant decorative art form, adorning mosques, homes, and shops.
