# Oath

## Overview

The oath functions as both a casual expression of emotion and a solemn legal instrument in *Saïd the Fisherman*. Characters swear oaths in moments of impatience and rage, but the oath also appears as a formal bond sworn before the Qadi (judge) — carrying religious and legal consequences. The dual register of the oath — profane exclamation and sacred covenant — reflects the deep integration of religious language into everyday life in the novel's Damascene setting.

## Definition

An oath (*yamīn*) in the novel's context is a verbal declaration that invokes Allah as witness to the truth of one's words or the sincerity of one's intentions. In its formal sense, an oath sworn before the Qadi is legally binding: "the oath which I swore before the Qadi" [PN=019-021] carries the weight of Islamic jurisprudence. In its informal register, the oath appears as a spontaneous exclamation of frustration, as when characters act "with an oath of impatience" [PN=002-005].

The oath bridges speech and action — it transforms words into obligations. When Saïd calls "Allah to witness that he had not meant" to cause harm [PN=001-034], he is performing an informal oath, staking his relationship with the divine on the truth of his claim.

## In the Narrative

Oaths punctuate moments of high emotion in the novel. The lawyer who has been pursuing Saïd's case "tore himself away" from a confrontation "with an oath" [PN=020-047], the oath serving as a verbal marker of his decision to disengage from a losing cause. When Saïd becomes the object of public mockery, "applause was turned to oaths and howls of rage" [PN=025-036] — the crowd's oaths representing the collapse of social order into raw hostility.

The most significant oath in the narrative appears in connection with a legal proceeding: an oath "swore before the Qadi" [PN=019-021] concerning a sister, indicating that formal oaths carry evidentiary weight in the novel's legal world. This reflects the historical reality of Ottoman courts, where oral testimony under oath was a primary form of evidence.

Saïd's own speech is punctuated by informal oaths and invocations. His declaration "by Allah!" [PN=001-019] is simultaneously an oath and a statement of wonder, while his prayer that "Allah pardon thee, O Saïd" [PN=001-032] functions as a self-directed oath of regret.

> "With an oath the lawyer tore himself away." [PN=020-047]

## Cultural Context

In Islamic jurisprudence, the oath (*yamīn*) is a serious matter. A false oath (*yamīn ghamūs*) is considered a major sin. The Qadi's court in the Ottoman Empire relied heavily on oaths as evidentiary instruments — in the absence of written documentation, a properly sworn oath could decide a case.

The pervasiveness of oath-language in everyday speech reflects the deep saturation of religious consciousness in traditional Arab society. Phrases like "by Allah" (*wallāhi*) and "praise be to Allah" (*al-ḥamdu lillāh*) are simultaneously oaths, prayers, and conversational fillers. The novel captures this linguistic texture precisely, showing characters who cannot speak of significant matters without invoking the divine.

In the novel's social world, breaking an oath is not merely a legal violation but a religious transgression. The Qadi's court enforces oaths with temporal authority, but the ultimate sanction is divine — a false oath-taker sins against Allah.
