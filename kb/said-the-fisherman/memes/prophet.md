# Prophet

## Overview

The Prophet Muhammad is the supreme religious authority in the world of *Saïd the Fisherman*, invoked by characters as a source of blessing, an arbiter of truth, and a symbol of communal identity. References to the Prophet mark the boundary of Muslim identity and provide the ultimate appeal in disputes and oaths.

## Definition

In Islamic theology, prophets (*anbiyāʾ*) are chosen by God to deliver divine revelation. Muhammad is the final prophet (*khātam al-anbiyāʾ*), and his example (*sunna*) is normative for Muslim conduct. In the novel, the Prophet is invoked in formulaic oaths — "By the Prophet" — and his name carries the weight of ultimate authority.

The novel's characters reference the Prophet casually in everyday speech and gravely in moments of crisis. "By the Prophet" functions as both a mild oath and a solemn appeal, bridging the sacred and the mundane.

## In the Narrative

Prophetic references appear throughout the novel as markers of Muslim identity and moral authority. Characters swear "By the Prophet" to underscore the truth of their claims. The name of Muhammad is invoked as both blessing and curse-warding.

Key appearances:
- "By the Prophet" as a characteristic oath in dialogue
- The faithful rallying under prophetic authority: the community defined by following the Prophet's way
- Characters appeal to the Prophet's example when judging moral questions
- The Prophet as a boundary marker: "children of sin" [PN=022-011] are contrasted with those who follow the Prophet
- Prophetic intercession (*shafāʿa*) as a background hope for the dying

The Prophet Muhammad, though never appearing as a character, is a constant presence in the moral universe of the novel. His authority legitimizes social norms, and his example provides the standard against which behavior is measured.

## Cultural Context

In the Ottoman period, devotion to the Prophet (*al-madīḥ al-nabawī*) was a central feature of popular Islamic practice. Poems in praise of the Prophet (*mawlid*) were recited on festive occasions, and his birthday was widely celebrated. The novel's characters inhabit a world where the Prophet's name is never far from the lips of the faithful.

The Ottoman state itself derived legitimacy from its role as protector of the holy places and servant of the Prophet's legacy. This political dimension of prophetic authority forms a background hum in the novel's treatment of religious identity.
