# Abdullah

## Overview

Abdullah is the betrayer-friend in *Saïd the Fisherman*, introduced as "his sometime friend and partner, Abdullah the fisherman" [PN=010-010]. His transformation from fishing companion to exploitative usurer mirrors the novel's concern with how wealth corrupts personal bonds.

## Biography

Abdullah was once Saïd's fishing partner, sharing the humble life of the Levantine coast. By the time he reappears in the narrative, he has become a usurer and a man of wealth, recognized by Saïd "in dismay and amazement" [PN=010-010].

## Relationships

Abdullah's relationship with Saïd is defined by the contrast between past friendship and present betrayal. Saïd once trusted him "no doubt for a minute the good faith of his more than brother" [PN=001-033]. This trust makes the betrayal all the more devastating.

## Character Traits

Abdullah represents the corruption of friendship by wealth. His usury is presented as morally repugnant, and his betrayal of Saïd compounds this with personal treachery. He is calculating where Saïd is trusting, patient where Saïd is impulsive.

## In the Narrative

Abdullah's appearance marks a turning point. Saïd's recognition of his former friend — "in dismay and amazement" [PN=010-010] — signals the collapse of past certainties. His role as antagonist drives plot toward its crises.

## Cultural Context

As a fisherman turned usurer, Abdullah embodies anxieties about social mobility in Ottoman society. Usury (*ribā*) was forbidden in Islamic law, making his profession morally suspect. His character represents the dark side of the commercial transformation the novel documents.
