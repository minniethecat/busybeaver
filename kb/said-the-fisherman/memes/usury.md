# Usury

## Overview

Usury (_ribâ_) is the dark twin of trade in _Saïd the Fisherman_ — the forbidden practice of profiting from loans that Islamic law condemns and that the novel associates with foreign exploitation and moral decay. When Selìm imagines putting Saïd's treasure to work "in trade and usury" [PN=006-001](#pn-006-001), he invokes a practice that marks the boundary between legitimate commerce and predatory gain.

## Definition

In Islamic law, _ribâ_ (ربا) is any unjustified increase in a loan or exchange transaction — most commonly, interest charged on a monetary loan. The prohibition is absolute and grounded in the Quran: "Allah has permitted trade and forbidden usury" (Q 2:275). The rationale is moral: money should not generate more money without labour or risk, and the lender should share in both the profit and the loss of the venture.

The novel's world is one in which this prohibition is under pressure from European commercial practices. "Frankish" merchants and consuls bring with them an economy based on interest-bearing loans, creating both new opportunities and new forms of dependency.

## In the Narrative

Usury appears first as an economic temptation. Selìm, holding Saïd's treasure, reflects:

> "He would employ in trade and usury, to such advantage that he would soon have doubled or trebled it." [PN=006-001](#pn-006-001)

The pairing of "trade and usury" suggests that for Selìm — practical, worldly Selìm — the two are natural partners. Trade earns profit through exchange; usury earns profit through time. Both increase wealth. But the novel's moral framework quietly distinguishes them: trade is blessed, usury is cursed.

The novel's most explicit critique of usury comes from a character who denounces foreign exploitation:

> "The Frankish consuls, I tell you, grow fat among us by trade and usury?" [PN=019-017](#pn-019-017)

Here usury is linked to colonial economic penetration — Franks (Europeans) enrich themselves at local expense through lending practices that Islamic law forbids. The question mark in the text suggests a rhetorical probing: is this not obvious to all who can see?

The thinness of usury's presence in the novel is itself significant: a practice that was becoming central to the Mediterranean economy is mentioned only in passing, as if the novel's moral world cannot quite accommodate it. Usury is what happens offstage, in the counting-houses of Frankish merchants and the back rooms of consulates, not in the sunlit bazaar where honest trade takes place.

## Cultural Context

The prohibition of _ribâ_ was one of the defining features of Islamic economic life. It did not prevent all forms of credit — partnerships (_mudâraba_), advance sales (_salam_), and other instruments allowed commerce to function — but it did mean that lending at interest was formally banned and often socially stigmatized.

In the nineteenth-century Ottoman Empire, European banks and merchants introduced interest-based finance on a large scale. The Ottoman Bank (founded 1856) and the Public Debt Administration (1881) were Franco-British institutions that lent to the Ottoman state at high interest, creating a form of financial dependency that many Ottomans experienced as a new kind of colonization. The novel's brief but pointed reference to Frankish usury reflects this historical context.
