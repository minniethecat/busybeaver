# Justice

## Overview

Justice in *Saïd the Fisherman* exists in tension between formal institutions and popular action. The novel presents Ottoman legal structures — courts (*mehkemeh*), magistrates (*wali*), and legal procedure — alongside the threat of vigilante justice that erupts when official channels fail. Characters invoke divine justice ("the justice of Allah" [PN=025-041]) as the ultimate arbiter, placing earthly justice within a theological frame.

## Definition

In the novel, justice (*ʿadl*) operates on three levels. First, there is the formal justice of the Ottoman state, administered by officials who are "servant[s] of justice in this province" [PN=020-028]. Second, there is communal justice — the people taking matters into their own hands, "vowing they would do justice on Jurji with their own hands" [PN=021-012]. Third, there is divine justice, the judgment of Allah that stands above and beyond human institutions.

The tension between these levels drives the novel's legal episodes. When the court system is perceived as slow, corrupt, or inaccessible, characters default to direct action. The phrase "they were come to do justice on Jurji the evildoer" [PN=021-015] captures this transformation from legal petition to physical retribution.

## In the Narrative

The novel's key legal episode centers on Jurji, an evildoer against whom the community demands justice. When Saïd appeals to formal authority — "thou that art a servant of justice in this province, I put a case before thee" [PN=020-028] — he invokes the institutional path. But the community's patience with institutions is limited. Men gather "vowing they would do justice on Jurji with their own hands" [PN=021-012], signaling the collapse of trust in official channels.

The threat of vigilante action reflects the real fragility of Ottoman legal order in provincial settings. When the crowd declares "they were come to do justice on Jurji the evildoer and destroy him" [PN=021-015], they appropriate the language of justice for extrajudicial action.

The novel also invokes divine justice as the ultimate standard: "Who dares withstand the justice of Allah? Slay him also" [PN=025-041]. This appeal to a higher court — Allah's judgment — relativizes all human legal proceedings.

> "O my master, thou that art a servant of justice in this province, I put a case before thee." [PN=020-028]

## Cultural Context

Justice in the Ottoman Empire operated through a dual system: the *sharīʿa* courts administered by *qadis* (judges) applying Islamic law, and the *kanun* (secular law) administered by officials of the Sultan. The *wali* (provincial governor) held both executive and judicial authority. For ordinary people, access to justice depended on wealth, connections, and the integrity of local officials — all variables the novel explores.

The Islamic concept of *ʿadl* (justice) is a divine attribute — one of the ninety-nine names of Allah. This makes justice both a legal obligation and a religious duty. The novel's invocation of "the justice of Allah" [PN=025-041] draws on this deep theological resonance. In popular understanding, divine justice corrects the failures of human justice — a theme that runs through Arabic literature from pre-Islamic poetry to the modern novel.

The Ottoman *Tanzimat* reforms of the 19th century sought to rationalize the legal system, creating new courts and codifying laws. The novel's setting in this period of transition is reflected in the gap between formal legal promise and practical delivery.
