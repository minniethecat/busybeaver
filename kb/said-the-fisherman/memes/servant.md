# Servant

## Overview

The servant is a ubiquitous figure in _Saïd the Fisherman_, mediating between the wealthy and the poor, the private and the public, the master's will and the world's resistance. From the "bare-legged servant" who opens the novel to the loyal Selìm who serves as Saïd's moral compass, servants are both background furniture and crucial agents in the narrative.

## Definition

In the Ottoman social order, a servant (_khâdim_) was a person employed in the household of a wealthier patron, performing domestic duties, running errands, and attending to the master's personal needs. Servants could be free labourers, indentured workers, or enslaved persons. Their status was low, but their proximity to power gave them a peculiar form of influence — they saw what others did not see, and their loyalty or disloyalty could determine fortunes.

The novel also draws on the Islamic theological sense of "servant" (_'abd_): every human being is a servant of God. Saïd frequently uses this language — "I, His servant, am much indebted to Him" [PN=001-004](#pn-001-004) — merging the social and religious meanings of servanthood. To be a servant to God is honourable; to be a servant to man is lowly; the novel plays these meanings against each other.

## In the Narrative

The novel opens not with Saïd but with a servant:

> "A bare-legged servant, whose brown heels peeped in and out of his flapping red slippers, preceded the rest of the servants and retainers who went before Saïd the Merchant." [PN=001-001](#pn-001-001)

This bare-legged servant — proud of his new tarbûsh [PN=001-006](#pn-001-006) — is the first figure the reader encounters, establishing the social hierarchy before the protagonist even appears. The servant runs ahead to clear the way, embodying the labour that makes Saïd's grandeur possible.

The most significant servant in the novel is Selìm, introduced as "that upright servant, before whose outspoken criticism Saïd was wont to bow." [PN=004-007](#pn-004-007) Selìm is no mere attendant — he is a moral witness, a man whose integrity commands even his master's deference. His role inverts the usual master-servant hierarchy:

> "Is it while that I have been thy servant it has been in my mind that I was doing thee a service, requiring no wage." [PN=006-014](#pn-006-014)

Selìm's servanthood is freely chosen, a form of devotion rather than economic necessity. His service to Saïd is modelled on the ideal of service to God — unconditional, honest, and oriented toward the master's true good rather than his immediate comfort.

Other servants populate the novel as markers of Saïd's changing fortunes. In wealth, he commands "kinsfolk and servants obsequious about him" [PN=002-002](#pn-002-002); a servant walks before him, crying "Oäh!" to clear his path [PN=007-004](#pn-007-004); servants carry his white parasol [PN=002-011](#pn-002-011) and bring ewers of water [PN=003-001](#pn-003-001). In ruin, he "dismissed the servants, all save Ibrahìm, the doorkeeper" [PN=009-005](#pn-009-005) — the reduction of his household measuring the reduction of his status.

The servants also function as a kind of chorus, whispering and observing:

> "He pictured the servants whispering together in corners." [PN=005-020](#pn-005-020)

Their gossip is the voice of public opinion, the judgment of the lower orders upon the follies of their betters. Servants in the novel know everything and say nothing — until the master falls, at which point their knowledge becomes dangerous.

## Cultural Context

Domestic service was a major form of employment in Ottoman cities. Wealthy households might employ dozens of servants: doorkeepers, cooks, coffee-makers, water-carriers, stable hands, and personal attendants. The relationship between master and servant was governed by a complex etiquette of deference and obligation.

In Islamic ethics, the treatment of servants was a measure of a person's character. The hadith literature repeatedly enjoins kindness to servants: feeding them what one eats, clothing them what one wears, and not overburdening them with work. Saïd's treatment of his servants — particularly his dismissal of them in hard times — is thus a moral index.

The figure of the loyal servant who speaks truth to power (like Selìm) belongs to a long tradition in Arabic and Persian advice literature, where the wise vizier or honest attendant serves as the ruler's conscience. The novel adapts this courtly trope to a mercantile setting, making Selìm the moral centre of Saïd's household.
