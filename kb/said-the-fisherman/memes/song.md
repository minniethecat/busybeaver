# Song

## Overview

Song permeates the soundscape of *Saïd the Fisherman* — from the work-chants of laborers to the love-songs of young women, from the "singsong shouting of the sailors" [PN=012-006] to the "old songs" crooned in solitude [PN=016-003]. The novel uses song as an index of emotional states: joy erupts into melody, grief subsides into crooning, and the rhythms of labor find expression in chant. Song is the novel's least institutionalized and most intimate form of verbal art.

## Description

The songs in the novel span a range of forms and functions:

| Type | Example | PN |
|------|---------|-----|
| Work song | "The singsong shouting of the sailors" — rhythmic labor chant | [PN=012-006] |
| Love song | "Crooning a love-song with her baby brother" — domestic intimacy | [PN=020-010] |
| Lament | "The catch of an evil song" — a remembered hurt that "rankled there" | [PN=005-020] |
| Solitary song | "Would croon old songs to himself where he sat apart" — private comfort | [PN=016-003] |
| Celebratory song | "Called forth a storm of compliment and blessing" — public performance | [PN=019-025] |

The novel's songs are characterized by their rhythmic quality: "Her heart made a song of passing sweetness rhythmic" [PN=003-017]. This rhythmic dimension links song to the body — the heartbeat, the turning of the grindstone, the rise and fall of the sea — embedding music in the physical experience of the narrative world.

## In the Narrative

Song marks emotional thresholds in the novel. It erupts at moments of heightened feeling — "burst out afresh in song" [PN=003-005] — and subsides into private ritual: "would croon old songs to himself where he sat apart" [PN=016-003]. The "singsong shouting of the sailors" [PN=012-006] provides the rhythmic backdrop to the maritime [[journey]], while the love-song crooned with "her baby brother in her arms" [PN=020-010] anchors domestic tenderness.

Song also carries memory: "the catch of an evil song, and rankled there" [PN=005-020] — a remembered melody becomes a wound, the song's refrain replaying a moment of betrayal. The "same song all the world over" [PN=013-017] suggests the universality of work rhythms, connecting Damascene laborers to a global chorus.

## Historical Context

Oral song traditions were central to Levantine culture in the Ottoman period. Work songs (*ʿatābā*), love songs (*mawwāl*), and religious chants (*dhikr*) structured daily life across class and region. The novel's portrayal of song as both public performance and private solace reflects this cultural reality.

Pickthall's attention to the sonic texture of Damascus — the grindstone's rhythm, the sailor's chant, the love-song's melody — represents a literary practice of ethnographic realism, capturing the aural dimension of a world before recorded sound. The novel treats song not as decorative but as constitutive of character and community.
