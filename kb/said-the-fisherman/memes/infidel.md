# Infidel

## Overview

The infidel (*kāfir*) is the defining religious Other in *Saïd the Fisherman*. The term marks the boundary between the Muslim faithful and non-Muslims — particularly Christians (Nazarenes) and Europeans (Franks). Deployed as both a descriptive category and a rhetorical weapon, "infidel" reveals the sectarian tensions simmering beneath the cosmopolitan surface of late Ottoman Damascus.

## Definition

In Islamic theology, *kufr* is disbelief in Allah and rejection of the Prophet Muhammad's message. A *kāfir* is one who commits *kufr*. In the novel, the term is applied broadly to all non-Muslims: "the faithful outnumbered the infidels" [PN=010-006]. Christians are specifically "Nazarenes," while Europeans are "Franks" — both fall under the umbrella of "infidel."

The term carries social weight beyond theology. To be an infidel is to exist outside the community's moral and legal protections. The novel shows both casual usage and politically charged deployment of the term, as when mobs are mobilized against "the heathen" [PN=019-013].

## In the Narrative

Infidel/unbeliever language pervades the novel's sectarian dynamics. The faithful are contrasted with "infidels" [PN=010-006], and Islamic community is defined in opposition to the unbelieving other. Mob rhetoric invokes "the heathen" as a threat demanding response [PN=019-013].

Key infidel-related episodes:
- "The faithful outnumbered the infidels" in one quarter: [PN=010-006]
- The cry against "heathen to triumph over Thy faithful": [PN=019-013]
- The mob's fury against the "Nazarene": [PN=021-012]
- Christians described as "pagans" in inflammatory rhetoric: [PN=018-015]
- The word of Allah: "Slay me the unbelievers wherever ye find them": [PN=025-028]

The novel does not endorse sectarian violence but documents its mechanisms. The language of infidelity is shown to be a powerful mobilizing force that can be deployed and manipulated.

## Cultural Context

In the Ottoman *millet* system, non-Muslims (Christians, Jews) were protected but subordinate communities (*dhimmī*). The Qurʾānic category of *kāfir* coexisted uneasily with this pragmatic pluralism. The novel's late 19th-century setting captures a period when European encroachment and Ottoman reform were straining traditional inter-communal relations.

The term "infidel" also reflected the Ottoman Empire's self-understanding as the defender of Islam against Christian Europe — a framing that became more charged as Ottoman power declined relative to European rivals.
