# Authority

## Overview

Authority in *Saïd the Fisherman* is portrayed as an impersonal and often hostile force. "Those in authority" are figures who control access — they open or close doors, permit or deny movement, and determine whose claims will be heard. Saïd encounters authority most directly in the form of officials who "refused to see him any more" [PN=009-003], transforming his grievance into a closed case through the simple exercise of bureaucratic power.

## Definition

Authority (*sulṭa*) in the novel denotes the legitimate power to command and to exclude. It is vested in persons — "those in authority" [PN=009-003], "certain persons of authority" [PN=025-010] — rather than abstract institutions. Authority manifests through presence: a man "whose mien was so full of authority that Saïd dared not disregard" [PN=014-008] his instructions, illustrating that authority operates as much through personal bearing as through official rank.

The novel distinguishes between rightful and wrongful authority. Selìm declares that "all who bear rightful authority over us are but the servants" [PN=019-013] of the people, implying that authority can be legitimate or usurped. Yet Saïd's experience is primarily of authority as an obstacle — the "slaves of authority" [PN=026-004] who obstruct rather than serve.

## In the Narrative

Saïd's relationship with authority is shaped by his origins as a fisherman. When he falls from wealth, those "in authority refused to see him any more" [PN=009-003], revealing that access to authority depends on social standing. The powerful man Saïd once was — a merchant whose "face was familiar to one in authority" [PN=002-003] — becomes invisible once his wealth is gone.

The most direct encounter with authority occurs when a stranger approaches Saïd with a commanding presence: "his mien was so full of authority that Saïd dared not disregard his order" [PN=014-008]. This moment distills authority to its essence — not a title or uniform, but the capacity to compel obedience through presence alone.

In the crowd scenes, "certain persons of authority were sorting the crowd, sending one here, another there" [PN=025-010], demonstrating how authority operates as a sorting mechanism, classifying and distributing human beings. Saïd, once a beneficiary of this sorting, becomes its victim.

> "Those in authority refused to see him any more, and at the office of the Qadi his case was put off from day to day." [PN=009-003]

## Cultural Context

In the Ottoman Empire, authority was distributed across multiple overlapping systems: the Sultan's imperial administration, local governors (Wâlys), religious courts (Qadi), military commanders, and tax farmers. The novel captures this fragmented landscape, where a petitioner must navigate competing jurisdictions, none of which feels obliged to offer redress.

The concept of "rightful authority" invoked by Selìm [PN=019-013] resonates with Islamic political thought, which traditionally distinguishes between just rule (*ʿadl*) and tyranny (*ẓulm*). A ruler who fails to serve his subjects forfeits his claim to legitimacy. Yet the novel's portrayal of authority is more cynical than idealistic — Saïd rarely encounters the just ruler of theory, finding instead officials who are indifferent when they are not actively hostile.

The "slaves of authority" [PN=026-004] — soldiers and enforcement agents — represent the coercive underside of power. These figures do not exercise authority in their own right but act as instruments of others, meeting resistance with "the marks of blows."
