# Shame

## Overview

Shame is a social emotion that pervades *Saïd the Fisherman*. In the honor-based society of Ottoman Damascus, shame functions as both an internal moral compass and an external mechanism of control. Characters fear shame more than poverty, and the threat of public humiliation drives crucial plot decisions. Saïd's journey is, in part, a navigation of shame — the shame of his low birth as a fisherman's son, the shame of being deceived, and the shame of public exposure.

## Definition

Shame in the novel operates on two levels: *ʿār* (public dishonor) and *khajal* (personal embarrassment). "The sometime fisherman hugged himself at the prospect" [PN=006-002] — Saïd's self-consciousness about his humble origins reflects internalized shame. When Nûr taunts him as "the son of low people, O fisherman, who breakest every law of behavior" [PN=005-018], she deploys shame as a weapon of social degradation.

Shame is the inverse of honor (*sharaf*). Where honor accrues through wealth, lineage, and virtue, shame attaches to poverty, low birth, and moral failure. The novel shows how shame can be inherited, weaponized, and internalized.

## In the Narrative

Shame appears at key moments of social exposure. Saïd's low birth as a fisherman is a recurring source of shame, used against him by antagonists: "thou art the son of low people" [PN=033-018]. The threat of public disgrace — being exposed before the community — looms larger than material loss.

Key episodes involving shame:
- Nûr's shaming of Saïd's origins: "the son of low people" [PN=005-018]
- Saïd's concealed identity as a fisherman, a shame to be hidden [PN=009-035]
- The usurer's shaming tactics: "the mean soul thinks shame that he was once a fisherman" [PN=005-022]
- Public humiliation as a sanction: the mob's fury at those who "dishonour the Faith" [PN=022-009]
- Saïd's fear of exposure and the shame of being seen as foolish

## Cultural Context

In Ottoman Levantine society, shame (*ʿār*) was one of the most powerful social sanctions. A family's honor depended on the behavior of its members, and shame was collective — an individual's disgrace reflected on their entire kinship group. The novel captures this through the intensity with which characters react to threats of public exposure.

The Islamic tradition also emphasizes the concept of *ḥayāʾ* (modesty, bashfulness) as a virtue, complicating the purely negative valence of shame. This tension between modest self-effacement and humiliating disgrace runs through the novel's treatment of the theme.
