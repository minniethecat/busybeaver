# Quran

## Overview

The Qurʾān (*al-Qurʾān*) pervades the narrative world of *Saïd the Fisherman* as both a sacred text and a material presence. Characters swear "by the Quran" [PN=002-031], recite its opening chapter, display it as an object of craftsmanship — "so exquisitely written" [PN=003-002] — and invoke its authority in moments of crisis and confidence. The Qurʾān functions in the novel as the ultimate warrant for truth-claims, the aesthetic standard against which beauty is measured, and the moral framework within which actions are judged.

## Description

As a work, the Qurʾān in the novel is encountered in multiple registers:
- **As recited text**: The Fātiḥa, "or opening chapter of the Quran, running all round under the ceiling" [PN=002-004], appears as architectural calligraphy, integrating sacred text into domestic space
- **As material object**: "a large and precious copy of the Quran, so exquisitely written" [PN=003-002] — the physical book as a work of art and a marker of wealth
- **As oath-formula**: Characters repeatedly swear "By the Quran" [PN=002-031], "By the Holy Quran" [PN=005-004] — the text as the ultimate guarantor of sincerity
- **As recited performance**: A character attempts to "recite to them a passage of the Quran" [PN=013-024], using the text's sonic power

## In the Narrative

The Qurʾān appears at pivotal narrative moments:

| Context | PN Reference | Function |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| Architectural decoration | [PN=002-004] | Sacred text integrated into domestic space |
| Oath of sincerity | [PN=005-004] | "By the Holy Quran; by Allah Most High" |
| Object of beauty | [PN=003-002] | A "large and precious copy" as cultural capital |
| Rhetorical emphasis | [PN=006-027] | "By the Quran, it angers me to see it!" |
| Performance of piety | [PN=013-024] | Recitation as religious display |

The novel's characters use Qurʾānic oaths to authenticate their speech, but the narrative itself ironizes this practice: those who swear most vehemently "By the Quran" are often the least trustworthy. The missionary who "swore by the Quran and accosted everyone in Arabic" [PN=016-009] represents the text's instrumentalization across religious boundaries.

## Historical Context

The Qurʾān occupied a central place in Ottoman literate culture. Manuscript production reached high levels of artistry in the empire, with calligraphers (*ḫaṭṭāṭ*) achieving celebrity status. Qurʾānic inscriptions adorned mosques, homes, and public buildings — the Fātiḥa "running all round under the ceiling" [PN=002-004] was a common decorative practice.

The use of Qurʾānic oaths in daily speech reflected the text's integration into the fabric of social life. Pickthall, as a British Muslim convert and translator of the Qurʾān (his English translation appeared in 1930), brought personal knowledge of the text's cultural significance to his novelistic portrayal.
