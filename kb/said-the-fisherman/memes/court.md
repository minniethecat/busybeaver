# Court

## Overview

The court (*mehkemeh*) is a shadow institution in *Saïd the Fisherman* — an Ottoman judicial body whose authority is acknowledged but whose integrity is suspect. The court's power over property, marriage, and criminal matters makes it a node in the novel's web of intrigue: characters invoke it as a threat, approach it as supplicants, and avoid it when its verdicts would disadvantage them.

## Structure

The court system was headed by a Qadi (*qāḍī*), an Islamic judge appointed by the Ottoman state. Beneath the Qadi were clerks, notaries, and bailiffs who handled the daily administration of justice. The court's proceedings combined Islamic jurisprudence (*fiqh*) with Ottoman statutory law (*ḳānūn*), creating a hybrid legal order.

In the novel's Damascus, the court is linked to the [[Wâly]]'s administration: "the Wâly is needy and loves money" [PN=005-018], suggesting that judicial outcomes could be influenced by wealth and connections. The "Council of Notables, enthroned at the Wâly's right hand, advising the Government" [PN=006-001] indicates the interpenetration of judicial and executive authority.

## Function

The court's responsibilities included:
- Adjudicating property disputes and inheritance
- Registering marriages and divorces
- Hearing criminal complaints
- Validating commercial contracts
- Administering oaths on the [[Quran]]

The court's power to enforce judgments made it a weapon in social conflicts. Nûr's threat to involve the Wâly — "the Wâly is needy and loves money" [PN=005-018] — illustrates how the court could be mobilized against personal enemies.

## In the Narrative

The court appears indirectly but pervasively. The Wâly, as the executive arm of justice, is invoked in moments of crisis. After Saïd's flight, "in his master's adversity, sought the Wâly, the chief of the police" [PN=009-001] — the judicial apparatus pursues him even beyond Damascus.

The "Council of Notables" [PN=006-001] represents the court's integration with the merchant elite, and the novel critiques this fusion of legal authority with class interest. The court's physical space — "a filthy court in sunlight" [PN=001-007] — suggests the gap between its formal dignity and its material reality.

## Historical Context

The Ottoman mehkemeh was a cornerstone of provincial administration. Qadis were appointed from Istanbul and served fixed terms, theoretically insulated from local pressures but practically dependent on local cooperation. The court system underwent significant reform during the Tanzimat period (1839–1876), with the introduction of secular (*niẓāmiyye*) courts alongside traditional sharīʿa courts — a transition that forms the backdrop of the novel's late Ottoman setting.

The characterization of the Wâly as "needy and loves money" [PN=005-018] reflects widespread criticisms of Ottoman judicial corruption in the nineteenth century, a theme common in contemporary literature and reformist discourse.
