# Trade

## Overview

Trade is the engine of social mobility in *Saïd the Fisherman*. Saïd abandons fishing for commerce, seeking to join the merchant class of Damascus. The novel depicts a vibrant commercial world of bazaars, traders, stock-in-trade, and trans-Mediterranean exchange, while also critiquing the moral hazards of commercial ambition.

## Definition

In the novel, trade (*tijāra*) encompasses the buying and selling of goods, from prayer mats to luxury items, in the markets and caravanserais of Damascus. "Stock-in-trade" is the merchant's capital — the goods and inventory that constitute his working wealth [PN=001-030]. Trade is distinguished from usury, which is presented as exploitative: Saïd plans to employ surplus "in trade and usury" [PN=006-001], conflating the two under the rubric of profit-making.

## In the Narrative

Saïd's transition from fisherman to trader is the foundational economic shift of the plot. He acquires goods, negotiates prices, and navigates the social networks of the marketplace. "Many of the traders who sat cross-legged behind their stalls" [PN=007-002] form the backdrop of commercial Damascus.

The novel traces trade across borders: the ship voyage introduces Mediterranean commerce, and Saïd's plans involve moving goods between regions. The stranger who engages Saïd on the ship notes that "the time is favourable for every kind of trade" [PN=009-041], though Saïd himself "carried no merchandise" [PN=012-002] — his true cargo being his hidden wealth.

Key trade-related episodes:
- Saïd's original trading partnership and his shop's "stock-in-trade" [PN=005-004]
- The plan to employ surplus "in trade and usury" [PN=006-001]
- The bazaar scene with traders "cross-legged behind their stalls" [PN=007-002]
- The allure of returning to "his first trade of a fisherman" [PN=009-035]
- Critique of foreign merchants: "grow fat among us by trade and usury" [PN=019-017]

## Cultural Context

Damascus in the late 19th century was a major trading hub, linking the Mediterranean coast with the interior. The bazaar (*sūq*) was the economic and social heart of the city, organized by guilds and trade specializations. The novel captures both the dignity and the precariousness of mercantile life.

European economic penetration — "the Frankish consuls" whose influence challenges local traders [PN=019-017] — forms a background tension. The tension between traditional market ethics and modern commercial practices mirrors the broader Ottoman experience of globalization in this period.
