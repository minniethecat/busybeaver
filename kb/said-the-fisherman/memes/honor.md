# Honor

## Overview

Honor is the central social currency of the world depicted in *Saïd the Fisherman*. Every character's actions — from Saïd's rise as a wealthy merchant to his eventual public exposure — are measured against the code of honor that governs late Ottoman Damascene society. The novel tracks honor not as a fixed possession but as a fragile condition, won and lost through wealth, conduct, and public perception.

## Definition

In the novel, honor (*sharaf*) encompasses both external social standing and internal moral worth. It is closely linked to wealth: "From the height of wealth and honour to which his native shrewdness and industry had raised him" [PN=001-014] establishes the fundamental equation that prosperity brings honor. Conversely, poverty is a condition of dishonor.

Honor has a public dimension — it must be recognized by others. Saïd is "quite overwhelmed by the honour paid to him" [PN=002-012] when he receives public acknowledgment. The "place of honour" [PN=001-012] in a room is a physical manifestation of social rank. Honor can be inherited ("born to wealth and eminence"), earned through achievement, or lost through disgrace.

## In the Narrative

Honor drives Saïd's ambitions and his vulnerabilities. His journey from fisherman to wealthy merchant is a pursuit of honor through wealth. When Hasneh asks "Hast thou not also another wife, who has already borne a son to inherit thy honour?" [PN=001-022], she reveals that honor is treated as a legacy to be passed down.

The novel's antagonists attack Saïd precisely through his honor. Nûr "heaps dishonour upon me" [PN=005-018] with her taunts about his low birth. Public dishonor is the ultimate weapon. Saïd's fear of losing honor — being exposed as a low-born fisherman — drives much of his behavior in Damascus.

Key narrative moments:
- Saïd reflects on his garment as "the root of my honour" [PN=001-032], symbolizing his achieved status
- The Council offers Saïd a "seat of honour" [PN=002-002], marking his social ascent
- Nûr's verbal assault strips away his honor by reminding him and others of his origins [PN=005-018]
- Saïd's desire to wed a woman of status is framed as "honourable" [PN=006-038]

> "From the height of wealth and honour to which his native shrewdness and industry had raised him, he had fallen in a single night to the depth of poverty and distress." [PN=001-014]

## Cultural Context

In 19th-century Ottoman society, honor was a comprehensive social code governing behavior, family reputation, and public standing. For men, honor was tied to wealth, professional standing, and the ability to protect one's dependents. The novel's setting in Damascus, a city with layered social hierarchies, makes honor particularly complex — Saïd must navigate the expectations of merchants, officials, religious authorities, and common people.

Honor in Islamic ethics (*sharaf*) is distinct from the concept of *ʿird* (sexual honor, particularly of women). The novel focuses primarily on the masculine code of *sharaf*, where a man's public reputation is his most valuable asset. The curse "May the fire, the mother of hospitality, be quenched on his hearth" that appears in relation to breaches of hospitality [PN=002-007] reflects how honor and hospitality are intertwined — to fail as a host is to lose honor.
