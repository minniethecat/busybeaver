# Ferideh

## Overview

Ferideh is a significant female presence in *Saïd the Fisherman*, connected to Saïd through romantic attachment and the complications of honor, family obligation, and social expectation in Ottoman Damascus.

## Biography

Ferideh is introduced as a figure of desire and attachment. Her relationship with Saïd unfolds against the backdrop of social conventions that constrain romantic choice — particularly for women in a patriarchal society. The novel traces her role in Saïd's emotional life and her navigation of the limited options available to her.

## Relationships

Ferideh's central relationship is with Saïd. The intensity of their bond is conveyed through the language of passion and possession: "O Saïd, O my beloved, hear me but a minute!" [PN=003-024]. Their relationship is tested by external circumstances including Saïd's reversals of fortune and the schemes of other characters.

## Character Traits

The novel presents Ferideh as emotionally expressive and devoted. Her address to Saïd — "O prince of my soul!" [PN=003-034] — captures the heightened romantic register through which her character is rendered. She is both a source of comfort and a locus of vulnerability for the protagonist.

## In the Narrative

Ferideh appears in emotionally charged scenes. Her plea — "O my beloved, hasten to my chamber" [PN=003-027] — conveys both urgency and intimacy. The novel uses her character to explore the tension between romantic love and social constraint, a theme that runs through the narrative.

## Cultural Context

As an Ottoman Muslim woman of the merchant class, Ferideh navigates a world of limited agency. Her romantic feelings for Saïd must contend with the institutions of arranged marriage, family honor, and the surveillance of female behavior. The novel's portrayal of her reflects both the constraints of her society and the intensity of feeling those constraints could not suppress.
