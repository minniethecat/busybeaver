# Curse

## Overview

Cursing is a prominent speech act in *Saïd the Fisherman*, appearing whenever characters are pushed to emotional extremes. Saïd curses his fate, his enemies, and the day he was born. The novel presents cursing as both a release valve for frustration and a form of verbal power — words that carry weight in a world where speech has moral and sometimes supernatural force.

## Definition

A curse (*laʿna*) in the novel's context is a verbal invocation of harm or damnation upon a person, object, or circumstance. Cursing ranges from casual profanity to solemn imprecation: "the fisherman leapt up and cursed the day he was born" [PN=020-012]. It functions as prayer's dark twin — where prayer petitions Allah for blessing, a curse petitions (or simply wishes) for harm.

The novel distinguishes between curses that invoke divine agency ("May Allah curse...") and curses that are purely expressive. The rhetorical force of cursing is acknowledged even when its supernatural dimension is ambiguous.

## In the Narrative

Cursing punctuates the novel at moments of betrayal, loss, and frustration. Saïd "cursed the day he was born" [PN=020-012] after a reversal of fortune. Nûr's lashing tongue "stung" Saïd with verbal abuse that amounted to cursing [PN=005-018]. The novel's dialogue is rich with Arabic-inflected imprecations and oaths.

Key cursing episodes:
- Saïd's curse at his misfortune: "the fisherman leapt up and cursed the day he was born" [PN=020-012]
- Nûr's torrent of verbal abuse that amounts to a curse: [PN=005-018]
- The righteous man who "did curse some Nazarene" while the mob demands punishment: [PN=022-012]
- Cursing as counterpoint to blessing: Selìm is blessed while enemies are cursed
- The curse of poverty — the fisherman's lot is itself a kind of curse from which Saïd tries to escape

## Cultural Context

In the Arabic-speaking world, cursing carries particular cultural weight. Traditional Arabic curses are elaborate, formulaic, and can invoke ancestors, honor, and divine wrath. The novel reflects this tradition through characters whose speech is peppered with imprecations.

Islamic teaching discourages excessive cursing, viewing it as a form of verbal impurity, yet folk practice often diverged from doctrinal norms. The novel captures this tension: characters curse freely in moments of passion, but righteous figures refrain, marking cursing as a sign of moral weakness or loss of control.
