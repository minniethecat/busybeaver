# Gratitude

## Overview

Gratitude (*shukr*) is the moral cement of the social world depicted in *Saïd the Fisherman*. Every gift, every act of hospitality, every instance of protection creates an obligation of thankfulness that binds the recipient to the giver. The novel's harshest moral condemnation is reserved for ingratitude: "O Saïd, lord of ingratitude" [PN=005-018] is an accusation that cuts deeper than any charge of poverty or low birth.

## Definition

Gratitude in the novel is not a private feeling but a public obligation. It must be expressed and demonstrated — through words ("like expressions of joy and gratitude" [PN=009-038]), through loyalty, and through the memory of benefits received. When Saïd is taken in by the little household, his daughter is described as having been "born years before of gratitude for the gift of a stolen garment" [PN=017-001], illustrating how gratitude can be generative, producing new relationships and obligations.

The Arabic root *shakara* links thankfulness to acknowledgment — to be grateful is to recognize and name the benefit one has received. Its antonym *kufr* means both ingratitude and unbelief, suggesting that to deny a benefit is akin to denying the Benefactor (Allah). This theological depth gives the novel's treatment of gratitude its particular moral weight.

## In the Narrative

The novel's most searing moral language is deployed against ingratitude. When Saïd is accused of being "lord of ingratitude" [PN=005-018], the charge is comprehensive — it denies him not just good character but full humanity. The speaker continues: "I would have served thee with my soul" [PN=005-018], establishing that gratitude was owed for services freely offered and now scorned.

Gratitude appears as a thread connecting events across time. Saïd's daughter owes her very existence to an act of gratitude: she was "born years before of gratitude for the gift of a stolen garment" [PN=017-001]. This striking formulation suggests that gratitude is not merely a response to gifts but a creative force that generates new life and new obligations.

The public expression of gratitude — "expressions of joy and gratitude" [PN=009-038] — serves a social function, reinforcing the bonds between benefactor and beneficiary in view of the community. Gratitude performed is gratitude sealed; private thankfulness is insufficient.

> "O Saïd, lord of ingratitude. I would have served thee with my soul." [PN=005-018]

## Cultural Context

In Arab-Islamic ethics, gratitude to fellow humans (*shukr al-makhlūq*) is inseparable from gratitude to God (*shukr al-khāliq*). A well-known hadith states: "He who does not thank people does not thank Allah." This principle elevates gratitude from a social courtesy to a religious obligation.

The bedouin code of hospitality, which underlies much of the novel's social world, demands that the recipient of generosity acknowledge the debt publicly and enduringly. To forget a benefit is shameful; to deny one is unforgivable. The novel's use of "ingratitude" as the ultimate personal condemnation reflects this cultural logic.

The etymological link between ingratitude (*kufr*) and unbelief in Arabic gives the concept a theological resonance absent from English. When the novel's characters accuse one another of ingratitude, they are simultaneously making a religious judgment — the ungrateful person is spiritually defective.
