# Hasneh

## Overview

Hasneh appears in *Saïd the Fisherman* as a woman whose position in Saïd's life illuminates the novel's treatment of marriage, jealousy, and the domestic politics of a polygamous society. Her presence complicates the narrative's romantic dynamics and reveals the constraints under which female characters operate.

## Biography

Hasneh is introduced in the context of Saïd's domestic arrangements. The question posed to him — "Hast thou not a beautiful woman of thy own people?" [PN=001-022] — references the existing bonds that constrain his romantic choices. Her role in the narrative involves the tensions that arise when multiple women lay claim to a man's affection and resources.

## Relationships

Hasneh's primary relationships are with Saïd and, by implication, with Ferideh as a rival for his attention. The novel explores the emotional landscape of a household where "thou hast not also another wife who loves thee" [PN=001-022] — the presence of multiple women creating a matrix of jealousy, obligation, and competition.

## Character Traits

Hasneh is portrayed through the lens of domestic emotion: jealousy, devotion, resentment. The novel renders her as a product of her circumstances — a woman whose identity is defined by her relationship to a man and whose agency is exercised within the confined space of the household.

## In the Narrative

Hasneh's presence is felt through Saïd's domestic choices and their consequences. The question of "another wife who loves thee" [PN=001-022] establishes the novel's concern with the emotional costs of polygamous marriage. Her character contributes to the novel's broader portrait of women navigating a patriarchal world.

## Cultural Context

As a Muslim wife in Ottoman Damascus, Hasneh's life was shaped by the legal and social frameworks of Islamic marriage. The novel's depiction of polygamy reflects historical practice — Ottoman law permitted up to four wives — while also suggesting the emotional tensions such arrangements produced.
