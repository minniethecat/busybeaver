## Description

Palazzo Pinti is a grand apartment building situated on the Lung' Arno della Zecca, the broad boulevard that runs along the northern bank of the Arno River in Florence (002-164). It is Mrs. Bowen's residence during her two-year sojourn in the city and the setting for the novel's most significant social and domestic scenes.

When Colville arrives for his first Thursday reception, he discovers that "Palazzo Pinti, when you came to it, was rather a grand affair, with a gold-banded porter eating salad in the lodge at the great doorway, and a handsome gate of iron cutting you off from the regions above till you had rung the bell of Mrs. Bowen's apartment, when it swung open of itself, and you mounted" (004-001). The apartment itself is elegantly appointed: at the door, "a man in modified livery received Colville," and "half a dozen other hats and coats on the carved chests that stood at intervals along the wall" marked the entrance hall, along with "some gayer wraps that exhaled a faint, fascinating fragrance on the chilly air" (004-005). Passing through "the yellow Abruzzi blanket portiere," the visitor entered "the brilliant interior" (004-020), where Mrs. Bowen presided over her cosmopolitan circle of expatriates and travellers.

The building's position on the Lung' Arno places it at the center of Florentine life. When Colville finally leaves in early summer, the setting is described in vivid detail: "the Lung' Arno was a glare of light that struck back from the curving line of the buff houses; the river had shrivelled to a rill in its bed; the black cypresses were dim in the tremor of the distant air on the hill-slopes beyond" (044-059).

## Significance in the Novel

Palazzo Pinti functions as the novel's primary social stage — the hub around which the expatriate community's life revolves. Mrs. Bowen holds Thursday receptions there from four until six (002-167), and the palazzo becomes the venue where the central relationships of the novel are forged, tested, and ultimately resolved. It is here that Colville re-enters polite society after years of absence, here that he courts Imogene Graham and gradually falls in love with Mrs. Bowen, and here that the novel's climactic emotional confrontations take place.

The palazzo serves as a domestic sanctuary as well as a social setting. After Colville is injured in a street accident, he is brought to Palazzo Pinti to convalesce, and when he wakes, a nurse informs him that "he was in Palazzo Pinti, whither he had been brought from the scene of his accident" (043-009). This period of enforced intimacy under Mrs. Bowen's roof accelerates the emotional trajectories of the characters. At the novel's conclusion, the palazzo is the site of the wedding feast — "The Rev. Mr. Waters gave away the bride, and then went home to Palazzo Pinti with the party, the single and singularly honoured guest at their wedding feast" (047-069) — and the newly married Colvilles "spent the summer in Palazzo Pinti" before departing for Rome (047-087).

The building thus embodies the expatriate experience that Howells depicts: a grand Old World setting temporarily occupied by Americans whose real lives are being shaped — and reshaped — within its ancient walls. Its beauty and permanence form an ironic backdrop to the transience and emotional turmoil of its temporary inhabitants.

## Key Scenes

### The First Thursday Reception (Chapter 4)

Colville's first visit to Palazzo Pinti establishes both the physical grandeur of the setting and the social dynamics that will drive the novel. The description combines architectural detail with the sensory experience of a man re-entering society after long absence:

> He found that Palazzo Pinti, when you came to it, was rather a grand affair, with a gold-banded porter eating salad in the lodge at the great doorway, and a handsome gate of iron cutting you off from the regions above till you had rung the bell of Mrs. Bowen's apartment, when it swung open of itself, and you mounted. (004-001)

Colville passes through the yellow Abruzzi blanket portiere into the brilliant interior, where "Mrs. Bowen stood in front of the fire in a brown silk of subdued splendour, and with her hands and fan and handkerchief tastefully composed before her" (004-114). This scene introduces the reader to the social world that will occupy the remainder of the novel.

### The Evening Ritual (Chapter 38)

By the later chapters, Palazzo Pinti has become the gravitational center of Colville's existence:

> In these days, when Colville came every night to Palazzo Pinti, he got back the tone he had lost in the past fortnight. He thought that it was the complete immunity from his late pleasures, and the regular and sufficient sleep, which had set him firmly on his feet again, but he did not inquire very closely. (038-113)

The house has become "pleasanter than it had ever been since the veglione" (038-118), and Colville's nightly visits mark the deepening of his attachment to Mrs. Bowen, even as he tells himself he goes there only because he no longer meets Imogene elsewhere.

### The Summer Departure (Chapter 44)

When Colville finally leaves Palazzo Pinti, the surrounding cityscape mirrors the emotional intensity of the moment:

> the spring had advanced to early summer, and the sun was lying hot and bright in the piazzas, and the shade dense and cool in the narrow streets, before he left Palazzo Pinti; the Lung' Arno was a glare of light that struck back from the curving line of the buff houses; the river had shrivelled to a rill in its bed; the black cypresses were dim in the tremor of the distant air on the hill-slopes beyond; the olives seemed to swelter in the sun, and the villa walls to burn whiter and whiter. (044-059)

### The Wedding Feast (Chapter 47)

The novel's resolution is sealed within Palazzo Pinti's walls, as the wedding party returns there after the ceremony:

> The Rev. Mr. Waters gave away the bride, and then went home to Palazzo Pinti with the party, the single and singularly honoured guest at their wedding feast, for which Effie Bowen went with Colville to Giacosa's to order the ices in person. (047-069)

The Colvilles then spend their first summer as a married couple in the palazzo before departing for Rome, bringing the narrative full circle: the place where Colville first re-entered society has become his home (047-087).

## Real-World Counterpart

The Lung' Arno della Zecca (now part of the Lungarno della Zecca Vecchia) is a real street along the northern bank of the Arno River in Florence, running east from the Piazza Piave toward the city center. In the late nineteenth century, this area was home to many of the wealthy Anglo-American expatriates who populate Howells's novel. The palazzi along the Lungarno were — and many remain — grand apartment buildings of the type Howells describes, with imposing facades, iron gates, porters' lodges, and spacious apartments suitable for long-term residents. The specific Palazzo Pinti appears to be a composite or a renamed actual building; Howells himself lived in Florence and knew the expatriate quarter intimately. The name "Pinti" evokes the Borgo Pinti, a street in the Santa Croce district, suggesting Howells may have adapted a real location for fictional purposes. The palazzo's proximity to the Cascine, the Ponte Vecchio, and the Piazza San Marco — all frequently visited by the characters — places it squarely in the geography of Anglophone Florence in the 1880s.
