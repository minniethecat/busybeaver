# Artistic Vocation

## Definition

Artistic vocation in *Indian Summer* denotes the calling to creative work — specifically architecture — that Theodore Colville experiences in his youth, abandons for journalism and politics, and attempts to revive upon his return to Florence at forty-one. The concept encompasses both the intrinsic pull of art as a life purpose and the external pressures that derail or reshape it: economic necessity, romantic disappointment, and the competing claims of American practical life. For Howells, artistic vocation is not a fixed identity but a recurring possibility that shadows the protagonist, offering the promise of meaningful work yet remaining perpetually vulnerable to the distractions of social existence and the erosion of self-discipline. The theme is treated with characteristic Howellsian irony: Colville's architectural ambitions are neither wholly sincere nor wholly fraudulent — they occupy the ambiguous middle ground between genuine passion and self-deception that the novel maps so precisely.

## Context in the Novel

Colville's artistic vocation is introduced in the novel's opening chapter as a recovered memory. Standing on the Ponte Vecchio in a mood of alienation and regret, he finds himself "finding a sort of vindictive satisfaction in the power to ignore the surrounding frippery of landscape and architecture" (001-028). This initial posture of rejection is significant — Colville's relationship to architecture begins in negation, a turning-away that paradoxically confirms the depth of his connection. He cannot be indifferent to the beauty around him; he can only will himself to ignore it.

The narrative then reaches backward to establish the origins of this vocation. Before journalism, before politics, before the ambition of Congress, Colville was "a very young architect, with an inclination toward the literary side of his profession, which made it seem profitable to linger, with his Ruskin in his hand, among the masterpieces of Italian Gothic" (001-109 to 001-111). The detail is precise and telling: his architectural calling was never purely professional but literary and aesthetic, shaped by Ruskin's prose as much as by Gothic vaulting. This literary inflection — the architect as writer, the builder as interpreter — prefigures his later career as a newspaperman and suggests that the vocation was always hybrid, always vulnerable to translation into other forms of expression.

Colville's architectural studies were interrupted first by romantic failure — a broken heart that left him "cowering within himself under the sting of wounded vanity" (001-123) — and then by his brother's practical intervention, inviting him to Des Vaches under the pretext of architectural appreciation but with the real purpose of recruiting him into newspaper work (001-136 to 001-144). The move from Florence to Indiana, from Ruskin to the *Democrat-Republican*, is the novel's central irony of vocation: architecture is displaced not by a rival art but by the antithesis of art — provincial journalism and political ambition. Colville "hated" Des Vaches but stayed, "and as an architect was the last thing any one wanted in Des Vaches since the jail and court-house had been built, he became, half without his willing it, a newspaper man" (001-144 to 001-147).

The return to Europe is explicitly framed as a return to vocation. Six weeks before selling his paper, Colville "would have scoffed at a prophecy of his return to Europe for the resumption of any artistic purpose whatever" (001-154). Yet here he is, in Florence, "with the intention of rubbing up his former studies, and of perhaps getting back to put them in practice at New York ultimately" (001-155 to 001-157). Crucially, Colville feels he must "keep architecture before him as an object, for he had lived long in a community where every one was intensely occupied, and he unconsciously paid to Des Vaches the tribute of feeling that an objectless life was disgraceful to a man" (001-160 to 001-162). Architecture serves as existential justification: it is not simply a profession but the guarantor that his life has direction and meaning.

The architectural project takes concrete form in the plan to write a book on Florence. Walking through the city, "the vague purpose which he had cherished of studying the past life and character of the Florentines in their architecture shaped itself anew," and Colville experiences "that mounting joy in its capabilities which attends the contemplation of any sort of artistic endeavour" (010-153 to 010-156). The language here is the novel's most direct celebration of the artistic impulse: the joy is not in accomplishment but in contemplation of possibility, the "capabilities" of a project not yet begun. Howells deepens this by having Colville reflect that "No people had ever more distinctly left the impress of their whole temper in their architecture, or more sharply distinguished their varying moods from period to period in their palaces and temples" (010-156 to 010-157). Architecture is conceived as the most complete expression of collective identity — a conviction that justifies Colville's own attempt to read Florentine character through its built environment.

Yet this vocational enthusiasm is unstable. Almost immediately, Colville experiences a counter-movement of doubt. Confronted with the depth of Florentine history, he asks himself: "What did he care for the Italians of to-day, or the history of the Florentines as expressed in their architectural monuments? It was the problems of the vast, tumultuous American life, which he had turned his back on, that really concerned him" (011-112 to 011-113). The architectural vocation is here revealed as a form of escape — a retreat from the pressing demands of American modernity into the contemplative pleasures of the European past. The doubt is never resolved; Colville persists in his studies, taking them up "with as much earnestness as he could reasonably expect of himself" (014-098), but the qualifying phrase — "as he could reasonably expect of himself" — betrays the half-heartedness that shadows the entire enterprise.

The novel extends the theme of vocation beyond Colville to include Mr. Waters, the elderly minister who "following the example of Mr. Emerson, renounced his calling for ever" (015-081). Waters's renunciation of the ministry parallels Colville's abandonment of architecture, but with a crucial difference: Waters renounced his calling from intellectual conviction, while Colville drifted away from his through circumstance and distraction. Together, the two figures represent contrasting models of vocational crisis — the principled rejection and the gradual erosion — and the novel refuses to valorise either.

## Key Passages

> At that period he was a very young architect, with an inclination toward the literary side of his profession, which made it seem profitable to linger, with his Ruskin in his hand, among the masterpieces of Italian Gothic, when perhaps he might have been better employed in designing red-roofed many-verandaed, consciously mullioned seaside cottages on the New England coast. (001-109 to 001-111)

This passage establishes the formative character of Colville's architectural vocation: literary, Ruskinian, aesthetically ambitious rather than commercially practical. The contrast between Italian Gothic masterpieces and New England seaside cottages encapsulates the tension between art and profession that runs through the entire treatment of vocation. The Ruskin in his hand is both badge of seriousness and hint of dilettantism — the young architect reads about architecture more than he practises it.

> The vague purpose which he had cherished of studying the past life and character of the Florentines in their architecture shaped itself anew in the half-hour which he gave himself over his coffee; and he turned it over in his mind with that mounting joy in its capabilities which attends the contemplation of any sort of artistic endeavour. No people had ever more distinctly left the impress of their whole temper in their architecture, or more sharply distinguished their varying moods from period to period in their palaces and temples. (010-153 to 010-157)

This passage represents the zenith of Colville's vocational enthusiasm — the moment when the architectural project crystallises from "vague purpose" into specific possibility. The "mounting joy in its capabilities" is a precisely observed description of the artistic temperament: pleasure derived not from the work itself but from the contemplation of what the work might become. The claim that Florentine architecture expresses the "whole temper" of its creators justifies Colville's undertaking as more than antiquarianism — it is the recovery of lived experience through built form.

> What did he care for the Italians of to-day, or the history of the Florentines as expressed in their architectural monuments? It was the problems of the vast, tumultuous American life, which he had turned his back on, that really concerned him. (011-112 to 011-113)

The counter-movement. Colville's architectural vocation is here exposed as potentially escapist — a flight from American reality into European aestheticism. The "vast, tumultuous American life" represents the authentic, if troubling, object of his concern, against which the study of Florentine monuments appears as a kind of dereliction. The passage dramatises the novel's central ambivalence about artistic vocation: is it a genuine calling or a sophisticated form of avoidance?

> He had taken up, with as much earnestness as he could reasonably expect of himself, that notion of studying the architectural expression of Florentine character at the different periods. (014-098)

The qualifying clause — "as much earnestness as he could reasonably expect of himself" — is characteristically Howellsian in its ironic self-awareness. Colville's commitment to his architectural project is measured, moderated, held at a self-conscious distance. The passage suggests that vocation in middle age is inevitably a compromise, undertaken with diminished energy and heightened self-scrutiny.

## Related Concepts

- **Indian Summer (Metaphor)** — Colville's architectural vocation is itself a form of Indian summer: a youthful ambition revived in middle age, glowing with the warmth of remembered passion but lacking the sustained heat of genuine commitment. The architectural project, like Colville's romantic attachments, belongs to the novel's pattern of late returns and deferred fulfilments.
- **Expatriate Identity** — The architectural vocation is inextricable from Colville's expatriate condition. His study of Florentine architecture is made possible by his removal from American life, yet this very removal undermines the vocational seriousness he seeks — he is, after all, a tourist of his own ambition.
- **Youth and Age** — The novel maps artistic vocation onto the life course, contrasting the unselfconscious ardour of the "very young architect" with the self-aware, qualified earnestness of the middle-aged returnee. Vocation changes meaning across the arc of a life.
- **Practical Life vs. Aesthetic Life** — The tension between Des Vaches journalism and Florentine architecture, between American practicality and European art, structures Colville's entire biography and constitutes one of the novel's fundamental thematic oppositions.
