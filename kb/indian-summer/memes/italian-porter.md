## Role

The Italian Porter is a minor character in *Indian Summer* who serves as the night porter at Colville's hotel in Florence. He appears in the novel as a figure of comic dignity, most memorably delivering the line "It is my duty" (010-109) when woken in the small hours of the morning to admit the late-returning Colville. Though he has no name beyond his occupation, he is drawn with Howells's characteristic eye for the humanity and quiet self-interest of minor functionaries, and his appearances frame two of the novel's most significant plot developments.

## Character Analysis

The Italian Porter embodies a gentle comic tension between professional rectitude and petty self-interest. Throughout the novel, his signature response — "It is my duty" (010-109) — is repeatedly associated with actions that also happen to involve charging a new candle to the guest's account. Howells treats this small chicanery with affectionate irony rather than indignation; Colville himself, even in a gloomy mood, "could not help admiring" the porter's sweetness of manner (010-112).

Physically, he is described as "the poor little porter" (010-106, 025-157), a phrase both diminutive and sympathetic, suggesting the pathos of a man roused from sleep at all hours to serve the whims of foreign guests. In a memorably incongruous image, he bows himself out of Colville's room with "a sweet plenitude of politeness" despite appearing "in his shirt sleeves, with only one suspender up" (010-112--010-113). This juxtaposition of formal courtesy with disheveled intimacy crystallizes the character: a man who maintains professional dignity no matter how undignified his circumstances.

When Colville departs for the Carnival veglione in mask and domino, the porter wishes him "a pleasant time in as matter-of-fact fashion as if he were going out to an evening party in his dress coat" (017-132). This imperturbable practicality — treating a masked ball with the same equanimity as an ordinary social call — suggests a wealth of Florentine experience accumulated through countless nights at a hotel door, a man who has seen everything and is surprised by nothing.

On the second occasion Colville returns late from the opera, the porter's small candle-lighting scheme repeats itself "almost mechanical[ly]" (025-158), and Colville's smile of recognition gives him the courage to venture beyond professional formality with the question "You have amused yourself this evening?" (025-161). This small moment of human curiosity — the porter presuming, with the familiarity of a repeated ritual, to take a personal interest in his guest's social life — reveals the quiet humanity beneath the commercial surface.

## Relationships

- **Theodore Colville** — The only guest with whom the porter interacts directly in the novel. Their relationship follows a ritual: Colville returns late, wakes the porter, the porter unlocks the door with sleepy apologies, lights a new candle (always charged to Colville's account), and conducts him upstairs with elaborate courtesy (010-106--010-113). The dynamic is gently comic — Colville is the indulgent guest who sees through the porter's small graft but admires his style; the porter is the amiable functionary who discharges his duty with grace while quietly profiting from it. Their second late-night encounter (025-157--025-161) shows the relationship warming, as the repetition of the ritual becomes a shared joke and the porter dares to ask after Colville's evening.

- **Paolo** — Colville's room-servant, who brings up breakfast and relays messages. It is Paolo who delivers the letter "which he said had been left with the porter an hour before" (023-135), establishing the operational chain: the porter receives items at the hotel door, and Paolo carries them upstairs. The two servants never appear together but function as complementary parts of the hotel's service infrastructure, each occupying a distinct niche in Colville's daily domestic economy.

## Key Scenes

### "It Is My Duty" — The First Late-Night Return (Chapter 10)

Returning from Madame Uccelli's evening party in the early hours, Colville finds the hotel door locked and must wake the porter. The scene is one of the most affectionately comic passages in the novel:

> The great outer door of the hotel was locked, of course, and the poor little porter kept Colville thumping at it some time before he unlocked it, full of sleepy smiles and apologies. "I'm sorry to wake you up," said Colville kindly.
> "It is my duty," said the porter, with amiable heroism. He discharged another duty by lighting a whole new candle, which would be set down to Colville's account, and went before him to his room, up the wide stairs, cold in their white linen path, and on through the crooked corridors haunted by the ghosts of extinct *tables d'hôte*, and full of goblin shadows. He had recovered a noonday suavity by the time he reached Colville's door, and bowed himself out, after lighting the candles within, with a sweet plenitude of politeness, which Colville, even in his gloomy mood, could not help admiring in a man in his shirt sleeves, with only one suspender up. (010-106--010-113)

The porter's "amiable heroism" in facing the small ordeal of being woken, the sly commercial instinct of lighting a fresh candle at the guest's expense, and the incongruous elegance of his bow despite his dishabille combine to produce a miniature masterpiece of comic characterization.

### Carnival Departure (Chapter 17)

When Colville leaves his room in mask and domino for the veglione during Carnival, he encounters only the porter in the darkened corridors:

> the corridors of the hotel were empty, and for the most part dark; there was no one up but the porter, who wished him a pleasant time in as matter-of-fact fashion as if he were going out to an evening party in his dress coat. (017-132)

The porter's unfazed response to a guest in fancy dress encapsulates his professional composure and his long experience of Florentine nightlife in all its forms.

### The Second Late-Night Return (Chapter 25)

Colville once again returns late, this time from the opera, and history repeats itself. But this time the ritual carries the weight of the novel's approaching crisis:

> It was so late when he got back to his hotel that the outer gate was shut, and he had to wake up the poor little porter, as on that night when he returned from Madame Uccelli's. The porter was again equal to his duty, and contrived to light a new candle to show him the way to his room. The repetition, almost mechanical, of this small chicane made Colville smile, and this apparently encouraged the porter to ask, as if he supposed him to have been in society somewhere — "You have amused yourself this evening?" (025-157--025-161)

The porter then informs Colville that a letter has arrived — the note from Mrs. Bowen that will precipitate the novel's climactic confrontation. In this way, the humble Italian porter — barely more than a recurring comic motif — becomes an unwitting instrument in the machinery of the plot, his small routines intersecting with the great currents of the characters' emotional lives.
