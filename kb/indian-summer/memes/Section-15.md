[015-001] He haunted the studios a good deal, and through a retrospective affinity

[015-002] with art, and a human sympathy with the sacrifice which it always

[015-003] involves, he was on friendly terms with sculptors and painters who were

[015-004] not in every case so friendly with one another. More than once he saw

[015-005] the scars of old rivalries, and he might easily have been an adherent of

[015-006] two or three parties. But he tried to keep the freedom of the different

[015-007] camps without taking sides; and he felt the pathos of the case when they

[015-008] all told the same story of the disaster which the taste for bric-à-brac

[015-009] had wrought to the cause of art; how people who came abroad no longer

[015-010] gave orders for statues and pictures, but spent their money on curtains

[015-011] and carpets, old chests and chairs, and pots and pans. There were some

[015-012] among these artists whom he had known twenty years before in [[florence|Florence]],

[015-013] ardent and hopeful beginners; and now the backs of their grey or bald

[015-014] heads, as they talked to him with their faces towards their work, and a

[015-015] pencil or a pinch of clay held thoughtfully between their fingers,

[015-016] appealed to him as if he had remained young and prosperous, and they had

[015-017] gone forward to age and hard work. They were very quaint at times. They

[015-018] talked the American slang of the war days and of the days before the

[015-019] war; without a mastery of Italian, they often used the idioms of that

[015-020] tongue in their English speech. They were dim and vague about the

[015-021] country, with whose affairs they had kept up through the newspapers.

[015-022] Here and there one thought he was going home very soon; others had

[015-023] finally relinquished all thoughts of return. These had, perhaps without

[015-024] knowing it, lost the desire to come back; they cowered before the

[015-025] expensiveness of life in America, and doubted of a future with which,

[015-026] indeed, only the young can hopefully grapple. But in spite of their

[015-027] accumulated years, and the evil times on which they had fallen, [[theodore-colville|Colville]]

[015-028] thought them mostly very happy men, leading simple and innocent lives in

[015-029] a world of the ideal, and rich in the inexhaustible beauty of the city,

[015-030] the sky, the air. They all, whether they were ever going back or not,

[015-031] were fervent Americans, and their ineffaceable nationality marked them,

[015-032] perhaps, all the more strongly for the patches of something alien that

[015-033] overlaid it in places. They knew that he was or had been a newspaper

[015-034] man; but if they secretly cherished the hope that he would bring them to

[015-035] the _dolce lume_ of print, they never betrayed it; and the authorship of

[015-036] his letter about the American artists in Florence, which he printed in

[015-037] the _American Register_ at Paris, was not traced to him for a whole

[015-038] week.

[015-039] Colville was a frequent visitor of [[mr-waters|Mr. Waters]], who had a lodging in

[015-040] [[piazza-san-marco|Piazza San Marco]], of the poverty which can always be decent in Italy. It

[015-041] was bare, but for the books that furnished it; with a table for his

[015-042] writing, on a corner of which he breakfasted, a wide sofa with cushions

[015-043] in coarse white linen that frankly confessed itself a bed by night, and

[015-044] two chairs of plain Italian walnut; but the windows, which had no sun,

[015-045] looked out upon the church and the convent sacred to the old Socinian

[015-046] for the sake of the meek, heroic mystic whom they keep alive in all the

[015-047] glory of his martyrdom. No two minds could well have been further apart

[015-048] than the New England minister and [[girolamo-savonarola|the Florentine monk]], and no two souls

[015-049] nearer together, as Colville recognised with a not irreverent smile.

[015-050] When the old man was not looking up some point of his saint's history in

[015-051] his books, he was taking with the hopefulness of youth and the patience

[015-052] of age a lesson in colloquial Italian from his landlady's daughter,

[015-053] which he pronounced with a scholarly scrupulosity and a sincere atonic

[015-054] Massachusetts accent. He practised the language wherever he could,

[015-055] especially at the trattoria where he dined, and where he made occasions

[015-056] to detain the waiter in conversation. They humoured him, out of their

[015-057] national good-heartedness and sympathy, and they did what they could to

[015-058] realise a strange American dish for him on Sundays--a combination of

[015-059] stockfish and potatoes boiled, and then fried together in small cakes.

[015-060] They revered him as a foreign gentleman of saintly amiability and

[015-061] incomprehensible preferences; and he was held in equal regard at the

[015-062] next green-grocer's where he spent every morning five centessimi for a

[015-063] bunch of radishes and ten for a little pat of butter to eat with his

[015-064] bread and coffee; he could not yet accustom himself to mere bread and

[015-065] coffee for breakfast, though he conformed as completely as he could to

[015-066] the Italian way of living. He respected the abstemiousness of the race;

[015-067] he held that it came from a spirituality of nature to which the North

[015-068] was still strange, with all its conscience and sense of individual

[015-069] accountability. He contended that he never suffered in his small

[015-070] dealings with these people from the dishonesty which most of his

[015-071] countrymen complained of; and he praised their unfailing gentleness of

[015-072] manner; this could arise only from goodness of heart, which was perhaps

[015-073] the best kind of goodness after all.

[015-074] None of these humble acquaintance of his could well have accounted for

[015-075] the impression they all had that he was some sort of ecclesiastic. They

[015-076] could never have understood--nor, for that matter, could any one have

[015-077] understood through European tradition--the sort of sacerdotal office

[015-078] that Mr. Waters had filled so long in the little deeply book-clubbed New

[015-079] England village where he had outlived most of his flock, till one day he

[015-080] rose in the midst of the surviving dyspeptics and consumptives and,

[015-081] following the example of Mr. Emerson, renounced his calling for ever. By

[015-082] that time even the pale Unitarianism thinning out into paler doubt was

[015-083] no longer tenable with him. He confessed that while he felt the Divine

[015-084] goodness more and more, he believed that it was a mistake to preach any

[015-085] specific creed or doctrine, and he begged them to release him from their

[015-086] service. A young man came to fill his place in their pulpit, but he kept

[015-087] his place in their hearts. They raised a subscription of seventeen

[015-088] hundred dollars and thirty-five cents; another being submitted to the

[015-089] new button manufacturer, who had founded his industry in the village, he

[015-090] promptly rounded it out to three thousand, and Mr. Waters came to

[015-091] Florence. His people parted with him in terms of regret as delicate as

[015-092] they were awkward, and their love followed him. He corresponded

[015-093] regularly with two or three ladies, and his letters were sometimes read

[015-094] from his pulpit.

[015-095] Colville took the Piazza San Marco in on his way to [[palazzo-pinti|Palazzo Pinti]] on the

[015-096] morning when he had made up his mind to go there, and he stood at the

[015-097] window looking out with the old man, when some more maskers passed

[015-098] through the place--two young fellows in old Florentine dress, with a

[015-099] third habited as a nun.

[015-100] "Ah," said the old man gently, "I wish they hadn't introduced the nun!

[015-101] But I suppose they can't help signalising their escape from the

[015-102] domination of the Church on all occasions. It's a natural reaction. It

[015-103] will all come right in time."

[015-104] "You preach the true American gospel," said Colville.

[015-105] "Of course; there is no other gospel. That is the gospel."

[015-106] "Do you suppose that Savonarola would think it had all come out right,"

[015-107] asked Colville, a little maliciously, "if he could look from the window

[015-108] with us here and see the wicked old Carnival, that he tried so hard to

[015-109] kill four hundred years ago, still alive? And kicking?" he added, in

[015-110] cognisance of the caper of one of the maskers.

[015-111] "Oh yes; why not? By this time he knows that his puritanism was all a

[015-112] mistake, unless as a thing for the moment only. I should rather like to

[015-113] have Savonarola here with us; he would find these costumes familiar;

[015-114] they are of his time. I shall make a point of seeing all I can of the

[015-115] Carnival, as part of my study of Savonarola, if nothing else."

[015-116] "I'm afraid you'll have to give yourself limitations," said Colville, as

[015-117] one of the maskers threw his arm round the mock-nun's neck. But the old

[015-118] man did not see this, and Colville did not feel it necessary to explain

[015-119] himself.

[015-120] The maskers had passed out of the piazza, now, and "Have you seen our

[015-121] friends at Palazzo Pinti lately?" said Mr. Waters.

[015-122] "Not very," said Colville. "I was just on my way there."

[015-123] "I wish you would make them my compliments. Such a beautiful young

[015-124] creature."

[015-125] "Yes," said Colville; "she is certainly a beautiful girl."

[015-126] "I meant [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]]," returned the old man quietly.

[015-127] "Oh, I thought you meant [[imogene-graham|Miss Graham]]. Mrs. Bowen is my contemporary, and

[015-128] so I didn't think of her when you said young. I should have called her

[015-129] pretty rather than beautiful."

[015-130] "No; she's beautiful. The young girl is good-looking--I don't deny that;

[015-131] but she is very crude yet."

[015-132] Colville laughed. "Crude in looks? I should have said Miss Graham was

[015-133] rather crude in mind, though I'm not sure I wouldn't have stopped at

[015-134] saying _young_."

[015-135] "No," mildly persisted the old man; "she couldn't be crude in mind

[015-136] without being crude in looks."

[015-137] "You mean," pursued Colville, smiling, but not wholly satisfied, "that

[015-138] she hasn't a lovely nature?"

[015-139] "You never can know what sort of nature a young girl has. Her nature

[015-140] depends so much upon that of the man whose fate she shares."

[015-141] "The woman is what the man makes her? That is convenient for the woman,

[015-142] and relieves her of all [[social-duty|responsibility]]."

[015-143] "The man is what the woman makes him, too, but not so much so. The man

[015-144] was cast into a deep sleep, you know----"

[015-145] "And the woman was what he dreamed her. I wish she were."

[015-146] "In most cases she is," said Mr. Waters.

[015-147] They did not pursue the matter. The truth that floated in the old

[015-148] minister's words pleased Colville by its vagueness, and flattered the

[015-149] man in him by its implication of the man's superiority. He wanted to say

[015-150] that if Mrs. Bowen were what [[mr-bowen|the late Mr. Bowen]] had dreamed her, then

[015-151] the late Mr. Bowen, when cast into his deep sleep, must have had Lina

[015-152] Ridgely in his eye. But this seemed to be personalising the fantasy

[015-153] unwarrantably, and pushing it too far. For like reason he forbore to say

[015-154] that if Mr. Waters's theory were correct, it would be better to begin

[015-155] with some one whom nobody else had dreamed before; then you could be

[015-156] sure at least of not having a wife to somebody else's mind rather than

[015-157] your own. Once on his way to Palazzo Pinti, he stopped, arrested by a

[015-158] thought that had not occurred to him before in relation to what Mr.

[015-159] Waters had been saying, and then pushed on with the sense of security

[015-160] which is the compensation the possession of the initiative brings to our

[015-161] sex along with many responsibilities. In the enjoyment of this, no man

[015-162] stops to consider the other side, which must wait his initiative,

[015-163] however they mean to meet it.

[015-164] In the Por San Maria Colville found masks and dominoes filling the shop

[015-165] windows and dangling from the doors. A devil in red and a clown in white

[015-166] crossed the way in front of him from an intersecting street; several

[015-167] children in pretty masquerading dresses flashed in and out among the

[015-168] crowd. He hurried to the [[lungarno|Lung' Arno]], and reached the palace where Mrs.

[015-169] Bowen lived, with these holiday sights fresh in his mind. Imogene turned

[015-170] to meet him at the door of the apartment, running from the window where

[015-171] she had left [[effie-bowen|Effie Bowen]] still gazing.

[015-172] "We saw you coming," she said gaily, without waiting to exchange formal

[015-173] greetings. "We didn't know at first but it might be somebody else

[015-174] disguised as you. We've been watching the maskers go by. Isn't it

[015-175] exciting?"
