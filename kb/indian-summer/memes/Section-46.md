[046-001] "Yes, thank you. Perhaps I will take it in place of the coffee. They

[046-002] forgot to offer us any ice at the _table d'hôte_ this evening."

[046-003] "This is rather luxurious for us," said [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]]. "It's a compromise

[046-004] with Effie. She wanted me to take her to Giacosa's this afternoon."

[046-005] "I _thought_ you would come," whispered the child to [[theodore-colville|Colville]].

[046-006] Her mother made a little face of mock surprise at her. "Don't give

[046-007] yourself away, Effie."

[046-008] "Why, let us go to Giacosa's too," said Colville, taking the ice. "We

[046-009] shall be the only foreigners there, and we shall not even feel ourselves

[046-010] foreign. It's astonishing how the hot weather has dispersed the

[046-011] tourists. I didn't see a Baedeker on the whole way up here, and I walked

[046-012] down Via Tornabuoni across through Porta Rosso and the Piazza della

[046-013] Signoria and the Uffizzi. You've no idea how comfortable and home-like

[046-014] it was--all the statues loafing about in their shirt sleeves, and the

[046-015] objects of interest stretching and yawning round, and having a good rest

[046-016] after their winter's work."

[046-017] Effie understood Colville's way of talking well enough to enjoy this;

[046-018] her mother did not laugh.

[046-019] "Walked?" she asked.

[046-020] "Certainly. Why not?"

[046-021] "You are getting well again. You'll soon be gone too."

[046-022] "I've _got_ well. But as to being gone, there's no hurry. I rather think

[046-023] I shall wait now to see how long you stay."

[046-024] "We may keep you all summer," said Mrs. Bowen, dropping her eyelids

[046-025] indifferently.

[046-026] "Oh, very well. All summer it is, then. [[mr-waters|Mr. Waters]] is going to stay, and

[046-027] he is such a very cool old gentleman that I don't think one need fear

[046-028] the wildest antics of the mercury where he is."

[046-029] When Colville had finished his ice, Mrs. Bowen led the way to the

[046-030] salotto; and they all sat down by the window there and watched the

[046-031] sunset die on [[san-miniato|San Miniato]]. The bronze copy of Michelangelo's David, in

[046-032] the Piazzale below the church, blackened in perfect relief against the

[046-033] pink sky and then faded against the grey while they talked. They were so

[046-034] domestic that Colville realised with difficulty that this was an image

[046-035] of what might be rather than what really was; the very ease with which

[046-036] he could apparently close his hand upon the happiness within his grasp

[046-037] unnerved him. The talk strayed hither and thither, and went and came

[046-038] aimlessly. A sound of singing floated in from the kitchen, and Effie

[046-039] eagerly asked her mother if she might go and see Maddalena. Maddalena's

[046-040] mother had come to see her, and she was from the mountains.

[046-041] "Yes, go," said Mrs. Bowen; "but don't stay too long."

[046-042] "Oh, I will be back in time," said the child, and Colville remembered

[046-043] that he had proposed going to Giacosa's.

[046-044] "Yes; don't forget." He had forgotten it himself.

[046-045] "Maddalena is the cook," explained Mrs. Bowen. "She sings ballads to

[046-046] Effie that she learned from her mother, and I suppose Effie wants to

[046-047] hear them at first hand."

[046-048] "Oh yes," said Colville dreamily.

[046-049] They were alone now, and each little silence seemed freighted with a

[046-050] meaning deeper than speech.

[046-051] "Have you seen Mr. Waters to-day?" asked Mrs. Bowen, after one of these

[046-052] lapses.

[046-053] "Yes; he came this afternoon."

[046-054] "He is a very strange old man. I should think he would be lonely here."

[046-055] "He seems not to be. He says he finds company in the history of the

[046-056] place. And his satisfaction at having got out of Haddam East Village is

[046-057] perennial."

[046-058] "But he will want to go back there before he dies."

[046-059] "I don't know. He thinks not. He's a strange old man, as you say. He has

[046-060] the art of putting all sorts of ideas into people's heads. Do you know

[046-061] what we talked about this afternoon?"

[046-062] "No, I don't," murmured Mrs. Bowen.

[046-063] "[[About]] you. And he encouraged me to believe--imagine--that I might speak

[046-064] to you--ask--tell you that--I loved you, Lina." He leaned forward and

[046-065] took one of the hands that lay in her lap. It trembled with a violence

[046-066] inconceivable in relation to the perfect quiet of her attitude. But she

[046-067] did not try to take it away. "Could you--do you love me?"

[046-068] "Yes," she whispered; but here she sprang up and slipped from his hold

[046-069] altogether, as with an inarticulate cry of rapture he released her hand

[046-070] to take her in his arms.

[046-071] He followed her a pace or two. "And you will--will be my wife?" he

[046-072] pursued eagerly.

[046-073] "Never!" she answered, and now Colville stopped short, while a cold

[046-074] bewilderment bathed him from head to foot. It must be some sort of jest,

[046-075] though he could not tell where the humour was, and he could not treat it

[046-076] otherwise than seriously.

[046-077] "Lina, I have loved you from the first moment that I saw you this

[046-078] winter, and Heaven knows how long before!"

[046-079] "Yes; I know that."

[046-080] "And every moment."

[046-081] "Oh, I know that too."

[046-082] "Even if I had no sort of hope that you cared for me, I loved you so

[046-083] much that I must tell you before we parted--"

[046-084] "I expected that--I intended it."

[046-085] "You intended it! and you do love me! And yet you won't--Ah, I don't

[046-086] understand!"

[046-087] "How could _you_ understand? I love you--I blush and burn for shame to

[046-088] think that I love you. But I will never marry you; I can at least help

[046-089] doing that, and I can still keep some little trace of self-respect. How

[046-090] you must really despise me, to think of anything else, after all that

[046-091] has happened! Did you suppose that I was merely waiting till that poor

[046-092] girl's back was turned, as you were? Oh, how can you be yourself, and

[046-093] still be yourself? Yes, Jenny Wheelwright was right. You are too much of

[046-094] a mixture, Theodore Colville"--her calling him so showed how often she

[046-095] had thought of him so--"too much for her, too much for [[imogene-graham|Imogene]], too much

[046-096] for me; too much for any woman except some wretched creature who enjoys

[046-097] being trampled on and dragged through the dust, as you have dragged me."

[046-098] "_I_ dragged _you_ through the dust? There hasn't been a moment in the

[046-099] past six months when I wouldn't have rolled myself in it to please you."

[046-100] "Oh, I knew that well enough! And do you think that was flattering to

[046-101] me?"

[046-102] "That has nothing to do with it. I only know that I love you, and that I

[046-103] couldn't help wishing to show it even when I wouldn't acknowledge it to

[046-104] myself. That is all. And now when I am free to speak, and you own that

[046-105] you love me, you won't--I give it up!" he cried desperately. But in the

[046-106] next breath he implored, "_Why_ do you drive me from you, Lina?"

[046-107] "Because you have humiliated me too much." She was perfectly steady, but

[046-108] he knew her so well that in the twilight he knew what bitterness there

[046-109] must be in the smile which she must be keeping on her lips. "I was here

[046-110] in the place of her mother, her best friend, and you made me treat her

[046-111] like an enemy. You made me betray her and cast her off."

[046-112] "I?"

[046-113] "Yes, you! I knew from the very first that you did not really care for

[046-114] her, that you were playing with yourself, as you were playing with her,

[046-115] and I ought to have warned her."

[046-116] "It appears to me you did warn her," said Colville, with some resentful

[046-117] return of courage.

[046-118] "I tried," she said simply, "and it made it worse. It made it worse

[046-119] because I knew that I was acting for my own sake more than hers, because

[046-120] I wasn't--disinterested." There was something in this explanation,

[046-121] serious, tragic, as it was to Mrs. Bowen, which made Colville laugh. She

[046-122] might have had some perception of its effect to him, or it may have been

[046-123] merely from a hysterical helplessness, but she laughed too a little.

[046-124] "But why," he gathered courage to ask, "do you still dwell upon that?

[046-125] Mr. Waters told me that [[mr-morton|Mr. Morton]]--that there was--"

[046-126] "He is mistaken. He offered himself, and she refused him. He told me."

[046-127] "Oh!"

[046-128] "Do you think she would do otherwise, with you lying here between life

[046-129] and death? No: you can have no hope from that."

[046-130] Colville, in fact, had none. This blow crushed and dispersed him. He had

[046-131] not strength enough to feel resentment against Mr. Waters for misleading

[046-132] him with this _ignis fatuus_.

[046-133] "No one warned him, and it came to that," said Mrs. Bowen. "It was of a

[046-134] piece with the whole affair. I was weak in that too."

[046-135] Colville did not attempt to reply on this point. He feebly reverted to

[046-136] the inquiry regarding himself, and was far enough from mirth in resuming

[046-137] it.

[046-138] "I couldn't imagine," he said, "that you cared anything for me when you

[046-139] warned another against me. If I could--"

[046-140] "You put me in a false position from the beginning. I ought to have

[046-141] sympathised with her and helped her instead of making the poor child

[046-142] feel that somehow I hated her. I couldn't even put her on guard against

[046-143] herself, though I knew all along that she didn't really care for you,

[046-144] but was just in love with her own fancy for you, Even after you were

[046-145] engaged I ought to have broken it off; I ought to have been frank with

[046-146] her; it was my [[social-duty|duty]]; but I couldn't without feeling that I was acting

[046-147] for myself too, and I would not submit to that degradation. No! I would

[046-148] rather have died. I dare say you don't understand. How could you? You

[046-149] are a man, and the kind of man who couldn't. At every point you made me

[046-150] violate every principle that was dear to me. I loathed myself for caring

[046-151] for a man who was in love with me when he was engaged to another. Don't

[046-152] think it was gratifying to me. It was detestable; and yet I did let you

[046-153] see that I cared for you. Yes, I even _tried_ to make you care for

[046-154] me--falsely, cruelly, treacherously."

[046-155] "You didn't have to try very hard," said Colville, with a sort of cold

[046-156] resignation to his fate.

[046-157] "Oh no; you were quite ready for any hint. I could have told her for her

[046-158] own sake that she didn't love you, but that would have been for my sake

[046-159] too; and I would have told you if I hadn't cared for you and known how

[046-160] you cared for me. I've saved at least the consciousness of this from the

[046-161] wreck."

[046-162] "I don't think it's a great treasure," said Colville. "I wish that you

[046-163] had saved the consciousness of having been frank even to your own

[046-164] advantage."

[046-165] "Do you dare to reproach me, Theodore Colville? But perhaps I've

[046-166] deserved this too."

[046-167] "No, Lina, you certainly don't deserve it, if it's unkindness, from me.

[046-168] I won't afflict you with my presence: but will you listen to me before I

[046-169] go?"

[046-170] She sank into a chair in sign of assent. He also sat down. He had a dim

[046-171] impression that he could talk better if he took her hand, but he did not

[046-172] venture to ask for it. He contented himself with fixing his eyes upon as

[046-173] much of her face as he could make out in the dusk, a pale blur in a

[046-174] vague outline of dark.

[046-175] "I want to assure you, Lina--Lina, my love, my dearest, as I shall call

[046-176] you for the first and last time!--that I _do_ understand everything, as

[046-177] delicately and fully as you could wish, all that you have expressed, and

[046-178] all that you have left unsaid. I understand how high and pure your

[046-179] ideals of duty are, and how heroically, angelically, you have struggled

[046-180] to fulfil them, broken and borne down by my clumsy and stupid

[046-181] selfishness from the start. I want you to believe, my dearest love--you

[046-182] must forgive me!--that if I didn't see everything at the time, I do see

[046-183] it now, and that I prize the love you kept from me far more than any

[046-184] love you could have given me to the loss of your self-respect. It isn't

[046-185] logic--it sounds more like nonsense, I am afraid--but you know what I

[046-186] mean by it. You are more perfect, more lovely to me, than any being in

[046-187] the world, and I accept whatever fate you choose for me. I would not win

[046-188] you against your will if I could. You are sacred to me. If you say we

[046-189] must part, I know that you speak from a finer discernment than mine, and

[046-190] I submit. I will try to console myself with the thought of your love, if

[046-191] I may not have you. Yes, I submit."
