# Romantic Disillusionment

## Definition

Romantic disillusionment in *Indian Summer* is the psychological and emotional condition produced when the ideals, expectations, and intensities of youthful love collide with the realities of time, rejection, and self-knowledge. It is not a single event but a process — one that can stretch across decades, shaping a character's subsequent choices, self-conception, and capacity for intimacy. For Theodore Colville, the novel's protagonist, romantic disillusionment is the originating wound of his adult life: a failed love affair in Florence at the age of twenty-five that leaves him with "a broken heart" and establishes the emotional pattern — irony, self-protection, retreat from vulnerability — that governs his behaviour through middle age and into his return to the same city seventeen years later.

The novel distinguishes between two kinds of disillusionment. The first is the disillusionment of the young, which is sharp, humiliating, and formative — what Mr. Waters calls the "disappointment that I met with early in life" (022-038). The second is the disillusionment of the middle-aged, which comes when one recognises that the attempt to recover or repeat youthful romance is itself an illusion, "the same cruelly egotistic dream" (045-030) of restoring the past through the love of a younger person. Romantic disillusionment is thus both the wound and, paradoxically, the cure: only by accepting the loss of romantic illusion can Colville arrive at the mature love the novel finally affirms.

## Context in the Novel

The novel's first chapter establishes romantic disillusionment as Colville's defining biographical fact. After breaking "his life sharp off from the course in which it had been set for many years" (001-035 to 001-036) as a newspaper editor in Des Vaches, Indiana, Colville returns to Italy, where he had spent his youth as an architectural student and aspiring writer. The narrative voice, in its characteristic blend of sympathy and irony, recounts the story of his earlier Florentine sojourn: "In Venice he met the young girl whom he followed to Florence. His love did not prosper; when she went away she left him in possession of that treasure to a man of his temperament, a broken heart" (001-117 to 001-120). The ironic framing of the broken heart as a "treasure" suited to Colville's "temperament" signals the novel's dual perspective on romantic suffering: it is both genuinely painful and, in its youthful self-absorption, faintly comic.

Colville's disillusionment operates on two temporal planes throughout the novel. In the past, it is the wound inflicted by the "girl — I am ashamed to call her a girl — [who] trifled with the priceless treasure" (031-165 to 031-166) of his affections, as Mrs. Bowen later describes it. In the present, it is the pattern of behaviour that the wound has produced — the ironic self-deprecation, the fear of commitment, the attraction to younger women that disguises itself as paternal gallantry. When Colville finds himself drawn to Imogene Graham, a girl young enough to be his daughter, the novel frames this as an unconscious attempt to undo the original disillusionment: to prove, through a new romance, that the old one can be transcended.

The novel's climactic chapters dismantle this project. In conversation with Mr. Waters, Colville arrives at the novel's most explicit statement of the self-deception underlying his pursuit of Imogene: "I dreamed, too, of recovering and restoring my own lost and broken past in the love of a young soul, and it was in essence the same cruelly egotistic dream" (045-028 to 045-030). The disillusionment that began with the rejection of twenty-five-year-old Colville by a young woman in Venice is completed by forty-one-year-old Colville's recognition that his attempt to reverse that rejection has been an act of self-deception. The "broken past" cannot be restored; it can only be accepted. And it is this acceptance — painful, humiliating, and finally liberating — that makes possible Colville's turn toward Lina Bowen, a woman of his own generation who shares his knowledge of loss and who has herself been "faithful to a cruel disappointment" (031-164 to 031-165).

The novel's treatment of romantic disillusionment thus follows a therapeutic arc: the wound of youthful rejection produces a pattern of emotional evasion; the pattern is exposed and dismantled through the failure of the Imogene Graham affair; and the exposure makes possible a love grounded not in illusion but in shared history, mutual recognition, and the willingness to accept happiness on terms that acknowledge imperfection. Mr. Waters's aphorism captures this trajectory with precision: "The young suffer terribly. But they recover. Afterward we don't suffer so much, but we don't recover" (045-044 to 045-045). Romantic disillusionment, for the middle-aged, is not something one gets over but something one learns to inhabit — and, if one is fortunate, to share.

## Key Passages

> He visited every part of the peninsula, but he gave the greater part of his time to North Italy, and in Venice he met the young girl whom he followed to Florence. His love did not prosper; when she went away she left him in possession of that treasure to a man of his temperament, a broken heart. From that time his vague dreams began to lift, and to let him live in the clear light of common day; but he was still lingering at Florence, ignorant of the good which had befallen him, and cowering within himself under the sting of wounded vanity. (001-117 to 001-123)

The novel's retrospective account of Colville's original romantic injury exemplifies the narrative voice's characteristic fusion of sympathy and irony. The broken heart is called a "treasure" — a word that acknowledges its genuine value to Colville's sense of himself while also suggesting the self-dramatising quality of youthful romantic suffering. The "vague dreams" that "began to lift" mark the onset of disillusionment as a form of clarity: the "clear light of common day" is what replaces romantic fantasy. Yet Colville, "ignorant of the good which had befallen him," cannot yet recognise disillusionment as anything other than loss. The final phrase — "cowering within himself under the sting of wounded vanity" — reframes the broken heart as wounded vanity, collapsing the distance between romantic tragedy and egoistic injury. This retrospective framing governs the novel's entire treatment of the theme: what feels like love's catastrophe is also, from a sufficient distance, the ego's tantrum.

> He then remembered that Lina Ridgely in many fine little ways had shown a kindness, almost a compassion, for him, as for one whose unconsciousness a hopeless doom impended over. He perceived that she had always seemed to like him — a thing that had not occurred to him in the stupid absorption of his passion for the other — and fragments of proof that she had probably defended and advocated him occurred to him, and inspired a vain and retrospective gratitude. (003-128 to 003-135)

This passage enacts the novel's most important structural insight about romantic disillusionment: it is not simply that one love fails, but that the failure blinds one to the possibility of another. The "stupid absorption of his passion" for one woman prevented Colville from perceiving Lina Ridgely's kindness and affection — the very qualities that, seventeen years later, will form the foundation of their marriage. The phrase "vain and retrospective gratitude" is devastating in its compression: the gratitude is "vain" because it cannot alter the past, and "retrospective" because it arrives too late to be acted upon. Yet the structure of the novel itself is an argument that it is not, in fact, too late. Colville's retrospective recognition of what he missed the first time becomes, in the novel's second half, the basis for a prospective choice — a love that is not blind but clear-sighted, grounded in the disillusioned knowledge of what was lost.

> "I sometimes feel," continued the old man, "that I made a mistake in yielding to a disappointment that I met with early in life, and in not permitting myself the chance of retrieval. I have missed a beautiful and consoling experience in my devotion to a barren regret." (022-037 to 022-040)

Mr. Waters, the elderly New England clergyman who functions as the novel's moral centre, delivers this confession as both warning and prophecy. His language is precise and devastating: "yielding to a disappointment" reframes passive suffering as a form of active consent — he chose his regret, or at least chose not to resist it. The phrase "barren regret" is the novel's definitive description of disillusionment untransformed: a regret that produces nothing, sustains nothing, and offers no consolation. The alternative he names — "a beautiful and consoling experience" — is marriage, the domestic intimacy he denied himself. The passage serves as a thematic hinge: it diagnoses Colville's condition before Colville himself can recognise it, and it offers a vision of what lies on the other side of disillusionment if one has the courage to "permit oneself the chance of retrieval."

> "I'm afraid I was flattered by an illusion of hers that ought to have pained and alarmed me, and the rest followed inevitably, though I was always just on the point of escaping the consequences of my weakness — my wickedness." (045-007 to 045-010)

Colville's confession to Mr. Waters — in which he finally names his behaviour toward Imogene Graham as weakness and wickedness — represents the terminal point of his romantic disillusionment. The "illusion" to which he refers is Imogene's perception of him as a romantic figure, a wounded hero worthy of rescue. That he was "flattered" by this illusion, rather than "pained and alarmed" by it, is his self-indictment: he allowed a young woman's romantic fantasy to govern his behaviour because it gratified his middle-aged vanity. The word "inevitably" denies him the comfort of accident or circumstance — once he permitted the illusion, the "rest followed" with a logic he should have foreseen. And yet the confession itself is also the turning point: naming the illusion is the act that disarms it, and Colville's subsequent turn toward Lina Bowen is made possible by this moment of humiliating clarity.

## Related Concepts

- **Indian Summer (Metaphor)** — The novel's title metaphor is structurally inseparable from romantic disillusionment: the Indian summer of middle-aged love is what becomes possible only after the frost of youthful disappointment has been absorbed. The love between Colville and Lina Bowen is not a recovery of lost youth but a new warmth that follows the cold season of regret.
- **Youth and Age** — Romantic disillusionment is distributed asymmetrically across the life course: the young suffer intensely but recover; the middle-aged suffer mutedly but sustain permanent damage. Colville's double position — wounded by a youthful disappointment, now inflicting a disappointment on a young woman — links these two registers of the theme.
- **Expatriate Identity** — Florence is not merely the novel's setting but the geographical container of Colville's romantic disillusionment. His return to the city where he was first rejected — to the same streets, the same galleries, the same Cascine — makes the gap between past and present inescapable, and the possibility of retrieval geographically specific.
- **Social Duty** — The novel presents romantic disillusionment as having social consequences that extend beyond the individual. Colville's self-absorption damages not only himself but Imogene Graham and, by extension, the social circle of the Anglo-American expatriate community in Florence. The recovery from disillusionment is equally social: it involves the restoration of proper relations, the fulfilment of obligations, and the acceptance of one's place in a community of shared norms.
