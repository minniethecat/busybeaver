[036-001] [[imogene-graham|Imogene]] still refused to smile. "No; I shall give it up. I don't think

[036-002] it's well, going so much without [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]]. People will begin to talk."

[036-003] "Talk?"

[036-004] "Yes; they will begin to say that I had better stay with her a little

[036-005] more, if she isn't well."

[036-006] "Why, isn't Mrs. Bowen well?" asked [[theodore-colville|Colville]], with trepidation.

[036-007] "No; she's miserable. Haven't you noticed?"

[036-008] "She sees me so seldom now. I thought it was only her headaches----"

[036-009] "It's much more than that. She seems to be failing every way. The doctor

[036-010] has told her she ought to get away from [[florence|Florence]]." Colville could not

[036-011] speak; Imogene went on. "She's always delicate, you know. And I feel

[036-012] that all that's keeping her here now is the news from home that I--we're

[036-013] waiting for."

[036-014] Colville got up. "This is ghastly! She mustn't do it!"

[036-015] "How can you help her doing it? If she thinks anything is right, she

[036-016] can't help doing it. Who could?"

[036-017] Colville thought to himself that he could have said; but he was silent.

[036-018] At the moment he was not equal to so much joke or so much truth; and

[036-019] Imogene went on--

[036-020] "She'd be all the more strenuous about it if it were disagreeable, and

[036-021] rather than accept any relief from _me_ she would die."

[036-022] "Is she--unkind to you?" faltered Colville.

[036-023] "She is only _too_ kind. You can feel that she's determined to be

[036-024] so--that she's said she will have nothing to reproach herself with, and

[036-025] she won't. You don't suppose Mrs. Bowen would be unkind to any one she

[036-026] disliked?"

[036-027] "Ah, I didn't know," sighed Colville.

[036-028] "The more she disliked them, the better she would use them. It's because

[036-029] our engagement is so distasteful to her that she's determined to feel

[036-030] that she did nothing to oppose it."

[036-031] "But how can you tell that it's distasteful, then?"

[036-032] "She lets you feel it by--not saying anything about it."

[036-033] "I can't see how--"

[036-034] "She never speaks of you. I don't believe she ever mentions your name.

[036-035] She asks me about the places where I've been, and about the

[036-036] people--every one but you. It's very uncomfortable."

[036-037] "Yes," said Colville, "it's uncomfortable."

[036-038] "And if I allude to letters from home, she merely presses her lips

[036-039] together. It's perfectly wretched."

[036-040] "I see. It's I whom she dislikes, and I would do anything to please her.

[036-041] She must know that," mused Colville aloud. "Imogene!" he exclaimed, with

[036-042] a sudden inspiration. "Why shouldn't I go away?"

[036-043] "Go away?" she palpitated. "What should I do?"

[036-044] The colours faded from his brilliant proposal. "Oh, I only meant till

[036-045] something was settled--determined--concluded; till this terrible

[036-046] suspense was over." He added hopelessly, "But nothing can be done!"

[036-047] "I proposed," said Imogene, "that we should all go away. I suggested Via

[036-048] Reggio--the doctor said she ought to have sea air--or Venice; but she

[036-049] wouldn't hear of it. No; we must wait."

[036-050] "Yes, we must wait," repeated Colville hollowly. "Then nothing can be

[036-051] done?"

[036-052] "Why, haven't you said it?"

[036-053] "Oh yes--yes. I can't go away, and you can't. But couldn't we do

[036-054] something--get up something?"

[036-055] "I don't know what you mean."

[036-056] "I mean, couldn't we--amuse her somehow? help her to take her mind off

[036-057] herself?"

[036-058] Imogene stared at him rather a long time. Then, as if she had satisfied

[036-059] herself in her own mind, she shook her head. "She wouldn't submit to

[036-060] it."

[036-061] "No; she seems to take everything amiss that I do," said Colville.

[036-062] "She has no right to do that," cried Imogene. "I'm sure that you're

[036-063] always considering her, and proposing to do things for her. I won't let

[036-064] you humble yourself, as if you had wronged her."

[036-065] "Oh, I don't call it humbling. I--I should only be too happy if I could

[036-066] do _anything_ that was agreeable to her."

[036-067] "Very well, I will tell her," said the girl haughtily. "Shall you object

[036-068] to my joining you in your amusements, whatever they are? I assure you I

[036-069] will be very unobtrusive."

[036-070] "I don't understand all this," replied Colville. "Who has proposed to

[036-071] exclude you? Why did you tell me anything about Mrs. Bowen if you didn't

[036-072] want me to say or do something? I supposed you did; but I'll withdraw

[036-073] the offensive proposition, whatever it was."

[036-074] "There was nothing offensive. But if you pity her so much, why can't you

[036-075] pity me a little?"

[036-076] "I didn't know anything was the matter with you. I thought you were

[036-077] enjoying yourself----"

[036-078] "Enjoying? Keeping you up at dances till you drop asleep whenever you

[036-079] sit down? And then coming home and talking to a person who won't mention

[036-080] your name! Do you call that enjoying? I can't speak of you to any one;

[036-081] and no one speaks to me----"

[036-082] "If you like, I will talk to you on the subject," Colville essayed, in

[036-083] dreary jest.

[036-084] "Oh, don't joke about it! This perpetual joking, I believe it's that

[036-085] that's wearing me out. When I come to you for a little comfort in

[036-086] circumstances that drive me almost distracted, you want to amuse Mrs.

[036-087] Bowen, and when I ask to be allowed to share in the amusement, you laugh

[036-088] at me! If you don't understand it all, I'm sure _I_ don't."

[036-089] "Imogene!"

[036-090] "No! It's very strange. There's only one explanation. You don't care for

[036-091] me."

[036-092] "Not care for you!" cried Colville, thinking of his sufferings in the

[036-093] past fortnight.

[036-094] "And I would have made any--_any_ sacrifice for you. At least I wouldn't

[036-095] have made you show yourself a mean and grudging person if you had come

[036-096] to me for a little sympathy."

[036-097] "O poor child!" he cried, and his heart ached with the sense that she

[036-098] really was nothing but an unhappy child. "I do sympathise with you, and

[036-099] I see how hard it is for you to manage with Mrs. Bowen's dislike for me.

[036-100] But you mustn't think of it. I dare say it will be different; I've no

[036-101] doubt we can get her to look at me in some brighter light. I--" He did

[036-102] not know what he should urge next; but he goaded his invention, and was

[036-103] able to declare that if they loved each other they needed not regard any

[036-104] one else. This flight, when accomplished, did not strike him as very

[036-105] original effect, and it was with a dull surprise that he saw it sufficed

[036-106] for her.

[036-107] "No; no one!" she exclaimed, accepting the platitude as if it were now

[036-108] uttered for the first time. She dried her eyes and smiled. "I will tell

[036-109] Mrs. Bowen how you feel and what you've said, and I know she will

[036-110] appreciate your generosity."

[036-111] "Yes," said Colville pensively; "there's nothing I won't _propose_ doing

[036-112] for people."

[036-113] She suddenly clung to him, and would not let him go. "Oh, what is the

[036-114] matter?" she moaned afresh. "I show out the worst that is in me, and

[036-115] only the worst. Do you think I shall always be so narrow-minded with

[036-116] you? I thought I loved you enough to be magnanimous. _You_ are. It

[036-117] seemed to me that our lives together would be grand and large; and here

[036-118] I am, grovelling in the lowest selfishness! I am worrying and scolding

[036-119] you because you wish to please some one that has been as good as my own

[036-120] mother to me. Do you call that noble?"

[036-121] Colville did not venture any reply to a demand evidently addressed to

[036-122] her own conscience.

[036-123] But when she asked if he really thought he had better go away, he said,

[036-124] "Oh no; that was a mistake."

[036-125] "Because, if you do, you shall--to punish me."

[036-126] "My dearest girl, why should I wish to punish you?"

[036-127] "Because I've been low and mean. Now I want you to do something for Mrs.

[036-128] Bowen--something to amuse her; to show that we appreciate her. And I

[036-129] don't want you to sympathise with me at all. When I ask for your

[036-130] sympathy, it's a sign that I don't deserve it."

[036-131] "Is that so?"

[036-132] "Oh, be serious with me. I mean it. And I want to beg your pardon for

[036-133] something."

[036-134] "Yes; what's that?"

[036-135] "Can't you guess?"

[036-136] "No."

[036-137] "You needn't have your lapels silk-lined. You needn't wear

[036-138] _boutonnières_."

[036-139] "Oh, but I've had the coat changed."

[036-140] "No matter! Change it back! It isn't for me to make you over. I must

[036-141] make myself over. It's my right, it's my sacred privilege to conform to

[036-142] you in every way, and I humble myself in the dust for having forgotten

[036-143] it at the very start. Oh, _do_ you think I can ever be worthy of you? I

[036-144] _will_ try; indeed I will! I shall not wear my light dresses another

[036-145] time! From this out, I shall dress more in keeping with you. I boasted

[036-146] that I should live to comfort and console you, to recompense you for the

[036-147] past, and what have I been doing? Wearying and degrading you!"

[036-148] "Oh no," pleaded Colville. "I am very comfortable. I don't need any

[036-149] compensation for the past. I need--sleep. I'm going to bed tonight at

[036-150] eight o'clock, and I am going to sleep twenty-four hours. Then I shall

[036-151] be fresh for [[mrs-fleming|Mrs. Fleming]]'s ball."

[036-152] "I'm not going," said Imogene briefly.

[036-153] "Oh yes, you are. I'll come round to-morrow evening and see."

[036-154] "No. There are to be no more parties."

[036-155] "Why?"

[036-156] "I can't endure them."

[036-157] She was looking at him and talking at him, but she seemed far aloof in

[036-158] the abstraction of a sublime regret; she seemed puzzled, bewildered at

[036-159] herself.

[036-160] Colville got away. He felt the pathos of the confusion and question to

[036-161] which he left her, but he felt himself powerless against it. There was

[036-162] but one solution to it all, and that was impossible. He could only

[036-163] grieve over her trouble, and wait; grieve for the irrevocable loss which

[036-164] made her trouble remote and impersonal to him, and submit.

[036-165] XVIII

[036-166] The young clergyman whom Colville saw talking to Imogene on his first

[036-167] evening at Mrs. Bowen's had come back from Rome, where he had been

[036-168] spending a month or two, and they began to meet at [[palazzo-pinti|Palazzo Pinti]] again.

[036-169] If they got on well enough together, they did not get on very far. The

[036-170] suave house-priest manners of [[mr-morton|the young clergyman]] offended Colville; he

[036-171] could hardly keep from sneering at his taste in art and books, which in

[036-172] fact was rather conventional; and no doubt Mr. Morton had his own

[036-173] reserves, under which he was perfectly civil, and only too deferential

[036-174] to Colville, as to an older man. Since his return, Mrs. Bowen had come

[036-175] back to her _salon_. She looked haggard; but she did what she could to

[036-176] look otherwise. She was always polite to Colville, and she was politely

[036-177] cordial with the clergyman. Sometimes Colville saw her driving out with

[036-178] him and Effie; they appeared to make excursions, and he had an

[036-179] impression, very obscure, that Mrs. Bowen lent the young clergyman

[036-180] money; that he was a superstition of hers, and she a patron of his; he

[036-181] must have been ten years younger than she; not more than twenty-five.

[036-182] The first Sunday after his return, Colville walked home with [[mr-waters|Mr. Waters]]

[036-183] from hearing a sermon of Mr. Morton's, which they agreed was rather well

[036-184] judged, and simply and fitly expressed.

[036-185] "And he spoke with the authority of the priest," said the old minister.

[036-186] "His Church alone of all the Protestant Churches has preserved that to

[036-187] its ministers. Sometimes I have thought it was a great thing."

[036-188] "Not always?" asked Colville, with a smile.

[036-189] "These things are matters of mood rather than conviction with me,"

[036-190] returned Mr. Waters. "Once they affected me very deeply; but now I shall

[036-191] so soon know all about it that they don't move me. But at times I think

[036-192] that if I were to live my life over again, I would prefer to be of some

[036-193] formal, some inflexibly ritualised, religion. At solemnities---weddings

[036-194] and funerals--I have been impressed with the advantage of the Anglican

[036-195] rite: it is the Church speaking to and for humanity--or seems so," he

[036-196] added, with cheerful indifference. "Something in its favour," he

[036-197] continued, after a while, "is the influence that every ritualised faith

[036-198] has with women. If they apprehend those mysteries more subtly than we,

[036-199] such a preference of theirs must mean a good deal. Yes; the other

[036-200] Protestant systems are men's systems. Women must have form. They don't

[036-201] care for freedom."
