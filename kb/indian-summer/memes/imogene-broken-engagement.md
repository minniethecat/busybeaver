## Summary

The broken engagement between Theodore Colville and Imogene Graham is the central emotional event of *Indian Summer*. What begins as an informal understanding — solemnized in Mrs. Bowen's salotto when Colville takes Imogene's hand and declares "if she will let me stay with her, we are not going to part again" (030-005-s1) — unravels across the novel's final chapters. The next day Imogene insists on the binding nature of their commitment: "I _wish_ to be bound, if he is" (032-158-s1), though Colville had offered to leave her free. The engagement is already shadowed by doubt. At Fiesole, when Imogene learns her mother is coming to Florence, she presses Colville for finality: "Then we are promised to each other, whatever happens" (042-099-s1), and he assents. The catastrophe comes not through a direct quarrel but through accident and revelation. Colville is injured when the carriage goes over a wall at Fiesole (042-181-s2), and in the fortnight of his convalescence Imogene discovers the truth she has been suppressing. Her mother, Mrs. Graham, arrives to deliver the definitive word: "She had found out that she did not love you" (043-114-s1). Colville, lying in his sickbed at Palazzo Pinti, releases her: "She is free," said Colville, and with the words a mighty load rolled from his heart (043-143-s1).

## Context

The engagement between Colville, a forty-one-year-old American newspaperman, and Imogene, a twenty-year-old girl from Buffalo, was always precarious. From its inception in Section 30, it bore the marks of Imogene's youthful idealism and Colville's own romantic weakness. Mrs. Bowen, Imogene's chaperone, opposed the match from the outset, warning Imogene that there was "too much difference, every way" (021-028-s1). But Imogene's defiance — "I think I am the one to go away, if any one" (026-025-s1) — crystallized in the wake of the Carnival veglione and overrode her guardian's caution. Colville himself was never entirely convinced of the engagement's wisdom. From the first, he hedged: Mrs. Bowen put the question directly, "Shall you wish to consider it an engagement?" (030-047-s1), but the arrangement remained provisional. Mrs. Bowen insisted she "must leave you perfectly free" (030-061-s1), and Colville's posture of chivalrous self-abnegation only deepened the predicament. The engagement was an informal understanding rather than a formal betrothal, kept from Florentine society and even from Imogene's family pending her mother's approval. The secret weighed on all three parties, and the arrival of Mrs. Graham's telegram at Fiesole brought the latent crisis to a head.

> "Then we are promised to each other, whatever happens," she said. "Yes." (042-099-s1)

## Participants

- **Theodore Colville** — The forty-one-year-old protagonist, who enters the engagement with uncertainty and leaves it with unconcealed relief. His conduct throughout the dissolution is marked by the same half-ashamed honesty that characterizes his entire Florentine sojourn (043-143-s1).
- **Imogene Graham** — The twenty-year-old American girl whose romantic idealism drives her toward engagement, and whose painful self-discovery drives her away from it. Her breakdown during Colville's illness reveals feelings she had "stifled insanely" (043-117-s1).
- **Mrs. Evalina Bowen** — Imogene's chaperone and Colville's eventual wife. She opposed the match from the beginning and served as the reluctant intermediary, receiving Imogene's apology letter and confronting Colville with it: "Mr. Colville came here with your letter, Imogene" (024-070-s1).
- **Mrs. Graham** — Imogene's mother, who travels from America to extricate her daughter from the engagement. She delivers the definitive verdict on Imogene's behalf: "She esteems you, she respects you, she admires you, she likes you; but—" (043-141-s1).

## Aftermath / Consequences

The broken engagement is the pivot upon which the novel's resolution turns. Colville's release from Imogene is, as Mr. Waters later observes, "merely sanative, and so far beneficent" (045-107-s1): it frees both parties from a mistake that would have brought lasting unhappiness. For Imogene, the break represents a painful but necessary education in the difference between romantic admiration and genuine love. She returns to America with her mother, having passed through a crisis that brought her close to "brain-fever" (043-137-s1). For Colville, the dissolution clears the path for his eventual marriage to Mrs. Bowen, the woman he has truly loved throughout the novel.

> "A broken engagement may be a bad thing in some cases, but I am inclined to think that it is the very best thing that could happen in most cases where it happens. The evil is done long before; the broken engagement is merely sanative, and so far beneficent." (045-104-s1, 045-106-s2, 045-107-s1)

The broken engagement also resolves the novel's central thematic tension — the question of whether youth and middle age can bridge their differences in love. Howells's answer is gentle but firm: Imogene's failure to love Colville is not a moral failure but a discovery of self. Her mother describes the "error which she had discovered in herself — discovered too late, as she felt" (043-113-s1). The dissolution is presented not as tragedy but as a necessary unmasking, the exposure of a romantic fantasy to the clarifying light of self-knowledge.
