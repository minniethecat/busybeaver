## Description

The Lung'Arno (literally "along the Arno") is the broad embanked quay that follows the course of the Arno River on both its northern and southern banks through Florence. In *Indian Summer*, it is the primary artery of the characters' social and emotional lives — the path along which they walk, drive, hurry toward confrontations, and linger in solitary reflection. Howells introduces it in the novel's opening pages as part of a rain-swollen winter cityscape: the yellow torrent "was swelling momently on the wall of the Lung' Arno, and rolling a threatening flood toward the Cascine" (001-019–001-020). Moments later, the narrator surveys "the city, with the sweep of the Lung' Arno on either hand, and its domes and towers hung in the dull air" (001-022–001-023), establishing the embankment as the geographic axis of Florence.

The Lung'Arno is lined with the pale buff and grey houses of the Florentine nobility and the furnished lodgings of the expatriate community. In Chapter 14, Howells notes the apartments "which stretch the whole length of the Lung' Arno on either hand, and abound in all the new streets approaching the Cascine" (014-024–014-026), capturing the neighbourhood's character as the residential quarter of choice for the Anglo-American sojourners who populate the novel. Mrs. Bowen's own address — "Palazzo Pinti, Lung' Arno della Zecca" (002-164) — situates her in this quarter, and the specific designation "della Zecca" (of the Mint) identifies the eastern stretch of the northern embankment, a detail that anchors the fiction in the real geography of 1880s Florence.

## Significance in the Novel

The Lung'Arno functions as the novel's connective tissue — the physical thread that links its settings and stages its encounters. Characters use it to move between Palazzo Pinti, the Cascine, the Ponte Vecchio, and the Piazza della Signoria, and their movements along it often mark emotional transitions. After a painful scene with Imogene in which Colville has won a moral victory at her expense, he "strolled along down the Arno, feeling flat and mean" (021-051–021-052), the riverside walk becoming the stage for his self-reproach. The Lung'Arno is thus more than a street; it is the space where private feeling meets public exposure, where the expatriate community performs its leisured rituals of walking and driving.

The embankment also serves as a threshold between the interior world of domestic drama and the exterior world of Florentine life. When Colville flees the veglione in Chapter 15 during Carnival, "he hurried to the Lung' Arno, and reached the palace where Mrs. Bowen lived, with these holiday sights fresh in his mind" (015-168–015-169). The Lung'Arno is the passage from the chaos of the masked streets to the refuge of Palazzo Pinti, from public masquerade to private emotional reckoning. In Chapter 27, Colville and Mr. Waters "walked out of the Piazza and down to the Lung' Arno, through the corridor of the Uffizzi, where the illustrious Florentines stand in marble under the arches" (027-061–027-064), a route that juxtaposes the grandeur of Renaissance history with the intimate conversation of two men in middle age.

By the novel's close, the Lung'Arno registers the seasonal and emotional climax. As Colville prepares to leave Palazzo Pinti in early summer, "the Lung' Arno was a glare of light that struck back from the curving line of the buff houses; the river had shrivelled to a rill in its bed; the black cypresses were dim in the tremor of the distant air on the hill-slopes beyond" (044-059–044-064). The embankment that was dark and threatening in January has become a sun-drenched stage for departure, mirroring the resolution of the novel's emotional entanglements.

## Key Scenes

### The Opening Flood (Chapter 1)

The novel's first description of Florence is mediated through the Lung'Arno in winter spate, establishing the embankment as the vantage point from which the city is viewed.

> The city, with the sweep of the Lung' Arno on either hand, and its domes and towers hung in the dull air, and the country with its white villas and black cypresses breaking the grey stretches of the olive orchards on its hill-sides, had alike been growing more and more insufferable. (001-022–001-026)

### A Sunlit Promenade (Chapter 11)

After a morning of unhappy self-reflection, Colville walks the Lung'Arno toward the Cascine, and the riverside is transformed from the turbulent flood of the opening chapter into a scene of serene beauty.

> He went out and walked down the Lung' Arno toward the Cascine. The sun danced on the river, and bathed the long line of pale buff and grey houses that followed its curve, and ceased in the mist of leafless tree-tops where the Cascine began. (011-121–011-123)

### The One-Armed Beggar (Chapter 41)

In a lighter moment late in the novel, Colville recalls a shared memory with Mrs. Bowen — a beggar they used to encounter on the Lung'Arno — revealing the embankment as a site of everyday charity and shared history between the characters.

> Do you remember the one-armed man whom we used to give to on the Lung' Arno? That persevering sufferer has been repeatedly arrested for mendicancy, and obliged to pay a fine out of his hard earnings to escape being sent to your Pia Casa. (041-005–041-008)

### The Summer Farewell (Chapter 44)

As Colville leaves Palazzo Pinti in the early summer heat, the Lung'Arno glares under the sun, the swollen torrent of January now reduced to a trickle — a transformation that echoes the novel's emotional arc.

> The Lung' Arno was a glare of light that struck back from the curving line of the buff houses; the river had shrivelled to a rill in its bed; the black cypresses were dim in the tremor of the distant air on the hill-slopes beyond. (044-059–044-064)

## Real-World Counterpart

The *lungarni* (plural) are the embanked roads that run along both banks of the Arno through the historic centre of Florence. The modern embankments were constructed in the 1860s during the period of Florence's brief tenure as capital of the newly unified Kingdom of Italy (1865–1871), part of Giuseppe Poggi's urban redevelopment plan that also created the Piazzale Michelangelo and the Viale dei Colli. By the 1880s, when *Indian Summer* is set, the lungarni were relatively recent additions to the cityscape — broad, tree-lined boulevards that replaced the narrower medieval quays and opened the river to carriage traffic and promenading. The Lungarno della Zecca (mentioned in the novel as Mrs. Bowen's address at 002-164) is the eastern stretch of the northern embankment, named for the Zecca (mint) that once stood nearby, and remains a prestigious residential address. The lungarni were a central feature of the Anglo-American expatriate experience in late-nineteenth-century Florence; Henry James, a close contemporary of Howells, set scenes along them in *The Portrait of a Lady* (1881), and they appear frequently in the travel writing and correspondence of the period. Howells himself knew Florence intimately from his travels, and the novel's use of the Lung'Arno as the connective thread of its geography reflects a precise knowledge of the city's layout and social topography.
