# American Girlhood

## Definition

American girlhood in *Indian Summer* is a recurring thematic subject through which William Dean Howells examines larger questions of national identity, cultural change, and the tension between American and European social values. The novel does not treat girlhood as a static condition but as a contested ideal — an ideological battleground where characters debate what American daughters should become, and in doing so, what America itself has become. At stake is not merely the proper upbringing of young women but the character of the nation: the "wild, sweet liberty which once made American girlhood a long rapture" (003-026 to 003-027) versus the "calculating and worldly and conventional" (005-085 to 005-086) modernity that threatens to replace it.

The concept operates at three interconnected levels: first, as nostalgia for a lost frontier ideal of girlhood freedom; second, as a critique of the worldly materialism that Howells saw overtaking American society in the Gilded Age; and third, as a transnational problem — the question of whether American girls abroad, like Effie Bowen raised under her mother's "Europeanised" methods (006-152 to 006-153), risk losing an essential American liberty along with its dangers.

## Context in the Novel

The novel's treatment of American girlhood unfolds primarily through the perspective of Theodore Colville, a middle-aged American journalist who returns to Florence after seventeen years. His intermediary position — old enough to remember the old ideal, young enough to desire the new — makes him the novel's primary vehicle for exploring the question. The debate is seeded early, in the novel's third chapter, when Colville observes Effie Bowen's strict deference to her mother. His "thoughts idled off to what Mrs. Bowen's own untrammelled girlhood must have been in her Western city" (003-020 to 003-021), and he reflects that for Effie "there were to be no buggy rides, or concerts, or dances at the invitation of young men; no picnics, free and unchaperoned as the casing air" (003-022 to 003-023). The catalogue of denied freedoms is rendered in lush, nostalgic detail — "sitting on the steps at dusk with callers," "lingering at the gate with her youthful escort home from the ball" (003-024 to 003-026) — before arriving at the novel's most memorable formulation: "nothing of that wild, sweet liberty which once made American girlhood a long rapture" (003-026 to 003-027). The word "once" signals the central temporal drama: this girlhood belongs to the past.

The thematic thread is picked up in Chapter 5 at a social gathering, where Colville relays a German acquaintance's complaint "that the American girls — especially the rich ones — seem very calculating and worldly and conventional" (005-085 to 005-086). Colville's sardonic response, offering "some notion of the ennobling influences of society in Newport" (005-086 to 005-088), masks a genuine concern. Dropping his irony, he articulates the novel's central dichotomy directly: "I think that we've much less to regret in their unsuspecting, unsophisticated freedom than in the type of hard materialism which we produce in young girls, perfectly wide awake, disenchanted, unromantic, who prefer the worldly vanities and advantages deliberately and on principle, recognising something better merely to despise it" (005-093 to 005-098). The opposition is clearly drawn: unsuspecting freedom versus hard materialism, romantic innocence versus disenchanted worldliness.

The debate reaches its fullest articulation in Chapter 6, where Colville and Mrs. Bowen engage in "a discussion which is always fruitful with Americans — the discussion of American girlhood" (006-148 to 006-149). Colville contends "for the old national ideal of girlish liberty as wide as the continent, as fast as the Mississippi" (006-150 to 006-151), spatialising the ideal in the language of American geography and expansion. Mrs. Bowen "withstood him with delicate firmness" (006-151 to 006-152), prompting Colville's accusation: "Oh, you're Europeanised" (006-152). She replies with equal conviction: "I certainly prefer the European plan of bringing up girls. I shouldn't think of letting a daughter of mine have the freedom I had" (006-153 to 006-155). The exchange is pivotal because it refuses to treat the European alternative as a simple corruption of American purity. Mrs. Bowen has known the frontier liberty herself and has deliberately rejected it — her Europeanisation is not ignorance but experience.

Throughout the novel, the younger generation provides a third position. Imogene Graham, the novel's representative of actual American girlhood, embodies the "splendid girlhood" (011-183) that Colville finds himself unable to connect with — "he was both too young and too old for it" (011-183 to 011-184). When Colville asks Miss Graham her preference between the two ideals, she answers evasively: "I don't think it's quite the same now as it used to be" (006-160 to 006-161), a response that leaves Colville feeling "obsolete" (006-163). Her evasion suggests that American girlhood has already moved beyond the terms of the debate, into something the older generation cannot fully grasp.

## Key Passages

> His thoughts idled off to what Mrs. Bowen's own untrammelled girlhood must have been in her Western city. For her daughter there were to be no buggy rides, or concerts, or dances at the invitation of young men; no picnics, free and unchaperoned as the casing air; no sitting on the steps at dusk with callers who never dreamed of asking for her mother; no lingering at the gate with her youthful escort home from the ball — nothing of that wild, sweet liberty which once made American girlhood a long rapture. (003-020 to 003-027)

This passage, the novel's most lyrical treatment of the theme, establishes the elegiac tone that pervades all subsequent discussions. Colville's catalogue of denied freedoms — each detail drawn from the texture of midwestern small-town life — constructs American girlhood as a lost pastoral. The phrase "wild, sweet liberty" joins two qualities that the novel treats as inseparable companions of the American character: untamed expansiveness and emotional warmth. That this liberty "once made" girlhood a "long rapture" implies both the greatness of what was lost and the finality of the loss.

> "I think that we've much less to regret in their unsuspecting, unsophisticated freedom than in the type of hard materialism which we produce in young girls, perfectly wide awake, disenchanted, unromantic, who prefer the worldly vanities and advantages deliberately and on principle, recognising something better merely to despise it." (005-093 to 005-098)

Colville's earnest speech, following his ironic treatment of the German's complaint, reveals the moral seriousness beneath his habitual sarcasm. The contrast between "unsuspecting, unsophisticated freedom" and "hard materialism" is the novel's central ethical dichotomy regarding American girlhood. The accusing series — "perfectly wide awake, disenchanted, unromantic" — equates enlightenment with diminishment, worldly knowledge with spiritual loss. Crucially, Colville identifies not ignorance but contempt as the mark of the new American girl: she "recognises something better merely to despise it," making her materialism not merely a preference but an active rejection of innocence.

> They entered into a discussion which is always fruitful with Americans — the discussion of American girlhood, and Colville contended for the old national ideal of girlish liberty as wide as the continent, as fast as the Mississippi. Mrs. Bowen withstood him with delicate firmness. "Oh," he said, "you're Europeanised." (006-148 to 006-152)

This is the novel's most concentrated articulation of the theme as a national debate. Colville's description of "the old national ideal" links girlhood to geography — "as wide as the continent, as fast as the Mississippi" — collapsing personal freedom into national expanse. The Mississippi reference invokes the American West as the geographical condition of girlish liberty, suggesting that the ideal is not merely cultural but spatial, dependent on the frontier's promise of open, unbounded possibility. Mrs. Bowen's "delicate firmness" refuses to engage the frontier myth on its own terms; her reply, "I certainly prefer the European plan of bringing up girls" (006-153 to 006-154), substitutes the practical language of child-rearing for Colville's romantic nationalism. The charge of being "Europeanised" — levelled in good-natured exasperation — is the novel's shorthand for the loss of the old American ideal, and it recurs as an accusation and an anxiety throughout the narrative.

## Related Concepts

- **Expatriate Identity** — The debate over American girlhood is inseparable from the novel's broader exploration of American identity abroad; the question of how to raise American girls in Europe is a specific instance of the larger problem of how to remain American while living outside America.
- **Europeanisation** — The charge of being "Europeanised" (006-152) is the novel's recurring anxiety about cultural transformation; American girlhood is the primary site where this anxiety is dramatised and debated.
- **Middle Age and Youth** — Colville's relationship to American girlhood is mediated by his age: he is old enough to remember the old ideal nostalgically and young enough to desire the new, a position of tragic intermediacy captured in his sense of being "both too young and too old" (011-183 to 011-184) for Imogene.
- **Frontier Ideology** — Colville's framing of girlish liberty "as wide as the continent, as fast as the Mississippi" (006-150 to 006-151) explicitly links American girlhood to the myth of the frontier, making the girl's freedom a synecdoche for national expansion and possibility.
