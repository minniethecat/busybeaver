[003-001] "Oh! And I supposed you must be indulging all sorts of historical

[003-002] associations with the place. Effie and I have been walking through the

[003-003] Via de' Bardi, where Romola lived, and I was bringing her back over the

[003-004] [[ponte-vecchio|Ponte Vecchio]], so as to impress the origin of [[florence|Florence]] on her mind."

[003-005] "Is that what makes Miss Effie hate it?" asked [[theodore-colville|Colville]], looking at the

[003-006] child, whose youthful resemblance to her mother was in all things so

[003-007] perfect that a fantastic question whether she could ever have had any

[003-008] other parent swept through him. Certainly, if [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]] were to marry

[003-009] again, there was nothing in this child's looks to suggest the idea of a

[003-010] predecessor to the second husband.

[003-011] "Effie doesn't hate any sort of useful knowledge," said her mother half

[003-012] jestingly. "She's just come to me from school at Vevay."

[003-013] "Oh, then, I think she might," persisted Colville. "Don't you hate the

[003-014] origin of Florence a little?" he asked of the child.

[003-015] "I don't know enough about it," she answered, with a quick look of

[003-016] question at her mother, and checking herself in a possibly indiscreet

[003-017] smile.

[003-018] "Ah, that accounts for it," said Colville, and he laughed. It amused him

[003-019] to see the child referring even this point of propriety to her mother,

[003-020] and his thoughts idled off to what Mrs. Bowen's own untrammelled

[003-021] girlhood must have been in her Western city. For her daughter there were

[003-022] to be no buggy rides, or concerts, or dances at the invitation of young

[003-023] men; no picnics, free and unchaperoned as the casing air; no sitting on

[003-024] the steps at dusk with callers who never dreamed of asking for her

[003-025] mother; no lingering at the gate with her youthful escort home from the

[003-026] ball--nothing of that wild, sweet liberty which once made American

[003-027] girlhood a long rapture. But would she be any the better for her

[003-028] privations, for referring not only every point of conduct, but every

[003-029] thought and feeling, to her mother? He suppressed a sigh for the

[003-030] inevitable change, but rejoiced that his own youth had fallen in the

[003-031] earlier time, and said, "You will hate it as soon as you've read a

[003-032] little of it."

[003-033] "The difficulty is to read a little of Florentine history. I can't find

[003-034] anything in less than ten or twelve volumes," said Mrs. Bowen. "Effie

[003-035] and I were going to [[vieusseux-reading-room|Viesseux's Library]] again, in desperation, to see if

[003-036] there wasn't something shorter in French."

[003-037] She now offered Colville her hand, and he found himself very reluctant

[003-038] to let it go. Something in her looks did not forbid him, and when she

[003-039] took her hand away, he said, "Let me go to Viesseux's with you, Mrs.

[003-040] Bowen, and give you the advantage of my unprejudiced ignorance in the

[003-041] choice of a book on Florence."

[003-042] "Oh, I was longing to ask you!" said Mrs. Bowen frankly. "It is really

[003-043] such a serious matter, especially when the book is for a young person.

[003-044] Unless it's very dry, it's so apt to be--objectionable."

[003-045] "Yes," said Colville, with a smile at her perplexity. He moved off down

[003-046] the slope of the bridge with her, between the jewellers' shops, and felt

[003-047] a singular satisfaction in her company. Women of fashion always

[003-048] interested him; he liked them; it diverted him that they should take

[003-049] themselves seriously. Their resolution, their suffering for their ideal,

[003-050] such as it was, their energy in dressing and adorning themselves, the

[003-051] pains they were at to achieve the trivialities they passed their lives

[003-052] in, were perpetually delightful to him. He often found them people of

[003-053] great simplicity, and sometimes of singularly good sense; their frequent

[003-054] vein of piety was delicious.

[003-055] Ten minutes earlier he would have said that nothing could have been less

[003-056] welcome to him than this encounter, but now he felt unwilling to leave

[003-057] Mrs. Bowen.

[003-058] "Go before, Effie," she said; and she added, to Colville, "How very

[003-059] Florentine all this is! If you dropped from the clouds on this spot

[003-060] without previous warning, you would know that you were on the Ponte

[003-061] Vecchio, and nowhere else."

[003-062] "Yes, it's very Florentine," Colville assented. "The bridge is very well

[003-063] as a bridge, but as a street I prefer the Main Street Bridge at Des

[003-064] Vaches. I was looking at the jewellery before you came up, and I don't

[003-065] think it's pretty, even the old pieces of peasant jewellery. Why do

[003-066] people come here to look at it? If you were going to buy something for a

[003-067] friend, would you dream of coming here for it?"

[003-068] "Oh _no_!" replied Mrs. Bowen, with the deepest feeling.

[003-069] They quitted the bridge, and turning to the left, moved down the street

[003-070] which with difficulty finds space between the parapet of the river and

[003-071] the shops of the mosaicists and dealers in statuary cramping it on the

[003-072] other hand.

[003-073] "Here's something distinctively Florentine too," said Colville. "These

[003-074] table-tops, and paper-weights, and caskets, and photograph frames, and

[003-075] lockets, and breast-pins; and here, this ghostly glare of undersized

[003-076] Psyches and Hebes and Graces in alabaster."

[003-077] "Oh, you mustn't think of any of them!" Mrs. Bowen broke in with horror.

[003-078] "If your friend wishes you to get her something characteristically

[003-079] Florentine, and at the same time very tasteful, you must go--"

[003-080] Colville gave a melancholy laugh. "My friend is an abstraction, Mrs.

[003-081] Bowen, without sex or any sort of entity."

[003-082] "Oh!" said Mrs. Bowen. Some fine drops had begun to sprinkle the

[003-083] pavement. "What a ridiculous blunder! It's raining! Effie, I'm afraid we

[003-084] must give up your book for to-day. We're not dressed for damp weather,

[003-085] and we'd better hurry home as soon as possible." She got promptly into

[003-086] the shelter of a doorway, and gathered her daughter to her, while she

[003-087] flung her sacque over her shoulder and caught her draperies from the

[003-088] ground for the next movement. "Mr. Colville, will you please stop the

[003-089] first closed carriage that comes in sight?"

[003-090] A figure of _primo tenore_ had witnessed the manoeuvre from the box of

[003-091] his cab; he held up his whip, and at a nod from Colville he drove

[003-092] abreast of the doorway, his broken-kneed, tremulous little horse gay in

[003-093] brass-mounted harness, and with a stiff turkey feather stuck upright at

[003-094] one ear in his head-stall.

[003-095] Mrs. Bowen had no more scruple than another woman in stopping travel and

[003-096] traffic in a public street for her convenience. She now entered into a

[003-097] brisk parting conversation with Colville, such as ladies love, blocking

[003-098] the narrow sidewalk with herself, her daughter, and her open carriage

[003-099] door, and making people walk round her cab, in the road, which they did

[003-100] meekly enough, with the Florentine submissiveness to the pretensions of

[003-101] any sort of vehicle. She said a dozen important things that seemed to

[003-102] have just come into her head, and, "Why, how stupid I am!" she called

[003-103] out, making Colville check the driver in his first start, after she had

[003-104] got into the cab. "We are to have a few people tonight. If you have no

[003-105] engagement, I should be so glad to have you come. Can't you?"

[003-106] "Yes, I can," said Colville, admiring the whole transaction and the

[003-107] parties to it with a passive smile.

[003-108] After finding her pocket, she found that her card-case was not in it,

[003-109] but in the purse she had given Effie to carry; but she got her address

[003-110] at last, and gave it to Colville, though he said he should remember it

[003-111] without. "Any time between nine and eleven," she said. "It's so nice of

[003-112] you to promise!"

[003-113] She questioned him from under her half-lifted eyelids, and he added,

[003-114] with a laugh, "I'll come!" and was rewarded with two pretty smiles, just

[003-115] alike, from mother and daughter, as they drove away.

[003-116] III

[003-117] Twenty years earlier, when Mrs. Bowen was Miss [[miss-ridgely|Lina Ridgely]], she used to

[003-118] be the friend and confidante of the girl who jilted Colville. They were

[003-119] then both so young that they could scarcely have been a year out of

[003-120] school before they left home for the year they were spending in Europe;

[003-121] but to the young man's inexperience they seemed the wisest and maturest

[003-122] of society women. His heart quaked in his breast when he saw them

[003-123] talking and laughing together, for fear they should be talking and

[003-124] laughing about him; he was even a little more afraid of Miss Ridgely

[003-125] than of her friend, who was dashing and effective, where Miss Ridgely

[003-126] was serene and elegant, according to his feeling at that time; but he

[003-127] never saw her after his rejection, and it was not till he read of her

[003-128] marriage with the Hon. [[mr-bowen|Mr. Bowen]] that certain vague impressions began to

[003-129] define themselves. He then remembered that Lina Ridgely in many fine

[003-130] little ways had shown a kindness, almost a compassion, for him, as for

[003-131] one whose unconsciousness a hopeless doom impended over. He perceived

[003-132] that she had always seemed to like him--a thing that had not occurred to

[003-133] him in the stupid absorption of his passion for the other--and fragments

[003-134] of proof that she had probably defended and advocated him occurred to

[003-135] him, and inspired a vain and retrospective gratitude; he abandoned

[003-136] himself to regrets, which were proper enough in regard to Miss Ridgely,

[003-137] but were certainly a little unlawful concerning Mrs. Bowen.

[003-138] As he walked away toward his hotel he amused himself with the conjecture

[003-139] whether he, with his forty-one years and his hundred and eighty five

[003-140] pounds, were not still a pathetic and even a romantic figure to this

[003-141] pretty and kindly woman, who probably imagined him as heart-broken as

[003-142] ever. He was very willing to see more of her, if she wished; but with

[003-143] the rain beginning to fall more thick and chill in the darkening street,

[003-144] he could have postponed their next meeting till a pleasanter evening

[003-145] without great [[sacrifice-and-renunciation|self-denial]]. He felt a little twinge of rheumatism in his

[003-146] shoulder when he got into his room, for your room in a Florentine hotel

[003-147] is always some degrees colder than outdoors, unless you have fire in it;

[003-148] and with the sun shining on his windows when he went out after lunch, it

[003-149] had seemed to Colville ridiculous to have his morning fire kept up. The

[003-150] sun was what he had taken the room for. It was in it, the landlord

[003-151] assured him, from ten in the morning till four in the afternoon; and so,

[003-152] in fact, it was, when it shone; but even then it was not fully in it,

[003-153] but had a trick of looking in at the sides of the window, and painting

[003-154] the chamber wall with a delusive glow. Colville raked away the ashes of

[003-155] his fire-place, and throwing on two or three fagots of broom and pine

[003-156] sprays, he had a blaze that would be very pretty to dress by after

[003-157] dinner, but that gave out no warmth for the present. He left it, and

[003-158] went down to the reading-room, as it was labelled over the door, in

[003-159] homage to a predominance of English-speaking people among the guests;

[003-160] but there was no fire there; that was kindled only by request, and he

[003-161] shivered at the bare aspect of the apartment, with its cold piano, its

[003-162] locked bookcases, and its table, where the London _Times_, the _Neue

[003-163] Freie Presse_ of Vienna, and the _Italie_ of Rome exposed their titles,

[003-164] one just beyond the margin of the other. He turned from the door and

[003-165] went into the dining-room, where the stove was ostentatiously roaring

[003-166] over its small logs and its lozenges of peat, But even here the fire had

[003-167] been so recently lighted that the warmth was potential rather than

[003-168] actual. By stooping down before the stove, and pressing his shoulder

[003-169] against its brass doors, Colville managed to lull his enemy, while he

[003-170] studied the figures of the woman-headed, woman-breasted hounds

[003-171] developing into vines and foliage that covered the frescoed trellising

[003-172] of the quadrangularly vaulted ceiling. The waiters, in their veteran

[003-173] dress-coats, were putting the final touches to the table, and the sound

[003-174] of voices outside the door obliged Colville to get up. The effort

[003-175] involved made him still more reluctant about going out to Mrs. Bowen's.
