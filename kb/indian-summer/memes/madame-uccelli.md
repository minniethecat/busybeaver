## Role

Madame Uccelli is a minor character in *Indian Summer* who functions as the gatekeeper of Florentine expatriate society. Her villa salon serves as the setting for the novel's central social event — the evening party in Chapter VI where Theodore Colville makes his disastrous attempt at the Lancers, an incident that reverberates through the rest of the narrative (008-182). As an American who married an Italian and made Florence her permanent home, she occupies a liminal cultural position, bridging the Anglo-American expatriate colony and native Italian society (009-092--009-099). Though she appears only fleetingly in the novel, her party constitutes one of the most richly rendered social tableaux in the book, and references to "Madame Uccelli's" recur as a touchstone for the characters' social and emotional lives (044-124, 031-079).

## Character Analysis

Madame Uccelli embodies the peculiar hybrid identity of the long-term American expatriate in late-nineteenth-century Europe. Howells sketches her in a single vivid paragraph that is one of the novel's most accomplished passages of social portraiture. She is an American from Philadelphia who returned to Italy with her Italian husband and, after his death, continued to inhabit the villa he built with her money (009-093--009-095). The narrator's observation that "such people grow very queer with the lapse of time" (009-095) encapsulates the faintly comic, faintly unsettling dislocation of the permanent expatriate, caught between two cultures without belonging wholly to either.

> She was an American who had returned with her Italian husband to Italy, and had long survived him in the villa which he had built with her money. Such people grow very queer with the lapse of time. Madame Uccelli's character remained inalienably American, but her manners and customs had become largely Italian; without having learned the language thoroughly, she spoke it very fluently, and its idioms marked her Philadelphia English. Her house was a menagerie of all the nationalities; she was liked in Italian society, and there were many Italians; English-speaking Russians abounded; there were many genuine English, Germans, Scandinavians, Protestant Irish, American Catholics, and then Americans of all kinds. (009-093--009-100)

Her "inalienably American" character coexists with Italianised manners, and her imperfect but fluent Italian is marked by the idioms of her native Philadelphia English — a linguistic hybridity that mirrors her social position (009-096--009-097). The catalogue of nationalities in her salon — "a menagerie of all the nationalities" (009-098) — underscores her function as a social nexus where the rigid hierarchies of Anglo-Florentine society are, if not dissolved, at least relaxed. Despite a "superstition of her exclusiveness among her compatriots" (009-101), the narrator assures us that "one really met every one there sooner or later" (009-101). Her religious identity is equally ambiguous: "she was supposed to be a convert to the religion of her late husband, but no one really knew what religion she was of, probably not even Madame Uccelli herself" (009-105--009-107).

The one certainty about her hospitality is its material generosity. The narrator closes the portrait with the droll observation that "one thing you were sure of at her house, and that was a substantial supper" (009-107--009-108), adding with gentle irony that "it is the example of such resident foreigners which has corrupted the Florentines, though many native families still hold out against it" (009-108--009-110). This comic deflation — from the mystery of Madame Uccelli's soul to the solidity of her buffet — is characteristic of Howells's gently satiric treatment of expatriate society.

## Relationships

- **Fanny Uccelli** — Madame Uccelli's daughter, who "spoke both English and Italian much better than her mother" (009-111). At the party, Fanny functions as the practical manager of the dancing, coming forward to "possess herself of Miss Graham" (009-111) and politely pressing Mrs. Bowen to find a partner. She is "full of her business of starting the dance" (009-112) and has little time for Colville, a contrast to her mother's more hospitable manner. Fanny represents the second generation of the expatriate family, fully bilingual and bicultural.

- **Theodore Colville** — Madame Uccelli "accepted him most hospitably among her guests when he was presented" (009-092). She finds him a lady to escort to the supper-room, and when that lady abandons him, Madame Uccelli later finds him and "introduced him to another mother of daughters" (009-124). Her hospitality is impersonal but efficient, oriented toward pairing unattached guests. Colville's comic humiliation in the Lancers at her party becomes a recurring motif; he later "laughed at him now alone, with a wholly impersonal sense of his absurdity" at "Madame Uccelli's party" (010-170).

- **Mrs. Evalina Bowen** — A regular guest at Madame Uccelli's salon. It is Mrs. Bowen who extends the invitation to Colville (008-182), and her carriage is "one of the few private equipages that drove up to Madame Uccelli's door" (009-072). Mrs. Bowen's status as "one of the very few Americans who were asked to Italian houses" (008-189--008-190) is confirmed by her presence at the Uccelli villa. Later, when Mrs. Bowen writes to Colville about Imogene, she recalls his Lancers performance at "Madam Uccelli's" (031-079) as evidence of his unsuitability for society.

- **Imogene Graham** — Imogene dances at the Uccelli party and is partnered by Fanny. Much later, after the collapse of her engagement to Colville, she recalls "that party at Madame Uccelli's" with fury (044-124), the singing in the streets reminding her of "voices from our youth" (044-126). The Uccelli salon thus persists in Imogene's memory as a site of lost innocence and romantic disillusionment.

## Key Scenes

### The Invitation and Arrival (Chapters VI--VII / Sections 8--9)

Mrs. Bowen's offer to take Colville to Madame Uccelli's "first struck his sense of the ludicrous" before beginning "to flatter him" (008-182--008-183). The arrival scene is one of Howells's most evocative Florentine nocturnes:

> Her carriage was one of the few private equipages that drove up to Madame Uccelli's door; most people had not even come in a _remise_, but, after the simple Florentine fashion, had taken the little cabs, which stretched in a long line up and down the way; the horses had let their weary heads drop, and were easing their broken knees by extending their forelegs while they drowsed; the drivers, huddled in their great-coats, had assembled around the doorway to see the guests alight, with that amiable, unenvious interest of the Italians in the pleasure of others. The deep sky glittered with stars; in the corner of the next villa garden the black plumes of some cypresses blotted out their space among them. (009-072--009-081)

### Hospitality and the Dancing (Chapter VII / Section 9)

Madame Uccelli receives Colville "most hospitably" (009-092), and the dancing begins under the management of her daughter Fanny. The narrator observes that the daughter "spoke both English and Italian much better than her mother" (009-111), a detail that suggests the degree to which Madame Uccelli remains culturally suspended between two worlds. Colville, after being briefly escorted to the supper-room, is left to "stand by the sofa where she sat till Madame Uccelli found him and introduced him to another mother of daughters" (009-124) — a comic vignette of the hostess's mechanical hospitality in action.

### Colville's Lancers Disaster and Its Aftermath (Chapter VII / Section 10)

At the Uccelli party, Colville attempts to join a set of the Lancers and "spoiled a set" (010-170), an embarrassment that follows him through the novel. The incident becomes a touchstone: Mrs. Bowen later cites it as proof that Colville is not "a society man" (031-078--031-079), and Imogene recalls "that party at Madame Uccelli's" with fury after her broken engagement, the memory of the singing in the Florentine streets that night evoking "voices from our youth" (044-124--044-127). The Uccelli villa near the baths of Lucca is mentioned in the same conversation, a reminder of the broader social world that Madame Uccelli's hospitality opens to the expatriate community (044-131).
