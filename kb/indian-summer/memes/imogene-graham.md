## Role

Imogene Graham is a major character in *Indian Summer* who functions as the romantic interest around whom the novel's central emotional triangle revolves. She is a twenty-year-old American girl from Buffalo, New York, staying in Florence for the winter under the chaperonage of her mother's friend, Mrs. Evalina Bowen (005-007). Her youthful ardour, romantic idealism, and susceptibility to the charm of the older and world-weary Theodore Colville drive the plot's central tension. As the story progresses, Imogene's infatuation with Colville deepens into a self-convinced love that leads to a private engagement, only for her to discover that she does not truly love him and must break it off (043-114). Her character serves the novel as an exploration of the gap between youthful romantic fantasy and genuine emotional commitment, a theme Howells examines with psychological subtlety.

## Character Analysis

Imogene Graham is, at the novel's opening, a magnificent physical presence. Colville's first impression of her across the length of Mrs. Bowen's *salon* is of "a heroic statue of herself" (005-013), and up close she possesses "a generous amplitude of womanhood" with "a sweet and youthful radiance of look that was very winning" (005-116). She is a celebrated beauty -- in Florentine society she is spoken of reverently by young men who stand in awe of "the wisdom and brilliancy and severity of Imogene Graham" (013-101). But she is also touchingly young: she remembers only "the end of the war" (006-044) and Colville reflects on her as belonging, essentially, to another generation.

Her personality is defined by fervent, sometimes theatrical idealism. She declares that young persons "can't be interesting" and professes a preference for people who have "gone *through* something" (005-183, 005-196). She is given to "intensities" of feeling (008-178) and is drawn to the romance of Colville's past heartbreak -- so much so that when Mrs. Bowen tells her of how Colville was jilted by Jenny Milbury, Imogene declares it "a heartless, shameless thing" that has "ruined his life" (008-125, 008-127). She idealises Colville precisely because he seems a man of depth and sadness, possessing, she imagines, "an undercurrent of sadness -- of tragedy -- in everything for him" (008-160).

Yet Imogene is also, in her own mind, deeply self-aware of her own immaturity. She confesses to Mrs. Bowen: "It seems as if I never should be grown up, or anything but a child" (008-137). She longs for Colville's kind of conversation -- "It makes you forget how old and plain he is" (008-139) -- and she tells Mrs. Bowen with regret that "the way young men talk, they do seem boys" (008-138). This intense admiration for Colville's maturity, combined with her own youthful romanticism, drives her toward the engagement that she will ultimately find herself incapable of sustaining.

## Relationships

- **Theodore Colville** — The central relationship of the novel. Imogene is fascinated by Colville's wit, his age, and his supposed tragic past. She declares that she enjoys "such a subject as Mr. Colville" with whom "there's no stopping" (014-003). Her feelings deepen through shared walks in the Cascine, dances, and Carnival outings. Ultimately she becomes engaged to him but discovers, in a crisis of remorse and self-abhorrence, that "she never had loved you and never should" (043-127). Colville himself releases her freely, and the broken engagement clears the way for him to marry Mrs. Bowen.

- **Mrs. Evalina Bowen** — Imogene's chaperone and, ultimately, her rival for Colville's affection. Imogene looks up to Mrs. Bowen but chafes at her caution. When Mrs. Bowen warns her about Colville, Imogene receives it with a "serious, bewildered look" (021-012), unable to imagine that her feelings are being questioned. Their relationship grows strained with the "barrier between them" (026-066) as the romantic triangle intensifies. Yet at the final crisis, Mrs. Bowen acts generously, writing on Imogene's behalf the letter that asks Colville to stay (026-103).

- **Effie Bowen** — Mrs. Bowen's young daughter. Effie displays an "adoration of Miss Graham" that is evident from the very first evening (005-126). Their bond is warm and sisterly: Effie accompanies Imogene on walks and outings, and Imogene is tender with her. Effie also serves as an innocent witness to the developing romance, reporting to her mother on Imogene and Colville's laughter together (014-026).

- **Mr. Morton** — The young English clergyman who courts Imogene earlier in the novel. He is the "young man" whose conversation and manners Imogene contrasts unfavourably with Colville's. She privately dismisses his lack of conversational appeal: "Oh, he's a clergyman" (006-116), and Mrs. Bowen notes that "I suppose that after talking with him [Colville] every one else seems insipid" (021-029).

- **Mrs. Graham** — Imogene's mother, who comes to Florence from America when Imogene falls into a crisis after Colville's accident. Mrs. Graham is described as having "the eyes of Imogene Graham, though the girl's were dark and the woman's were blue" (043-059). It is Mrs. Graham who delivers the news to Colville that Imogene has discovered "that she did not love you" (043-115) and who secures her daughter's release from the engagement.

## Key Scenes

### First Meeting at Mrs. Bowen's Party (Chapter V)

Colville is introduced to Imogene at Mrs. Bowen's evening party, after having been deliberately kept waiting. She greets him "frankly, with an innocent, nervous laugh" (005-128). Their banter is immediate and lively. Imogene declares that no young person can be interesting and says she would make the hero of a novel "an old person -- thirty-five or forty," looking directly at the forty-one-year-old Colville and blushing (005-187). She quotes Heine on young hands that have "enjoyed nothing and suffered nothing" and expresses her longing for experience (005-193). This first conversation establishes both her romantic idealism and the immediate chemistry between them:

> "Oh, I like people to go _through_ something. Don't you?" (005-196)

### The Dance at Madame Uccelli's (Chapters IX–X)

At a Florentine ball, Imogene dances with an abandon that moves Colville deeply. As she moves across the floor, "her face was rapt, intense, full of an unsmiling delight, which shone in her dark eyes, glowing like low stars" (009-132). When she catches sight of him watching, "the light of an exquisite smile flashed over her face. His heart was in his throat" (009-138). After the dance, she devours salad and ices with "a childish joy" (009-150), and later, in the carriage home, she is still euphoric, humming and inadvertently tapping Colville's foot with her own while keeping time (010-097). The contrast between her unjaded youth and Colville's self-conscious middle-age is the scene's emotional core:

> Her _abandon_ interested Colville, and then awed him; the spectacle of that young, unjaded capacity for pleasure touched him with a profound sense of loss. (009-134)

### Mrs. Bowen's Warning and Imogene's Defiance (Chapters XX–XXI)

The turning point of the novel's emotional architecture. After the Carnival veglione, Mrs. Bowen confronts Imogene about Colville, warning her of "too much difference, every way" (021-028). Imogene is stunned: "I never thought of him in that way at all. I don't know what to say yet. It -- confuses me" (021-022). But Mrs. Bowen's warning, rather than deterring Imogene, crystallises something in her. In the days that follow, unable to sleep and tormented by the thought of Colville leaving Florence, she breaks down before Mrs. Bowen and declares:

> "I think I am the one to go away, if any one... I shall never see any one like him... He made every moment interesting. He was always thinking of us -- he never thought of himself... Oh, he's a true gentleman -- I shall always say it." (026-025, 026-027, 026-031, 026-037)

She demands that Colville be asked to stay, and Mrs. Bowen, though recoiling inwardly, agrees to write the letter on her behalf (026-103).

### The Broken Engagement (Chapter XLIII)

After Colville is injured in a carriage accident, Imogene's true feelings reveal themselves. Rather than being awakened to love by his danger, she finds that "if your present danger did not awaken her to some such feeling for you as she had once imagined she had; if they both only increased her despair and self-abhorrence, then the case was indeed hopeless" (043-133). Her mother arrives to extricate her, and the engagement is dissolved. Colville, lying in his sickbed, receives the news with relief:

> "She is free," said Colville, and with the words a mighty load rolled from his heart. (043-143)

The scene is remarkable for its emotional honesty: Imogene's failure to love Colville is not a moral failure but a discovery of self. Her mother tells Colville, "She esteems you, she respects you, she admires you, she likes you; but --" (043-141) -- the "but" that seals the broken engagement and, ultimately, liberates both of them.

## Critical Reception

Imogene Graham has been read by critics as Howells's most psychologically complex portrait of a young woman. Unlike the sentimental heroines of earlier American fiction, Imogene is neither punished for her romantic error nor rewarded with a conventional happy ending; instead, she is allowed to make a mistake, discover it, and retreat from it without moral condemnation. Some scholars view her trajectory as a *Bildungsroman* in miniature -- a young woman who mistakes admiration and infatuation for love and learns, through painful self-examination, the difference. Her role as a foil to Mrs. Bowen -- youth versus maturity, romantic fantasy versus settled affection -- places her at the thematic heart of what many consider Howells's finest novel.
