[043-001] "Where am I?" he asked, with what appeared to him very unnecessary

[043-002] feebleness of voice.

[043-003] The young man begged his pardon in Italian, and when [[theodore-colville|Colville]] repeated

[043-004] his question in that tongue, he told him that he was in [[palazzo-pinti|Palazzo Pinti]],

[043-005] whither he had been brought from the scene of his accident. He added

[043-006] that Colville must not talk till the doctor had seen him and given him

[043-007] leave, and he explained that he was himself a nurse from the hospital,

[043-008] who had been taking care of him.

[043-009] Colville moved his head and felt the bandage upon it; he desisted in his

[043-010] attempt to lift his right arm to it before the attendant could interfere

[043-011] in behalf of the broken limb. He recalled dimly and fragmentarily long

[043-012] histories that he had dreamed, but he forbore to ask how long he had

[043-013] been in his present case, and he accepted patiently the apparition of

[043-014] the doctor and other persons who came and went, and were at his bedside

[043-015] or not there, as it seemed to him, between the opening and closing of an

[043-016] eye. As the days passed they acquired greater permanence and maintained

[043-017] a more uninterrupted identity. He was able to make quite sure of Mr.

[043-018] Morton and of [[mr-waters|Mr. Waters]]; [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]] came in, leading Effie, and this

[043-019] gave him a great pleasure. Mrs. Bowen seemed to have grown younger and

[043-020] better. [[imogene-graham|Imogene]] was not among the phantoms who visited him; and he

[043-021] accepted her absence as quiescently as he accepted the presence of the

[043-022] others. There was a cheerfulness in those who came that permitted him no

[043-023] anxiety, and he was too weak to invite it by any conjecture. He

[043-024] consented to be spared and to spare himself; and there were some things

[043-025] about the affair which gave him a singular and perhaps not wholly sane

[043-026] content. One of these was the man nurse who had evidently taken care of

[043-027] him throughout. He celebrated, whenever he looked at this capable

[043-028] person, his escape from being, in the odious helplessness of sickness, a

[043-029] burden upon the strength and sympathy of the two women for whom he had

[043-030] otherwise made so much trouble. His satisfaction in this had much to do

[043-031] with his recovery, which, when it once began, progressed rapidly to a

[043-032] point where he was told that Imogene and her mother were at a hotel in

[043-033] [[florence|Florence]], waiting till he should be strong enough to see them. It was

[043-034] Mrs. Bowen who told him this with an air which she visibly strove to

[043-035] render non-committal and impersonal, but which betrayed, nevertheless, a

[043-036] faint apprehension for the effect upon him. The attitude of Imogene and

[043-037] her mother was certainly not one to have been expected of people holding

[043-038] their nominal relation to him, but Colville had been revising his

[043-039] impressions of events on the day of his accident; Imogene's last look

[043-040] came back to him, and he could not think the situation altogether

[043-041] unaccountable.

[043-042] "Have I been here a long time?" he asked, as if he had not heeded what

[043-043] she told him.

[043-044] "[[About]] a fortnight," answered Mrs. Bowen.

[043-045] "And Imogene--how long has she been away?"

[043-046] "Since they knew you would get well."

[043-047] "I will see them any time," he said quietly.

[043-048] "Do you think you are strong enough?"

[043-049] "I shall never be stronger till I have seen them," he returned, with a

[043-050] glance at her. "Yes; I want them to come to-day. I shall not be excited;

[043-051] don't be troubled--if you were going to be," he added. "Please send to

[043-052] them at once."

[043-053] Mrs. Bowen hesitated, but after a moment left the room. She returned in

[043-054] half an hour with a lady who revealed even to Colville's languid regard

[043-055] evidences of the character which Mrs. Bowen had attributed to Imogene's

[043-056] mother. She was a large, robust person, laced to sufficient shapeliness,

[043-057] and she was well and simply dressed. She entered the room with a waft of

[043-058] some clean, wholesome perfume, and a quiet temperament and perfect

[043-059] health looked out of her clear, honest eyes--the eyes of Imogene Graham,

[043-060] though the girl's were dark and the woman's were blue. When Mrs. Bowen

[043-061] had named them to each other, in withdrawing, Mrs. Graham took

[043-062] Colville's weak left hand in her fresh, strong, right, and then lifted

[043-063] herself a chair to his bedside, and sat down.

[043-064] "How do you do to-day, sir?" she said, with a touch of old-fashioned

[043-065] respectfulness in the last word. "Do you think you are quite strong

[043-066] enough to talk with me?"

[043-067] "I think so," said Colville, with a faint smile. "At least I can listen

[043-068] with fortitude."

[043-069] Mrs. Graham was not apparently a person adapted to joking. "I don't know

[043-070] whether it will require much fortitude to hear what I have to say or

[043-071] not," she said, with her keen gaze fixed upon him. "It's simply this: I

[043-072] am going to take Imogene home."

[043-073] She seemed to expect that Colville would make some reply to this, and he

[043-074] said blankly, "Yes?"

[043-075] "I came out prepared to consent to what she wished, after I had seen

[043-076] you, and satisfied myself that she was not mistaken; for I had always

[043-077] promised myself that her choice should be perfectly untrammelled, and I

[043-078] have tried to bring her up with principles and ideas that would enable

[043-079] her to make a good choice."

[043-080] "Yes," said Colville again. "I'm afraid you didn't take her temperament

[043-081] and her youth into account, and that she disappointed you."

[043-082] "No; I can't say that she did. It isn't that at all. I see no reason to

[043-083] blame her for her choice. Her mistake was of another kind."

[043-084] It appeared to Colville that this very sensible and judicial lady found

[043-085] an intellectual pleasure in the analysis of the case, which modified the

[043-086] intensity of her maternal feeling in regard to it, and that, like many

[043-087] people who talk well, she liked to hear herself talk in the presence of

[043-088] another appreciative listener. He did not offer to interrupt her, and

[043-089] she went on. "No, sir, I am not disappointed in her choice. I think her

[043-090] chances of happiness would have been greater, in the abstract, with one

[043-091] nearer her own age; but that is a difference which other things affect

[043-092] so much that it did not alarm me greatly. Some people are younger at

[043-093] your age than at hers. No, sir, that is not the point." Mrs. Graham

[043-094] fetched a sigh, as if she found it easier to say what was not the point

[043-095] than to say what was, and her clear gaze grew troubled. But she

[043-096] apparently girded herself for the struggle. "As far as you are

[043-097] concerned, Mr. Colville, I have not a word to say. Your conduct

[043-098] throughout has been most high-minded and considerate and delicate."

[043-099] It is hard for any man to deny merits attributed to him, especially if

[043-100] he has been ascribing to himself the opposite demerits. But Colville

[043-101] summoned his dispersed forces to protest against this.

[043-102] "Oh, no, no," he cried. "Anything but that. My conduct has been selfish

[043-103] and shameful. If you could understand all--"

[043-104] "I think I do understand all--at least far more, I regret to say, than

[043-105] my daughter has been willing to tell me. And I am more than satisfied

[043-106] with you. I thank you and honour you."

[043-107] "Oh no; don't say that," pleaded Colville. "I really can't stand it."

[043-108] "And when I came here it was with the full intention of approving and

[043-109] confirming Imogene's decision. But I was met at once by a painful and

[043-110] surprising state of things. You are aware that you have been very sick?"

[043-111] "Dimly," said Colville.

[043-112] "I found you very sick, and I found my daughter frantic at the error

[043-113] which she had discovered in herself--discovered too late, as she felt."

[043-114] Mrs. Graham hesitated, and then added abruptly, "She had found out that

[043-115] she did not love you."

[043-116] "Didn't love me?" repeated Colville feebly.

[043-117] "She had been conscious of the truth before, but she had stifled her

[043-118] misgivings insanely, and, as I feel, almost wickedly, pushing on, and

[043-119] saying to herself that when you were married, then there would be no

[043-120] escape, and she _must_ love you."

[043-121] "Poor girl! poor child! I see, I see."

[043-122] "But the accident that was almost your death saved her from that

[043-123] miserable folly and iniquity. Yes," she continued, in answer to the

[043-124] protest in his face, "folly and iniquity. I found her half crazed at

[043-125] your bedside. She was fully aware of your danger, but while she was

[043-126] feeling all the remorse that she ought to feel--that any one could

[043-127] feel--she was more and more convinced that she never had loved you and

[043-128] never should. I can give you no idea of her state of mind."

[043-129] "Oh, you needn't! you needn't! Poor, poor child!"

[043-130] "Yes, a child indeed. If it had not been for the pity I felt for

[043-131] her--But no matter about that. She saw at last that if your heroic

[043-132] devotion to her"--Colville did his best to hang his pillowed head for

[043-133] shame--"if your present danger did not awaken her to some such feeling

[043-134] for you as she had once imagined she had; if they both only increased

[043-135] her despair and self-abhorrence, then the case was indeed hopeless. She

[043-136] was simply distracted. I had to tear her away almost by force. She has

[043-137] had a narrow escape from brain-fever. And now I have come to implore, to

[043-138] _demand_"--Mrs. Graham, with all her poise and calm, was rising to the

[043-139] hysterical key--"her release from a fate that would be worse than death

[043-140] for such a girl. I mean marrying without the love of her whole soul. She

[043-141] esteems you, she respects you, she admires you, she likes you; but--"

[043-142] Mrs. Graham pressed her lips together, and her eyes shone.

[043-143] "She is free," said Colville, and with the words a mighty load rolled

[043-144] from his heart. "There is no need to demand anything."

[043-145] "I know."

[043-146] "There hasn't been an hour, an instant, during--since I--we--spoke

[043-147] together that I wouldn't have released her if I could have known what

[043-148] you tell me now."

[043-149] "Of course!--of course!"

[043-150] "I have had my fears--my doubts; but whenever I approached the point I

[043-151] found no avenue by which we could reach a clearer understanding. I could

[043-152] not say much without seeming to seek for myself the release I was

[043-153] offering her."

[043-154] "Naturally. And what added to her wretchedness was the suspicion at

[043-155] the bottom of all that she had somehow forced herself upon

[043-156] you--misunderstood you, and made you say and do things to spare her that

[043-157] you would not have done voluntarily." This was advanced tentatively. In

[043-158] the midst of his sophistications Colville had, as most of his sex have,

[043-159] a native, fatal, helpless truthfulness, which betrayed him at the most

[043-160] unexpected moments, and this must now have appeared in his countenance.

[043-161] The lady rose haughtily. She had apparently been considering him, but,

[043-162] after all, she must have been really considering her daughter. "If

[043-163] anything of the kind was the case," she said, "I will ask you to spare

[043-164] her the killing knowledge. It's quite enough for _me_ to know it. And

[043-165] allow me to say, Mr. Colville, that it would have been far kinder in

[043-166] you--"

[043-167] "Ah, _think,_ my dear madam!" he exclaimed. "How _could_ I?"

[043-168] She did think, evidently, and when she spoke it was with a generous

[043-169] emotion, in which there was no trace of pique.

[043-170] "You couldn't. You have done right; I feel that, and I will trust you to

[043-171] say anything you will to my daughter."

[043-172] "To your daughter? Shall I see her?"

[043-173] "She came with me. She wished to beg your forgiveness."

[043-174] Colville lay silent. "There is no forgiveness to be asked or granted,"

[043-175] he said, at length. "Why should she suffer the pain of seeing me?--for

[043-176] it would be nothing else. What do you think? Will it do her any good

[043-177] hereafter? I don't care for myself."

[043-178] "I don't know what to think," said Mrs. Graham. "She is a strange child.

[043-179] She may have some idea of reparation."
