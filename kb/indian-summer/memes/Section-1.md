[001-008] INDIAN SUMMER

[001-009] * * * * *

[001-010] I

[001-011] Midway of the [[ponte-vecchio|Ponte Vecchio]] at [[florence|Florence]], where three arches break the

[001-012] lines of the little jewellers' booths glittering on either hand, and

[001-013] open an approach to the parapet, [[theodore-colville|Colville]] lounged against the corner of

[001-014] a shop and stared out upon the river. It was the late afternoon of a day

[001-015] in January, which had begun bright and warm, but had suffered a change

[001-016] of mood as its hours passed, and now, from a sky dimmed with flying grey

[001-017] clouds, was threatening rain. There must already have been rain in the

[001-018] mountains, for the yellow torrent that seethed and swirled around the

[001-019] piers of the bridge was swelling momently on the wall of the [[lungarno|Lung' Arno]],

[001-020] and rolling a threatening flood toward the [[cascine|Cascine]], where it lost itself

[001-021] under the ranks of the poplars that seemed to file across its course,

[001-022] and let their delicate tops melt into the pallor of the low horizon.

[001-023] The city, with the sweep of the Lung' [[arno-river|Arno]] on either hand, and its domes

[001-024] and towers hung in the dull air, and the country with its white villas

[001-025] and black cypresses breaking the grey stretches of the olive orchards on

[001-026] its hill-sides, had alike been growing more and more insufferable; and

[001-027] Colville was finding a sort of vindictive satisfaction in the power to

[001-028] ignore the surrounding frippery of landscape and architecture. He

[001-029] isolated himself so perfectly from it, as he brooded upon the river,

[001-030] that, for any sensible difference, he might have been standing on the

[001-031] Main Street Bridge at [[des-vaches|Des Vaches]], Indiana, looking down at the tawny

[001-032] sweep of the Wabash. He had no love for that stream, nor for the

[001-033] ambitious town on its banks, but ever since he woke that morning he had

[001-034] felt a growing conviction that he had been a great ass to leave them. He

[001-035] had, in fact, taken the prodigious risk of breaking his life sharp off

[001-036] from the course in which it had been set for many years, and of

[001-037] attempting to renew it in a direction from which it had long been

[001-038] diverted. Such an act could be precipitated only by a strong impulse of

[001-039] conscience or a profound disgust, and with Colville it sprang from

[001-040] disgust. He had experienced a bitter disappointment in the city to whose

[001-041] prosperity he had given the energies of his best years, and in whose

[001-042] favour he imagined that he had triumphantly established himself.

[001-043] He had certainly made the Des Vaches _[[democratic-republican|Democrat-Republican]]_ a very good

[001-044] paper; its ability was recognised throughout the State, and in Des

[001-045] Vaches people of all parties were proud of it. They liked every morning

[001-046] to see what Colville said; they believed that in his way he was the

[001-047] smartest man in the State, and they were fond of claiming that there was

[001-048] no such writer on any of the Indianapolis papers. They forgave some

[001-049] political heresies to the talent they admired; they permitted him the

[001-050] whim of free trade, they laughed tolerantly when he came out in favour

[001-051] of civil service reform, and no one had much fault to find when the

[001-052] _Democrat-Republican_ bolted the nomination of a certain politician of

[001-053] its party for Congress. But when Colville permitted his own name to be

[001-054] used by the opposing party, the people arose in their might and defeated

[001-055] him by a tremendous majority. That was what the regular nominee said. It

[001-056] was a withering rebuke to treason, in the opinion of this gentleman; it

[001-057] was a good joke, anyway, with the Democratic managers who had taken

[001-058] Colville up, being all in the Republican family; whichever it was, it

[001-059] was a mortification for Colville which his pride could not brook. He

[001-060] stood disgraced before the community not only as a theorist and

[001-061] unpractical doctrinaire, but as a dangerous man; and what was worse, he

[001-062] could not wholly acquit himself of a measure of bad faith; his

[001-063] conscience troubled him even more than his pride. Money was found, and a

[001-064] printer bought up with it to start a paper in opposition to the

[001-065] _Democrat-Republican_. Then Colville contemptuously offered to sell out

[001-066] to the Republican committee in charge of the new enterprise, and they

[001-067] accepted his terms.

[001-068] In private life he found much of the old kindness returning to him; and

[001-069] his successful opponent took the first opportunity of heaping coals of

[001-070] fire on his head in the public street, when he appeared to the outer eye

[001-071] to be shaking hands with Colville. During the months that he remained to

[001-072] close up his affairs after the sale of his paper, the

[001-073] _[[post-democratic-republican|Post-Democrat-Republican]]_ (the newspaper had agglutinated the titles of

[001-074] two of its predecessors, after the fashion of American journals) was

[001-075] fulsome in its complimentary allusions to him. It politely invented the

[001-076] fiction that he was going to Europe for his health, impaired by his

[001-077] journalistic labours, and adventurously promised its readers that they

[001-078] might hope to hear from him from time to time in its columns. In some

[001-079] of its allusions to him Colville detected the point of a fine irony, of

[001-080] which he had himself introduced the practice in the _Democrat-Republican_;

[001-081] and he experienced, with a sense of personal impoverishment, the

[001-082] curious fact that a journalist of strong characteristics leaves

[001-083] the tradition of himself in such degree with the journal he has

[001-084] created that he seems to bring very little away. He was obliged

[001-085] to confess in his own heart that the paper was as good as ever.

[001-086] The assistants, who had trained themselves to write like him, seemed to

[001-087] be writing quite as well, and his honesty would not permit him to

[001-088] receive the consolation offered him by the friends who told him that

[001-089] there was a great falling off in the _Post-Democrat-Republican_. Except

[001-090] that it was rather more Stalwart in its Republicanism, and had turned

[001-091] quite round on the question of the tariff, it was very much what it had

[001-092] always been. It kept the old decency of tone which he had given it, and

[001-093] it maintained the literary character which he was proud of. The new

[001-094] management must have divined that its popularity, with the women at

[001-095] least, was largely due to its careful selections of verse and fiction,

[001-096] its literary news, and its full and piquant criticisms, with their long

[001-097] extracts from new books. It was some time since he had personally looked

[001-098] after this department, and the young fellow in charge of it under him

[001-099] had remained with the paper. Its continued excellence, which he could

[001-100] not have denied if he had wished, seemed to leave him drained and

[001-101] feeble, and it was partly from the sense of this that he declined the

[001-102] overtures, well backed up with money, to establish an independent paper

[001-103] in Des Vaches. He felt that there was not fight enough in him for the

[001-104] work, even if he had not taken that strong disgust for public life which

[001-105] included the place and its people. He wanted to get away, to get far

[001-106] away, and with the abrupt and total change in his humour he reverted to

[001-107] a period in his life when journalism and politics and the ambition of

[001-108] Congress were things undreamed of.

[001-109] At that period he was a very young architect, with an inclination toward

[001-110] the literary side of his profession, which made it seem profitable to

[001-111] linger, with his Ruskin in his hand, among the masterpieces of Italian

[001-112] Gothic, when perhaps he might have been better employed in designing

[001-113] red-roofed many-verandaed, consciously mullioned seaside cottages on the

[001-114] New England coast. He wrote a magazine paper on the zoology of the

[001-115] Lombardic pillars in Verona, very Ruskinian, very scornful of modern

[001-116] motive. He visited every part of the peninsula, but he gave the greater

[001-117] part of his time to North Italy, and in Venice he met the young girl

[001-118] whom he followed to Florence. His love did not prosper; when she went

[001-119] away she left him in possession of that treasure to a man of his

[001-120] temperament, a [[romantic-disillusionment|broken heart]]. From that time his vague dreams began to

[001-121] lift, and to let him live in the clear light of common day; but he was

[001-122] still lingering at Florence, ignorant of the good which had befallen

[001-123] him, and cowering within himself under the sting of wounded vanity, when

[001-124] he received a letter from his elder brother suggesting that he should

[001-125] come and see how he liked the architecture of Des Vaches. His brother

[001-126] had been seven years at Des Vaches, where he had lands, and a lead-mine,

[001-127] and a scheme for a railroad, and had lately added a daily newspaper to

[001-128] his other enterprises. He had, in fact, added two newspapers; for having

[001-129] unexpectedly and almost involuntarily become the owner of the Des Vaches

[001-130] _Republican_, the fancy of building up a great local journal seized him,

[001-131] and he bought the _Wabash Valley Democrat_, uniting them under the name

[001-132] of the _Democrat-Republican_. But he had trouble almost from the first

[001-133] with his editors, and he naturally thought of the brother with a turn

[001-134] for writing who had been running to waste for the last year or two in

[001-135] Europe. His real purpose was to work Colville into the management of his

[001-136] paper when he invited him to come out and look at the architecture of

[001-137] Des Vaches.

[001-138] Colville went, because he was at that moment in the humour to go

[001-139] anywhere, and because his money was running low, and he must begin work

[001-140] somehow. He was still romantic enough to like the notion of the place a

[001-141] little, because it bore the name given to it by the old French

[001-142] _voyageurs_ from a herd of buffalo cows which they had seen grazing on

[001-143] the site of their camp there; but when he came to the place itself he

[001-144] did not like it. He hated it; but he stayed, and as an architect was the

[001-145] last thing any one wanted in Des Vaches since the jail and court-house

[001-146] had been built, he became, half without his willing it, a newspaper man.

[001-147] He learned in time to relish the humorous intimacy of the life about

[001-148] him; and when it was decided that he was no fool--there were doubts,

[001-149] growing out of his Eastern accent and the work of his New York tailor,

[001-150] at first--he found himself the object of a pleasing popularity. In due

[001-151] time he bought his brother out; he became very fond of newspaper life,

[001-152] its constant excitements and its endless variety; and six weeks before

[001-153] he sold his paper he would have scoffed at a prophecy of his return to

[001-154] Europe for the resumption of any artistic purpose whatever. But here he

[001-155] was, lounging on the Ponte Vecchio at Florence, whither he had come with

[001-156] the intention of rubbing up his former studies, and of perhaps getting

[001-157] back to put them in practice at New York ultimately. He had said to

[001-158] himself before coming abroad that he was in no hurry; that he should

[001-159] take it very easily--he had money enough for that; yet he would keep

[001-160] architecture before him as an object, for he had lived long in a

[001-161] community where every one was intensely occupied, and he unconsciously

[001-162] paid to Des Vaches the tribute of feeling that an objectless life was

[001-163] disgraceful to a man.

[001-164] In the meantime he suffered keenly and at every moment the loss of the

[001-165] occupation of which he had bereaved himself; in thinking of quite other

[001-166] things, in talk of totally different matters, from the dreams of night,

[001-167] he woke with a start to the realisation of the fact that he had no

[001-168] longer a newspaper. He perceived now, as never before, that for fifteen

[001-169] years almost every breath of his life had been drawn with reference to

[001-170] his paper, and that without it he was in some sort lost, and, as it

[001-171] were, extinct. A tide of ridiculous home-sickness, which was an

[001-172] expression of this passionate regret for the life he had put behind him,

[001-173] rather than any longing for Des Vaches, swept over him, and the first

[001-174] passages of a letter to the _Post-Democrat-Republican_ began to shape

[001-175] themselves in his mind. He had always, when he left home for New York or

[001-176] Washington, or for his few weeks of summer vacation on the Canadian

[001-177] rivers or the New England coast, written back to his readers, in whom he

[001-178] knew he could count upon quick sympathy in all he saw and felt, and he

[001-179] now found himself addressing them with that frank familiarity which

[001-180] comes to the journalist, in minor communities, from the habit of print.

[001-181] He began by confessing to them the defeat of certain expectations with

[001-182] which he had returned to Florence, and told them that they must not look

[001-183] for anything like the ordinary letters of travel from him. But he was

[001-184] not so singular in his attitude toward the place as he supposed; for any

[001-185] tourist who comes to Florence with the old-fashioned expectation of

[001-186] impressions will probably suffer a disappointment, unless he arrives

[001-187] very young and for the first time. It is a city superficially so well

[001-188] known that it affects one somewhat like a collection of views of itself;

[001-189] they are from the most striking points, of course, but one has examined

[001-190] them before, and is disposed to be critical of them. Certain emotions,

[001-191] certain sensations failed to repeat themselves to Colville at sight of

[001-192] the familiar monuments, which seemed to wear a hardy and indifferent

[001-193] air, as if being stared at so many years by so many thousands of

[001-194] travellers had extinguished in them that sensibility which one likes to

[001-195] fancy in objects of interest everywhere.
