[040-001] [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]] turned white; she did not speak.

[040-002] The girl wrung her hands. "Sometimes it seems as if he didn't--as if I

[040-003] had forced myself on him through a mistake, and he had taken me to save

[040-004] me from the shame of knowing that I had made a mistake. Do you think

[040-005] that is true? If you can only tell me that it isn't--Or, no! If it is

[040-006] true, tell me that! _That_ would be real mercy."

[040-007] The other trembled, as if physically beaten upon by this appeal. But she

[040-008] gathered herself together rigidly. "How can I answer you such a thing as

[040-009] that? I mustn't listen to you; you mustn't ask me." She turned and left

[040-010] the girl standing still in her attitude of imploring. But in her own

[040-011] room, where she locked herself in, sobs mingled with the laughter which

[040-012] broke crazily from her lips as she removed this ribbon and that jewel,

[040-013] and pulled the bracelets from her wrists. A man would have plunged from

[040-014] the house and walked the night away; a woman must wear it out in her

[040-015] bed.

[040-016] XX

[040-017] In the morning Mrs. Bowen received a note from her banker covering a

[040-018] despatch by cable from America. It was from [[imogene-graham|Imogene]]'s mother; it

[040-019] acknowledged the letters they had written, and announced that she sailed

[040-020] that day for Liverpool. It was dated at New York, and it was to be

[040-021] inferred that after perhaps writing in answer to their letters, she had

[040-022] suddenly made up her mind to come out.

[040-023] "Yes, that is it," said Imogene, to whom Mrs. Bowen hastened with the

[040-024] despatch. "Why should she have telegraphed to _you_?" she asked coldly,

[040-025] but with a latent fire of resentment in her tone.

[040-026] "You must ask her when she comes," returned Mrs. Bowen, with all her

[040-027] gentleness. "It won't be long now."

[040-028] They looked as if they had neither of them slept; but the girl's vigil

[040-029] seemed to have made her wild and fierce, like some bird that has beat

[040-030] itself all night against its cage, and still from time to time feebly

[040-031] strikes the bars with its wings. Mrs. Bowen was simply worn to apathy.

[040-032] "What shall you do about this?" she asked.

[040-033] "Do about it? Oh, I will think. I will try not to trouble you."

[040-034] "Imogene!"

[040-035] "I shall have to tell Mr. [[theodore-colville|Colville]]. But I don't know that I shall tell

[040-036] him at once. Give me the despatch, please." She possessed herself of it

[040-037] greedily, offensively. "I shall ask you not to speak of it."

[040-038] "I will do whatever you wish."

[040-039] "Thank you."

[040-040] Mrs. Bowen left the room, but she turned immediately to re-open the door

[040-041] she had closed behind her.

[040-042] "We were to have gone to [[fiesole|Fiesole]] to-morrow," she said inquiringly.

[040-043] "We can still go if the day is fine," returned the girl. "Nothing is

[040-044] changed. I wish very much to go. Couldn't we go to-day?" she added, with

[040-045] eager defiance.

[040-046] "It's too late to-day," said Mrs. Bowen quietly. "I will write to remind

[040-047] the gentlemen."

[040-048] "Thank you. I wish we could have gone to-day."

[040-049] "You can have the carriage if you wish to drive anywhere," said Mrs.

[040-050] Bowen.

[040-051] "I will take Effie to see [[mrs-amsden|Mrs. Amsden]]." But Imogene changed her mind,

[040-052] and went to call upon two Misses Guicciardi, the result of an

[040-053] international marriage, whom Mrs. Bowen did not like very well. Imogene

[040-054] drove with them to the [[cascine|Cascine]], where they bowed to a numerous military

[040-055] acquaintance, and they asked her if Mrs. Bowen would let her join them

[040-056] in a theatre party that evening: they were New-Yorkers by birth, and it

[040-057] was to be a theatre party in the New York style; they were to be

[040-058] chaperoned by a young married lady; two young men cousins of theirs,

[040-059] just out from America, had taken the box.

[040-060] When Imogene returned home she told Mrs. Bowen that she had accepted

[040-061] this invitation. Mrs. Bowen said nothing, but when one of the young men

[040-062] came up to hand Imogene down to the carriage, which was waiting with the

[040-063] others at the gate, she could not have shown a greater tolerance of his

[040-064] second-rate New Yorkiness if she had been a Boston dowager offering him

[040-065] the scrupulous hospitalities of her city.

[040-066] Imogene came in at midnight; she hummed an air of the opera as she took

[040-067] off her wraps and ornaments in her room, and this in the quiet of the

[040-068] hour had a terrible, almost profane effect: it was as if some other kind

[040-069] of girl had whistled. She showed the same nonchalance at breakfast,

[040-070] where she was prompt, and answered Mrs. Bowen's inquiries about her

[040-071] pleasure the night before with a liveliness that ignored the polite

[040-072] resolution that prompted them.

[040-073] [[mr-morton|Mr. Morton]] was the first to arrive, and if his discouragement began at

[040-074] once, the first steps masked themselves in a reckless welcome, which

[040-075] seemed to fill him with joy, and Mrs. Bowen with silent perplexity. The

[040-076] girl ran on about her evening at the opera, and about the weather, and

[040-077] the excursion they were going to make; and after an apparently needless

[040-078] ado over the bouquet which he brought her, together with one for Mrs.

[040-079] Bowen, she put it into her belt, and made Colville notice it when he

[040-080] came: he had not thought to bring flowers.

[040-081] He turned from her hilarity with anxious question to Mrs. Bowen, who did

[040-082] not meet his eye, and who snubbed Effie when the child found occasion to

[040-083] whisper: "_I_ think Imogene is acting very strangely, for _her_; don't

[040-084] you, mamma? It seems as if going with those Guicciardi girls just once

[040-085] had spoiled her."

[040-086] "Don't make remarks about people, Effie," said her mother sharply. "It

[040-087] isn't nice in little girls, and I don't want you to do it. You talk too

[040-088] much lately."

[040-089] Effie turned grieving away from this rejection, and her face did not

[040-090] light up even at the whimsical sympathy in Colville's face, who saw that

[040-091] she had met a check of some sort; he had to take her on his knee and

[040-092] coax and kiss her before her wounded feelings were visibly healed. He

[040-093] put her down with a sighing wish that some one could take him up and

[040-094] soothe his troubled sensibilities too, and kept her hand in his while he

[040-095] sat waiting for the last of those last moments in which the hurrying

[040-096] delays of ladies preparing for an excursion seem never to end.

[040-097] When they were ready to get into the carriage, the usual contest of

[040-098] [[sacrifice-and-renunciation|self-sacrifice]] arose, which Imogene terminated by mounting to the front

[040-099] seat; Mr. Morton hastened to take the seat beside her, and Colville was

[040-100] left to sit with Effie and her mother. "You old people will be safer

[040-101] back there," said Imogene. It was a little joke which she addressed to

[040-102] the child, but a gleam from her eye as she turned to speak to the young

[040-103] man at her side visited Colville in desperate defiance. He wondered what

[040-104] she was about in that allusion to an idea which she had shrunk from so

[040-105] sensitively hitherto. But he found himself in a situation which he could

[040-106] not penetrate at any point. When he spoke with Mrs. Bowen, it was with a

[040-107] dark undercurrent of conjecture as to how and when she expected him to

[040-108] tell Mr. Morton of his relation to Imogene, or whether she still

[040-109] expected him to do it; when his eyes fell upon the face of the young

[040-110] man, he despaired as to the terms in which he should put the fact; any

[040-111] form in which he tacitly dramatised it remained very embarrassing, for

[040-112] he felt bound to say that while he held himself promised in the matter,

[040-113] he did not allow her to feel herself so.

[040-114] A sky of American blueness and vastness, a mellow sun, and a delicate

[040-115] breeze did all that these things could for them, as they began the long,

[040-116] devious climb of the hills crowned by the ancient Etruscan city. At

[040-117] first they were all in the constraint of their own and one another's

[040-118] moods, known or imagined, and no talk began till the young clergyman

[040-119] turned to Imogene and asked, after a long look at the smiling landscape,

[040-120] "What sort of weather do you suppose they are having at Buffalo to-day?"

[040-121] "At Buffalo?" she repeated, as if the place had only a dim existence in

[040-122] her remotest consciousness. "Oh! The ice isn't near out of the lake yet.

[040-123] You can't count on it before the first of May."

[040-124] "And the first of May comes sooner or later, according to the season,"

[040-125] said Colville. "I remember coming on once in the middle of the month,

[040-126] and the river was so full of ice between Niagara Falls and Buffalo that

[040-127] I had to shut the car window that I'd kept open all the way through

[040-128] Southern Canada. But we have very little of that local weather at home;

[040-129] our weather is as democratic and continental as our political

[040-130] constitution. Here it's March or May any time from September till June,

[040-131] according as there's snow on the mountains or not."

[040-132] The young man smiled. "But don't you like," he asked with deference,

[040-133] "this slow, orderly advance of the Italian spring, where the flowers

[040-134] seem to come out one by one, and every blossom has its appointed time?"

[040-135] "Oh yes, it's very well in its way; but I prefer the rush of the

[040-136] American spring; no thought of mild weather this morning; a warm, gusty

[040-137] rain to-morrow night; day after to-morrow a burst of blossoms and

[040-138] flowers and young leaves and birds. I don't know whether we were made

[040-139] for our climate or our climate was made for us, but its impatience and

[040-140] lavishness seem to answer some inner demand of our go-ahead souls. This

[040-141] happens to be the week of the peach blossoms here, and you see their

[040-142] pink everywhere to-day, and you don't see anything else in the blossom

[040-143] line. But imagine the American spring abandoning a whole week of her

[040-144] precious time to the exclusive use of peach blossoms! She wouldn't do

[040-145] it; she's got too many other things on hand."

[040-146] Effie had stretched out over Colville's lap, and with her elbow sunk

[040-147] deep in his knee, was resting her chin in her hand and taking the facts

[040-148] of the landscape thoroughly in. "Do they have just a week?" she asked.

[040-149] "Not an hour more or less," said Colville. "If they found an almond

[040-150] blossom hanging round anywhere after their time came, they would make an

[040-151] awful row; and if any lazy little peach-blow hadn't got out by the time

[040-152] their week was up, it would have to stay in till next year; the pear

[040-153] blossoms wouldn't let it come out."

[040-154] "Wouldn't they?" murmured the child, in dreamy sympathy with this

[040-155] belated peach-blow.

[040-156] "Well, that's what people say. In America it would be allowed to come

[040-157] out any time. It's a free country."

[040-158] Mrs. Bowen offered to draw Effie back to a posture of more decorum, but

[040-159] Colville put his arm round the little girl. "Oh, let her stay! It

[040-160] doesn't incommode me, and she must be getting such a novel effect of the

[040-161] landscape."

[040-162] The mother fell back into her former attitude of jaded passivity. He

[040-163] wondered whether she had changed her mind about having him speak to Mr.

[040-164] Morton; her quiescence might well have been indifference; one could have

[040-165] said, knowing the whole situation, that she had made up her mind to let

[040-166] things take their course, and struggle with them no longer.

[040-167] He could not believe that she felt content with him; she must feel far

[040-168] otherwise; and he took refuge, as he had the power of doing, from the

[040-169] discomfort of his own thoughts in jesting with the child, and mocking

[040-170] her with this extravagance and that; the discomfort then became merely a

[040-171] dull ache that insisted upon itself at intervals, like a grumbling

[040-172] tooth.

[040-173] The prospect was full of that mingled wildness and subordination that

[040-174] gives its supreme charm to the Italian landscape; and without elements

[040-175] of great variety, it combined them in infinite picturesqueness. There

[040-176] were olive orchards and vineyards, and again vineyards and olive

[040-177] orchards. Closer to the farm-houses and cottages there were peaches and

[040-178] other fruit trees and kitchen-gardens; broad ribbons of grain waved

[040-179] between the ranks of trees; around the white villas the spires of the

[040-180] cypresses pierced the blue air. Now and then they came to a villa with

[040-181] weather-beaten statues strutting about its parterres. A mild, pleasant

[040-182] heat brooded upon the fields and roofs, and the city, dropping lower and

[040-183] lower as they mounted, softened and blended its towers and monuments in

[040-184] a sombre mass shot with gleams of white.
