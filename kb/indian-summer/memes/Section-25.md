[025-001] The squalid accident had all the effect of intention, as he viewed it

[025-002] from without himself, and considered that the money ought to have been

[025-003] the first thing in his thoughts after he determined to go away. He must

[025-004] get the money somehow, and be off to Rome by the seven o'clock train. A

[025-005] whimsical suggestion, which was so good a bit of irony that it made him

[025-006] smile, flashed across him: he might borrow it of [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]]. She was, in

[025-007] fact, the only person in [[florence|Florence]] with whom he was at all on borrowing

[025-008] terms, and a sad sense of the sweetness of her lost friendship followed

[025-009] upon the antic notion. No; for once he could not go to Mrs. Bowen. He

[025-010] recollected now the many pleasant talks they had had together,

[025-011] confidential in virtue of their old acquaintance, and harmlessly

[025-012] intimate in many things. He recalled how, when he was feeling dull from

[025-013] the Florentine air, she had told him to take a little quinine, and he

[025-014] had found immediate advantage in it. These memories did (not) strike him as

[025-015] grotesque or ludicrous; he only felt their pathos. He was ashamed even

[025-016] to seem in anywise recreant further. If she should ever hear that he had

[025-017] lingered for thirty-six hours in Florence after he had told her he was

[025-018] going away, what could she think but that he had repented his decision?

[025-019] He determined to go down to the office of the hotel, and see if he could

[025-020] not make some arrangement with the landlord. It would be extremely

[025-021] distasteful, but his ample letter of credit would be at least a voucher

[025-022] of his final ability to pay. As a desperate resort he could go and try

[025-023] to get the money of [[mr-waters|Mr. Waters]].

[025-024] He put on his coat and hat, and opened the door to some one who was just

[025-025] in act to knock at it, and whom he struck against in the obscurity.

[025-026] "I beg your pardon," said the visitor.

[025-027] "Mr. Waters! Is it possible?" cried [[theodore-colville|Colville]], feeling something fateful

[025-028] in the chance. "I was just going to see you."

[025-029] "I'm fortunate in meeting you, then. Shall we go to my room?" he asked,

[025-030] at a hesitation in Colville's manner.

[025-031] "No, no," said the latter; "come in here." He led the way back into his

[025-032] room, and struck a match to light the candles on his chimney. Their dim

[025-033] rays fell upon the disorder his packing had left. "You must excuse the

[025-034] look of things," he said. "The fact is, I'm just going away. I'm going

[025-035] to Rome at seven o'clock."

[025-036] "Isn't this rather sudden?" asked the minister, with less excitement

[025-037] than the fact might perhaps have been expected to create in a friend. "I

[025-038] thought you intended to pass the winter in Florence."

[025-039] "Yes, I did--sit down, please--but I find myself obliged to cut my stay

[025-040] short. Won't you take off your coat?" he asked, taking off his own.

[025-041] "Thank you; I've formed the habit of keeping it on indoors," said Mr.

[025-042] Waters. "And I oughtn't to stay long, if you're to be off so soon."

[025-043] Colville gave a very uncomfortable laugh. "Why, the fact is, I'm not off

[025-044] so very soon unless you help me."

[025-045] "Ah?" returned the old gentleman, with polite interest.

[025-046] "Yes, I find myself in the absurd position of a man who has reckoned

[025-047] without his host. I have made all my plans for going, and have had my

[025-048] hotel bill sent to me in pursuance of that idea, and now I discover that

[025-049] I not only haven't money enough to pay it and get to Rome, but I haven't

[025-050] much more than half enough to pay it. I have credit galore," he said,

[025-051] trying to give the situation a touch of liveliness, "but the bank is

[025-052] shut."

[025-053] Mr. Waters listened to the statement with a silence concerning which

[025-054] Colville was obliged to form his conjectures. "That is unfortunate," he

[025-055] said sympathetically, but not encouragingly.

[025-056] Colville pushed on desperately. "It is, unless you can help me, Mr.

[025-057] Waters. I want you to lend me fifty dollars for as many hours."

[025-058] Mr. Waters shook his head with a compassionate smile. "I haven't fifty

[025-059] francs in cash. You are welcome to what there is. I'm very forgetful

[025-060] about money matters, and haven't been to the bankers."

[025-061] "Oh, don't excuse yourself to me, unless you wish to embitter my shame.

[025-062] I'm obliged to you for offering to share your destitution with me. I

[025-063] must try to run my face with the landlord," said Colville.

[025-064] "Oh no," said Mr. Waters gently. "Is there such haste as all that?"

[025-065] "Yes, I must go at once."

[025-066] "I don't like to have you apply to a stranger," said the old man, with

[025-067] fatherly kindness. "Can't you remain over till Monday? I had a little

[025-068] excursion to propose."

[025-069] "No, I can't possibly stay; I must go to-night," cried Colville.

[025-070] The minister rose. "Then I really mustn't detain you, I suppose.

[025-071] Good-bye." He offered his hand. Colville took it, but could not let it

[025-072] go at once. "I would like extremely to tell you why I'm leaving Florence

[025-073] in such haste. But I don't see what good it would do, for I don't want

[025-074] you to persuade me to stay."

[025-075] The old gentleman looked at him with friendly interest.

[025-076] "The fact is," Colville proceeded, as if he had been encouraged to do

[025-077] so, "I have had the misfortune--yes, I'm afraid I've had the fault--to

[025-078] make myself very displeasing to Mrs. Bowen, and in such a way that the

[025-079] very least I can do is to take myself off as far and as soon as I

[025-080] conveniently can."

[025-081] "Yes?" said Mr. Waters, with the cheerful note of incredulity in his

[025-082] voice with which one is apt to respond to others' confession of

[025-083] extremity. "Is it so bad as that? I've just seen Mrs. Bowen, and she

[025-084] told me you were going."

[025-085] "Oh," said Colville, with disagreeable sensation, "perhaps she told you

[025-086] why I was going."

[025-087] "No," answered Mr. Waters; "she didn't do that." Colville imagined a

[025-088] consciousness in him, which perhaps did not exist. "She didn't allude to

[025-089] the subject further than to state the fact, when I mentioned that I was

[025-090] coming to see you."

[025-091] Colville had dropped his hand. "She was very forbearing," he said, with

[025-092] bitterness that might well have been incomprehensible to Mr. Waters upon

[025-093] any theory but one.

[025-094] "Perhaps," he suggested, "you are precipitate; perhaps you have

[025-095] mistaken; perhaps you have been hasty. These things are often the result

[025-096] of impulse in women. I have often wondered how they could make up their

[025-097] minds; I believe they certainly ought to be allowed to change them at

[025-098] least once."

[025-099] Colville turned very red. "What in the world do you mean? Do you imagine

[025-100] that I have been offering myself to Mrs. Bowen?"

[025-101] "Wasn't it that which you wished to--which you said you would like to

[025-102] tell me?"

[025-103] Colville was suddenly silent, on the verge of a self-derisive laugh.

[025-104] When he spoke, he said gently: "No; it wasn't that. I never thought of

[025-105] offering myself to her. We have always been very good friends. But now

[025-106] I'm afraid we can't be friends any more--at least we can't be

[025-107] acquaintances."

[025-108] "Oh!" exclaimed Mr. Waters. He waited a while as if for Colville to say

[025-109] more, but the latter remained silent, and the old man gave his hand

[025-110] again in farewell. "I must really be going. I hope you won't think me

[025-111] intrusive in my mistaken conjecture?"

[025-112] "Oh no."

[025-113] "It was what I supposed you had been telling me----"

[025-114] "I understand. You mustn't be troubled," said Colville, though he had to

[025-115] own to himself that it seemed superfluous to make this request of Mr.

[025-116] Waters, who was taking the affair with all the serenity of age

[025-117] concerning matters of sentiment. "I wish you were going to Rome with

[025-118] me," he added, to disembarrass the moment of parting.

[025-119] "Thank you. But I shall not go to Rome for some years. Shall you come

[025-120] back on your way in the spring?"

[025-121] "No, I shall not come to Florence again," said Colville sadly.

[025-122] "Ah, I'm sorry. Good-bye, my dear young friend. It's been a great

[025-123] pleasure to know you." Colville walked down to the door of the hotel

[025-124] with his visitor and parted with him there. As he turned back he met the

[025-125] landlord, who asked him if he would have the omnibus for the station.

[025-126] The landlord bowed smilingly, after his kind, and rubbed his hands. He

[025-127] said he hoped Colville was pleased with his hotel, and ran to his desk

[025-128] in the little office to get some cards for him, so that he might

[025-129] recommend it accurately to American families.

[025-130] Colville looked absently at the cards. "The fact is," he said, to the

[025-131] little bowing, smiling man; "I don't know but I shall be obliged to

[025-132] postpone my going till Monday." He smiled too, trying to give the fact a

[025-133] jocose effect, and added, "I find myself out of money, and I've no means

[025-134] of paying your bill till I can see my bankers."

[025-135] After all his heroic intention, this was as near as he could come to

[025-136] asking the landlord to let him send the money from Rome.

[025-137] The little man set his head on one side.

[025-138] "Oh, well, occupy the room until Monday, then," he cried hospitably. "It

[025-139] is quite at your disposition. You will not want the omnibus?"

[025-140] "No, I shall not want the omnibus," said Colville, with a laugh,

[025-141] doubtless not perfectly intelligible to the landlord, who respectfully

[025-142] joined him in it.

[025-143] He did not mean to stop that night without writing to Mrs. Bowen, and

[025-144] assuring her that though an accident had kept him in Florence till

[025-145] Monday, she need not be afraid of seeing him again. But he could not go

[025-146] back to his room yet; he wandered about the town, trying to pick himself

[025-147] up from the ruin into which he had fallen again, and wondering with a

[025-148] sort of alien compassion what was to become of his aimless, empty

[025-149] existence. As he passed through the [[piazza-san-marco|Piazza San Marco]] he had half a mind

[025-150] to pick a pebble from the gardened margin of the fountain there and toss

[025-151] it against the Rev. Mr. Waters's window, and when he put his skull-cap

[025-152] out, to ask that optimistic agnostic what a man had best do with a life

[025-153] that had ceased to interest him. But, for the time being, he got rid of

[025-154] himself as he best could by going to the opera. They professed to give

[025-155] _Rigoletto_, but it was all Mrs. Bowen and [[imogene-graham|Imogene Graham]] to Colville.

[025-156] It was so late when he got back to his hotel that the outer gate was

[025-157] shut, and he had to wake up the poor little porter, as on that night

[025-158] when he returned from [[madame-uccelli|Madame Uccelli]]'s. The porter was again equal to

[025-159] his duty, and contrived to light a new candle to show him the way to his

[025-160] room. The repetition, almost mechanical, of this small chicane made

[025-161] Colville smile, and this apparently encouraged the porter to ask, as if

[025-162] he supposed him to have been in society somewhere--

[025-163] "You have amused yourself this evening?

[025-164] "Oh, very much."

[025-165] "I am glad. There is a letter for you.'

[025-166] "A letter! Where?"

[025-167] "I sent it to your room. It came just before midnight."

[025-168] XIII

[025-169] Mrs. Bowen sat before the hearth in her _salon_, with her hands fallen

[025-170] in her lap. At thirty-eight the emotions engrave themselves more deeply

[025-171] in the face than they do in our first youth, or than they will when we

[025-172] have really aged, and the pretty woman looked haggard.

[025-173] Imogene came in, wearing a long blue robe, flung on as if with desperate

[025-174] haste; her thick hair fell crazily out of a careless knot, down her

[025-175] back. "I couldn't sleep," she said, with quivering lips, at the sight of

[025-176] which Mrs. Bowen's involuntary smile hardened. "Isn't it eleven yet?"

[025-177] she added, with a glance at the clock. "It seems years since I went to

[025-178] bed."
