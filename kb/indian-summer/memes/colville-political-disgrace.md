## Summary

Theodore Colville's political disgrace in Des Vaches, Indiana, is the backstory event that precipitates the entire action of *Indian Summer*. After fifteen years building the *Democrat-Republican* into a respected and widely admired newspaper, Colville permitted his own name to be used as a congressional candidate by the opposing party — an act of political independence that the community of Des Vaches interpreted as treachery (001-054–001-055). He was defeated by a tremendous majority, a result the regular nominee called "a withering rebuke to treason" (001-056). Colville stood disgraced before the community not only as a theorist and unpractical doctrinaire, but as a dangerous man; worse still, his own conscience troubled him, for he could not wholly acquit himself of a measure of bad faith (001-060–001-063).

In the aftermath, money was found and a printer bought up to launch an opposition paper. Colville contemptuously offered to sell out to the Republican committee in charge of the new enterprise, and they accepted his terms (001-064–001-065). During the months that he remained to close up his affairs, the *Post-Democrat-Republican* was fulsome in its complimentary allusions to him, politely inventing the fiction that he was going to Europe for his health, impaired by his journalistic labours (001-072–001-076). The disgrace — and his profound disgust with public life — drove Colville to abandon his fifteen-year career and return to Florence, setting the novel's plot in motion.

## Context

Colville's path to political disgrace began years earlier, when his elder brother invited him to Des Vaches to look at the architecture — a pretext for working him into the management of the newly consolidated *Democrat-Republican* (001-122–001-127). Colville went because he was at that moment in the humour to go anywhere, and because his money was running low. He hated Des Vaches but stayed, becoming, "half without his willing it, a newspaper man" (001-146).

Over the next fifteen years, Colville built the *Democrat-Republican* into a paper of genuine distinction. Its ability was recognised throughout the State, and in Des Vaches people of all parties were proud of it (001-083–001-084). They forgave his political heresies — free trade, civil service reform — because they admired his talent and believed him "the smartest man in the State" (001-085–001-088). He bought his brother out and became very fond of newspaper life, its constant excitements and its endless variety (001-151). But when the *Democrat-Republican* bolted the nomination of a certain politician of its own party for Congress, and Colville permitted his own name to be used by the opposing party, the community's tolerance snapped (001-052–001-054).

The disgrace was both public and private. Publicly, Colville was branded a traitor and a dangerous man; privately, his conscience troubled him even more than his pride, for he recognised that he had not acted in perfect good faith (001-060–001-063). The experience left him with a profound disgust for public life that "included the place and its people" (001-103). He declined overtures, well backed with money, to establish an independent paper in Des Vaches, feeling that "there was not fight enough in him for the work" (001-101–001-104). He wanted to get far away, and with the abrupt and total change in his humour he reverted to a period in his life when journalism and politics were undreamed of — his youth as an architectural student in Italy (001-105–001-109).

## Participants

- **Theodore Colville** — The central figure of the event. As editor and proprietor of the *Democrat-Republican*, he had spent fifteen years building the paper's reputation and his own standing in Des Vaches (001-083–001-088, 001-151). His decision to permit his name to be used by the opposing party was the act that triggered the scandal, and his inability to acquit himself of bad faith compounded his public disgrace with private shame (001-054, 001-060–001-063).

- **Colville's elder brother** — The brother who had settled in Des Vaches seven years earlier, acquiring lands, a lead-mine, a railroad scheme, and two newspapers which he consolidated into the *Democrat-Republican* (001-122–001-127). It was he who invited Colville to Des Vaches under the pretext of looking at the architecture, while his real purpose was to work Colville into the management of the paper. Colville eventually bought him out (001-151).

- **The Republican committee and the regular nominee** — The political opposition that capitalised on Colville's misstep. The regular nominee — the politician whose nomination the *Democrat-Republican* had bolted — characterised Colville's defeat as "a withering rebuke to treason" (001-055–001-056). The Republican committee raised money to start the rival *Post-Democrat-Republican* and ultimately purchased Colville's paper (001-064–001-065).

## Aftermath / Consequences

The political disgrace was the decisive rupture in Colville's life — the event that "broke his life sharp off from the course in which it had been set for many years" (001-039–001-041). Its consequences radiate through the entire novel.

The most immediate consequence was the sale of the *Democrat-Republican* and Colville's abandonment of the career that had defined him for fifteen years. The sale left him financially secure — he had "money enough" to live comfortably in Europe (001-159) — but emotionally impoverished. He experienced, with "a sense of personal impoverishment, the curious fact that a journalist of strong characteristics leaves the tradition of himself in such degree with the journal he has created that he seems to bring very little away" (001-078–001-080). The paper continued as well without him; the assistants who had trained themselves to write like him seemed to be writing quite as well, a discovery that left him drained and feeble (001-100–001-101).

His return to Europe was an attempt to recover an earlier, pre-journalistic self — the young architect who had lingered in Florence with his Ruskin, studying Italian Gothic (001-109–001-111). But this return was itself haunted by loss: "a tide of ridiculous home-sickness, which was an expression of this passionate regret for the life he had put behind him, rather than any longing for Des Vaches, swept over him" (001-172–001-174). The disgrace thus functions not merely as backstory but as the novel's psychological ground-bass — the reason Colville is in Florence at all, and the unresolved wound that makes him vulnerable to the novel's central romantic entanglements.

> He had, in fact, taken the prodigious risk of breaking his life sharp off from the course in which it had been set for many years, and of attempting to renew it in a direction from which it had long been diverted. Such an act could be precipitated only by a strong impulse of conscience or a profound disgust, and with Colville it sprang from disgust. (001-039–001-041)

> He stood disgraced before the community not only as a theorist and unpractical doctrinaire, but as a dangerous man; and what was worse, he could not wholly acquit himself of a measure of bad faith; his conscience troubled him even more than his pride. (001-060–001-063)
