[014-001] [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]] fingered the edges of her book. "You mustn't let your fancy

[014-002] run away with you, [[imogene-graham|Imogene]]," she said, with a little painful smile.

[014-003] "Oh, I _like_ to let it run away with me. And when I get such a subject

[014-004] as Mr. [[theodore-colville|Colville]], there's no stopping. I can't stop, and I don't _wish_

[014-005] to stop. Shouldn't you have thought that he would have been perfectly

[014-006] crushed at the exhibition he made of himself in the Lancers last night?

[014-007] He wasn't the least embarrassed when he met me, and the only allusion he

[014-008] made to it was to say that he had been up late, and had danced too much.

[014-009] Wasn't it wonderful he could do it? Oh, if _I_ could do that!"

[014-010] "I wish he could have avoided the occasion for his bravado," said Mrs.

[014-011] Bowen.

[014-012] "I think I was a little to blame, perhaps," said the girl. "I beckoned

[014-013] him to come and take the vacant place."

[014-014] "I don't see that that was an excuse," returned Mrs. Bowen primly.

[014-015] Imogene seemed insensible to the tone, as it concerned herself; it only

[014-016] apparently reminded her of something. "Guess what Mr. Colville said,

[014-017] when I had been silly, and then tried to make up for it by being very

[014-018] dignified all of a sudden?"

[014-019] "I don't know. How had you been silly?"

[014-020] The servant brought in some cards. Imogene caught up the pelisse which

[014-021] she had been gradually shedding as she sat talking to Mrs. Bowen, and

[014-022] ran out of the room by another door.

[014-023] They did not recur to the subject. But that night, when Mrs. Bowen went

[014-024] to say good night to Effie, after the child had gone to bed, she

[014-025] lingered.

[014-026] "Effie," she said at last, in a husky whisper, "what did Imogene say to

[014-027] Mr. Colville to-day that made him laugh?"

[014-028] "I don't know," said the child. "They kept laughing at so many things."

[014-029] "Laughing?"

[014-030] "Yes; he laughed. Do you mean toward the last, when he had been throwing

[014-031] stones into the river?"

[014-032] "It must have been then."

[014-033] The child stretched herself drowsily. "Oh I couldn't understand it all.

[014-034] She wanted to throw a stone in the river, but he told her she had better

[014-035] not. But that didn't make _him_ laugh. She was so very stiff just

[014-036] afterward that he said the weather had changed, and that made _us_

[014-037] laugh."

[014-038] "Was that all?"

[014-039] "We kept laughing ever so long. I never saw any one like Mr. Colville.

[014-040] How queerly the fire shines on your face! It gives you such a beautiful

[014-041] complexion."

[014-042] "Does it?"

[014-043] "Yes, lovely." The child's mother stooped over and kissed her. "You're

[014-044] the prettiest mamma in the world," she said, throwing her arms round her

[014-045] neck. "Sometimes I can't tell whether Imogene is prettier or not, but

[014-046] to-night I'm certain you are. Do you like to have me think that?"

[014-047] "Yes--yes. But don't pull me down so; you hurt my neck. Good night."

[014-048] The child let her go. "I haven't said my prayer yet, mamma. I was

[014-049] thinking."

[014-050] "Well, say it now, then," said the mother gently.

[014-051] When the child had finished she turned upon her cheek. "Good night,

[014-052] mamma."

[014-053] Mrs. Bowen went about the room a little while, picking up its pretty

[014-054] disorder. Then she sat down in a chair by the hearth, where a log was

[014-055] still burning. The light of the flame flickered upon her face, and threw

[014-056] upon the ceiling a writhing, fantastic shadow, the odious caricature of

[014-057] her gentle beauty.

[014-058] VIII

[014-059] In that still air of the Florentine winter, time seems to share the

[014-060] arrest of the natural forces, the repose of the elements. The pale blue

[014-061] sky is frequently overcast, and it rains two days out of five;

[014-062] sometimes, under extraordinary provocation from the north a snow-storm

[014-063] whirls along under the low grey dome, and whitens the brown roofs, where

[014-064] a growth of spindling weeds and grass clothes the tiles the whole year

[014-065] round, and shows its delicate green above the gathered flakes. But for

[014-066] the most part the winds are laid, and the sole change is from quiet sun

[014-067] to quiet shower. This at least is the impression which remains in the

[014-068] senses of the sojourning stranger, whose days slip away with so little

[014-069] difference one from another that they seem really not to have passed,

[014-070] but, like the grass that keeps the hillsides fresh round [[florence|Florence]] all

[014-071] the winter long, to be waiting some decisive change of season before

[014-072] they begin.

[014-073] The first of the Carnival sights that marked the lapse of a month since

[014-074] his arrival took Colville by surprise. He could not have believed that

[014-075] it was February yet if it had not been for the straggling maskers in

[014-076] armour whom he met one day in Via Borgognissanti, with their visors up

[014-077] for their better convenience in smoking. They were part of the chorus at

[014-078] one of the theatres, and they were going about to eke out their salaries

[014-079] with the gifts of people whose windows the festival season privileged

[014-080] them to play under. The silly spectacle stirred Colville's blood a

[014-081] little, as any sort of holiday preparation was apt to do. He thought

[014-082] that it afforded him a fair occasion to call at [[palazzo-pinti|Palazzo Pinti]], where he

[014-083] had not been so much of late as in the first days of his renewed

[014-084] acquaintance with Mrs. Bowen. He had at one time had the fancy that Mrs.

[014-085] Bowen was cool toward him. He might very well have been mistaken in

[014-086] this; in fact, she had several times addressed him the politest

[014-087] reproaches for not coming, but he made some evasion, and went only on

[014-088] the days when she was receiving other people, and when necessarily he

[014-089] saw very little of the family.

[014-090] Miss Graham was always very friendly, but always very busy, drawing tea

[014-091] from the samovar, and looking after others. [[effie-bowen|Effie Bowen]] dropped her eyes

[014-092] in re-established strangeness when she brought the basket of cake to

[014-093] him. There was one moment when he suspected that he had been talked over

[014-094] in family council, and put under a certain regimen. But he had no proof

[014-095] of this, and it had really nothing to do with his keeping away, which

[014-096] was largely accidental. He had taken up, with as much earnestness as he

[014-097] could reasonably expect of himself, that notion of studying the

[014-098] architectural expression of Florentine character at the different

[014-099] periods. He had spent a good deal of money in books, he had revived his

[014-100] youthful familiarity with the city, and he had made what acquaintance he

[014-101] could with people interested in such matters. He met some of these in

[014-102] the limited but very active society in which he mingled daily and

[014-103] nightly. After the first strangeness to any sort of social life had worn

[014-104] off, he found himself very fond of the prompt hospitalities which his

[014-105] introduction at Mrs. Bowen's had opened to him. His host--or more

[014-106] frequently it was his hostess--had sometimes merely an apartment at a

[014-107] hotel; perhaps the family was established in one of the furnished

[014-108] lodgings which stretch the whole length of the [[lungarno|Lung' Arno]] on either

[014-109] hand, and abound in all the new streets approaching the [[cascine|Cascine]], and had

[014-110] set up the simple and facile housekeeping of the sojourner in Florence

[014-111] for a few months; others had been living in the villa or the palace they

[014-112] had taken for years.

[014-113] The more recent and transitory people expressed something of the

[014-114] prevailing English and American aestheticism in the decoration of their

[014-115] apartments, but the greater part accepted the Florentine drawing-room as

[014-116] their landlord had imagined it for them, with furniture and curtains in

[014-117] yellow satin, a cheap ingrain carpet thinly covering the stone floor,

[014-118] and a fire of little logs ineffectually blazing on the hearth, and

[014-119] flickering on the carved frames of the pictures on the wall and the

[014-120] nakedness of the frescoed allegories in the ceiling. Whether of longer

[014-121] or shorter stay, the sojourners were bound together by a common language

[014-122] and a common social tradition; they all had a Day, and on that day there

[014-123] was tea and bread and butter for every comer. They had one another to

[014-124] dine; there were evening parties, with dancing and without dancing.

[014-125] Colville even went to a fancy ball, where he was kept in countenance by

[014-126] several other Florentines of the period of Romola. At all these places

[014-127] he met nearly the same people, whose alien life in the midst of the

[014-128] native community struck him as one of the phases of modern civilisation

[014-129] worthy of note, if not particular study; for he fancied it destined to a

[014-130] wider future throughout Europe, as the conditions in England and America

[014-131] grow more tiresome and more onerous. They seemed to see very little of

[014-132] Italian society, and to be shut out from practical knowledge of the

[014-133] local life by the terms upon which they had themselves insisted. Our

[014-134] race finds its simplified and cheapened London or New York in all its

[014-135] Continental resorts now, but nowhere has its taste been so much studied

[014-136] as in Italy, and especially in Florence. It was not, perhaps, the real

[014-137] Englishman or American who had been considered, but a _forestière_

[014-138] conventionalised from the Florentine's observation of many Anglo-Saxons.

[014-139] But he had been so well conjectured that he was hemmed round with a very

[014-140] fair illusion of his national circumstances.

[014-141] It was not that he had his English or American doctor to prescribe for

[014-142] him when sick, and his English or American apothecary to compound his

[014-143] potion; it was not that there was an English tailor and an American

[014-144] dentist, an English bookseller and an English baker, and chapels of

[014-145] every shade of Protestantism, with Catholic preaching in English every

[014-146] Sunday. These things were more or less matters of necessity, but

[014-147] Colville objected that the barbers should offer him an American shampoo;

[014-148] that the groceries should abound in English biscuit and our own canned

[014-149] fruit and vegetables, and that the grocers' clerks should be ambitious

[014-150] to read the labels of the Boston baked beans. He heard--though he did

[014-151] not prove this by experiment--that the master of a certain trattoria had

[014-152] studied the doughnut of New England till he had actually surpassed the

[014-153] original in the qualities that have undermined our digestion as a

[014-154] people. But above all it interested him to see that intense expression

[014-155] of American civilisation, the horse-car, triumphing along the

[014-156] magnificent avenues that mark the line of the old city walls; and he

[014-157] recognised an instinctive obedience to an abtruse natural law in the

[014-158] fact that whereas the omnibus, which the Italians have derived from the

[014-159] English, was not filled beyond its seating capacity, the horse-car was

[014-160] overcrowded without and within at Florence, just as it is with us who

[014-161] invented it.

[014-162] "I wouldn't mind even that," he said one day to the lady who was drawing

[014-163] him his fifth or sixth cup of tea for that afternoon, and with whom he

[014-164] was naturally making this absurd condition of things a matter of

[014-165] personal question; "but you people here pass your days in a round of

[014-166] unbroken English, except when you talk with your servants. I'm not sure

[014-167] you don't speak English with the shop people. I can hardly get them to

[014-168] speak Italian to me."

[014-169] "Perhaps they think you can speak English better," said the lady.

[014-170] This went over Florence; in a week it was told to Colville as something

[014-171] said to some one else. He fearlessly reclaimed it as said to himself,

[014-172] and this again was told. In the houses where he visited he had the

[014-173] friendly acceptance of any intelligent and reasonably agreeable person

[014-174] who comes promptly and willingly when he is asked, and seems always to

[014-175] have enjoyed himself when he goes away. But besides this sort of general

[014-176] favour, he enjoyed a very pleasing little personal popularity which came

[014-177] from his interest in other people, from his good-nature, and from his

[014-178] inertness. He slighted no acquaintance, and talked to every one with the

[014-179] same apparent wish to be entertaining. This was because he was incapable

[014-180] of the cruelty of open indifference when his lot was cast with a dull

[014-181] person, and also because he was mentally too lazy to contrive pretences

[014-182] for getting away; besides he did not really find anybody altogether a

[014-183] bore, and he had no wish to shine. He listened without shrinking to

[014-184] stories that he had heard before, and to things that had already been

[014-185] said to him; as has been noted, he had himself the habit of repeating

[014-186] his ideas with the recklessness of maturity, for he had lived long

[014-187] enough to know that this can be done with almost entire safety.
