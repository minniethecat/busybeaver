# Moral Cowardice

## Definition

Moral cowardice in *Indian Summer* names a specific and recurring pattern of behaviour in Theodore Colville: not the cowardice that flees physical danger, but the cowardice that cannot face a difficult truth, that postpones an honest confrontation, that drifts rather than decides. It is the paralysis of the will when moral action is called for — the evasion of responsibility through delay, self-deception, and a kind of ironic detachment that serves as armour against genuine self-examination. Howells treats this not as mere weakness but as a form of cowardice with real consequences: a young woman's heart is wrung, a friendship is nearly destroyed, and Colville repeatedly comes "just on the point of escaping the consequences" of his own passivity (045-009). The motif runs from Colville's first hesitant approaches to Imogene Graham through to the final crisis in which he at last confesses what he has done and permits himself to act.

## Context in the Novel

From the moment Colville arrives in Florence, his behaviour is marked by a curious passivity masquerading as good manners. He drifts into a flirtation with Imogene Graham without ever deciding to do so; he allows her illusions about him to grow unchecked because he is "flattered by an illusion of hers that ought to have pained and alarmed me" (045-007). He knows, at some level, that the engagement is a mistake, but he cannot bring himself to say so — and so he allows it to continue until Imogene herself breaks it off. His moral cowardice is most visible not in what he does but in what he fails to do.

The novel supplies a diagnostic vocabulary for this failure. When Colville goes to confront Mrs. Bowen after the veglione, he must first overcome "the cowardly impulse of his own heart, which would have deceived him with the suggestion that Mrs. Bowen might be occupied with Effie" (019-147 to 019-149). The language is precise: the impulse is not merely timid or cautious — it is "cowardly," and it operates through self-deception, inventing plausible excuses to avoid the confrontation that Colville's conscience demands.

The most extended treatment of the motif occurs in the long conversation with Mr. Waters in Chapter 45, which functions as the novel's moral inquest. Colville makes his confession in language that traces the arc of his evasion: he admits that he "might have prevented it altogether" if he had "behaved with greater wisdom and dignity at the outset" (045-005 to 045-006), but instead he was "flattered by an illusion" and allowed events to follow "inevitably" — a word that serves to disguise his own agency. Mr. Waters listens and then delivers the novel's sharpest rebuke, not in the form of direct condemnation but through a philosophical reflection on puppet-strings and the "long time learning to act with common-sense or even common sanity in what are called matters of the affections" (045-102 to 045-103). The old minister's refusal to let Colville off the hook is itself a judgment on the moral cowardice that Colville has only just begun to recognise in himself.

The recognition, when it comes, is devastating: "The old gentleman rose, and Colville, dazed by the recognition of his own cowardice and absurdity, did not try to detain him" (045-108 to 045-109). The pairing of "cowardice" with "absurdity" is significant. Colville's moral failure is not tragic in the grand sense; it is faintly ridiculous — the cowardice of a man who prides himself on his sophistication and irony and discovers that these qualities are not a substitute for courage.

The novel does not leave Colville in this state of paralysis. In the final chapter, his "instinct of forbearance" serves him "better than the subtlest art" (047-001), and his "submission was the best defence" (047-002). When Lina Bowen confronts him, he does not evade: "Remember," he says, "that I confess all you accuse me of, and that I acknowledge the justice of what you do — because you do it" (047-003 to 047-005). This is the moment at which moral cowardice is finally overcome: Colville stops drifting and submits to judgment, and in doing so earns the love that the novel has been preparing for him all along. The progression from cowardly evasion to honest submission constitutes the novel's moral arc.

## Key Passages

> But when a woman has done nothing she fills the soul of the man whose conscience troubles him with an instinctive apprehension. There is then no safety, his nerves tell him, except in bringing the affair, whatever it is, to an early issue — in having it out with her. Colville subdued the cowardly impulse of his own heart, which would have deceived him with the suggestion that Mrs. Bowen might be occupied with Effie, and it would be better to ask for Miss Graham. He asked for Mrs. Bowen, and she came in. (019-144 to 019-150)

This passage explicitly names Colville's moral failing as cowardice and anatomises its mechanism. The "cowardly impulse" does not present itself honestly as fear; it presents itself as reasonable prudence — Mrs. Bowen is busy, it would be better to ask for Imogene instead. The cowardly heart "deceives" its owner with plausible alternatives. That Colville "subdues" this impulse on this occasion marks one of the novel's few early victories over moral cowardice, but the language of subjugation suggests how strong the impulse remains and how much effort resistance requires.

> "The fact is that if I stay with you any longer I shall tell you something that I'm too old to tell and you're too old to hear." ... As he walked along, he seemed to himself to be merely crumbling away in this impulse and that, in one abortive intent and another. What did it matter? Had he been his whole life one of these weak wills which are a curse to themselves and others, and most a curse when they mean the best? Was that the secret of his failure in life? ... And now was he running away from Florence because his will was weak? (027-147 to 027-174)

Colville's aborted confession to Mr. Waters — he is "too old to tell" and Waters "too old to hear" — is an act of moral cowardice disguised as tact. He retreats from honesty into a solitary meditation on his own weakness of will, in which the central question — "Had he been his whole life one of these weak wills?" — is posed but not answered. The passage is remarkable for the way it converts the failure to act into a subject for ironic self-reflection, as if diagnosing the disease were the same as curing it. The final question — "was he running away from Florence because his will was weak?" — is the one Colville cannot bring himself to answer directly, and his very evasion of it confirms what it asks.

> "That is broken off — if it were ever really on. It was a great mistake for both of us — a tragical one for her, poor child, a ridiculous one for me. My only consolation is that it was a mistake and no more; but I don't conceal from myself that I might have prevented it altogether if I had behaved with greater wisdom and dignity at the outset. But I'm afraid I was flattered by an illusion of hers that ought to have pained and alarmed me, and the rest followed inevitably, though I was always just on the point of escaping the consequences of my weakness —" (045-002 to 045-009)

Colville's confession to Mr. Waters is the novel's most concentrated exercise in self-examination, and it reveals the architecture of moral cowardice with painful clarity. He concedes that he "might have prevented it altogether" — the counterfactual is the confession of his own agency — but immediately converts agency into passivity: he was "flattered by an illusion," events "followed inevitably," he was "always just on the point of escaping." The dash at the end of the passage marks the moment at which language fails him; the word he cannot speak is the one the novel supplies elsewhere — "cowardice." The grammatical structure of the confession, in which Colville is perpetually the object of verbs rather than their subject, enacts the moral evasion it describes.

> The old gentleman rose, and Colville, dazed by the recognition of his own cowardice and absurdity, did not try to detain him. But he followed him down to the outer gate of the hotel. ... Colville fancied him doubly defended against the torrid waves not only by the stored cold of half a century of winters at Haddam East Village, but by an inner coolness of spirit, which appeared to diffuse itself in an appreciable atmosphere about him. It was not till he was gone that Colville found himself steeped in perspiration, and glowing with a strange excitement. (045-108 to 045-119)

The climactic moment of self-recognition. Colville is "dazed" — the word suggests not a sharp moral awakening but a stunned disorientation — by the simultaneous recognition of his "cowardice and absurdity." The conjunction matters: moral cowardice, in Howells's treatment, is also faintly comic, the predicament of a man who has talked and joked and ironised his way out of every honest commitment. The contrast between Colville's agitation (steeped in perspiration, glowing with excitement) and Mr. Waters's "inner coolness of spirit" images the distance between the man who evades and the man who has made peace with his own moral life. Mr. Waters, with his seventy New England winters, has nothing left to run from; Colville, drenched in the Florentine heat, is only beginning to learn the cost of his evasions.

## Related Concepts

- **Romantic Disillusionment** — Moral cowardice supplies the mechanism by which romantic illusion is sustained and then shattered: Colville cannot bring himself to tell Imogene the truth about his feelings, and his evasion prolongs an engagement that ought never to have begun.
- **Youth and Age** — Colville's moral paralysis is inseparable from his middle-aged self-consciousness; his fear of acting decisively is in part a fear of seeming ridiculous — the middle-aged man who presumes to love a young girl and cannot even do that honestly.
- **Sacrifice and Renunciation** — The novel's resolution turns on the question of whether Colville's renunciation of Imogene and his submission to Lina Bowen's judgment constitute genuine moral growth or merely another form of passivity disguised as virtue.
- **Social Duty** — Moral cowardice is the failure of social duty: Colville owes honesty to Imogene, to Lina, and to himself, and his evasion of that obligation is the central moral problem the novel must resolve.
