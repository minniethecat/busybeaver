[045-001] "Yes."

[045-002] "That is broken off--if it were ever really on. It was a great mistake

[045-003] for both of us--a tragical one for her, poor child, a ridiculous one for

[045-004] me. My only consolation is that it was a mistake and no more; but I

[045-005] don't conceal from myself that I might have prevented it altogether if I

[045-006] had behaved with greater wisdom and dignity at the outset. But I'm

[045-007] afraid I was flattered by an illusion of hers that ought to have pained

[045-008] and alarmed me, and the rest followed inevitably, though I was always

[045-009] just on the point of escaping the consequences of my weakness--my

[045-010] wickedness."

[045-011] "Ah, there is something extremely interesting in all that," said the old

[045-012] minister thoughtfully. "The situation used to be figured under the old

[045-013] idea of a compact with the devil. His debtor was always on the point of

[045-014] escaping, as you say, but I recollect no instance in which he did not

[045-015] pay at last. The myth must have arisen from man's recognition of the

[045-016] inexorable sequence of cause from effect, in the moral world, which even

[045-017] repentance cannot avert. Goethe tries to imagine an atonement for

[045-018] Faust's trespass against one human soul in his benefactions to the race

[045-019] at large; but it is a very cloudy business."

[045-020] "It isn't quite a parallel case," said [[theodore-colville|Colville]], rather sulkily. He had,

[045-021] in fact, suffered more under [[mr-waters|Mr. Waters]]'s generalisation than he could

[045-022] from a more personal philosophy of the affair.

[045-023] "Oh no; I didn't think that," consented the old man.

[045-024] "And I don't think I shall undertake any extended scheme of drainage or

[045-025] subsoiling in atonement for my little dream," Colville continued,

[045-026] resenting the parity of outline that grew upon him in spite of his

[045-027] protest. They were both silent for a while, and then Colville cried out,

[045-028] "Yes, yes; they are alike. _I_ dreamed, too, of recovering and restoring

[045-029] my own lost and broken past in the love of a young soul, and it was in

[045-030] essence the same cruelly egotistic dream; and it's nothing in my defence

[045-031] that it was all formless and undirected at first, and that as soon as I

[045-032] recognised it I abhorred it."

[045-033] "Oh yes, it is," replied the old man, with perfect equanimity. "Your

[045-034] assertion is the hysterical excess of Puritanism in all times and

[045-035] places. In the moral world we are responsible only for the wrong that we

[045-036] intend. It can't be otherwise."

[045-037] "And the evil that's suffered from the wrong we didn't intend?"

[045-038] "Ah, perhaps that isn't evil."

[045-039] "It's pain!"

[045-040] "It's pain, yes."

[045-041] "And to have wrung a young and innocent heart with the anguish of

[045-042] self-doubt, with the fear of wrong to another, with the shame of an

[045-043] error such as I allowed, perhaps encouraged her to make--"

[045-044] "Yes," said the old man. "The young suffer terribly. But they recover.

[045-045] Afterward we don't suffer so much, but we don't recover. I wouldn't

[045-046] defend you against yourself if I thought you seriously in the wrong. If

[045-047] you know yourself to be, you shouldn't let me."

[045-048] Thus put upon his honour, Colville was a long time thoughtful. "How can

[045-049] I tell?" he asked. "You know the facts; you can judge."

[045-050] "If I were to judge at all, I should say you were likely to do a greater

[045-051] wrong than any you have committed."

[045-052] "I don't understand you."

[045-053] "[[imogene-graham|Miss Graham]] is a young girl, and I have no doubt that the young

[045-054] clergyman--what was his name?"

[045-055] "Morton. Do you think--do you suppose there was anything in that?"

[045-056] demanded Colville, with eagerness, that a more humorous observer than

[045-057] Mr. Waters might have found ludicrous. "He was an admirable young

[045-058] fellow, with an excellent head and a noble heart. I underrated him at

[045-059] one time, though I recognised his good qualities afterward; but I was

[045-060] afraid she did not appreciate him."

[045-061] "I'm not so sure of that," said the old man, with an astuteness of

[045-062] manner which Colville thought authorised by some sort of definite

[045-063] knowledge.

[045-064] "I would give the world if it were so!" he cried fervently.

[045-065] "But you are really very much more concerned in something else."

[045-066] "In what else?"

[045-067] "Can't you imagine?"

[045-068] "No," said Colville; but he felt himself growing very red in the face.

[045-069] "Then I have no more to say."

[045-070] "Yes, speak!" And after an interval Colville added, "Is it anything

[045-071] about--you hinted at something long ago--[[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]]?"

[045-072] "Yes;" the old man nodded his head. "Do you owe her nothing?"

[045-073] "Owe her nothing? Everything! My life! What self-respect is left me!

[045-074] Immeasurable gratitude! The homage of a man saved from himself as far as

[045-075] his stupidity and selfishness would permit! Why, I--I love her!" The

[045-076] words gave him courage. "In every breath and pulse! She is the most

[045-077] beautiful and gracious and wisest and best woman in the world! I have

[045-078] loved her ever since I met her here in [[florence|Florence]] last winter. Good

[045-079] heavens! I must have always loved her! But," he added, falling from the

[045-080] rapture of this confession, "she simply loathes _me_!"

[045-081] "It was certainly not to your credit that you were willing at the same

[045-082] time to marry some one else."

[045-083] "Willing! I wasn't willing! I was bound hand and foot! Yes--I don't care

[045-084] what you think of my weakness--I was not a free agent. It's very well to

[045-085] condemn one's-self, but it may be carried too far; injustice to others

[045-086] is not the only injustice, or the worst. What I was willing to do was to

[045-087] keep my word--to prevent that poor child, if possible, from ever finding

[045-088] out her mistake."

[045-089] If Colville expected this heroic confession to impress his listener he

[045-090] was disappointed. Mr. Waters made him no reply, and he was obliged to

[045-091] ask, with a degree of sarcastic impatience, "I suppose you scarcely

[045-092] blame me for that?"

[045-093] "Oh, I don't know that I blame people for things. There are times when

[045-094] it seems as if we were all puppets, pulled this way or that, without

[045-095] control of our own movements. Hamlet was able to browbeat Rosencrantz

[045-096] and Guildenstern with his business of the pipe; but if they had been in

[045-097] a position to answer they might have told him that it required far less

[045-098] skill to play upon a man than any other instrument. Most of us, in fact,

[045-099] go sounding on without any special application of breath or fingers,

[045-100] repeating the tunes that were played originally upon other men. It

[045-101] appears to me that you suffered yourself to do something of the kind in

[045-102] this affair. We are a long time learning to act with common-sense or

[045-103] even common sanity in what are called matters of the affections. A

[045-104] [[imogene-broken-engagement|broken engagement]] may be a bad thing in some cases, but I am inclined to

[045-105] think that it is the very best thing that could happen in most cases

[045-106] where it happens. The evil is done long before; the broken engagement is

[045-107] merely sanative, and so far beneficent."

[045-108] The old gentleman rose, and Colville, dazed by the recognition of his

[045-109] own cowardice and absurdity, did not try to detain him. But he followed

[045-110] him down to the outer gate of the hotel. The afternoon sun was pouring

[045-111] into the piazza a sea of glimmering heat, into which Mr. Waters plunged

[045-112] with the security of a salamander. He wore a broad-brimmed Panama hat, a

[045-113] sack coat of black alpaca, and loose trousers of the same material, and

[045-114] Colville fancied him doubly defended against the torrid waves not only

[045-115] by the stored cold of half a century of winters at Haddam East Village,

[045-116] but by an inner coolness of spirit, which appeared to diffuse itself in

[045-117] an appreciable atmosphere about him. It was not till he was gone that

[045-118] Colville found himself steeped in perspiration, and glowing with a

[045-119] strange excitement.

[045-120] XXIII

[045-121] Colville went back to his own room, and spent a good deal of time in the

[045-122] contemplation of a suit of clothes, adapted to the season, which had

[045-123] been sent home from the tailor's just before Mr. Waters came in. The

[045-124] coat was of the lightest serge, the trousers of a pearly grey tending to

[045-125] lavender, the waistcoat of cool white duck. On his way home from Palazzo

[045-126] Pinti he had stopped in Via Tornabuoni and bought some silk gauze

[045-127] neckties of a tasteful gaiety of tint, which he had at the time thought

[045-128] very well of. But now, as he spread out the whole array on his bed, it

[045-129] seemed too emblematic of a light and blameless spirit for his wear. He

[045-130] ought to put on something as nearly analogous to sackcloth as a modern

[045-131] stock of dry-goods afforded; he ought, at least, to wear the grave

[045-132] materials of his winter costume. But they were really insupportable in

[045-133] this sudden access of summer. Besides, he had grown thin during his

[045-134] sickness, and the things bagged about him. If he were going to see Mrs.

[045-135] Bowen that evening, he ought to go in some decent shape. It was perhaps

[045-136] providential that he had failed to find her at home in the morning, when

[045-137] he had ventured thither in the clumsy attire in which he had been

[045-138] loafing about her drawing-room for the past week. He now owed it to her

[045-139] to appear before her as well as he could. How charmingly punctilious she

[045-140] always was herself!

[045-141] As he put on his new clothes he felt the moral support which the

[045-142] becomingness of dress alone can give. With the blue silk gauze lightly

[045-143] tied under his collar, and the lapels of his thin coat thrown back to

[045-144] admit his thumbs to his waistcoat pockets, he felt almost cheerful

[045-145] before his glass. Should he shave? As once before, this important

[045-146] question occurred to him. His thinness gave him some advantages of

[045-147] figure, but he thought that it made his face older. What effect would

[045-148] cutting off his beard have upon it? He had not seen the lower part of

[045-149] his face for fifteen years. No one could say what recent ruin of a

[045-150] double chin might not be lurking there. He decided not to shave, at

[045-151] least till after dinner, and after dinner he was too impatient for his

[045-152] visit to brook the necessary delay.

[045-153] He was shown into the salotto alone, but [[effie-bowen|Effie Bowen]] came running in to

[045-154] meet him. She stopped suddenly, bridling.

[045-155] "You never expected to see me looking quite so pretty," said Colville,

[045-156] tracing the cause of her embarrassment to his summer splendour. "Where

[045-157] is your mamma?"

[045-158] "She is in the dining-room," replied the child, getting hold of his

[045-159] hand. "She wants you to come and have coffee with us."

[045-160] "By all means--not that I haven't had coffee already, though."

[045-161] She led the way, looking up at him shyly over her shoulder as they went.

[045-162] Mrs. Bowen rose, napkin in lap, and gave him a hand of welcome. "How are

[045-163] you feeling to-day?" she asked, politely ignoring his finery.

[045-164] "Like a new man," he said. And then he added, to relieve the strain of

[045-165] the situation, "Of the best tailor's make in Florence."

[045-166] "You look very well," she smiled.

[045-167] "Oh, I always do when I take pains," said Colville. "The trouble is that

[045-168] I don't always take pains. But I thought I would to-night, in upon a

[045-169] lady."

[045-170] "Effie will feel very much flattered," said Mrs. Bowen.

[045-171] "Don't refuse a portion of the satisfaction," he cried.

[045-172] "Oh, is it for me too?"

[045-173] This gave Colville consolation which no religion or philosophy could

[045-174] have brought him, and his pleasure was not marred, but rather

[045-175] heightened, by the little pangs of expectation, bred by long custom,

[045-176] that from moment to moment Imogene would appear. She did not appear, and

[045-177] a thrill of security succeeded upon each alarm. He wished her well with

[045-178] all his heart; such is the human heart that he wished her arrived home

[045-179] the betrothed of that excellent, that wholly unobjectionable young man,

[045-180] [[mr-morton|Mr. Morton]].

[045-181] "Will you have a little of the ice before your coffee?" asked Mrs.

[045-182] Bowen, proposing one of the moulded creams with her spoon.
