[039-001] "Oh, you might criticise back, and I shouldn't like that."

[039-002] "Then you don't approve of a course of Kingsley?" asked the girl.

[039-003] "Does that follow? But if you're going in for earnestness, why don't you

[039-004] take up a course of Carlyle?"

[039-005] "Do you think that would be better than Kingsley?"

[039-006] "Not a bit. But Carlyle's so earnest that he can't talk straight."

[039-007] "I can't make out what you mean. Wouldn't you like me to improve?"

[039-008] "Not much," laughed [[theodore-colville|Colville]]. "If you did, I don't know what I should

[039-009] do. I should have to begin to improve too, and I'm very comfortable as I

[039-010] am."

[039-011] "I should wish to do it to--to be more worthy of you," grieved the girl,

[039-012] as if deeply disappointed at his frivolous behaviour.

[039-013] He could not help laughing, but he was sorry, and would have taken her

[039-014] hand; she kept it from him, and removed to the farthest corner of the

[039-015] sofa. Apparently, however, her ideal did not admit of open pique, and

[039-016] she went on trying to talk seriously with him.

[039-017] "You think, don't you, that we oughtn't to let a day pass without

[039-018] storing away some thought--suggestion----"

[039-019] "Oh, there's no hurry," he said lazily. "Life is rather a long

[039-020] affair--if you live. There appears to be plenty of time, though people

[039-021] say not, and I think it would be rather odious to make every day of use.

[039-022] Let a few of them go by without doing anything for you! And as for

[039-023] reading, why not read when you're hungry, just as you eat? Shouldn't you

[039-024] hate to take up a course of roast beef, or a course of turkey?"

[039-025] "Very well, then," said [[imogene-graham|Imogene]]. "I shall not begin Kingsley."

[039-026] "Yes, do it. I dare say [[mr-morton|Mr. Morton]]'s quite right. He will look at these

[039-027] things more from your own point of view. All the Kingsley novels are in

[039-028] the Tauchnitz. By all means do what he says."

[039-029] "I will do what _you_ say."

[039-030] "Oh, but I say nothing."

[039-031] "Then I will do nothing."

[039-032] Colville laughed at this too, and soon after the clergyman appeared.

[039-033] Imogene met him so coldly that Colville felt obliged to make him some

[039-034] amends by a greater show of cordiality than he felt. But he was glad of

[039-035] the effort, for he began to like him as he talked to him; it was easy

[039-036] for him to like people; the young man showed sense and judgment, and if

[039-037] he was a little academic in his mind and manners, Colville tolerantly

[039-038] reflected that some people seemed to be born so, and that he was

[039-039] probably not artificial, as he had once imagined from the ecclesiastical

[039-040] scrupulosity of his dress.

[039-041] Imogene ebbed away to the piano in the corner of the room, and struck

[039-042] some chords on it. At each stroke the young clergyman, whose eyes had

[039-043] wandered a little toward her from the first, seemed to vibrate in

[039-044] response. The conversation became incoherent before [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]] joined

[039-045] them. Then, by a series of illogical processes, the clergyman was

[039-046] standing beside Imogene at the piano, and Mrs. Bowen was sitting beside

[039-047] Colville on the sofa.

[039-048] "Isn't there to be any Effie, to-night?" he asked.

[039-049] "No. She has been up too much of late. And I wished to speak with

[039-050] you--about Imogene."

[039-051] "Yes," said Colville, not very eagerly. At that moment he could have

[039-052] chosen another topic.

[039-053] "It is time that her mother should have got my letter. In less than a

[039-054] fortnight we ought to have an answer."

[039-055] "Well?" said Colville, with a strange constriction of the heart.

[039-056] "Her mother is a person of very strong character; her husband is

[039-057] absorbed in business, and defers to her in everything."

[039-058] "It isn't an uncommon American situation," said Colville, relieving his

[039-059] tension by this excursion.

[039-060] Mrs. Bowen ignored it. "I don't know how she may look at the affair. She

[039-061] may give her assent at once, or she may decide that nothing has taken

[039-062] place till--she sees you."

[039-063] "I could hardly blame her for that," he answered submissively.

[039-064] "It isn't a question of that," said Mrs. Bowen. "It's a question

[039-065] of--others. Mr. Morton was here before you came, and I know he was

[039-066] interested in Imogene--I am certain of it. He has come back, and he sees

[039-067] no reason why he should not renew his attentions."

[039-068] "No--o--o," faltered Colville.

[039-069] "I wish you to realise the fact."

[039-070] "But what would you----"

[039-071] "I told you," said Mrs. Bowen, with a full return of that severity whose

[039-072] recent absence Colville had found so comfortable, "that I can't advise

[039-073] or suggest anything at all."

[039-074] He was long and miserably silent. At last, "Did you ever think," he

[039-075] asked, "did you ever suppose--that is to say, did you ever suspect

[039-076] that--she--that Imogene was--at all interested in him?"

[039-077] "I think she was--at one time," said Mrs. Bowen promptly.

[039-078] Colville sighed, with a wandering disposition to whistle.

[039-079] "But that is nothing," she went on. "People have many passing fancies.

[039-080] The question is, what are you going to do now? I want to know, as Mr.

[039-081] Morton's friend."

[039-082] "Ah, I wish you wanted to know as _my_ friend, Mrs. Bowen!" A sudden

[039-083] thought flashed upon him. "Why shouldn't I go away from [[florence|Florence]] till

[039-084] Imogene hears from her mother? That seemed to me right in the first

[039-085] place. There is no tie that binds her to me. I hold her to nothing. If

[039-086] she finds in my absence that she likes this young man better--" An

[039-087] expression of Mrs. Bowen's face stopped him. He perceived that he had

[039-088] said something very shocking to her; he perceived that the thing was

[039-089] shocking in itself, but it was not that which he cared for. "I don't

[039-090] mean that I won't hold myself true to her as long as she will. I

[039-091] recognise my [[social-duty|responsibility]] fully. I know that I am answerable for all

[039-092] this, and that no one else is; and I am ready to bear any penalty. But

[039-093] what I can't bear is that you should misunderstand me, that you

[039-094] should--I have been so wretched ever since you first began to blame me

[039-095] for my part in this, and so happy this past fortnight that I can't--I

[039-096] _won't_--go back to that state of things. No; you have no right to

[039-097] relent toward me, and then fling me off as you have tried to do

[039-098] to-night! I have some feeling too--some rights. You shall receive me as

[039-099] a friend, or not at all! How can I live if you----"

[039-100] She had been making little efforts as if to rise; now she forced herself

[039-101] to her feet, and ran from the room.

[039-102] The young people looked up from their music; some wave of the sensation

[039-103] had spread to them, but seeing Colville remain seated, they went on with

[039-104] their playing till he rose. Then Imogene called out, "Isn't Mrs. Bowen

[039-105] coming back?"

[039-106] "I don't know; I think not," answered Colville stupidly, standing where

[039-107] he had risen.

[039-108] She hastened questioning toward him. "What is the matter? Isn't she

[039-109] well?"

[039-110] Mr. Morton's face expressed a polite share in her anxiety.

[039-111] "Oh yes; quite, I believe," Colville replied.

[039-112] "She heard Effie call, I suppose," suggested the girl.

[039-113] "Yes, yes; I think so; that is--yes. I must be going. Good night."

[039-114] He took her hand and went away, leaving the clergyman still there; but

[039-115] he lingered only for a report from Mrs. Bowen, which Imogene hurried to

[039-116] get. She sent word that she would join them presently. But Mr. Morton

[039-117] said that it was late already, and he would beg Miss Graham to say

[039-118] good-night for him. When Mrs. Bowen returned Imogene was alone.

[039-119] She did not seem surprised or concerned at that. "Imogene, I have been

[039-120] talking to Mr. Colville about you and Mr. Morton."

[039-121] The girl started and turned pale.

[039-122] "It is almost time to hear from your mother, and she may consent to your

[039-123] engagement. Then you must be prepared to act."

[039-124] "Act?"

[039-125] "To make it known. Matters can't go on as they have been going. I told

[039-126] Mr. Colville that Mr. Morton ought to know at once. Don't try to blind yourself, Imogene, to what you see as plainly as I do. He is in love with you."

[039-127] "Why ought he to know?" asked Imogene, doubtless with that impulse to

[039-128] temporise which is natural to the human soul in questions of right and

[039-129] interest. She sank into the chair beside which she had been standing.

[039-130] "If your mother consents, you will feel bound to Mr. Colville?"

[039-131] "Yes," said the girl.

[039-132] "And if she refuses?"

[039-133] "He has my word. I will keep my word to him," replied Imogene huskily.

[039-134] "Nothing shall make me break it."

[039-135] "Very well, then!" exclaimed Mrs. Bowen. "We need not wait for your

[039-136] mother's answer. Mr. Morton ought to know, and he ought to know at once.

[039-137] Don't try to blind yourself, Imogene, to what you see as plainly as I

[039-138] do. He is in love with you."

[039-139] "Oh," moaned the girl.

[039-140] "Yes; you can't deny it. And it's cruel, it's treacherous, to let him go

[039-141] on thinking that you are free."

[039-142] "I will never see him again."

[039-143] "Ah! that isn't enough. He has a claim to know why. I will not let him

[039-144] be treated so."

[039-145] They were both silent. Then, "What did Mr. Colville say?" asked Imogene.

[039-146] "He? I don't know that he said anything. He----" Mrs. Bowen stopped.

[039-147] Imogene rose from her chair.

[039-148] "I will not let him tell Mr. Morton. It would be too indelicate."

[039-149] "And shall you let it go on so?"

[039-150] "No. I will tell him myself."

[039-151] "How will you tell him?"

[039-152] "I will tell him if he speaks to me."

[039-153] "You will let it come to that?"

[039-154] "There is no other way. I shall suffer more than he."

[039-155] "But you will deserve to suffer, and your suffering will not help him."

[039-156] Imogene trembled into her chair again.

[039-157] "I see," said Mrs. Bowen bitterly, "how it will be at last. It will be

[039-158] as it has been from the first." She began to walk up and down the room,

[039-159] mechanically putting the chairs in place, and removing the disorder in

[039-160] which the occupancy of several people leaves a room at the end of an

[039-161] evening. She closed the piano, which Imogene had forgot to shut, with a

[039-162] clash that jarred the strings from their silence. "But I will do it, and

[039-163] I wonder----"

[039-164] "You will speak to him?" faltered the girl.

[039-165] "Yes!" returned Mrs. Bowen vehemently, and arresting herself in her

[039-166] rapid movements. "It won't do for you to tell him, and you won't let Mr.

[039-167] Colville."

[039-168] "No, I can't," said Imogene, slowly shaking her head. "But I will

[039-169] discourage him; I will not see him anymore." Mrs. Bowen silently

[039-170] confronted her. "I will not see any one now till I have heard from

[039-171] home."

[039-172] "And how will that help? He must have some explanation, and I will have

[039-173] to make it. What shall it be?"

[039-174] Imogene did not answer. She said: "I will not have any one know what is

[039-175] between me and Mr. Colville till I have heard from home. If they try to

[039-176] refuse, then it will be for him to take me against their will. But if he

[039-177] doesn't choose to do that, then he shall be free, and I won't have him

[039-178] humiliated a second time before the world. _This_ time _he_ shall be the

[039-179] one to reject. And I don't care who suffers. The more I prize the

[039-180] person, the gladder I shall be; and if I could suffer before everybody I

[039-181] would. If people ever find it out, I will tell them that it was he who

[039-182] broke it off." She rose again from her chair, and stood flushed and

[039-183] thrilling with the notion of her [[sacrifice-and-renunciation|self-sacrifice]]. Out of the tortuous

[039-184] complexity of the situation she had evolved this brief triumph, in which

[039-185] she rejoiced as if it were enduring success. But she suddenly fell from

[039-186] it in the dust. "Oh, what can I do for him? How can I make him feel more

[039-187] and more that I would give up anything, everything, for him! It's

[039-188] because he asks nothing and wants nothing that it's so hard! If I could

[039-189] see that he was unhappy, as I did once! If I could see that he was at

[039-190] all different since--since----Oh, what I dread is this smooth

[039-191] tranquillity! If our lives could only be stormy and full of cares and

[039-192] anxieties and troubles that I could take on myself, then, then I

[039-193] shouldn't be afraid of the future! But I'm afraid they won't be so--no,

[039-194] I'm afraid that they will be easy and quiet, and then what shall I do? O

[039-195] Mrs. Bowen, do you think he cares for me?"
