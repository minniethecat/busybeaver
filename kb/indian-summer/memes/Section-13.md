[013-001] "Yes, it's a great poem, and deep," said [[theodore-colville|Colville]]. They had come to a

[013-002] place where the bank sloped invitingly to the river. "Miss Effie," he

[013-003] asked, "wouldn't you like to go down and throw stones into the [[arno-river|Arno]]?

[013-004] That's what a river is for," he added, as the child glanced toward

[013-005] [[imogene-graham|Imogene]] for authorisation, "to have stones thrown into it."

[013-006] "Oh, let us!" cried Imogene, rushing down to the brink. "I don't want to

[013-007] throw stones into it, but to get near it--to get near to any bit of

[013-008] nature. They do pen you up so from it in Europe!" She stood and watched

[013-009] Colville skim stones over the current. "When you stand by the shore of a

[013-010] swift river like this, or near a railroad train when it comes whirling

[013-011] by, don't you ever have a morbid impulse to fling yourself forward?"

[013-012] "Not at my time of life," said Colville, stooping to select a flat

[013-013] stone. "Morbid impulses are one of the luxuries of youth." He threw the

[013-014] stone, which skipped triumphantly far out into the stream. "That was

[013-015] beautiful, wasn't it, Miss Effie?"

[013-016] "Lovely!" murmured the child.

[013-017] He offered her a flat pebble. "Would you like to try one?"

[013-018] "It would spoil my gloves," she said, in deprecating refusal.

[013-019] "Let _me_ try it!" cried Imogene. "I'm not afraid of my gloves."

[013-020] Colville yielded the pebble, looking at her with the thought of how

[013-021] intoxicating he should once have found this bit of wilful _abandon_, but

[013-022] feeling rather sorry for it now. "Oh, perhaps not?" he said, laying his

[013-023] hand upon hers, and looking into her eyes.

[013-024] She returned his look, and then she dropped the pebble and put her hand

[013-025] back in her muff, and turned and ran up the bank. "There's the carriage.

[013-026] It's time we should be going." At the top of the bank she became a

[013-027] mirror of dignity, a transparent mirror to his eye. "Are you going back

[013-028] to town, Mr. Colville?" she asked, with formal state. "We could set you

[013-029] down anywhere!"

[013-030] "Thank you, Miss Graham. I shall be glad to avail myself of your very

[013-031] kind offer. Allow me." He handed her ceremoniously to the carriage; he

[013-032] handed [[effie-bowen|Effie Bowen]] even more ceremoniously to the carriage, holding his

[013-033] hat in one hand while he offered the other. Then he mounted to the seat

[013-034] in front of them. "The weather has changed," he said.

[013-035] Imogene hid her face in her muff, and Effie Bowen bowed hers against

[013-036] Imogene's shoulder.

[013-037] A sense of the girl's beauty lingered in Colville's thought all day, and

[013-038] recurred to him again and again; and the ambitious intensity and

[013-039] enthusiasm of her talk came back in touches of amusement and compassion.

[013-040] How divinely young it all was, and how lovely! He patronised it from a

[013-041] height far aloof.

[013-042] He was not in the frame of mind for the hotel table, and he went to

[013-043] lunch, at a restaurant. He chose a simple trattoria, the first he came

[013-044] to, and he took his seat at one of the bare, rude tables, where the

[013-045] joint saucers for pepper and salt, and a small glass for toothpicks,

[013-046] with a much-scraped porcelain box for matches, expressed an uncorrupted

[013-047] Florentinity of custom. But when he gave his order in offhand Italian,

[013-048] the waiter answered in the French which waiters get together for the

[013-049] traveller's confusion in Italy, and he resigned himself to whatever

[013-050] chance of acquaintance might befall him. The place had a companionable

[013-051] smell of stale tobacco, and the dim light showed him on the walls of a

[013-052] space dropped a step or two lower, at the end of the room, a variety of

[013-053] sketches and caricatures. A waiter was laying a large table in this

[013-054] space, and when Colville came up to examine the drawings he jostled him,

[013-055] with due apologies, in the haste of a man working against time for

[013-056] masters who will brook no delay. He was hurrying still when a party of

[013-057] young men came in and took their places at the table, and began to rough

[013-058] him for his delay. Colville could recognise several of them in the

[013-059] vigorous burlesques on the walls, and as others dropped in the grotesque

[013-060] portraitures made him feel as if he had seen them before. They all

[013-061] talked at once, each man of his own interests, except when they joined

[013-062] in a shout of mockery and welcome for some new-comer. Colville, at his

[013-063] _risotto_, almost the room's length away, could hear what they thought,

[013-064] one and another, of Botticelli and Michelangelo; of old Piloty's things

[013-065] at Munich; of the dishes they had served to them, and of the quality of

[013-066] the Chianti; of the respective merits of German and Italian tobacco; of

[013-067] whether Inglehart had probably got to Venice yet; of the personal habits

[013-068] of Billings, and of the question whether the want of modelling in

[013-069] Simmons's nose had anything to do with his style of snoring; of the

[013-070] overrated colouring of some of those Venetian fellows; of the delicacy

[013-071] of Mino da [[fiesole|Fiesole]], and of the genius of Babson's tailor. Babson was

[013-072] there to defend the cut of his trousers, and Billings and Simmons were

[013-073] present to answer for themselves at the expense of the pictures of those

[013-074] who had called their habits and features into question. When it came to

[013-075] this all the voices joined in jolly uproar. Derision and denial broke

[013-076] out of the tumult, and presently they were all talking quietly of a

[013-077] reception which some of them were at the day before. Then Colville heard

[013-078] one of them saying that he would like a chance to paint some lady whose

[013-079] name he did not catch, and "She looks awfully sarcastic," one of the

[013-080] young fellows said.

[013-081] "They say she _is_," said another. "They say she's awfully

[013-082] intellectual."

[013-083] "Boston?" queried a third.

[013-084] "No, Kalamazoo. The centre of culture is out there now."

[013-085] "She knows how to dress, anyhow," said the first commentator. "I wonder

[013-086] what Parker would talk to her about when he was painting her. He's never

[013-087] read anything but Poe's 'Ullalume.'"

[013-088] "Well, that's a good subject--'Ullalume.'"

[013-089] "I suppose she's read it?"

[013-090] "She's read 'most everything, they say."

[013-091] "What's an Ullalume, anyway, Parker?"

[013-092] One of the group sprang up from the table and drew on the wall what he

[013-093] labelled "An Ullalume." Another rapidly depicted Parker in the moment of

[013-094] sketching a young lady; her portrait had got as far as the eyes and nose

[013-095] when some one protested: "Oh, hello! No personalities."

[013-096] The draughtsman said, "Well, all right!" and sat down again.

[013-097] "Hall talked with her the most. What did she say, Hall?"

[013-098] "Hall can't remember words in three syllables, but he says it was mighty

[013-099] brilliant and mighty deep."

[013-100] "They say she's a niece of [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]]'s. She's staying with Mrs. Bowen."

[013-101] Then it was the wisdom and brilliancy and severity of Imogene Graham

[013-102] that these young men stood in awe of! Colville remembered how the minds

[013-103] of girls of twenty had once dazzled him. "And yes," he mused, "she must

[013-104] have believed that we were talking literature in the [[cascine|Cascine]]. Certainly

[013-105] I should have thought it an intellectual time when I was at that age,"

[013-106] he owned to himself with forlorn irony.

[013-107] The young fellows went on to speak of Mrs. Bowen, whom it seemed they

[013-108] had known the winter before. She had been very polite to them; they

[013-109] praised her as if she were quite an old woman.

[013-110] "But she must have been a very pretty girl," one of them put in.

[013-111] "Well, she has a good deal of style yet."

[013-112] "Oh yes, but she never could have been a beauty like the other one."

[013-113] On her part, Imogene was very sober when she met Mrs. Bowen, though she

[013-114] had come in flushed and excited from the air and the morning's

[013-115] adventure. Mrs. Bowen was sitting by the fire, placidly reading; a vase

[013-116] of roses on the little table near her diffused the delicate odour of

[013-117] winter roses through the room; all seemed very still and dim, and of

[013-118] another time, somehow.

[013-119] Imogene kept away from the fire, sitting down, in the provisional

[013-120] fashion of women, with her things on; but she unbuttoned her pelisse and

[013-121] flung it open. Effie had gone to her room.

[013-122] "Did you have a pleasant drive?" asked Mrs. Bowen.

[013-123] "Very," said the girl.

[013-124] "[[mr-morton|Mr. Morton]] brought you these roses," continued Mrs. Bowen.

[013-125] "Oh," said Imogene, with a cold glance at them.

[013-126] "The Flemmings have asked us to a party Thursday. There is to be

[013-127] dancing."

[013-128] "The Flemmings?"

[013-129] "Yes." As if she now saw reason to do so, Mrs. Bowen laid the book face

[013-130] downward in her lap. She yawned a little, with her hand on her mouth.

[013-131] "Did you meet any one you knew?"

[013-132] "Yes; Mr. Colville." Mrs. Bowen cut her yawn in half. "We got out to

[013-133] walk in the Cascine, and we saw him coming in at the gate. He came up

[013-134] and asked if he might walk with us."

[013-135] "Did you have a pleasant walk?" asked Mrs. Bowen, a breath more chillily

[013-136] than she had asked if they had a pleasant drive.

[013-137] "Yes, pleasant enough. And then we came back and went down the river

[013-138] bank, and he skipped stones, and we took him to his hotel."

[013-139] "Was there anybody you knew in the Cascine?"

[013-140] "Oh no; the place was a howling wilderness. I never saw it so deserted,"

[013-141] said the girl impatiently. "It was terribly hot walking. I thought I

[013-142] should burn up."

[013-143] Mrs. Bowen did not answer anything; she let the book lie in her lap.

[013-144] "What an odd person Mr. Colville is!" said Imogene, after a moment.

[013-145] "Don't you think he's very different from other gentlemen?"

[013-146] "Why?"

[013-147] "Oh, he has such a peculiar way of talking."

[013-148] "What peculiar way?"

[013-149] "Oh, I don't know. Plenty of the young men I see talk cynically, and I

[013-150] do sometimes myself--desperately, don't you know. But then I know very

[013-151] well we don't mean anything by it."

[013-152] "And do you think Mr. Colville does? Do you think he talks cynically?"

[013-153] Imogene leaned back in her chair and reflected. "No," she returned

[013-154] slowly, "I can't say that he does. But he talks lightly, with a kind of

[013-155] touch and go that makes you feel that he has exhausted all feeling. He

[013-156] doesn't parade it at all. But you hear between the words, don't you

[013-157] know, just as you read between the lines in some kinds of poetry. Of

[013-158] course it's everything in knowing what he's been through. He's perfectly

[013-159] unaffected; and don't you think he's good?"

[013-160] "Oh yes," sighed Mrs. Bowen. "In his way."

[013-161] "But he sees through you. Oh, quite! Nothing escapes him, and pretty

[013-162] soon he lets out that he has seen through you, and then you feel so

[013-163] _flat_! Oh, it's perfectly intoxicating to be with him. I would give the

[013-164] world to talk as he does."

[013-165] "What was your talk all about?"

[013-166] "Oh, I don't know. I suppose it would have been called rather

[013-167] intellectual."

[013-168] Mrs. Bowen smiled infinitesimally. But after a moment she said gravely,

[013-169] "Mr. Colville is very much older than you. He's old enough to be your

[013-170] father."

[013-171] "Yes, I know that. You feel that he feels old, and it's perfectly

[013-172] tragical. Sometimes when he turns that slow, dull, melancholy look on

[013-173] you, he seems a thousand years old."

[013-174] "I don't mean that he's positively old," said Mrs. Bowen. "He's only old

[013-175] comparatively."

[013-176] "Oh yes; I understand that. And I don't mean that he really seems a

[013-177] thousand years old. What I meant was, he seems a thousand years off, as

[013-178] if he were still young, and had got left behind somehow. He seems to be

[013-179] on the other side of some impassable barrier, and you want to get over

[013-180] there and help him to our side, but you can't do it. I suppose his

[013-181] talking in that light way is merely a subterfuge to hide his feeling, to

[013-182] make him forget."
