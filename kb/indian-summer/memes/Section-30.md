[030-001] [[theodore-colville|Colville]] became very red. He put out his hand and took [[imogene-graham|Imogene]]'s, and

[030-002] now his eyes and [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]]'s met in the kind of glance in which people

[030-003] intercept and turn each other aside before they have reached a

[030-004] resting-place in each other's souls. But at the girl's touch his courage

[030-005] revived--in some physical sort. "Yes, and if she will let me stay with

[030-006] her, we are not going to part again."

[030-007] Mrs. Bowen did not answer at once, and in the hush Colville heard the

[030-008] breathing of all three.

[030-009] "Of course," he said, "we wished you to know at once, and I came in with

[030-010] Imogene to tell you."

[030-011] "What do you wish me," asked Mrs. Bowen, "to do?"

[030-012] Colville forced a nervous laugh. "Really, I'm so little used to this

[030-013] sort of affair that I don't know whether I have any wish. Imogene is

[030-014] here with you, and I suppose I supposed you would wish to do something."

[030-015] "I will do whatever you think best."

[030-016] "Thank you: that's very kind of you." He fell into a silence, in which

[030-017] he was able only to wish that he knew what was best, and from which he

[030-018] came to the surface with, "Imogene's family ought to know, of course."

[030-019] "Yes; they put her in my charge. They will have to know. Shall I write

[030-020] to them?"

[030-021] "Why, if you will."

[030-022] "Oh, certainly."

[030-023] "Thank you."

[030-024] He had taken to stroking with his right hand the hand of Imogene which

[030-025] he held in his left, and now he looked round at her with a glance which

[030-026] it was a relief not to have her meet. "And till we can hear from them, I

[030-027] suppose you will let me come to see her?"

[030-028] "You know you have always been welcome here."

[030-029] "Thank you very much." It seemed as if there ought to be something else

[030-030] to say, but Colville could not think of anything except: "We wish to act

[030-031] in every way with your approval, Mrs. Bowen. And I know that you are

[030-032] very particular in some things"--the words, now that they were said,

[030-033] struck him as unfortunate, and even vulgar--"and I shouldn't wish to

[030-034] annoy you--"

[030-035] "Oh, I understand. I think it will be--I have no doubt you will know how

[030-036] to manage all that. It isn't as if you were both--"

[030-037] "Young?" asked Colville. "No; one of us is quite old enough to be

[030-038] thoroughly up in the _convenances_. We are qualified, I'm afraid, as far

[030-039] as that goes," he added bitterly, "to set all [[florence|Florence]] an example of

[030-040] correct behaviour."

[030-041] He knew there must be pain in the face which he would not look at; he

[030-042] kept looking at Mrs. Bowen's face, in which certainly there was not much

[030-043] pleasure, either.

[030-044] There was another silence, which became very oppressive before it ended

[030-045] in a question from Mrs. Bowen, who stirred slightly in her chair, and

[030-046] bent forward as if about to rise in asking it. "Shall you wish to

[030-047] consider it an engagement?"

[030-048] Colville felt Imogene's hand tremble in his, but he received no definite

[030-049] prompting from the tremor. "I don't believe I know what you mean."

[030-050] "I mean, till you have heard from Imogene's mother."

[030-051] "I hadn't thought of that. Perhaps under the circumstances--" The tremor

[030-052] died out of the hand he held; it lay lax between his. "What do you say,

[030-053] Imogene?"

[030-054] "I can't say anything. Whatever you think will be right--for me."

[030-055] "I wish to do what will seem right and fair to your mother."

[030-056] "Yes."

[030-057] Colville heaved a hopeless sigh. Then with a deep inward humiliation, he

[030-058] said, "Perhaps if you know Imogene's mother, Mrs. Bowen, you can

[030-059] suggest--advise--You--"

[030-060] "You must excuse me; I can't suggest or advise anything. I must leave

[030-061] you perfectly free." She rose from her chair, and they, both rose too

[030-062] from the sofa on which he had seated himself at Imogene's side. "I shall

[030-063] have to leave you, I'm afraid; my head aches still a little. Imogene!"

[030-064] She advanced toward the girl, who stood passively letting her come the

[030-065] whole distance. As if sensible of the rebuff expressed in this attitude,

[030-066] she halted a very little. Then she added, "I hope you will be very

[030-067] happy," and suddenly cast her arms round the girl, and stood long

[030-068] pressing her face into her neck. When she released her, Colville

[030-069] trembled lest she should be going to give him her hand in

[030-070] congratulation. But she only bowed slightly to him, with a sidelong,

[030-071] aversive glance, and walked out of the room with a slow, rigid pace,

[030-072] like one that controls a tendency to giddiness.

[030-073] Imogene threw herself on Colville's' breast. It gave him a shock, as if

[030-074] he were letting her do herself some wrong. But she gripped him fast, and

[030-075] began to sob and to cry. "Oh! oh! oh!"

[030-076] "What is it?--what is it, my poor girl?" he murmured. "Are you unhappy?

[030-077] Are you sorry? Let it all end, then!"

[030-078] "No, no; it isn't that! But I am very unhappy--yes, very, very unhappy!

[030-079] Oh, I didn't suppose I should ever feel so toward any one. I hate her!"

[030-080] "You hate her?" gasped Colville.

[030-081] "Yes, I hate her. And she--she is so good to me! It must be that I've

[030-082] done her some deadly wrong, without knowing it, or I couldn't hate her

[030-083] as I know I do."

[030-084] "Oh no," said Colville soothingly; "that's just your fancy. You haven't

[030-085] harmed her, and you don't hate her."

[030-086] "Yes, yes, I do! You can't understand how I feel toward her."

[030-087] "But you can't feel so toward her long," he urged, dealing as he might

[030-088] with what was wholly a mystery to him. She is so good--"

[030-089] "It only makes my badness worse, and makes me hate her more."

[030-090] "I don't understand. But you're excited now. When you're calmer you'll

[030-091] feel differently, of course. I've kept you restless and nervous a long

[030-092] time, poor child; but now our peace begins, and everything will be

[030-093] bright and--" He stopped: the words had such a very hollow sound.

[030-094] She pushed herself from him, and dried her eyes. "Oh yes."

[030-095] "And, Imogene--perhaps--perhaps--Or, no; never mind, now. I must go

[030-096] away--" She looked at him, frightened but submissive. "But I will be

[030-097] back to-night, or perhaps to-morrow morning. I want to think--to give

[030-098] you time to think. I don't want to be selfish about you--I want to

[030-099] consider you, all the more because you won't consider yourself.

[030-100] Good-bye." He stooped over and kissed her hair. Even in this he felt

[030-101] like a thief; he could not look at the face she lifted to his.

[030-102] Mrs. Bowen sent word from her room that she was not coming to dinner,

[030-103] and Imogene did not come till the dessert was put on. Then she found

[030-104] [[effie-bowen|Effie Bowen]] sitting alone at the table, and served in serious formality

[030-105] by the man, whom she had apparently felt it right to repress, for they

[030-106] were both silent. The little girl had not known how to deny herself an

[030-107] excess of the less wholesome dishes, and she was perhaps anticipating

[030-108] the regret which this indulgence was to bring, for she was very pensive.

[030-109] "Isn't mamma coming at _all_?" she asked plaintively, when Imogene sat

[030-110] down, and refused everything but a cup of coffee. "Well," she went on,

[030-111] "I can't make out what is coming to this family. You were all crying

[030-112] last night because Mr. Colville was going away, and now, when he's going

[030-113] to stay, it's just as bad. I don't think you make it very pleasant for

[030-114] _him_. I should think he would be perfectly puzzled by it, after he's

[030-115] done so much to please you all. I don't believe he thinks it's very

[030-116] polite. I suppose it _is_ polite, but it doesn't seem so. And he's

[030-117] always so cheerful and nice. I should think he would want to visit in

[030-118] some family where there was more amusement. There used to be plenty in

[030-119] this family, but now it's as dismal! The first of the winter you and

[030-120] mamma used to be so pleasant when he came, and would try everything to

[030-121] amuse him, and would let me come in to get some of the good of it; but

[030-122] now you seem to fly every way as soon as he comes in sight of the house,

[030-123] and I'm poked off in holes and corners before he can open his lips. And

[030-124] I've borne it about as long as I can. I would rather be back in Vevay.

[030-125] Or anywhere." At this point her own pathos overwhelmed her, and the

[030-126] tears rolling down her cheeks moistened the crumbs of pastry at the

[030-127] corners of her pretty mouth. "What was so strange, I should like to

[030-128] know, about his staying, that mamma should pop up like a ghost, when I

[030-129] told her he had come home with us, and grab me by the wrist, and twitch

[030-130] me about, and ask me all sorts of questions I couldn't answer, and

[030-131] frighten me almost to death? I haven't got over it yet. And I don't

[030-132] think it's very nice. It used to be a very polite family, and pleasant

[030-133] with each other, and always having something agreeable going on in it;

[030-134] but if it keeps on very much longer in this way, I shall think the

[030-135] Bowens are beginning to lose their good-breeding. I suppose that if Mr.

[030-136] Colville were to go down on his knees to mamma and ask her to let him

[030-137] take me somewhere now, she wouldn't do it." She pulled her handkerchief

[030-138] out of her pocket, and dried her eyes on a ball of it. "I don't see what

[030-139] _you've_ been crying about, Imogene. _You've_ got nothing to worry you."

[030-140] "I'm not very well, Effie," returned the girl gently. "I haven't been

[030-141] well all day."

[030-142] "It seems to me that nobody is well any more. I don't believe Florence

[030-143] is a very healthy place. Or at least this house isn't. _I_ think it must

[030-144] be the drainage. If we keep on, I suppose we shall all have diphtheria.

[030-145] Don't you, Imogene?"

[030-146] "Yes," asserted the girl distractedly.

[030-147] "The girls had it at Vevay frightfully. And none of them were as strong

[030-148] afterward. Some of the parents came and took them away; but Madame

[030-149] Schebres never let mamma know. Do you think that was right?"

[030-150] "No; it was very wrong."

[030-151] "I suppose Mr. Colville will have it if we do. That is, if he keeps

[030-152] coming here. Is he coming any more?"

[030-153] "Yes; he's coming to-morrow morning."

[030-154] "_Is_ he?" A smile flickered over the rueful face. "What time is he

[030-155] coming?"

[030-156] "I don't know exactly," said Imogene, listlessly stirring her coffee.

[030-157] "Some time in the forenoon."

[030-158] "Do you suppose he's going to take us anywhere?"

[030-159] "Yes--I think so. I can't tell exactly."

[030-160] "If he asks me to go somewhere, will you tease mamma? She always lets

[030-161] you, Imogene, and it seems sometimes as if she just took a pleasure in

[030-162] denying me."

[030-163] "You mustn't talk so of your mother, Effie."

[030-164] "No; I wouldn't to _every_body. I know that she means for the best; but

[030-165] I don't believe she understands how much I suffer when she won't let me

[030-166] go with Mr. Colville. Don't you think he's about the nicest gentleman we

[030-167] know, Imogene?"

[030-168] "Yes; he's very kind."

[030-169] "And I think he's handsome. A good many people would consider him

[030-170] old-looking, and of course he isn't so young as [[mr-morton|Mr. Morton]] was, or the

[030-171] Inglehart boys; but that makes him all the easier to get along with. And

[030-172] his being just a little fat, that way, seems to suit so well with his

[030-173] character." The smiles were now playing across the child's face, and her

[030-174] eyes sparkling. "_I_ think Mr. Colville would make a good Saint

[030-175] Nicholas--the kind they have going down chimneys in America. I'm going

[030-176] to tell him, for the next veglione. It would be such a nice surprise."

[030-177] "No, better not tell him that," suggested Imogene.
