# Cosmopolitan Society

## Definition

Cosmopolitan society in Howells's *Indian Summer* designates the distinctive social world of the Anglo-American expatriate community in Florence — a floating, transient society of English-speaking sojourners who have established themselves in the city's hotels, furnished lodgings, and palaces. This society is "cosmopolitan" in its multinational composition: English, American, French, Russian, and Italian figures mingle at the Hotel d'Atene's table-d'hote (004-001 to 004-009). Yet it is also strikingly insular — bound internally by a "common language and a common social tradition" (014-121 to 014-122) while remaining largely "shut out from practical knowledge of the local life" (014-132 to 014-133). The term captures both the glamour of international society and its paradox: a community of people who have travelled thousands of miles only to reproduce, in simplified form, the social patterns of London and New York on Italian soil.

This cosmopolitan formation is a defining feature of the novel's social landscape. Colville moves through a world where English ladies discuss the Pitti Gallery, a French lady analyses American metaphysics, an Italian republican debates constitutional monarchy, and an American family hushes its talk to inaudibility — all within the compass of a single dinner (004-001 to 004-059). The society Howells depicts is one in which national identity is simultaneously conspicuous and performative, where the very act of being "American" or "English" becomes something one does rather than simply is.

## Context in the Novel

Colville enters this cosmopolitan society through two portals: the Hotel d'Atene, where he takes rooms on arrival, and Mrs. Bowen's salon at Palazzo Pinti, where her Thursday reception days draw the English-speaking colony to tea, bread and butter, and conversation. The hotel table-d'hote serves as his first exposure to the expatriate milieu. The reading-room is labelled "in homage to a predominance of English-speaking people among the guests" (003-159), and at dinner the language of the "English-speaking race" immediately becomes "the law of the table" (004-008 to 004-009). Howells anatomises this microcosm with characteristic irony: the French lady's English "proved to be much better than the French that he began to talk to her in" (004-019 to 004-020); the Italian gentleman is "politely offering him the salad" (004-040); the Englishman "relieved Colville from the difficulty" of political argument (004-050); while the American family "kept its talk within itself, and hushed to a tone so low that no one else could hear it" (004-054 to 004-055). Colville reflects, "for the honour of the country" (004-056), that "the English manner was better" (004-059).

Mrs. Bowen's salon provides the novel's second and more intimate vantage on cosmopolitan society. Her Thursday at-homes are attended by "not more than twenty-five or thirty people" (004-123), and it is through these gatherings that Colville gains entree to the broader expatriate network. He discovers that Mrs. Bowen's position in Florence is "rather authoritative" (008-188): "She was one of the very few Americans who were asked to Italian houses, and Italian houses lying even beyond the neutral ground of English-speaking intermarriages" (008-189 to 008-191). This hierarchy is carefully calibrated — she is not invited to "the great Princess Strozzi ball, where the Florentine nobility appeared in the mediaeval pomp" (008-192 to 008-193), but "any glory short of this was within Mrs. Bowen's reach" (008-195 to 008-196). An old lady at the party boasts of Mrs. Bowen's "acceptance among the best English residents, who, next after the natives, seem to constitute the social ambition of Americans living in Italian cities" (008-198 to 008-200). The passage reveals a finely graded social ladder in which Italians occupy the inaccessible summit, the English form the middle tier that Americans aspire to, and American prestige is measured by proximity to both.

Colville's rapid assimilation into this society forms a major thread of the novel's middle chapters. After his introduction at Mrs. Bowen's, "the prompt hospitalities which his introduction at Mrs. Bowen's had opened to him" (014-104 to 014-105) carry him into a round of visits, dinners, and evening parties. The sojourners — whether lodged in hotel apartments, furnished flats along the Lung' Arno, or villas occupied for years — are "bound together by a common language and a common social tradition; they all had a Day, and on that day there was tea and bread and butter for every comer" (014-121 to 014-123). Howells renders the texture of this life with ethnographic precision: "They had one another to dine; there were evening parties, with dancing and without dancing" (014-124 to 014-125). Colville even attends "a fancy ball, where he was kept in countenance by several other Florentines of the period of Romola" (014-125 to 014-126).

Yet the novel's treatment of cosmopolitan society is not merely descriptive; it is analytical. Colville reflects that this "alien life in the midst of the native community struck him as one of the phases of modern civilisation worthy of note, if not particular study; for he fancied it destined to a wider future throughout Europe, as the conditions in England and America grow more tiresome and more onerous" (014-128 to 014-131). This is a remarkably prescient observation — the recognition that expatriate communities are not anomalous but prophetic, anticipating a future in which mobility and cultural transplantation become increasingly common. The society functions, in effect, as a portable Anglo-America: "Our race finds its simplified and cheapened London or New York in all its Continental resorts now, but nowhere has its taste been so much studied as in Italy, and especially in Florence" (014-134 to 014-136).

The paradox of this cosmopolitanism, however, is its insularity. The expatriates "seemed to see very little of Italian society, and to be shut out from practical knowledge of the local life by the terms upon which they had themselves insisted" (014-131 to 014-133). The Florentines, for their part, have constructed a "forestière conventionalised from the Florentine's observation of many Anglo-Saxons" (014-137 to 014-138) — a generic foreigner so well conjectured "that he was hemmed round with a very fair illusion of his national circumstances" (014-139 to 014-140). Colville objects to the ersatz quality of this environment: American shampoos at the barber's, English biscuits at the grocer's, Boston baked beans at the trattoria, and above all "that intense expression of American civilisation, the horse-car, triumphing along the magnificent avenues" (014-154 to 014-156). The cosmopolitan society of Florence, for all its international composition, turns out to be a kind of cultural echo chamber in which English predominates so thoroughly that Colville complains, "I can hardly get them to speak Italian to me" (014-167 to 014-168).

## Key Passages

> The door opened, and some English ladies entered, faintly acknowledging, provisionally ignoring, his presence, and talking of what they had been doing since lunch. They agreed that it was really too cold in the churches for any pleasure in the pictures, and that the Pitti Gallery, where they had those braziers, was the only place you could go with comfort. A French lady and her husband came in; a Russian lady followed; an Italian gentleman, an American family, and three or four detached men of the English-speaking race, whose language at once became the law of the table. (004-001 to 004-009)

This passage from the Hotel d'Atene dinner scene is the novel's most compressed representation of cosmopolitan society as a lived experience. The sequential entrance of English, French, Russian, Italian, and American figures — each acknowledged in a single phrase — creates a sense of the dining-room as a stage upon which national types assemble. The English ladies' "faintly acknowledging, provisionally ignoring" manner captures the delicate calibration of social recognition among strangers, while the detail that their conversation turns on the practical question of which galleries are warm enough to visit anchors the cosmopolitan tableau in mundane reality. The crowning phrase — that the language of the "English-speaking race" at once "became the law of the table" — encapsulates the quiet imperialism of Anglo-American culture within this apparently international setting.

> He liked also the notion of being seen and accepted in Florentine society as the old friend of Mrs. Bowen's, for he had not been long in discovering that her position in Florence was, among the foreign residents, rather authoritative. She was one of the very few Americans who were asked to Italian houses, and Italian houses lying even beyond the neutral ground of English-speaking intermarriages. ... So an old lady who possessed herself of Colville the night before had told him, celebrating Mrs. Bowen at length, and boasting of her acceptance among the best English residents, who, next after the natives, seem to constitute the social ambition of Americans living in Italian cities. (008-186 to 008-200)

This passage maps the social geography of cosmopolitan Florence with remarkable precision. The hierarchy is tripartite: at the summit, the native Italian aristocracy (the Princess Strozzi ball, "the veritable costumes of their ancestors"); below them, the established English residents; and below them, the Americans who aspire to acceptance among the English. Mrs. Bowen's distinction — she is "one of the very few Americans who were asked to Italian houses" — positions her at the permeable boundary between levels. The phrase "neutral ground of English-speaking intermarriages" is telling: it suggests that the only reliable bridge between Anglo-American and Italian society is marriage, and that even this ground is "neutral" rather than fully integrated. The old lady's "boasting" of Mrs. Bowen's English connections reveals that assimilation into cosmopolitan society is itself a form of social capital, to be claimed and celebrated by association.

> They seemed to see very little of Italian society, and to be shut out from practical knowledge of the local life by the terms upon which they had themselves insisted. Our race finds its simplified and cheapened London or New York in all its Continental resorts now, but nowhere has its taste been so much studied as in Italy, and especially in Florence. It was not, perhaps, the real Englishman or American who had been considered, but a *forestière* conventionalised from the Florentine's observation of many Anglo-Saxons. But he had been so well conjectured that he was hemmed round with a very fair illusion of his national circumstances. (014-131 to 014-140)

This passage contains the novel's most searching critique of cosmopolitan society. The expatriates are not integrated into Italian life — they are "shut out" from it, and the exclusion is self-imposed: it operates "by the terms upon which they had themselves insisted." This is the paradox at the heart of cosmopolitanism as Howells depicts it: the freedom to live abroad is exercised in such a way as to minimise actual contact with the foreign. The phrase "our race finds its simplified and cheapened London or New York" suggests that the expatriate experience is fundamentally a reproduction, not a transformation, of the culture left behind. The concept of the *forestière* — a generic foreigner constructed from "observation of many Anglo-Saxons" — adds a further layer of irony: the expatriates believe they are experiencing Italy, but what they encounter is a mirror held up by Italians who have learned to anticipate Anglo-Saxon expectations. "Hemmed round with a very fair illusion of his national circumstances," the expatriate enjoys the sensation of being abroad without the discomfort of genuine cultural displacement.

> The more recent and transitory people expressed something of the prevailing English and American aestheticism in the decoration of their apartments, but the greater part accepted the Florentine drawing-room as their landlord had imagined it for them, with furniture and curtains in yellow satin, a cheap ingrain carpet thinly covering the stone floor, and a fire of little logs ineffectually blazing on the hearth, and flickering on the carved frames of the pictures on the wall and the nakedness of the frescoed allegories in the ceiling. Whether of longer or shorter stay, the sojourners were bound together by a common language and a common social tradition; they all had a Day, and on that day there was tea and bread and butter for every comer. (014-113 to 014-123)

This passage renders the material culture of cosmopolitan society with Howells's characteristic attention to the texture of everyday life. The yellow satin furniture, the thin ingrain carpet, the ineffectual log fire, the frescoed allegories on the ceiling — these are the props of a life that is neither fully Italian nor fully Anglo-American, but something in between: the Florentine drawing-room "as their landlord had imagined it for them." The description captures the slightly shabby gentility of expatriate domesticity, in which grandeur (the frescoed ceiling) coexists with cheapness (the ingrain carpet). Against this material backdrop, the social ritual — "they all had a Day, and on that day there was tea and bread and butter for every comer" — acquires the force of a cultural constant. The "Day" (the formal reception day, an English social institution transplanted to Italian soil) and the invariable offering of tea and bread and butter represent the community's most durable bonds: not language alone, but the shared rituals that language makes possible.

## Related Concepts

- **Expatriate Identity** — The psychological and cultural condition of the Anglo-American sojourner in Florence. Cosmopolitan society provides the external framework for expatriate identity; the two concepts are inseparable, though cosmopolitan society emphasises the collective formation while expatriate identity emphasises individual experience.
- **American Girlhood and European Refinement** — The novel's running debate about the effects of European exposure on American young women (005-088, 006-096). Cosmopolitan society is the medium in which this debate occurs — the salons where Americans encounter European manners and where the "wild, sweet liberty" of American girlhood (003-026 to 003-027) is measured against Continental propriety.
- **The Florentine Salon** — The specific institutional form through which cosmopolitan society organises itself. Mrs. Bowen's Thursday at-homes, with their samovar, their bread and butter, and their twenty-five to thirty guests, are the novel's primary instance of the salon as a mechanism of social integration and display.
- **Language and Power** — The linguistic dimension of cosmopolitan society, in which English "at once became the law of the table" (004-008 to 004-009) and Colville finds himself unable to get Florentine shopkeepers to speak Italian to him (014-167 to 014-168). The dominance of English within a purportedly international setting is a marker of cultural power that the novel registers with quiet irony.
