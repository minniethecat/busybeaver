# Effie Bowen

## Role

Effie Bowen is a supporting character in William Dean Howells's _Indian Summer_. As the young daughter of Mrs. Bowen (Evelina Bowen), she functions as an emotional touchstone within the Palazzo Pinti household. Her presence softens social interactions, reveals the maternal dimensions of her mother's character, and serves as an innocent witness to the unfolding romantic tensions between the adult characters. Her child's perspective often provides candid, unguarded commentary on the emotional weather of the household (031-112).

## Character Analysis

Effie Bowen is portrayed as a well-bred, obedient child who has been raised with European propriety under her mother's watchful guidance. From her first introduction, Colville notes that "the child, hitherto keeping herself discreetly in the background, came forward and promptly gave her hand to Colville" (002-117). She is dressed in good taste, and Howells emphasizes the studied maturity of her presentation — "an effect of infancy had possibly been studied in the brevity of her skirts and the immaturity of her corsage" (002-117). Throughout the novel, Effie consistently defers to her mother's authority, even on the smallest points of social conduct. When Colville asks if she hates Florentine history, she checks herself: "I don't know enough about it," she answered, with a quick look of question at her mother, and checking herself in a possibly indiscreet smile" (003-011). Colville muses that for Effie, unlike her mother's generation, there would be "none of that wild, sweet liberty which once made American girlhood a long rapture" (003-011).

Despite her carefully cultivated propriety, Effie possesses a natural warmth and curiosity that gradually emerges as she grows comfortable with Colville. At an evening gathering, "Effie Bowen's eyes widened; she kept them fastened upon Colville in silent worship" (006-089) as she watches his playful banter. Her most spontaneous moment comes when, after saying goodnight, "she suddenly stooped forward and kissed him. 'You're so _funny_!' she cried, and ran and escaped beyond the _portiere_" (006-089). This impulsive gesture delights Colville and marks her growing affection for him as a family friend. Later, she becomes a quiet confidante, with Colville often walking about Florence with "Effie's daintily-gloved little hand in his" (016-179).

Effie also serves as an unwitting emotional barometer for the household. When tensions rise between Colville, Imogene, and Mrs. Bowen, Effie's innocent questions cut to the heart of the matter. Observing the unhappiness around her, she remarks, "I should think that now Mr. Colville's concluded to stay, we would all be happy again. But we don't seem to. We're — we're perfectly demoralised!" (031-112). The word "demoralised," one she picked up from Colville's Western slang, becomes a poignant signal of how the adult conflicts have unsettled the child's world. Her face "broke in a passion of tears, and Effie sobbed herself to sleep" (031-114), underscoring the emotional cost borne by the innocent in the midst of adult romantic entanglements.

## Relationships

- **Mrs. Bowen (Evelina Bowen)** — Effie's mother, with whom she shares a strikingly close resemblance (004-139). Effie is deeply obedient to her mother, referring "not only every point of conduct, but every thought and feeling, to her mother" (003-011). Mrs. Bowen's protective severity — "No, I wouldn't let Effie. The times are changed. I wouldn't let Effie go to the theatre alone with a young gentleman" (016-146) — reflects the Europeanised upbringing she has chosen for her daughter.

- **Theodore Colville** — Colville becomes a fond, avuncular presence in Effie's life. He takes an interest in her reading, plans to write a Florentine history for her (020-070), spoils her with sweets and presents (016-179), and teaches her "picturesque Western phrases" (016-187). Effie adores him for his humor and gifts, and her impulsive goodnight kiss (006-089) is one of the novel's most tender moments.

- **Imogene Graham** — Effie adores the beautiful young Imogene, whose "face expressed an adoration of Miss Graham far beyond her allegiance to her mother" (005-123). Imogene in turn is protective of Effie, helping tuck her into bed (020-138) and gently advocating for her.

- **Mr. Waters** — A peripheral connection; Effie is present at the _salon_ gatherings where the elderly clergyman appears, and she deferentially brings tea and biscuits to the guests (007-108).

## Key Scenes

**1. First Introduction on the Ponte Vecchio (Chapter 2)**

Colville first meets Effie when Mrs. Bowen introduces her on the bridge. The child is demure, well-dressed, and immediately establishes her role as her mother's quiet shadow.

> "This is my little Effie," said Mrs. Bowen after a moment; and now the child, hitherto keeping herself discreetly in the background, came forward and promptly gave her hand to Colville, who perceived that she was not so small as he had thought her at first; an effect of infancy had possibly been studied in the brevity of her skirts and the immaturity of her corsage, but both were in good taste, and really to the advantage of her young figure. (002-117)

**2. The Goodnight Kiss (Chapter 6)**

After an evening of lively conversation during which Effie watches Colville with silent admiration, her spontaneous goodnight kiss signals her acceptance of him as a beloved family friend.

> Effie Bowen's eyes widened; she kept them fastened upon Colville in silent worship. ... The child came with instant submissiveness and kissed her mother good-night; she kissed Miss Graham, and gave her hand to Colville. He held it a moment, letting her pull shyly away from him, while he lolled back in his chair, and laughed at her with his sad eyes. ... when Colville released her hand she suddenly stooped forward and kissed him. "You're so _funny_!" she cried, and ran and escaped beyond the _portiere_. (006-089)

**3. Writing History for Effie (Chapter 20)**

Colville's plan to write a children's history of Florence for Effie reveals both his affection for the child and his desire to remain connected to the Bowen household. Sitting with Effie on his knee, he tells her the legend of San Miniato, and her gentle, innocent responses show her sensitive nature.

> "I'm going to put every one I can find into the history I'm writing for Effie. ..." "Did he have to die when he got to the oratory?" asked the child, with gentle regret. "It appears so," said Colville, sketching. "He would have been dead by this time, anyway, you know." "Yes," she reluctantly admitted. "I never quite like those things, either, Effie," he said, pressing her to him. (020-070, 020-078)

**4. "Perfectly Demoralised" (Chapter 31)**

As the emotional crisis between Colville, Imogene, and Mrs. Bowen deepens, Effie becomes the unconscious voice of the household's unhappiness. Her use of Colville's own word — "demoralised" — and her tearful breakdown reveal how deeply the adult drama has affected her.

> "You don't seem very happy either," said the child, watching her own face as it quivered in the mirror. "I should think that now Mr. Colville's concluded to stay, we would all be happy again. But we don't seem to. We're — we're perfectly demoralised!" It was one of the words she had picked up from Colville. The quivering face in the glass broke in a passion of tears, and Effie sobbed herself to sleep. (031-112, 031-114)

## Biographical Sketch

Effie Bowen is the young, American-born daughter of Mrs. Evelina Bowen, a wealthy American widow residing in Florence. Effie attends school in Vevay, Switzerland (003-011), receiving a European education under her mother's careful supervision. She spends her holidays with her mother at the Palazzo Pinti on the Lung' Arno della Zecca in Florence (002-164). Her late father, Mr. Bowen, is deceased before the novel begins (002-114). Effie is perhaps nine or ten years old — young enough to kneel on chairs and hold hands during walks, yet old enough to read and to observe the emotional currents around her with unsettling perceptiveness.
