[035-001] [[imogene-graham|Imogene]] returned to the salotto, where the little girl was standing with

[035-002] her face to the window, drearily looking out; her back expressed an

[035-003] inner desolation which revealed itself in her eyes when Imogene caught

[035-004] her head between her hands, and tilted up her face to kiss it.

[035-005] "What is the matter, Effie?" she demanded gaily.

[035-006] "Nothing."

[035-007] "Oh yes, there is."

[035-008] "Nothing that you will care for. As long as he's pleasant to you, you

[035-009] don't care what he does to me."

[035-010] "What has he done to you?"

[035-011] "He didn't take the slightest notice of me when I came into the room. He

[035-012] didn't speak to me, or even look at me."

[035-013] Imogene caught the little grieving, quivering face to her breast "He is

[035-014] a wicked, wicked wretch! And I will give him the awfulest scolding he

[035-015] ever had when he comes here again. I will teach him to neglect my pet. I

[035-016] will let him understand that if he doesn't notice you, he needn't notice

[035-017] me. I will tell you, Effie--I've just thought of a way. The next time he

[035-018] comes we will both receive him. We will sit up very stiffly on the sofa

[035-019] together, and just answer Yes, No, Yes, No, to everything he says, till

[035-020] he begins to take the hint, and learns how to behave himself. Will you?"

[035-021] A smile glittered through the little girl's tears; but she asked, "Do

[035-022] you think it would be very polite?"

[035-023] "No matter, polite or not, it's what he deserves. Of course, as soon as

[035-024] he begins to take the hint, we will be just as we always are."

[035-025] Imogene despatched a note, which [[theodore-colville|Colville]] got the next morning, to tell

[035-026] him of his crime, and apprise him of his punishment, and of the sweet

[035-027] compunction that had pleaded for him in the breast of the child. If he

[035-028] did not think he could help play the comedy through, he must come

[035-029] prepared to offer Effie some sort of atonement.

[035-030] It was easy to do this: to come with his pockets full of presents, and

[035-031] take the little girl on his lap, and pour out all his troubled heart in

[035-032] the caresses and tendernesses which would bring him no remorse. He

[035-033] humbled himself to her thoroughly, and with a strange sincerity in the

[035-034] harmless duplicity, and promised, if she would take him back into

[035-035] favour, that he would never offend again. [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]] had sent word that

[035-036] she was not well enough to see him; she had another of her headaches;

[035-037] and he sent back a sympathetic and respectful message by Effie, who

[035-038] stood thoughtfully at her mother's pillow after she had delivered it,

[035-039] fingering the bouquet Colville had brought her, and putting her head

[035-040] first on this side, and then on that to admire it.

[035-041] "I think Mr. Colville and Imogene are much more affectionate than they

[035-042] used to be," she said.

[035-043] Mrs. Bowen started up on her elbow. "What do you mean, Effie?"

[035-044] "Oh, they're both so good to me."

[035-045] "Yes," said her mother, dropping back to her pillow. "Both?"

[035-046] "Yes; he's the _most_ affectionate."

[035-047] The mother turned her face the other way. "Then he must be," she

[035-048] murmured.

[035-049] "What?" asked the child.

[035-050] "Nothing. I didn't know I spoke."

[035-051] The little girl stood a while still playing with her flowers. "I think

[035-052] Mr. Colville is about the pleasantest gentleman that comes here. Don't

[035-053] you, mamma?"

[035-054] "Yes."

[035-055] "He's so interesting, and says such nice things. I don't know whether

[035-056] children ought to think of such things, but I wish I was going to marry

[035-057] some one like Mr. Colville. Of course I should want to be tolerably old

[035-058] if I did. How old do you think a person ought to be to marry him?"

[035-059] "You mustn't talk of such things, Effie," said her mother.

[035-060] "No; I suppose it isn't very nice." She picked out a bud in her bouquet,

[035-061] and kissed it; then she held the nosegay at arm's-length before her, and

[035-062] danced away with it.

[035-063] XVII

[035-064] In the ensuing fortnight a great many gaieties besides the Egyptian ball

[035-065] took place, and Colville went wherever he and Imogene were both invited.

[035-066] He declined the quiet dinners which he liked, and which his hearty

[035-067] appetite and his habit of talk fitted him to enjoy, and accepted

[035-068] invitations to all sorts of evenings and At Homes, where dancing

[035-069] occupied a modest corner of the card, and usurped the chief place in the

[035-070] pleasures. At these places it was mainly his business to see Imogene

[035-071] danced with by others, but sometimes he waltzed with her himself, and

[035-072] then he was complimented by people of his own age, who had left off

[035-073] dancing, upon his vigour. They said they could not stand that sort of

[035-074] thing, though they supposed, if you kept yourself in practice, it did

[035-075] not come so hard. One of his hostesses, who had made a party for her

[035-076] daughters, told him that he was an example to everybody, and that if

[035-077] middle-aged people at home mingled more in the amusements of the young,

[035-078] American society would not be the silly, insipid, boy-and-girl affair

[035-079] that it was now. He went to these places in the character of a young

[035-080] man, but he was not readily accepted or recognised in that character.

[035-081] They gave him frumps to take out to supper, mothers and maiden aunts,

[035-082] and if the mothers were youngish, they threw off on him, and did not

[035-083] care for his talk.

[035-084] At one of the parties Imogene seemed to become aware for the first time

[035-085] that the lapels of his dress-coat were not faced with silk.

[035-086] "Why don't you have them so?" she asked. "All the _other_ young men

[035-087] have. And you ought to wear a _boutonnière_."

[035-088] "Oh, I think a man looks rather silly in silk lapels at my--" He

[035-089] arrested himself, and then continued: "I'll see what the tailor can do

[035-090] for me. In the meantime, give me a bud out of your bouquet."

[035-091] "How sweet you are!" she sighed. "You do the least thing so that it is

[035-092] ten times as good as if any one else did it."

[035-093] The same evening, as he stood leaning against a doorway, behind Imogene

[035-094] and a young fellow with whom she was beginning a quadrille, he heard her

[035-095] taking him to task.

[035-096] "Why do you say 'Sir' to Mr. Colville?"

[035-097] "Well, I know the English laugh at us for doing it, and say it's like

[035-098] servants; but I never feel quite right answering just 'Yes' and 'No' to

[035-099] a man of his age."

[035-100] This was one of the Inglehart boys, whom he met at nearly all of these

[035-101] parties, and not all of whom were so respectful. Some of them treated

[035-102] him upon an old-boy theory, joking him as freely as if he were one of

[035-103] themselves, laughing his antiquated notions of art to scorn, but

[035-104] condoning them because he was good-natured, and because a man could not

[035-105] help being of his own epoch anyway. They put a caricature of him among

[035-106] the rest on the walls of their _trattoria_, where he once dined with

[035-107] them.

[035-108] Mrs. Bowen did not often see him when he went to call upon Imogene, and

[035-109] she was not at more than two or three of the parties. [[mrs-amsden|Mrs. Amsden]] came

[035-110] to chaperon the girl, and apparently suffered an increase of unrequited

[035-111] curiosity in regard to his relations to the Bowen household, and the

[035-112] extraordinary development of his social activity. Colville not only went

[035-113] to all those evening parties, but he was in continual movement during

[035-114] the afternoon at receptions and at "days," of which he began to think

[035-115] each lady had two or three. Here he drank tea, cup after cup, in

[035-116] reckless excitement, and at night when he came home from the dancing

[035-117] parties, dropping with fatigue, he could not sleep till toward morning.

[035-118] He woke at the usual breakfast-hour, and then went about drowsing

[035-119] throughout the day till the tea began again in the afternoon. He fell

[035-120] asleep whenever he sat down, not only in the reading-room at Viesseux's,

[035-121] where he disturbed the people over their newspapers by his

[035-122] demonstrations of somnolence, but even at church, whither he went one

[035-123] Sunday to please Imogene, and started awake during the service with the

[035-124] impression that the clergyman had been making a joke. Everybody but

[035-125] Imogene was smiling. At the café he slept without scruple, selecting a

[035-126] corner seat for the purpose, and proportioning his _buonamano_ to the

[035-127] indulgence of the _giovane_. He could not tell how long he slept at

[035-128] these places, but sometimes it seemed to him hours.

[035-129] One day he went to see Imogene, and while [[effie-bowen|Effie Bowen]] stood prattling to

[035-130] him as he sat waiting for Imogene to come in, he faded light-headedly

[035-131] away from himself on the sofa, as if he had been in his corner at the

[035-132] café. Then he was aware of some one saying "Sh!" and he saw Effie

[035-133] Bowen, with her finger on her lip, turned toward Imogene, a figure of

[035-134] beautiful despair in the doorway. He was all tucked up with sofa

[035-135] pillows, and made very comfortable, by the child, no doubt. She slipped

[035-136] out, seeing him awake, so as to leave him and Imogene alone, as she had

[035-137] apparently been generally instructed to do, and Imogene came forward.

[035-138] "What is the matter, Theodore?" she asked patiently. She had taken to

[035-139] calling him Theodore when they were alone. She owned that she did not

[035-140] like the name, but she said it was right she should call him by it,

[035-141] since it was his. She came and sat down beside him, where he had raised

[035-142] himself to a sitting posture, but she did not offer him any caress.

[035-143] "Nothing," he answered. "But this climate is making me insupportably

[035-144] drowsy; or else the spring weather."

[035-145] "Oh no; it isn't that," she said, with a slight sigh. He had left her in

[035-146] the middle of a german at three o'clock in the morning, but she now

[035-147] looked as fresh and lambent as a star. "It's the late hours. They're

[035-148] killing you."

[035-149] Colville tried to deny it; his incoherencies dissolved themselves in a

[035-150] yawn, which he did not succeed in passing for a careless laugh.

[035-151] "It won't do," she said, as if speaking to herself; "no, it won't do."

[035-152] "Oh yes, it will," Colville protested. "I don't mind being up. I've been

[035-153] used to it all my life on the paper. It's just some temporary thing.

[035-154] It'll come all right."

[035-155] "Well, no matter," said Imogene. "It makes you ridiculous, going to all

[035-156] those silly places, and I'd rather give it up."

[035-157] The tears began to steal down her cheeks, and Colville sighed. It seemed

[035-158] to him that somebody or other was always crying. A man never quite gets

[035-159] used to the tearfulness of women.

[035-160] "Oh, don't mind it," he said. "If you wish me to go, I will go! Or die

[035-161] in the attempt," he added, with a smile.

[035-162] Imogene did not smile with him. "I don't wish you to go any more. It was

[035-163] a mistake in the first place, and from this out I will adapt myself to

[035-164] you."

[035-165] "And give up all your pleasures? Do you think I would let you do that?

[035-166] No, indeed! Neither in this nor in anything else. I will not cut off

[035-167] your young life in any way, Imogene--not shorten it or diminish it. If I

[035-168] thought I should do that, or you would try to do it for me, I should

[035-169] wish I had never seen you."

[035-170] "It isn't that. I know how good you are, and that you would do anything

[035-171] for me."

[035-172] "Well, then, why don't you go to these fandangoes alone? I can see that

[035-173] you have me on your mind all the time, when I'm with you."

[035-174] "Oughtn't I?"

[035-175] "Yes, up to a certain point, but not up to the point of spoiling your

[035-176] fun. I will drop in now and then, but I won't try to come to all of

[035-177] them, after this; you'll get along perfectly well with Mrs. Amsden, and

[035-178] I shall be safe from her for a while. That old lady has marked me for

[035-179] her prey: I can see it in her glittering eyeglass. I shall fall asleep

[035-180] some evening between dances, and then she will get it all out of me."
