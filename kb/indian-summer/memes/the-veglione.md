## Summary

The veglione at the [[teatro-della-pergola]] is the novel's central social set-piece and its decisive narrative hinge. Paolo brings [[theodore-colville|Colville]] the news of the masked Carnival ball (017-052), and though the ladies have already heard of it, the decision to attend requires [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]] to overcome her scruples about propriety (017-067–017-073). They hire dominoes and masks -- Mrs. Bowen in purple, [[imogene-graham|Imogene]] in light blue, [[effie-bowen|Effie]] in black -- and arrive at the theatre at eleven (017-129), where "the brilliantly lighted theatre flashed its colours and splendours upon them" (017-147–017-148).

On the floor, a red-masked stranger immediately attaches himself to [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]] in elaborate pantomime (017-156). [[mr-waters|Mr. Waters]] appears in philosophic disguise (018-062–018-064). But the evening's centre of gravity shifts when Mrs. Bowen, in a surprising reversal, permits [[imogene-graham|Imogene]] to dance, and Colville sweeps the girl into a waltz (018-120–018-123). During the dance, Colville is visited by the memory of the veglione of seventeen years before and his lost youth (018-138–018-143). Imogene experiences the waltz as a moment of transcendent romance, declaring "This very instant it was just as if I had been here before, long ago" (018-147–018-148). The evening ends in disarray: Effie falls ill, Mrs. Bowen departs without Colville or Imogene, and the two are left to return home alone under the white moonlight of Carnival (019-097–019-128).

## Context

The veglione occurs during the Florentine Carnival season and represents the culmination of the social intimacy that has developed between [[theodore-colville|Colville]] and [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]]'s household throughout the winter. Colville has been frequenting [[palazzo-pinti]] as a companion and cultural guide, and the Carnival ball offers an arena where the tensions latent in this arrangement -- romantic rivalry, the claims of [[youth-and-age|youth versus middle age]], the conflict between American propriety and European license -- can erupt into the open (017-037–017-057).

Mrs. Bowen's decision to attend is fraught from the start. She acknowledges that "people go in boxes" and that "if we went upon the floor, I shouldn't wish it to be known at all, though foreigners can do almost anything they like" (017-067–017-073). The veglione thus carries the weight of a transgression even before it begins. The choice to go in masks and dominoes -- to participate rather than observe -- signals a departure from the social caution that has heretofore governed Mrs. Bowen's conduct. That she allows Effie to attend, and further allows Imogene to dance, marks successive abandonments of the boundaries she had set, each with accumulating consequence.

- **[[theodore-colville|Theodore Colville]]** — The middle-aged protagonist who proposes the veglione outing, dances with [[imogene-graham|Imogene]], and finds himself caught between the romantic implications of the waltz and his ironic self-awareness. The evening revives his memory of an earlier Florentine veglione and the woman who jilted him (018-138–018-143).
- **[[imogene-graham|Imogene Graham]]** — The young American protegee for whom the veglione becomes a transformative romantic experience. Her waltz with Colville and their dialogue about pre-existence (018-146–018-150) mark the point at which her imaginative sympathy for his [[youth-and-age|lost youth]] deepens into dangerous infatuation.
- **[[lina-bowen|Lina Bowen]]** — The widow and chaperone who reluctantly agrees to the veglione, sets the ground rules against dancing, then breaks them. Her withdrawal from the theatre with the ailing [[effie-bowen|Effie]], and her cold irony toward Colville the next day (019-163–019-182), signal the rupture the veglione creates in her relationship with him.
- **[[effie-bowen|Effie Bowen]]** — Mrs. Bowen's young daughter, permitted to attend in a black domino. Her faintness provides the occasion for Mrs. Bowen's early departure and becomes the pretext for the estrangement between Colville and Mrs. Bowen (019-097–019-098).
- **[[mr-waters|Mr. Waters]]** — The elderly American minister whose unexpected appearance at the veglione in a black domino, regarding the Carnival with philosophical serenity, provides a moment of comic deflation (018-062–018-064). He assists Mrs. Bowen in escorting Effie out (019-174).
- **The Red Mask** — An anonymous Florentine in red from head to foot who "attached himself to the party, and began to make love to her in excellent pantomime" (017-156–017-157). He returns multiple times and ultimately delivers the news that Mrs. Bowen has departed, revealing himself as a well-known painter (019-110–019-112).

## Aftermath / Consequences

The veglione fractures the equilibrium of the [[palazzo-pinti]] circle. The next morning, Colville arrives to find [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]] "cold" and ironic: her politeness is a weapon (019-163–019-182). When Colville attempts to apologise for keeping [[imogene-graham|Imogene]] on the dance floor, Mrs. Bowen deflects him with a devastating civility: "What exemplary young people you are! The way each of you confesses and assumes all the blame would leave the severest chaperone without a word" (019-168–019-169). Her later remark -- "As I told Imogene, young people will be young people; and I knew how fond you were of dancing" (019-181–019-182) -- drives the irony home, classifying Colville not as the mature guardian he imagines himself but as an equal participant in [[youth-and-age|youthful folly]].

The consequences reverberate through the novel's second half. Chapter 24 brings a reckoning when Mrs. Bowen confesses: "I didn't approve of going to the veglione. A great many people would be shocked if they knew I went; I wouldn't at all like to have it known" (024-133–024-135). Imogene in turn admits, "I knew you didn't want to go to the veglione. If I hadn't been the most selfish girl in the world I wouldn't have let you. I forced you to go" (024-140–024-141). The veglione thus becomes the symbolic centre around which the characters' [[self-deception|self-knowledge]] and mutual recriminations coalesce. As the narrator later observes, Mrs. Bowen's "relenting was not continuous" after the veglione -- the evening has permanently altered the terms of every relationship in the novel.

## Key Dialogue

> "Do you believe in pre-existence?" he demanded of Imogene.
> "Oh yes!" she flashed back. "This very instant it was just as if I had been here before, long ago."
> "Dancing with me?"
> "With you? Yes--yes--I think so."
> (018-146–018-150)

This exchange, suspended between flirtation and genuine romantic transport, captures the veglione's doubleness: it is at once a Carnival game and a moment of real emotional consequence. Colville knows that [[imogene-graham|Imogene]] "was making herself believe what she said, and that she had not lived long enough to know this" (018-151–018-152), but the knowledge does not inoculate him against the charm of her sincerity.

> Colville was astonished. "The veglione has gone to your head, Mrs. Bowen," he tacitly made his comment.
> (018-115–018-116)

[[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]]'s sudden permission for Imogene to dance, after having "circumscribed the adventure so as to exclude dancing from it" (017-177), is the moment the veglione tilts from controlled adventure into something unpredictable. Colville's unspoken aside registers that the rules have changed and that the responsibility for what follows has shifted.

## Historical / Cultural Context

The veglione (Italian: "grand ball," from *veglia*, "vigil" or "wake") was a Carnival institution in nineteenth-century [[florence]]. Held at the [[teatro-della-pergola]], the city's principal opera house, the veglione transformed the theatre into a ballroom by levelling the pit floor to the stage. Masked attendance allowed for a temporary suspension of social distinctions and proprieties; the anonymity of the domino and mask created a licensed space for freedoms -- particularly for foreign visitors -- that would have been impossible in ordinary Florentine society. Howells draws on this real institution to dramatise the collision between American moral categories and the more permissive codes of European [[cosmopolitan-society|social life]]. The veglione functions simultaneously as a realistic depiction of Carnival custom and as a symbolic arena in which the novel's central conflicts -- between [[youth-and-age|youth and age]], innocence and experience, restraint and abandon -- are acted out under the cover of masks. The motif of masking and unmasking links the veglione thematically to the novel's preoccupation with [[self-deception]] and the difficulty of knowing one's own heart.
