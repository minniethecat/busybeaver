Lina Bowen (née Ridgely), known throughout the novel primarily as Mrs. Bowen, is the female protagonist of William Dean Howells's *Indian Summer*. A poised and self-possessed American widow living at the [[palazzo-pinti|Palazzo Pinti]] on the [[lungarno|Lung'Arno della Zecca]], she presides over [[mrs-bowens-salon|a cosmopolitan salon]] of expatriates and visitors in [[florence|Florence]] (002-164). Her reunion with [[theodore-colville|Theodore Colville]] after seventeen years on the [[ponte-vecchio|Ponte Vecchio]] sets in motion the novel's central romantic triangle, as she finds herself caught between her guarded love for Colville and her duty as chaperone to the young [[imogene-graham|Imogene Graham]]. Mrs. Bowen embodies the tension between personal desire and [[sacrifice-and-renunciation|moral principle]] that Howells presents as the defining challenge of mature love — a love shaped not by youthful passion but by the chastened wisdom of experience, the novel's [[indian-summer-metaphor|Indian summer]] of the heart.

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Bowen is introduced as a woman of quiet elegance and understated charm, a figure whose composure masks a depth of feeling that the novel gradually reveals. Howells describes her with meticulous attention to the qualities of grace that define her presence:

> She put out her round, slender arm, and gave him a frank clasp of her gloved hand. The glove wrinkled richly up the sleeve of her dress half-way to her elbow.
> (002-151–002-153)

Her physical presentation is matched by an inner poise that Colville, with his habitual irony, cannot help but admire: she possesses "a certain charming stiffness, like the stiffness of a very tall feather" (002-137). Howells repeatedly emphasizes the "lady-like" quality of her bearing — "the turn of her head, the management of her arm from the elbow, the curve of her hand from wrist to finger-tips, the smile, subdued, but sufficiently sweet, playing about her little mouth" (002-130).

Twenty years before the novel's main events, when she was Miss Lina Ridgely, she was the friend and confidante of the young woman who jilted Colville in Venice (003-117). Even then, she showed a quiet kindness toward him that Colville only later recognizes as significant: "Lina Ridgely in many fine little ways had shown a kindness, almost a compassion, for him, as for one whose unconsciousness a hopeless doom impended over" (003-128). He eventually realizes that "she had always seemed to like him" and that "she had probably defended and advocated him" (003-132). This retrospective recognition — that Lina had been present and caring all along while he was preoccupied with her more glamorous friend — is one of the novel's psychological insights into how love matures over time.

Beneath her composed exterior, Mrs. Bowen possesses a deeply principled nature that becomes the novel's moral fulcrum. When Colville finally declares his love, she confesses her own feelings but refuses to marry him out of moral scruple, articulating a code of [[self-deception|self-honesty]] that shames his own evasions:

> "I love you — I blush and burn for shame to think that I love you. But I will never marry you; I can at least help doing that, and I can still keep some little trace of self-respect."
> (046-175–046-177)

Her internal conflict arises from having been placed in the position of guardian to Imogene while harboring feelings for the same man Imogene became engaged to. She feels she has "violate[d] every principle that was dear to me" (046-150). This moral agony — loving a man who was, however unwittingly, engaged to her charge — is not priggishness but a genuine crisis of integrity, and Howells treats it with full seriousness. Her eventual acceptance of Colville, prompted by her daughter [[effie-bowen|Effie]]'s distress at his departure (047-063), reveals the human vulnerability beneath her moral rigor and completes her arc from guarded widow to a woman who can accept love without betraying her principles.

## Key Scenes

### The Reunion on the Ponte Vecchio (Chapter II)

Mrs. Bowen and Effie encounter Colville on the [[ponte-vecchio|Ponte Vecchio]], where she is pointing out the bridges of Florence to her daughter. Her recognition of him is immediate and unguarded:

> "Why, Mrs. Bowen!" he said.
> She put out her round, slender arm, and gave him a frank clasp of her gloved hand. The glove wrinkled richly up the sleeve of her dress half-way to her elbow.
> (002-151–002-153)

She invites him to her Thursday receptions. When Colville notes that seventeen years have passed, she responds with characteristic charm: "Is it so many?" cried Mrs. Bowen, with lovely dismay. "Yes, it is," she sighed, and she did not speak for an appreciable interval (002-148–002-149). He knows, in that pause, that she is thinking of his old love affair — a shared knowledge that makes their reunion something more than mere reacquaintance.

### The Evening Reception at Palazzo Pinti (Chapter IV)

Mrs. Bowen appears as the composed and elegant hostess of [[mrs-bowens-salon|her weekly salon]], greeting Colville in her drawing room before a fire. The scene establishes her social world and her centrality within it — the [[cosmopolitan-society|cosmopolitan expatriate circle]] of Florence that forms the novel's social backdrop.

> Mrs. Bowen stood in front of the fire in a brown silk of subdued splendour, and with her hands and fan and handkerchief tastefully composed before her.
> (004-114)

When Colville remarks on the striking resemblance between mother and daughter — "I thought it was you, Mrs. Bowen, who had got round to my side with a sash on" — her delighted response reveals the pleasure she takes in being seen clearly by him: "You notice it, then?" (004-140).

### The Declaration and Refusal (Chapter XLVI)

In the climactic scene of the [[the-final-confrontation|final confrontation]], alone with Mrs. Bowen after Effie leaves the room, Colville confesses his love. The scene's power comes from the contrast between the violent trembling of her hand and the "perfect quiet of her attitude" (046-130–046-131):

> He leaned forward and took one of the hands that lay in her lap. It trembled with a violence inconceivable in relation to the perfect quiet of her attitude. But she did not try to take it away. "Could you — do you love me?"
> "Yes," she whispered.
> (046-130–046-132)

But her "Yes" is immediately followed by a firm refusal. The ensuing dialogue reveals the depth of her moral anguish — she could not act on her feelings without betraying Imogene and violating her own principles (046-147–046-152). The impasse is broken not by Colville's arguments but by Effie's return: the child sobs "I can't *bear* it!" at the prospect of Colville's departure, and Mrs. Bowen finally relents with "Oh, you must stay!" (047-027–047-055).

### Effie's Bedtime (Chapter XLIV)

In a quiet domestic scene on Colville's last night in the [[palazzo-pinti|Palazzo Pinti]], Mrs. Bowen sits with him in the darkened room while Effie sleeps on his lap. In the stillness, Colville calls her by her girlhood name — "It seemed like voices from our youth — Lina" — and "She had no resentment of his use of her name" (044-126). This moment of intimate stillness, suspended between farewell and possibility, is the emotional pivot on which the novel's resolution turns — a shared memory of [[the-past|the past]] that points toward a shared future.

### The Dance Memory (Chapter IX)

Colville recalls Mrs. Bowen's past as Lina Ridgely, remembering the social dynamics of their shared youth when she and her friend felt "the disadvantage of having come from the Western side of an imaginary line" (009-007). This memory underscores Mrs. Bowen's journey from a young Western girl who felt socially inferior to a poised and respected society hostess in Florence — a transformation that mirrors the novel's broader interest in [[american-innocence|American identity]] remade by European experience.
