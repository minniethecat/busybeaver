[038-001] "I should imagine that Venice would be good for you," he said.

[038-002] "It's still very harsh there, I hear. No; when we leave [[florence|Florence]], I

[038-003] think we will go to Switzerland."

[038-004] "Oh, not to Madame Schebres's," pleaded the child, turning upon her.

[038-005] "No, not to Madame Schebres," consented the mother. She continued,

[038-006] addressing [[theodore-colville|Colville]]: "I was thinking of Lausanne. Do you know Lausanne

[038-007] at all?"

[038-008] "Only from Gibbon's report. It's hardly up to date."

[038-009] "I thought of taking a house there for the summer," said [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]],

[038-010] playing with Effie's fingers. "It's pleasant by the lake, I suppose."

[038-011] "It's lovely by the lake!" cried the child. "Oh, do go, mamma! I could

[038-012] get a boat and learn to row. Here you can't row, the [[arno-river|Arno]]'s so swift."

[038-013] "The air would bring you up," said Colville to Mrs. Bowen.

[038-014] "Switzerland's the only country where you're perfectly sure of waking

[038-015] new every morning."

[038-016] This idea interested the child. "Waking new!" she repeated.

[038-017] "Yes; perfectly made over. You wake up another person. Shouldn't you

[038-018] think that would be nice?"

[038-019] "No."

[038-020] "Well, I shouldn't, in your place. But in mine, I much prefer to wake up

[038-021] another person. Only it's pretty hard on the other person."

[038-022] "How queer you are!" The child set her teeth for fondness of him, and

[038-023] seizing his cheeks between her hands, squeezed them hard, admiring the

[038-024] effect upon his features, which in some respects was not advantageous.

[038-025] "Effie!" cried her mother sternly; and she dropped to her place again,

[038-026] and laid hold of Colville's arm for protection. "You are really very

[038-027] rude. I shall send you to bed."

[038-028] "Oh no, don't, Mrs. Bowen," he begged. "I'm responsible for these

[038-029] violences. Effie used to be a very well behaved child before she began

[038-030] playing with me. It's all my fault."

[038-031] They remained talking on the sofa together, while [[imogene-graham|Imogene]] and [[mr-morton|Mr. Morton]]

[038-032] continued to interest themselves in the book. From time to time she

[038-033] looked over at them, and then turned again to the young clergyman, who,

[038-034] when he had closed the book, rested his hands on its top and began to

[038-035] give an animated account of something, conjecturably his sojourn in

[038-036] Rome.

[038-037] In a low voice, and with pauses adjusted to the occasional silences of

[038-038] the young people across the room, Mrs. Bowen told Colville how Mr.

[038-039] Morton was introduced to her by an old friend who was greatly interested

[038-040] in him. She said, frankly, that she had been able to be of use to him,

[038-041] and that he was now going back to America very soon; it was as if she

[038-042] were privy to the conjecture that had come to the surface in his talk

[038-043] with [[mr-waters|Mr. Waters]], and wished him to understand exactly how matters stood

[038-044] with the young clergyman and herself. Colville, indeed, began to be more

[038-045] tolerant of him; he succeeded in praising the sermon he had heard him

[038-046] preach.

[038-047] "Oh, he has talent," said Mrs. Bowen.

[038-048] They fell into the old, almost domestic strain, from which she broke at

[038-049] times with an effort, but returning as if helplessly to it. He had the

[038-050] gift of knowing how not to take an advantage with women; that sense of

[038-051] unconstraint in them fought in his favour; when Effie dropped her head

[038-052] wearily against his arm, her mother even laughed in sending her off to

[038-053] bed; she had hitherto been serious. Imogene said she would go to see her

[038-054] tucked in, and that sent the clergyman to say good-night to Mrs. Bowen,

[038-055] and to put an end to Colville's audience.

[038-056] In these days, when Colville came every night to [[palazzo-pinti|Palazzo Pinti]], he got

[038-057] back the tone he had lost in the past fortnight. He thought that it was

[038-058] the complete immunity from his late pleasures, and the regular and

[038-059] sufficient sleep, which had set him firmly on his feet again, but he did

[038-060] not inquire very closely. Imogene went two or three times, after she had

[038-061] declared she would go no more, from the necessity women feel of blunting

[038-062] the edge of comment; but Colville profited instantly and fully by the

[038-063] release from the parties which she offered him. He did not go even to

[038-064] afternoon tea-drinkings; the "days" of the different ladies, which he

[038-065] had been so diligent to observe, knew him no more. At the hours when

[038-066] society assembled in this house or that and inquired for him, or

[038-067] wondered about him, he was commonly taking a nap, and he was punctually

[038-068] in bed every night at eleven, after his return from Mrs. Bowen's.

[038-069] He believed, of course, that he went there because he now no longer met

[038-070] Imogene elsewhere, and he found the house pleasanter than it had ever

[038-071] been since the veglione. Mrs. Bowen's relenting was not continuous,

[038-072] however. There were times that seemed to be times of question and of

[038-073] struggle with her, when she vacillated between the old cordiality and

[038-074] the later alienation; when she went beyond the former, or lapsed into

[038-075] moods colder and more repellent than the latter. It would have been

[038-076] difficult to mark the moment when these struggles ceased altogether, and

[038-077] an evening passed in unbroken kindness between them. But afterwards

[038-078] Colville could remember an emotion of grateful surprise at a subtle word

[038-079] or action of hers in which she appeared to throw all restraint--scruple

[038-080] or rancour, whichever it might be--to the winds, and become perfectly

[038-081] his friend again. It must have been by compliance with some wish or

[038-082] assent to some opinion of his; what he knew was that he was not only

[038-083] permitted, he was invited, to feel himself the most favoured guest. The

[038-084] charming smile, so small and sweet, so very near to bitterness, came

[038-085] back to her lips, the deeply fringed eyelids were lifted to let the

[038-086] sunny eyes stream upon him. She did, now, whatever he asked her. She

[038-087] consulted his taste and judgment on many points; she consented to

[038-088] resume, when she should be a little stronger, their visits to the

[038-089] churches and galleries: it would be a shame to go away from Florence

[038-090] without knowing them thoroughly. It came to her asking him to drive with

[038-091] her and Imogene in the [[cascine|Cascine]]; and when Imogene made some excuse not to

[038-092] go, Mrs. Bowen did not postpone the drive, but took Colville and Effie.

[038-093] They drove quite down to the end of the Cascine, and got out there to

[038-094] admire the gay monument, with the painted bust, of the poor young Indian

[038-095] prince who died in Florence. They strolled all about, talking of the old

[038-096] times in the Cascine, twenty years before; and walking up the road

[038-097] beside the canal, while the carriage slowly followed, they stopped to

[038-098] enjoy the peasants lying asleep in the grass on the other bank. Colville

[038-099] and Effie gathered wild-flowers, and piled them in her mother's lap when

[038-100] she remounted to the carriage and drove along while they made excursions

[038-101] into the little dingles beside the road. Some people who overtook them

[038-102] in these sylvan pleasures reported the fact at a reception to which they

[038-103] were going, and [[mrs-amsden|Mrs. Amsden]], whose mind had been gradually clearing

[038-104] under the simultaneous withdrawal of Imogene and Colville from society,

[038-105] professed herself again as thickly clouded as a weather-glass before a

[038-106] storm. She appealed to the sympathy of others against this hardship.

[038-107] Mrs. Bowen took Colville home to dinner; Mr. Morton was coming, she

[038-108] said, and he must come too. At table the young clergyman made her his

[038-109] compliment on her look of health, and she said, Yes; she had been

[038-110] driving, and she believed that she needed nothing but to be in the air a

[038-111] little more, as she very well could, now the spring weather was really

[038-112] coming. She said that they had been talking all winter of going to

[038-113] [[fiesole|Fiesole]], where Imogene had never been yet; and upon comparison it

[038-114] appeared that none of them had yet been to Fiesole except herself. Then

[038-115] they must all go together, she said; the carriage would hold four very

[038-116] comfortably.

[038-117] "Ah! that leaves me out," said Colville, who had caught sight of Effie's

[038-118] fallen countenance.

[038-119] "Oh no. How is that? It leaves Effie out."

[038-120] "It's the same thing. But I might ride, and Effie might give me her hand

[038-121] to hold over the side of the carriage; that would sustain me."

[038-122] "We could take her between us, Mrs. Bowen," suggested Imogene. "The back

[038-123] seat is wide."

[038-124] "Then the party is made up," said Colville, "and Effie hasn't demeaned

[038-125] herself by asking to go where she wasn't invited."

[038-126] The child turned inquiringly toward her mother, who met her with an

[038-127] indulgent smile, which became a little flush of grateful appreciation

[038-128] when it reached Colville; but Mrs. Bowen ignored Imogene in the matter

[038-129] altogether.

[038-130] The evening passed delightfully. Mr. Morton had another book which he

[038-131] had brought to show Imogene, and Mrs. Bowen sat a long time at the

[038-132] piano, striking this air and that of the songs which she used to sing

[038-133] when she was a girl: Colville was trying to recall them. When he and

[038-134] Imogene were left alone for their adieux, they approached each other in

[038-135] an estrangement through which each tried to break.

[038-136] "Why don't you scold me?" she asked. "I have neglected you the whole

[038-137] evening."

[038-138] "How have you neglected me?"

[038-139] "How? Ah! if you don't know----"

[038-140] "No. I dare say I must be very stupid. I saw you talking with Mr.

[038-141] Morton, and you seemed interested. I thought I'd better not intrude."

[038-142] She seemed uncertain of his intention, and then satisfied of its

[038-143] simplicity.

[038-144] "Isn't it pleasant to have Mrs. Bowen in the old mood again?" he asked.

[038-145] "Is she in the old mood?"

[038-146] "Why, yes. Haven't you noticed how cordial she is?

[038-147] "I thought she was rather colder than usual."

[038-148] "Colder!" The chill of the idea penetrated even through the density of

[038-149] Colville's selfish content. A very complex emotion, which took itself

[038-150] for indignation, throbbed from his heart. "Is she cold with you,

[038-151] Imogene?"

[038-152] "Oh, if you saw nothing----"

[038-153] "No; and I think you must be mistaken. She never speaks of you without

[038-154] praising you."

[038-155] "Does she speak of me?" asked the girl, with her honest eyes wide open

[038-156] upon him.

[038-157] "Why, no," Colville acknowledged. "Come to reflect, it's I who speak of

[038-158] you. But how--how is she cold with you?"

[038-159] "Oh, I dare say it's a delusion of mine. Perhaps I'm cold with her."

[038-160] "Then don't be so, my dear! Be sure that she's your friend--true and

[038-161] good. Good night."

[038-162] He caught the girl in his arms, and kissed her tenderly. She drew away,

[038-163] and stood a moment with her repellent fingers on his breast.

[038-164] "Is it all for me?" she asked.

[038-165] "For the whole obliging and amiable world," he answered gaily.

[038-166] XIX

[038-167] The next time Colville came he found himself alone with Imogene, who

[038-168] asked him what he had been doing all day.

[038-169] "Oh, living along till evening. What have you?"

[038-170] She did not answer at once, nor praise his speech for the devotion

[038-171] implied in it. After a while she said: "Do you believe in courses of

[038-172] reading? Mr. Morton has taken up a course of reading in Italian poetry.

[038-173] He intends to master it."

[038-174] "Does he?"

[038-175] "Yes. Do you think something of the kind would be good for me?"

[038-176] "Oh, if you thirst for conquest. But I should prefer to rest on my

[038-177] laurels if I were you."

[038-178] Imogene did not smile. "Mr. Morton thinks I should enjoy a course of

[038-179] Kingsley. He says he's very earnest."

[038-180] "Oh, immensely. But aren't you earnest enough already, my dear?"

[038-181] "Do you think I'm too earnest?"

[038-182] "No; I should say you were just right."

[038-183] "You know better than that. I wish you would criticise me sometimes."

[038-184] "Oh, I'd rather not."

[038-185] "Why? Don't you see anything to criticise in me? Are you satisfied with

[038-186] me in every way? You ought to think. You ought to think now. Do you

[038-187] think that I am doing right in all respects? Am I all that I could be to

[038-188] you, and to you alone? If I am wrong in the least thing, criticise me,

[038-189] and I will try to be better."
