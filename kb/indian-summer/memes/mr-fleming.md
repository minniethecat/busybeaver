# Mr. Fleming

## Role

Mr. Fleming is a minor, entirely offstage character in William Dean Howells's *Indian Summer* (1886). Though never directly named or described in the novel's narrative, he belongs to the American expatriate community residing in Florence — a circle of compatriots who form the social backdrop against which the principal drama of Colville, Mrs. Bowen, and Imogene Graham unfolds. Mr. Fleming is the husband of Mrs. Fleming, who serves as a chaperone to Imogene Graham and who hosts one of the season's notable balls. His implied presence in the Florentine social fabric, conveyed solely through references to "Mrs. Fleming" and "Mrs. Fleming's ball," marks him as a representative figure of the married American couples populating Howells's cosmopolitan Florence (005-050).

## Character Analysis

Mr. Fleming receives no direct characterization in the novel; his existence is entirely inferred from the presence and social activities of his wife. The narrative's silence about him is itself characteristic of Howells's realist method — the novel is focalized through Theodore Colville's consciousness, and the men of the Fleming household simply fall outside Colville's field of interest. The American society described in Chapter 5 comprises "mostly Americans, but there were a good many evident English also," with "three or four Italians and there were some Germans, who spoke English" (005-050). Within this gathering, the Flemings are among the married couples who provide the stable, conventional framework of expatriate social life.

What can be inferred about Mr. Fleming from the role his wife plays is that the Fleming household holds a respectable position within Florentine society. Mrs. Fleming is trusted to act as chaperone to a young unmarried woman — Imogene Graham — at a picture exhibition (023-017), indicating that the Flemings are regarded as a proper, established couple. The fact that Mrs. Fleming hosts a ball of sufficient importance to be mentioned as a calendared event — Colville declares he will "be fresh for Mrs. Fleming's ball" (036-151) — further suggests that the Flemings possess the means and social standing to entertain on a significant scale. Mr. Fleming, as the head of this household, would be the silent enabler of this hospitality.

Howells's decision to leave Mr. Fleming unnamed and undescribed aligns with the novel's thematic focus on the interior lives of its three central characters. The minor married couples of the Anglo-American colony — the Flemings, the Inglehart family, and others — serve as the backdrop of normalcy against which the romantic complications of Colville, Mrs. Bowen, and Imogene are played out. Mr. Fleming's invisibility is in this sense functional: he represents the unremarkable, settled domesticity that the protagonists have either lost or not yet attained.

## Relationships

- **Mrs. Fleming (wife)**: Mr. Fleming is married to Mrs. Fleming, who functions as a chaperone to Imogene Graham and as a hostess within the American expatriate circle in Florence. Their household is sufficiently established to lend social credibility to the young women under Mrs. Fleming's supervision (023-017).
- **Imogene Graham**: Through his wife's chaperonage, Mr. Fleming is indirectly connected to Imogene Graham. At the picture exhibition in Chapter 23, Imogene attends under Mrs. Fleming's escort (023-017, 023-018). Imogene's distressed appeal — "Oh, I wish Mrs. Fleming——" (023-111) — during her tense exchange with Colville underscores the Fleming household's role as a social safety net for the young protagonist.
- **The Inglehart Boys / Florentine Artistic Circle**: The Flemings' social world includes the younger artistic set of Florence. At the same exhibition where Mrs. Fleming chaperones Imogene, the "Inglehart boys" arrive, described as young artists whose "fresh young faces" and "delightful shop of the studio" conversations mark them as part of the same extended social network (023-082, 023-087).
- **The American Expatriate Colony**: The Flemings are members of the broader American community in Florence that includes Mrs. Bowen, Effie Bowen, Mr. Waters, and the succession of American and English visitors who populate Mrs. Bowen's Thursday receptions. This community is introduced at the party in Chapter 5 (005-050, 005-053), where the tone of ironic self-awareness among the American residents is established through Colville's conversation with the old gentleman, Rev. Mr. Waters.

## Key Scenes

### 1. The Picture Exhibition — Mrs. Fleming as Chaperone (Chapter 23)

The most substantial window into the Fleming household comes in Chapter 23, when Imogene Graham attends a picture exhibition under Mrs. Fleming's care. When Colville approaches Imogene and asks after Mrs. Bowen, Imogene replies coldly:

> "No," answered the girl, still very lifelessly; "I came with Mrs. Fleming." She looked about the room as if not to look at him. (023-017, 023-018)

Mrs. Fleming's physical presence — visible only as "the top of her bonnet" in the next room (023-046, 023-115) — serves as a social anchor throughout the scene. When the conversation between Colville and Imogene grows painfully charged and Imogene nearly breaks down, she instinctively wishes for Mrs. Fleming's return (023-111). Colville ultimately delivers her back to the chaperone's vicinity before making a hasty exit.

### 2. Mrs. Fleming's Ball — The Household as Social Hub (Chapter 36)

By Chapter 36, Mrs. Fleming's ball has become a fixture on the Florentine social calendar. Colville, exhausted by the emotional strain of his engagement to Imogene and Mrs. Bowen's silent disapproval, looks forward to the event:

> "I need—sleep. I'm going to bed tonight at eight o'clock, and I am going to sleep twenty-four hours. Then I shall be fresh for Mrs. Fleming's ball." (036-151)

Imogene initially declares she will not attend, signaling the deepening personal crisis that overshadows even the principal social events of the season (036-152). The ball — hosted by the Fleming household and therefore implicating Mr. Fleming as co-host — represents the glittering surface of expatriate life beneath which the novel's emotional turmoil runs its course. The fact that the ball proceeds regardless of the protagonists' private anguish underscores the Flemings' role as providers of the stable social infrastructure that Howells's characters both rely upon and chafe against.
