[032-001] "And he, how forbearing, how considerate, how anxious for me, how full

[032-002] of generous warning he has been! always putting me in mind, at every

[032-003] step, of the difference in years between us; never thinking of himself,

[032-004] and shrinking so much from even seeming to control me or sway me, that I

[032-005] don't know really whether I have not made all the advances!

[032-006] "I cannot write his name yet, and you must not ask it till I can; and I

[032-007] cannot tell you anything about his looks or his life without seeming to

[032-008] degrade him, somehow, and make him a common man like others.

[032-009] "How can I make myself his companion in everything? How can I convince

[032-010] him that there is no sacrifice for me, and that he alone is [[sacrifice-and-renunciation|giving up]]?

[032-011] These are the thoughts that keep whirling through my mind. I hope I

[032-012] shall be helped, and I hope that I shall be tried, for that is the only

[032-013] way for me to be helped. I feel strong enough for anything that people

[032-014] can say. I should _welcome_ criticism and opposition from any quarter.

[032-015] But I can see that _he_ is very sensitive--it comes from his keen sense

[032-016] of the ridiculous--and if I suffer, it will be on account of this grand

[032-017] unselfish nature, and I shall be glad of that.

[032-018] "I know you will understand me, Di, and I am not afraid of your laughing

[032-019] at these ravings. But if you did I should not care. It is such a comfort

[032-020] to say these things about him, to exalt him, and get him in the true

[032-021] light at last.

[032-022] "Your faithful JOURNAL.

[032-023] "I shall tell him about you, one of the first things, and perhaps he can

[032-024] suggest some way out of your trouble, he has had so much experience of

[032-025] every kind. You will worship him, as I do, when you see him; for you

[032-026] will feel at once that he understands you, and that is such a rest.

[032-027] "J."

[032-028] Before [[imogene-graham|Imogene]] fell asleep, [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]] came to her in the dark, and

[032-029] softly closed the door that opened from the girl's room into Effie's.

[032-030] She sat down on the bed, and began to speak at once, as if she knew

[032-031] Imogene must be awake. "I thought you would come to me, Imogene; but as

[032-032] you didn't, I have come to you, for if you can go to sleep with hard

[032-033] thoughts of me to-night, I can't let you. You need me for your friend,

[032-034] and I wish to be your friend; it would be wicked in me to be anything

[032-035] else; I would give the world if your mother were here; but I tried to

[032-036] make my letter to her everything that it should be. If you don't think

[032-037] it is, I will write it over in the morning."

[032-038] "No," said the girl coldly; "it will do very well. I don't wish to

[032-039] trouble you so much."

[032-040] "Oh, how can you speak so to me? Do you think that I blame Mr. [[theodore-colville|Colville]]?

[032-041] Is that it? I don't ask you--I shall never ask you--how he came to

[032-042] remain, but I know that he has acted truthfully and delicately. I knew

[032-043] him long before you did, and no one need take his part with me." This

[032-044] was not perhaps what Mrs. Bowen meant to say when she began. "I have

[032-045] told you all along what I thought, but if you imagine that I am not

[032-046] satisfied with Mr. Colville, you are very much mistaken. I can't burst

[032-047] out into praises of him to your mother: that would be very patronising

[032-048] and very bad taste. Can't you see that it would?"

[032-049] "Oh yes."

[032-050] Mrs. Bowen lingered, as if she expected Imogene to say something more,

[032-051] but she did not, and Mrs. Bowen rose. "Then I hope we understand each

[032-052] other," she said, and went out of the room.

[032-053] XVI

[032-054] When Colville came in the morning, Mrs. Bowen received him. They shook

[032-055] hands, and their eyes met in the intercepting glance of the night

[032-056] before.

[032-057] "Imogene will be here in a moment," she said, with a naturalness that

[032-058] made him awkward and conscious.

[032-059] "Oh, there is no haste," he answered uncouthly. "That is, I am very glad

[032-060] of the chance to speak a moment with you, and to ask your--to profit by

[032-061] what you think best. I know you are not very well pleased with me, and I

[032-062] don't know that I can ever put myself in a better light with you--the

[032-063] true light. It seems that there are some things we must not do even for

[032-064] the truth's sake. But that's neither here nor there. What I am most

[032-065] anxious for is not to take a shadow of advantage of this child's--of

[032-066] Imogene's inexperience, and her remoteness from her family. I feel that

[032-067] I must in some sort protect her from herself. Yes--that is my idea. But

[032-068] I have to do this in so many ways that I hardly know how to begin. I

[032-069] should be very willing, if you thought best, to go away and stay away

[032-070] till she has heard from her people, and let her have that time to think

[032-071] it all over again. She is very young--so much younger than I! Or, if you

[032-072] thought it better, I would stay, and let her remain free while I held

[032-073] myself bound to any decision of hers. I am anxious to do what is right.

[032-074] At the same time"--he smiled ruefully--"there is such a thing as being

[032-075] so _dis_interested that one may seem _un_interested. I may leave her so

[032-076] very free that she may begin to suspect that I want a little freedom

[032-077] myself. What shall I do? I wish to act with your approval."

[032-078] Mrs. Bowen had listened with acquiescence and intelligence that might

[032-079] well have looked like sympathy, as she sat fingering the top of her

[032-080] hand-screen, with her eyelids fallen. She lifted them to say, "I have

[032-081] told you that I will not advise you in any way. I cannot. I have no

[032-082] longer any wish in this matter. I must still remain in the place of

[032-083] Imogene's mother; but I will do only what you wish. Please understand

[032-084] that, and don't ask me for advice any more. It is painful." She drew her

[032-085] lower lip in a little, and let the screen fall into her lap.

[032-086] "I'm sorry, Mrs. Bowen, to do anything--say anything--that is painful to

[032-087] you," Colville began. "You know that I would give the world to please

[032-088] you----" The words escaped him and left him staring at her.

[032-089] "What are you saying to me, Theodore Colville?" she exclaimed, flashing

[032-090] a full-eyed glance upon him, and then breaking into a laugh, as

[032-091] unnatural for her. "Really, I don't believe you know!"

[032-092] "Heaven knows I meant nothing but what I said," he answered, struggling

[032-093] stupidly with a confusion of desires which every man but no woman will

[032-094] understand. After eighteen hundred years, the man is still imperfectly

[032-095] monogamous. "Is there anything wrong in it?"

[032-096] "Oh no! Not for you," she said scornfully.

[032-097] "I am very much in earnest," he went on hopelessly, "in asking your

[032-098] opinion, your help, in regard to how I shall treat this affair."

[032-099] "And I am still more in earnest in telling you that I will give you no

[032-100] opinion, no help. I forbid you to recur to the subject." He was silent,

[032-101] unable to drop his eyes from hers. "But for her," continued Mrs. Bowen,

[032-102] "I will do anything in my power. If she asks my advice I will give it,

[032-103] and I will give her all the help I can."

[032-104] "Thank you," said Colville vaguely.

[032-105] "I will not have your thanks," promptly retorted Mrs. Bowen, "for I mean

[032-106] you no kindness. I am trying to do my [[social-duty|duty]] to Imogene, and when that is

[032-107] ended, all is ended. There is no way now for you to please me--as you

[032-108] call it--except to keep her from regretting what she has done."

[032-109] "Do you think I shall fail in that?" he demanded indignantly.

[032-110] "I can offer you no opinion. I can't tell what you will do."

[032-111] "There are two ways of keeping her from regretting what she has done;

[032-112] and perhaps the simplest and best way would be to free her from the

[032-113] consequences, as far as they're involved in me," said Colville.

[032-114] Mrs. Bowen dropped herself back in her armchair. "If you choose to force

[032-115] these things upon me, I am a woman, and can't help myself. Especially, I

[032-116] can't help myself against a guest."

[032-117] "Oh, I will relieve you of my presence," said Colville. "I've no wish to

[032-118] force anything upon you--least of all myself." He rose, and moved toward

[032-119] the door.

[032-120] She hastily intercepted him. "Do you think I will let you go without

[032-121] seeing Imogene? Do you understand me so little as that? It's _too late_

[032-122] for you to go! You know what I think of all this, and I know, better

[032-123] than you, what you think. I shall play my part, and you shall play

[032-124] yours. I have refused to give you advice or help, and I never shall do

[032-125] it. But I know what my duty to her is, and I will fulfil it. No matter

[032-126] how distasteful it is to either of us, you must come here as before. The

[032-127] house is as free to you as ever--freer. And we are to be as good friends

[032-128] as ever--better. You can see Imogene alone or in my presence, and, as

[032-129] far as I am concerned, you shall consider yourself engaged or not, as

[032-130] you choose. Do you understand?"

[032-131] "Not in the least," said Colville, in the ghost of his old bantering

[032-132] manner. "But don't explain, or I shall make still less of it."

[032-133] "I mean simply that I do it for Imogene and not for you."

[032-134] "Oh, I understand that you don't do it for me."

[032-135] At this moment Imogene appeared between the folds of the _portière_, and

[032-136] her timid, embarrassed glance from Mrs. Bowen to Colville was the first

[032-137] gleam of consolation that had visited him since he parted with her the

[032-138] night before. A thrill of inexplicable pride and fondness passed through

[032-139] his heart, and even the compunction that followed could not spoil its

[032-140] sweetness. But if Mrs. Bowen discreetly turned her head aside that she

[032-141] need not witness a tender greeting between them, the precaution was

[032-142] unnecessary. He merely went forward and took the girl's hand, with a

[032-143] sigh of relief. "Good morning, Imogene," he said, with a kind of

[032-144] compassionate admiration.

[032-145] "Good morning," she returned half-inquiringly.

[032-146] She did not take a seat near him, and turned, as if for instruction, to

[032-147] Mrs. Bowen. It was probably the force of habit. In any case, Mrs.

[032-148] Bowen's eyes gave no response. She bowed slightly to Colville, and

[032-149] began, "I must leave Imogene to entertain you for the present, Mr.--"

[032-150] "No!" cried the girl impetuously; "don't go." Mrs. Bowen stopped. "I

[032-151] wish to speak with you--with you and Mr. Colville together. I wish to

[032-152] say--I don't know how to say it exactly; but I wish to know--You asked

[032-153] him last night, Mrs. Bowen, whether he wished to consider it an

[032-154] engagement?"

[032-155] "I thought perhaps you would rather hear from your mother--"

[032-156] "Yes, I would be glad to know that my mother approved; but if she

[032-157] didn't, I couldn't help it. Mr. Colville said he was bound, but I was

[032-158] not. That can't be. I _wish_ to be bound, if he is."

[032-159] "I don't quite know what you expect me to say."

[032-160] "Nothing," said Imogene. "I merely wished you to know. And I don't wish

[032-161] you to sacrifice anything to us. If you think best, Mr. Colville will

[032-162] not see me till I hear from home; though it won't make any difference

[032-163] with me _what_ I hear."

[032-164] "There's no reason why you shouldn't meet," said Mrs. Bowen absently.

[032-165] "If you wish it to have the same appearance as an Italian

[032-166] engagement----"
