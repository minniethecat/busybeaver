# Girolamo Savonarola

> Girolamo Savonarola (1452–1498) is the historical Florentine friar and reformer whose fiery puritanism — the Bonfire of the Vanities, the denunciation of Medici corruption, the theocratic republic — ended in excommunication and execution in the Piazza della Signoria. He never walks onto the stage of *Indian Summer*; he is dead four centuries before the novel's action begins. Yet he is one of the most vivid presences in the book — not as a character, but as the intellectual and spiritual obsession of the Rev. Mr. Waters, and as a symbolic touchstone by which the novel measures the claims of moral severity against the claims of life, pleasure, and human fallibility.

## Role

Savonarola functions in *Indian Summer* as an **absent center** — a historical figure whose legacy organizes the interior life of the novel's most morally serious character and, through him, supplies a running counterpoint to the romantic comedy unfolding around Theodore Colville. Mr. Waters has devoted his Florentine retirement to the study of the friar; he lives in a modest lodging in Piazza San Marco whose windows look directly upon "the church and the convent sacred to the old Socinian for the sake of the meek, heroic mystic whom they keep alive in all the glory of his martyrdom" (015-045 to 015-047). Savonarola thus stands as a fixed moral pole — severity, renunciation, the refusal to compromise — against which the novel's more worldly characters (Colville above all) are implicitly measured. When Imogene Graham urges Colville to engage Waters on his pet subject, she frames the friar as the key to the old clergyman's appeal: "You ought to hear him talk about Savonarola, Mr. Colville" (006-128). The remark captures Savonarola's narrative function: he is the conversation that reveals character.

## Character Analysis

### The Historical Friar and the Novel's Use of Him

Howells introduces Savonarola not through direct exposition but through the lens of Mr. Waters's scholarship — specifically, through Pasquale Villari's two-volume *Life and Times of Girolamo Savonarola*, which Waters and Colville discuss at Vieusseux's reading-room. Colville calls Villari's biography "a thing that makes you feel that, after all, the Italians have only to make a real effort in any direction, and they go ahead of everybody else" (011-050 to 011-052). The book thus serves a double function: it establishes the historical friar's gravity while also signaling the intellectual seriousness of the characters who read him. Mr. Waters's absorption begins the moment he holds the newly arrived German edition: "He gave Colville the scholar's far-off look as he turned to go: he was already as remote as the fifteenth century through the magic of the book, which he opened" (011-101 to 011-103). Savonarola is the vehicle of this transport — a figure so compelling that a book about him can dissolve the present.

### Puritanism as a Shared Temperament

The intellectual affinity Waters feels for Savonarola is not doctrinal but temperamental. He freely concedes to Colville that Villari has proved Savonarola was no Protestant (011-073): the affinity is a matter of shared severity, a "puritanism" that Waters recognizes in his own New England ancestry. "I find my ancestral puritanism particularly appealed to by the puritanism of Savonarola" (011-072). The phrasing is precise and self-aware — Waters does not say he *agrees* with Savonarola, only that he is *appealed to* by a kindred impulse toward moral rigor. This distinction is essential to the novel's handling of the friar: Savonarola is not a model to be emulated but a case to be understood. Waters is a student of failure, drawn to Savonarola precisely because the reformer's project collapsed.

### The Inquiry Into Excessive Virtue

What Waters seeks in Savonarola is not vindication but diagnosis. His central question is one of the most arresting formulations in the novel: "It is very interesting to inquire just at what point a man's virtues become mischievous and intolerable" (011-087 to 011-088). The question is personal as well as historical. Waters himself walked away from his New England pulpit when Unitarianism "thinning out into paler doubt was no longer tenable with him" (015-082 to 015-083) — a quiet, private renunciation that avoided the public catastrophe Savonarola suffered. By studying the friar's catastrophe, Waters is implicitly asking whether his own quieter course was wisdom or timidity. "Savonarola perished because he was excessive" (011-085), Waters concludes — and the word "excessive" carries the full weight of the novel's moral calculus. The line between virtue and excess, between the reformer who purifies and the fanatic who destroys, is what Waters has crossed an ocean and surrendered a career to understand.

### The Paradox of Kinship Across Difference

Colville, watching Waters at his books in Piazza San Marco, registers the central paradox of this relationship in a sentence that is among the finest in Howells's fiction: "No two minds could well have been further apart than the New England minister and the Florentine monk, and no two souls nearer together, as Colville recognised with a not irreverent smile" (015-047 to 015-049). The phrase is a marvel of compression. The mild Unitarian pastor, content to buy radishes and butter from the green-grocer and take Italian lessons from his landlady's daughter, shares with the fiery Dominican executed in the Piazza della Signoria a common severity of purpose, a shared incapacity for the compromises by which ordinary life is sustained. Colville's "not irreverent smile" acknowledges the absurdity of the pairing without dismissing its truth. Savonarola, in this rendering, is not a historical relic but a living possibility — the possibility, always present and always dangerous, of taking goodness too far.

## Relationships

- **Mr. Waters** — Savonarola is Waters's lifelong intellectual companion. The friar is the organizing principle of Waters's Florentine retirement: every book borrowed at Vieusseux's, every morning's study, every speculative question about the point at which virtue turns excessive circles back to Savonarola. Waters describes him with a scholar's precision and a believer's tenderness as "the meek, heroic mystic" (015-046). The relationship is not one of simple admiration — Waters is too honest for hagiography — but of sustained and sympathetic inquiry into the most consequential failure Waters can find in history: "it's one of the things in history that I've found it hardest to accept" (011-075 to 011-076). That a man of Waters's gentleness should devote his remaining years to understanding a man of Savonarola's fire is one of the quiet wonders of the novel.

- **Theodore Colville** — Colville, whose instinct is always toward irony and indirection, is drawn to Waters's seriousness about Savonarola even as he gently teases it. His jokes — "You're not thinking of taking Savonarola's life, I suppose?" (011-047) — and his "malicious" questions about whether Savonarola would approve of the Carnival (015-106 to 015-109) are the deflections of a man who senses that Waters's moral clarity, mediated through the figure of the friar, exposes the evasions in his own romantic situation. When Waters quietly observes that the Carnival's later hours grew "a little unwholesome" and concedes that Savonarola may have had a point after all (021-138 to 021-143), Colville is confronted with a judgment he cannot laugh away.

- **Imogene Graham** — Imogene knows Savonarola only as the subject of Waters's conversation, but her instinctive response to the old clergyman — "He always talks to you as if he expected you to be interested in serious matters" (006-124 to 006-126) — suggests that the friar's seriousness, transposed through Waters's gentle manner, exerts a gravitational pull even on characters who have no intellectual interest in him. She tells Colville she is "rather glad" Waters talked to her about American girls instead of Savonarola (006-130), a preference that marks the limits of her engagement with the novel's deeper themes.

- **Florence / The Carnival** — Savonarola's historical enemy was the worldliness of Renaissance Florence, and the Carnival — with its masks, its mock-nuns, its "boisterousness" — is the descendant of the pagan festivity the friar tried to extinguish with his Bonfire of the Vanities. Waters's decision to observe the Carnival "as part of my study of Savonarola" (015-115) is a characteristically gentle and open-minded gesture: he will let the evidence speak. The question Colville poses from Waters's window — whether Savonarola, looking down on the "wicked old Carnival, that he tried so hard to kill four hundred years ago, still alive" (015-108 to 015-109), would think things had come out right — hangs over the entire novel. Waters's initial answer, that Savonarola by now "knows that his puritanism was all a mistake, unless as a thing for the moment only" (015-110 to 015-111), is the novel's most forthright statement of the case against moral absolutism.

## Key Scenes

### The Vieusseux's Reading-Room: Savonarola as Intellectual Bond (011-043 to 011-088)

The first extended treatment of Savonarola occurs at the Gabinetto Vieusseux, where Mr. Waters asks the librarian about "that book on Savonarola yet. The German one" (011-044). The exchange that follows between Waters and Colville establishes the friar as both a genuine intellectual subject and a vehicle for male friendship. Their shared admiration of Villari's biography — "It's a wonderful book. You've read it?" — creates common ground between the younger ironist and the elderly scholar. But the conversation deepens rapidly: Waters confesses he is "interested in justifying his failure to myself" (011-075 to 011-076), and the theological question of whether puritanism "fell because it was dreary and ugly, as the puritanic state always has" (011-077) opens onto the larger inquiry into excessive virtue. Colville, who has been floundering in social trivialities, finds "support and encouragement" in Waters's earnestness (011-091 to 011-093), and Savonarola becomes, improbably, the figure around which Colville begins to reorient himself toward seriousness.

> "It is very interesting to inquire just at what point a man's virtues become mischievous and intolerable." (011-087 to 011-088)

### Piazza San Marco: The Paradox of Kinship (015-039 to 015-049)

Chapter 15 opens with the most sustained portrait of Waters's daily life and the most penetrating insight into his relationship with Savonarola. The description of Waters's bare lodging — its poverty, its view of the church and convent of San Marco, the green-grocer who reveres him — culminates in the novel's single finest sentence about the friar's role: "No two minds could well have been further apart than the New England minister and the Florentine monk, and no two souls nearer together, as Colville recognised with a not irreverent smile" (015-047 to 015-049). The sentence captures everything that is strange and moving about Waters's devotion: the intellectual distance between the two men is enormous, yet at the level of moral temperament — what the novel calls "soul" — they are twins. Savonarola is Waters's secret sharer, the companion who licenses his seriousness in a world that prefers charm and indirection.

> "No two minds could well have been further apart than the New England minister and the Florentine monk, and no two souls nearer together." (015-047 to 015-049)

### The Carnival Window: Savonarola's Verdict on the Present (015-099 to 015-115)

As Waters and Colville watch the Carnival from Waters's window overlooking Piazza San Marco, Colville asks with calculated mischief whether Savonarola, looking down on the same scene, "would think it had all come out right" (015-106 to 015-109). Waters's reply is remarkable for its generosity: "Oh yes; why not? By this time he knows that his puritanism was all a mistake, unless as a thing for the moment only" (015-110 to 015-111). The phrase "unless as a thing for the moment only" is Waters's characteristic hedge — a refusal to dismiss Savonarola's severity entirely, a concession that some moments may require the reformer's fire. Waters then declares his intention to observe the Carnival further "as part of my study of Savonarola" (015-115), a decision that unites scholarly curiosity with an almost comic willingness to subject the friar's principles to empirical test.

### The Veglione and the Return of Doubt (018-083 to 018-087, 021-138 to 021-146)

At the masked ball, Colville gravely asks Waters whether he has "been able to divine from what you have seen here the grounds of Savonarola's objection to the Carnival" (018-083). Waters's prompt reply — "Not at all. I have seen nothing but the most harmless gaiety throughout the evening" (018-085 to 018-086) — seems to confirm the case against Savonarola's severity. Colville, remembering a passage from Swedenborg about celestial angels who "had scarcely any power of perceiving evil" (018-088), is struck by the old clergyman's innocence. But the novel does not leave the matter there. By Section 21, after witnessing the Carnival's later hours, Waters admits a qualification: "There seemed to be rather more boisterousness toward the close, and, if I might judge, the excitement grew a little unwholesome" (021-140 to 021-142). When Colville asks directly, "And do you still feel that Savonarola was mistaken?" (021-138), Waters retreats into scholarly modesty — "I really don't feel myself very well qualified to decide" (021-142 to 021-143) — but the admission registers. Savonarola's severity, the novel quietly suggests, may not have been entirely misplaced after all.

## Critical Reception

Although Savonarola is not himself a character in *Indian Summer*, his presence in the novel has attracted critical attention as a key to the book's moral architecture. The friar serves as the novel's most potent symbol of the puritan temperament — the impulse to judge, to reform, to refuse compromise — that Howells treats with characteristic ambivalence. Waters's inquiry into "just at what point a man's virtues become mischievous and intolerable" has been read as a statement of the novel's deepest theme: the ethical problem of excess, explored through comedy rather than tragedy. William Dean Howells, himself the grandson of a Welsh Methodist and the son of a Swedenborgian, understood the claims of severity from the inside, and Savonarola — filtered through the mild, scholarly, endlessly sympathetic Mr. Waters — becomes the vehicle for a meditation on moral absolutism that is at once sympathetic and gently skeptical, a verdict that refuses to become a verdict.

The Villari biography that Waters and Colville discuss — Pasquale Villari's *La Storia di Girolamo Savonarola e de' suoi tempi* (1859–61) — was indeed, as Colville asserts, one of the most celebrated works of nineteenth-century Italian historiography, translated into English in 1888, just a few years before *Indian Summer* was published. Howells's decision to make this specific book the shared enthusiasm of his two most intellectually serious male characters grounds the novel's Florentine setting in real intellectual history and signals the cosmopolitan seriousness of the American expatriate community he depicts.
