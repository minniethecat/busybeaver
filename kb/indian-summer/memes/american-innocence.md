# American Innocence

## Definition

American innocence in *Indian Summer* names a complex thematic field where the novel engages a longstanding transatlantic cultural opposition: the notion that Americans arrive in Europe morally and socially simpler than Europeans, unburdened by Old World cynicism, convention, and moral compromise. This trope — central to nineteenth-century American self-understanding from Cooper through James — appears in Howells's novel not as a settled ideological commitment but as a site of sustained examination. The novel simultaneously deploys the language of American innocence and exposes its internal contradictions: Americans who claim simplicity prove capable of sophisticated self-deception; Europeanised characters who appear worldly demonstrate the deepest moral integrity; and the "innocent" American heart, far from being a shield against moral error, becomes an agent of harm to others.

Howells's treatment of the trope is characteristically dialectical. He never simply debunks American innocence — the novel values sincerity, spontaneity, and the "unsuspecting, unsophisticated freedom" (005-094) Colville defends — but he refuses to let innocence function as an alibi. The innocent may do as much damage as the worldly, and the novel's moral resolution depends not on preserving innocence but on accepting its loss.

## Context in the Novel

The opposition between American simplicity and European worldliness is established in the novel's opening section, where Colville stands on the Ponte Vecchio and deliberately ignores "the surrounding frippery of landscape and architecture," imagining himself instead on "the Main Street Bridge at Des Vaches, Indiana, looking down at the tawny sweep of the Wabash" (001-021 to 001-022). The gesture is performative: Colville asserts his American indifference to European aesthetic seduction even as the very act of standing on a Florentine bridge belies it. From the outset, American innocence is something Colville performs rather than simply possesses — and the performance is directed as much at himself as at others.

The novel's debate about American girlhood crystallises the trope most explicitly. Colville champions "the old national ideal of girlish liberty as wide as the continent, as fast as the Mississippi," accusing Mrs. Bowen of having become "Europeanised" when she prefers "the European plan of bringing up girls" (006-149 to 006-153). The spatial metaphor — liberty mapped onto continental geography — frames American innocence as a frontier condition, a freedom from European social constraint. Yet the debate is immediately complicated by the novel's larger architecture: the "Europeanised" Mrs. Bowen will prove to be the novel's most morally reliable character, while the "innocent" Imogene Graham, with her "innocent, nervous laugh" (005-129), will become entangled in a romantic debacle that wounds everyone involved.

The American expatriate artists of Section 15 provide the novel's most sympathetic portrait of the innocence trope. Colville observes them as "mostly very happy men, leading simple and innocent lives in a world of the ideal, and rich in the inexhaustible beauty of the city, the sky, the air" (015-028). Crucially, Howells adds that "they all, whether they were ever going back or not, were fervent Americans, and their ineffaceable nationality marked them, perhaps, all the more strongly for the patches of something alien that overlaid it in places" (015-031). American innocence here is not a pure state but a layered one — nationality persists, ineffaceably, but it is marked by the "patches of something alien" that European residence has stitched onto it. The passage refuses both the fantasy of untouched American purity and the fear of European corruption; identity is additive, not substitutive.

The novel's most radical complication of the innocence trope comes through the Colville-Imogene plot. When Colville dances with Imogene at the veglione, he feels "those innocent, trustful throbs upbraid him" (018-167) — her physical response to his presence is innocent, but his awareness of the power it grants him is not. Colville's self-consciousness about his own position — a middle-aged man receiving the adoration of a young woman — transforms innocence from a moral quality into a moral problem. He is not innocent, and he knows it; her innocence becomes the very thing that makes his behaviour culpable. The waltz scene thus inverts the expected valence: innocence is vulnerability, not virtue, and the worldly man who recognises this is more ethically awake than the innocent girl who does not.

Mrs. Bowen's confrontation with Imogene in Section 21 further complicates the opposition. When the two women face each other in silence, the narrator observes that "the years teach us something of self-control, perhaps, but they weaken and unstring the nerves. In this opposition of silence to silence, the woman of the world was no match for the inexperienced girl" (021-017 to 021-019). The worldliness that should confer strategic advantage proves disabling; experience produces self-control but also nervous exhaustion. The "inexperienced girl" wins the silence contest not through moral superiority but through sheer youth's imperviousness — an innocence that is less a virtue than an unearned physiological advantage. Mrs. Bowen's subsequent warning — that "the very good in him makes it the worse" (021-028) — encapsulates the novel's dialectical method: Colville's virtues (his conversational charm, his genuine interest in Imogene) are precisely what make him dangerous to her.

The novel's theological register, voiced primarily through Mr. Waters, provides the final complication. When Colville confesses to having "wrung a young and innocent heart with the anguish of self-doubt" (045-041), Mr. Waters refuses the consolations of absolution, replying with the novel's most compressed statement of its moral vision: "The young suffer terribly. But they recover. Afterward we don't suffer so much, but we don't recover" (045-044 to 045-045). The aphorism separates suffering from recovery — the innocent suffer more intensely but bounce back; the worldly suffer mutedly but sustain permanent damage. American innocence, in this final framing, is not something to be protected (Colville's project) or recovered (Imogene's project) but something that necessarily passes, leaving behind not purity but the compromised, bearable wisdom that is the novel's version of maturity.

## Key Passages

> They entered into a discussion which is always fruitful with Americans — the discussion of American girlhood, and Colville contended for the old national ideal of girlish liberty as wide as the continent, as fast as the Mississippi. Mrs. Bowen withstood him with delicate firmness. "Oh," he said, "you're Europeanised." "I certainly prefer the European plan of bringing up girls," she replied steadfastly. "I shouldn't think of letting a daughter of mine have the freedom I had." (006-149 to 006-153)

This exchange is the novel's most concentrated dramatisation of the American-innocence-versus-European-worldliness opposition. Colville's metaphor — liberty measured in continental geography and river-speed — makes the American ideal spatial and kinetic, a freedom defined by boundlessness and movement. Mrs. Bowen's reply is personal and generational: she "shouldn't think of letting a daughter of mine have the freedom I had," grounding her preference in lived experience rather than abstract principle. The irony that will govern the novel's treatment of this opposition is already latent: Colville, who champions the old American freedom, will prove morally evasive; Mrs. Bowen, who has embraced European restraint, will prove morally courageous.

> Colville thought them mostly very happy men, leading simple and innocent lives in a world of the ideal, and rich in the inexhaustible beauty of the city, the sky, the air. They all, whether they were ever going back or not, were fervent Americans, and their ineffaceable nationality marked them, perhaps, all the more strongly for the patches of something alien that overlaid it in places. (015-028 to 015-031)

The American artists represent innocence at its most attractive: unworldly, devoted to beauty, sustained by an ideal realm. Yet Howells's language complicates even this sympathetic portrait. Their nationality is "ineffaceable" — indelible, impossible to erase — but it is overlaid with "patches of something alien." The metaphor is sartorial: identity as fabric, mended and patched with foreign material, the American cloth still visible beneath. Innocence here is not isolation from the world but a particular way of inhabiting it — fervently national while irreversibly cosmopolitan.

> The long, pulsating stream of the music broke and fell. The dancers crookedly dispersed in wandering lines. She took his arm; he felt her heart leap against it; those innocent, trustful throbs upbraided him. At the same time his own heart beat with a sort of fond, protecting tenderness; he felt the witchery of his power to make this young, radiant, and beautiful creature hang flattered and bewildered on his talk. (018-167 to 018-169)

This passage performs the novel's most precise dissection of how innocence functions as a moral hazard. Imogene's "innocent, trustful throbs" are genuine — her body registers an uncalculated response — but the narrative voice immediately pivots to Colville's consciousness, where the throbs "upbraid him." The verb is judicial: her innocence indicts him. Yet even as he feels the reproach, he also feels "the witchery of his power" — the spell he casts, the intoxicating effect of his own charm on a receptive audience. Innocence enables the exercise of a power that the worldly man knows he should not wield and cannot resist wielding. The passage makes innocence structurally dangerous: it is not that Colville corrupts Imogene, but that her innocence creates the conditions in which his worst qualities — vanity, self-indulgence, the need to be admired — can operate without resistance.

> "And to have wrung a young and innocent heart with the anguish of self-doubt, with the fear of wrong to another, with the shame of an error such as I allowed, perhaps encouraged her to make —" "Yes," said the old man. "The young suffer terribly. But they recover. Afterward we don't suffer so much, but we don't recover." (045-041 to 045-045)

Colville's confession and Mr. Waters's response mark the terminal point of the novel's engagement with the innocence trope. Colville frames his guilt in terms of what he has done to innocence — "wrung a young and innocent heart" — and the verb implies a violent extraction: innocence is not merely damaged but tortured out of existence. Mr. Waters's reply reorganises the moral calculus entirely. He does not deny the suffering Colville has caused; he recontextualises it within a developmental scheme where the young recover precisely because they are young, while the middle-aged bear wounds that never fully heal. Innocence, in this final formulation, is not a moral category but a temporal one — the condition of being young enough to heal. To be innocent is simply to possess the biological capacity for recovery; to lose innocence is to exchange the intensity of suffering for its permanence.

## Related Concepts

- **Europeanisation** — The novel's counter-trope to American innocence: the process by which American characters adopt European manners and values. The novel refuses to treat Europeanisation as simple corruption, instead exploring it as a form of moral education that may produce the very integrity American innocence lacks.
- **American Girlhood** — The specific site where the innocence trope is most intensely contested: Colville's nostalgic ideal of frontier liberty versus Mrs. Bowen's European-influenced preference for restraint. Imogene Graham's trajectory through the novel enacts the failure of both models.
- **Romantic Disillusionment** — American innocence in the novel is structurally linked to romantic disappointment. Colville's youthful broken heart is the wound that produces his ironic self-consciousness; his middle-aged entanglement with Imogene is an attempt to recover innocence that only produces more disillusionment. The arc from innocence to disillusionment to mature acceptance is the novel's emotional plot.
- **Youth and Age** — The innocence-worldliness opposition is mapped onto the age-youth axis throughout the novel. Mr. Waters's aphorism — the young recover, the middle-aged do not — makes innocence a function of biological time rather than moral quality, complicating any simple reading of innocence as virtue.
- **Expatriate Identity** — American innocence is always inflected by the expatriate condition. The American in Europe performs innocence as a national identity, but European residence inevitably produces the "patches of something alien" that complicate the performance. Innocence abroad is never quite the same as innocence at home.

## Related Characters

- **Theodore-Colville** — The novel's central figure of compromised innocence: an American who performs indifference to Europe while being deeply shaped by it, whose self-consciousness about his own worldliness makes him neither innocent nor straightforwardly corrupt.
- **Imogene-Graham** — The novel's figure of American innocence as vulnerability: young, sincere, and dangerously unaware of the dynamics her sincerity sets in motion. Her "innocent, trustful throbs" (018-167) make her both sympathetic and, structurally, the agent of the novel's moral crisis.
- **Lina-Bowen** — The "Europeanised" woman who proves to be the novel's moral anchor, demonstrating that the loss of American innocence — the embrace of European restraint, self-control, and social duty — may produce the deepest ethical competence.
- **Mr-Waters** — The elderly clergyman who provides the novel's theological and philosophical commentary on innocence, articulating the temporal framework (youth recovers, age does not) that reconfigures innocence as a biological condition rather than a moral achievement.
