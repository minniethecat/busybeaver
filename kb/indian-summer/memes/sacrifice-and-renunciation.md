# Sacrifice and Renunciation

## Definition

Sacrifice and renunciation in *Indian Summer* form a linked motif that runs through the novel's central romantic relationships, functioning simultaneously as an idiom of love, a mode of self-dramatisation, and a source of moral confusion. Characters in the novel repeatedly invoke sacrifice — offering it, refusing it, performing it, and mistaking it for something else — yet the novel's distinctive contribution is its sceptical anatomy of what sacrifice actually means when examined closely. Imogene Graham insists that giving herself to Colville is not sacrifice but "self-glorification" (034-001 to 034-002); Colville recoils from her "hideous" offer of sacrificial devotion (033-174 to 033-176); Mrs. Bowen performs the novel's only genuine renunciation, and she does so without ever naming it as such. The motif thus traces an arc from theatrical self-display through erotic bargaining to its most authentic form — a quiet, unremarked, and costly surrender of personal happiness for the sake of others. Howells refuses to let sacrifice settle into a stable moral category: it is always entangled with vanity, self-deception, and the desire to be seen as virtuous, yet the novel also insists that genuine renunciation exists, visible only in its effects and never in its self-description.

## Context in the Novel

The language of sacrifice enters the novel most insistently through Imogene Graham. In the long letter she writes to her friend Di (Chapter 32), she frames her love for the older Colville in explicitly sacrificial terms even as she denies that any sacrifice is involved: "How can I convince him that there is no sacrifice for me, and that he alone is giving up?" (032-009 to 032-010). The paradox is revealing. Imogene understands her relationship as a transaction of sacrifices — she must persuade Colville that she gives up nothing, while he gives up everything, and this asymmetry is precisely what makes the match acceptable to her. Her generosity consists in refusing to acknowledge her own generosity. The letter's rhetoric enacts the pattern that will define the novel's treatment of sacrifice: an assertion of selflessness that cannot help drawing attention to itself.

This pattern intensifies when Imogene declares her love to Colville directly (Chapter 33). She offers to "give back" the youth he lost — "I want you to feel that I am your youth — the youth you were robbed of — given back to you" (033-165 to 033-166) — framing her entire existence as a restorative sacrifice. Colville's response is the novel's most violent rejection of the sacrificial logic: "And do you think I could be such a vampire as to let you? Yes, yes; I have had my dreams of such a thing; but I see now how hideous they were. You shall make no such sacrifice to me" (033-174 to 033-176). His recoil transforms Imogene's beautiful offer into something monstrous — vampiric — and his refusal of her sacrifice is itself framed as a moral obligation, a duty to protect her from her own self-abnegation. The passage establishes the novel's core insight: that the refusal of sacrifice can be as consequential, and as morally fraught, as sacrifice itself.

Imogene's own understanding of sacrifice shifts as the novel progresses. In a moment of striking self-awareness, she revises her offer entirely: "Do you think it is self-sacrifice for me to give myself to you? It's self-glorification!" (034-001 to 034-002). This is the novel's most radical unmasking of the sacrificial rhetoric. Imogene recognises that what she had framed as self-denial is in fact self-fulfilment — that giving herself to the man she loves, far from costing her anything, would be the consummation of her deepest wish. The insight is bracingly honest, and it destabilises the entire vocabulary of sacrifice on which the novel's romantic discourse has been built. If sacrifice can be self-glorification, then the entire moral calculus of giving and giving up must be re-examined.

The novel extends its anatomy of sacrifice beyond Imogene. Colville himself, reflecting on his entanglement, acknowledges to the minister Mr. Waters that Imogene "loves me through her mistaken pity, and because she thinks she can sacrifice herself to me. But she can't. Everything is against that; she doesn't know how, and there is no reason why" (037-084 to 037-087). The diagnosis is precise: Imogene's sacrificial impulse is grounded in "mistaken pity" — an emotion that misreads its object — and in any case the sacrifice she imagines is structurally impossible. Colville's clarity here is also a form of renunciation: he gives up the consolation of believing himself worth sacrificing for. Later, when Imogene imagines breaking off the engagement as a theatrical act of self-sacrifice — "thrilling with the notion of her self-sacrifice" (039-183) — the novel exposes how easily renunciation can become performance, a private drama in which the self is simultaneously actor and audience. Imogene's fantasy of public humiliation followed by vindication ("If people ever find it out, I will tell them that it was he who broke it off," 039-181 to 039-182) reveals sacrifice as revenge by other means: the power to reject, dressed in the costume of submission.

The novel's most authentic renunciation belongs to Mrs. Lina Bowen, and it is characteristic of Howells's method that this renunciation is almost invisible. Mrs. Bowen never speaks the language of sacrifice; she does not offer to give anything up. Instead, she simply does it — suppressing her feelings for Colville, performing her duty to Imogene, maintaining the social forms that protect the younger woman even as they immolate her own happiness. When she finally confesses her failure — "I ought to have broken it off; I ought to have been frank with her; it was my duty; but I couldn't without feeling that I was acting for myself too, and I would not submit to that degradation" (046-146) — the language is not of sacrifice but of duty and degradation. What Mrs. Bowen renounces is not merely Colville but the consolations of self-justification: she refuses to act on her duty because she cannot purify her motives, and this refusal — to claim virtue for an act tainted by self-interest — is the novel's most demanding form of renunciation. It costs her everything and leaves her nothing to point to, no language in which to name what she has done.

## Key Passages

> "How can I make myself his companion in everything? How can I convince him that there is no sacrifice for me, and that he alone is giving up? These are the thoughts that keep whirling through my mind." (032-009 to 032-011)

Imogene's letter to Di establishes the novel's sacrificial logic in its most concentrated form. The question — "How can I convince him that there is no sacrifice for me?" — is rhetorically ingenious because it advertises the sacrifice it denies. Imogene wants Colville to recognise her generosity, but her generosity consists in insisting she is not being generous. The phrase "he alone is giving up" positions Colville as the sole sacrificial victim, and Imogene's self-appointed task is to persuade him to accept this asymmetrical arrangement. The passage exposes the paradox at the heart of romantic sacrifice: it can never be entirely selfless, because the act of offering it asserts the offerer's moral superiority over the recipient. Imogene's "whirling" thoughts convey the genuine agitation beneath the rhetorical performance — she is not merely posing; she is genuinely torn between her desire to give and her need to be recognised as giving.

> Colville wrenched himself loose from the hold that had been growing more tenderly close and clinging. "And do you think I could be such a vampire as to let you? Yes, yes; I have had my dreams of such a thing; but I see now how hideous they were. You shall make no such sacrifice to me. You must put away the fancies that could never be fulfilled, or if by some infernal magic they could, would only bring sorrow to you and shame to me. God forbid! And God forgive me, if I have done or said anything to put this in your head! And thank God it isn't too late yet for you to take yourself back." (033-173 to 033-181)

This is the novel's central scene of sacrificial refusal and one of its most emotionally violent passages. Colville's physical recoil — "wrenched himself loose" — translates moral horror into bodily movement: he must literally break free of Imogene's embrace to refuse her offer. The vampire metaphor is crucial: to accept Imogene's sacrifice would be to feed on her youth, to sustain himself by consuming her future. The word "hideous" collapses aesthetic and moral judgment — the offer is not merely wrong but ugly, a violation of proportion and fitness. The religious language that follows ("God forbid! And God forgive me") elevates the moment beyond social propriety into the register of sin and damnation. Colville's closing exclamation — "thank God it isn't too late yet for you to take yourself back" — frames the refusal of sacrifice as a rescue: he is not rejecting Imogene but saving her from herself. Yet the intensity of his language also betrays his own complicity ("if I have done or said anything to put this in your head"), acknowledging that he may have solicited the very sacrifice he now refuses.

> "Oh," she murmured. "Do you think it is self-sacrifice for me to give myself to you? It's self-glorification! You don't understand — I haven't told you what I mean, or else I've told it in such a way that I've made it hateful to you." (034-001 to 034-004)

Imogene's revision of her own sacrificial rhetoric is the novel's most penetrating act of self-criticism. The antithesis — "self-sacrifice" versus "self-glorification" — dismantles the moral architecture on which her earlier declarations rested. If giving herself to Colville fulfils her deepest desire, then it cannot simultaneously be a sacrifice; the two categories are mutually exclusive. The insight is devastating because it is true, and it is true in a way that Imogene herself cannot fully sustain — she will later return to the language of sacrifice, thrilling to its theatrical possibilities. But in this moment, she sees clearly what the novel wants us to see: that romantic sacrifice, when the sacrificer wants what the sacrifice achieves, is a contradiction in terms. Her acknowledgement that she may have "made it hateful" to Colville is also a recognition that the rhetoric of self-abnegation, however sincerely intended, can become repellent to its intended beneficiary.

> She rose again from her chair, and stood flushed and thrilling with the notion of her self-sacrifice. Out of the tortuous complexity of the situation she had evolved this brief triumph, in which she rejoiced as if it were enduring success. But she suddenly fell from it in the dust. "Oh, what can I do for him? How can I make him feel more and more that I would give up anything, everything, for him!" (039-183 to 039-187)

This passage anatomises the psychology of performative sacrifice with clinical precision. Imogene's "thrilling" is somatic — sacrifice is felt in the body as excitement, as a surge of power — and the narrator's choice of "notion" rather than "act" or "decision" emphasises that what thrills her is an idea, a mental representation of herself as sacrificial heroine. The phrase "out of the tortuous complexity of the situation she had evolved this brief triumph" is Howells at his most astringent: sacrifice here is not a moral achievement but a cognitive simplification, a way of reducing an intractable tangle of feelings to a single, satisfying narrative. The triumph is "brief" and the narrator compares it to "enduring success" only to mark the distance between them. Imogene's fall "in the dust" is the collapse of the fantasy into the real — the sudden awareness that her self-sacrifice exists only in her imagination, and that the man for whom she would sacrifice everything does not want her sacrifice. Her final question — "How can I make him feel more and more that I would give up anything, everything, for him!" — is the novel's most painful articulation of the sacrificial paradox: the offering is real, the feeling is genuine, but the object of the sacrifice has no use for it. Sacrifice without a recipient is only a private drama, and Imogene is left performing for an audience of one.

## Related Concepts

- **Social Duty** — The ethical framework within which sacrifice and renunciation are negotiated. Mrs. Bowen's renunciation of Colville is framed as duty rather than sacrifice, and her insistence on pure motives reveals how duty can demand a renunciation even of the satisfactions of self-justification (046-146). Imogene's sacrificial impulses, by contrast, operate outside any stable framework of obligation — they are personal, emotional, and theatrical rather than social and moral.

- **Youth and Age** — The sacrificial dynamic in the novel is inseparable from the age difference between Colville and Imogene. Imogene's offer to "give back" Colville's lost youth (033-165 to 033-166) is the novel's most explicit linking of sacrifice to the generational theme: what she proposes to sacrifice is her own youth, offered as a restorative gift to a man who cannot recover his. Colville's refusal of this sacrifice — "You shall make no such sacrifice to me" (033-176) — is simultaneously a refusal to accept the premise that youth can be transferred, and a renunciation of his own longing for it.

- **Indian Summer (Metaphor)** — The novel's governing metaphor of belated warmth is itself a form of renunciation: the Indian summer accepts its own transience, embraces warmth without demanding permanence. Colville's eventual union with Mrs. Bowen represents a love that has renounced the illusions of youth — a love that does not pretend to be spring. The sacrificial drama with Imogene, by contrast, belongs to a false Indian summer, one that tries to reverse time rather than accept its passage.

- **Romantic Illusion and Self-Deception** — The novel's treatment of sacrifice is inseparable from its interest in self-deception. Characters consistently mistake self-interest for self-sacrifice, performative generosity for genuine renunciation. Imogene's "thrilling" at the notion of her own self-sacrifice (039-183) is the most explicit case, but the pattern is general: sacrifice in *Indian Summer* is always shadowed by the question of whether the sacrificer is acting for another or for the gratification of seeing herself act for another.
