# Mr. Bowen

## Role

Mr. Bowen is a minor, entirely off-stage character in William Dean Howells's _Indian Summer_ (1886). As the late husband of Lina Bowen (nee Ridgely) and the father of Effie Bowen, he never appears directly in the narrative but is referenced throughout as a shaping influence on Mrs. Bowen's character and circumstances. His prior marriage to Lina Ridgely — transforming her from a young Western girl travelling in Europe into the poised, cosmopolitan widow of the Palazzo Pinti — is one of the novel's submerged biographical facts. His political career and subsequent death provide essential backstory for understanding Mrs. Bowen's situation as a wealthy American widow living abroad with her daughter in Florence.

## Character Analysis

Mr. Bowen was a man of substantial professional and political accomplishment. He had been "a leading lawyer of her Western city," who subsequently "had gone to Congress" (002-114). After his congressional term, he took up residence in Washington, D.C., occupying what the newspapers described as "his elegant mansion at the corner of & Street and Idaho Avenue" (002-114). The honorific "the Hon. Mr. Bowen" (003-128), by which Theodore Colville remembers reading of him in the press, confirms his standing as a former member of Congress — a figure of sufficient public prominence that news of his marriage reached his wife's old acquaintances through newspaper reports.

Beyond these professional facts, Mr. Bowen's character emerges only obliquely, through the lens of Colville's retrospective musings and the evidence of the life Mrs. Bowen has built in his absence. Colville, reading of Lina Ridgely's marriage to Bowen years after the event, experienced the news as a catalyst for reinterpreting his own youthful memories: it was "not till he read of her marriage with the Hon. Mr. Bowen that certain vague impressions began to define themselves" (003-128). The marriage announcement thus functions in the novel as a narrative hinge — the moment at which the past, including Lina Ridgely's quiet kindnesses toward the younger Colville, begins to acquire legible meaning.

The most suggestive passage concerning Mr. Bowen comes not from direct description but from Colville's half-suppressed thought during a philosophical conversation with the elderly minister Mr. Waters. When Waters invokes the Adamic myth — "The man was cast into a deep sleep, you know" — and suggests that "the woman was what he dreamed her" (015-150), Colville privately reflects "that if Mrs. Bowen were what the late Mr. Bowen had dreamed her, then the late Mr. Bowen, when cast into his deep sleep, must have had Lina Ridgely in his eye" (015-150, 015-151). This wry internal observation casts Mr. Bowen as a man of discernment — someone who, in choosing Lina Ridgely as his wife, had the insight to recognise her essential qualities, the same qualities that Colville himself only belatedly comes to appreciate. The submerged compliment to Bowen is that he saw in the young Lina what Colville, in his youthful absorption with another woman, had been too blind to see.

Colville's further reflection — that he forbore to say "that if Mr. Waters's theory were correct, it would be better to begin with some one whom nobody else had dreamed before; then you could be sure at least of not having a wife to somebody else's mind rather than your own" (015-152) — positions Mr. Bowen as a kind of spectral rival. Even in death, Bowen represents a prior claim on Mrs. Bowen's nature: the man who "dreamed" her into the woman she became, and whose shaping influence can never be entirely superseded by any second husband. The late Mr. Bowen thus haunts the novel's romantic trajectory not as a character but as a structural absence — the predecessor whose place Colville might eventually take, but whose shadow cannot be fully dispelled.

## Relationships

- **Lina Bowen (wife)** — Mr. Bowen married Lina Ridgely at some point after the events of Colville's youthful romance in Florence, transforming her from "Miss Ridgely" into "Mrs. Bowen" (003-128). Their marriage produced one daughter, Effie. Colville's inference, upon learning that Mrs. Bowen had gone abroad for Effie's education, was not obviously charitable — he "made his own inferences concerning her marriage" (002-114) — though whether this reflects actual marital discord or merely Colville's self-serving interpretation is left productively ambiguous in the text. Mrs. Bowen herself speaks of her late husband with practised composure, asking Colville with "fit obsequy of tone" whether he knew Mr. Bowen was no longer living (002-114).

- **Effie Bowen (daughter)** — Mr. Bowen's only known child, who so perfectly resembles her mother that Colville entertains "a fantastic question whether she could ever have had any other parent" (003-010). Effie attends school at Vevay, Switzerland (003-011), and spends holidays with her mother at the Palazzo Pinti. Her existence as Mr. Bowen's daughter is acknowledged only indirectly; she is always "Mrs. Bowen's little Effie" (002-117), her identity defined almost entirely through her mother. The total physical erasure of Mr. Bowen from his daughter's features carries a symbolic weight: "Certainly, if Mrs. Bowen were to marry again, there was nothing in this child's looks to suggest the idea of a predecessor to the second husband" (003-010).

- **Theodore Colville** — Though the two men never meet, Mr. Bowen occupies a complicated position in Colville's imagination. Colville first learns of Bowen only through newspaper reports of the marriage (003-128) and, years later, the brief acknowledgement of his death (002-114). Yet as Colville's feelings for Mrs. Bowen deepen over the course of the novel, Mr. Bowen becomes an unspoken presence — the "predecessor" (003-010) whose prior claim to having "dreamed" Lina Ridgely into the woman she became stands as both an implicit model and an inescapable barrier to Colville's own romantic aspirations (015-150, 015-151).

## Key Scenes

### 1. Acknowledged in Death (Chapter 2)

Mrs. Bowen's first mention of her late husband comes with practised composure, as she fills in the outline of her biography for Colville during their chance reunion on the Ponte Vecchio. Colville already knows the public facts; the conversation merely confirms them:

> She had married a leading lawyer of her Western city, who in due time had gone to Congress, and after his term was out had "taken up his residence" in Washington, as the newspapers said, "in his elegant mansion at the corner of & Street and Idaho Avenue." After that he remembered reading that Mrs. Bowen was going abroad for the education of her daughter, from which he made his own inferences concerning her marriage. And "You knew Mr. Bowen was no longer living?" she said, with fit obsequy of tone. (002-114)

### 2. The Invisible Predecessor (Chapter 3)

Walking with Mrs. Bowen and Effie through the streets of Florence, Colville is struck by the child's uncanny resemblance to her mother — a resemblance so complete that it seems to erase Mr. Bowen from the genetic record:

> ...whose youthful resemblance to her mother was in all things so perfect that a fantastic question whether she could ever have had any other parent swept through him. Certainly, if Mrs. Bowen were to marry again, there was nothing in this child's looks to suggest the idea of a predecessor to the second husband. (003-010)

### 3. The Dreamer of Lina Ridgely (Chapter 15)

During a philosophical conversation with the elderly minister Mr. Waters about the Adamic theory that woman was fashioned from man's dream of her, Colville's unspoken thought connects Mr. Bowen to the biblical figure of Adam — casting the late husband as the man who perceived Lina Ridgely's true worth long before Colville himself did:

> He wanted to say that if Mrs. Bowen were what the late Mr. Bowen had dreamed her, then the late Mr. Bowen, when cast into his deep sleep, must have had Lina Ridgely in his eye. But this seemed to be personalising the fantasy unwarrantably, and pushing it too far. (015-150, 015-151)

## Biographical Sketch

Mr. Bowen was an American lawyer who practised in an unnamed Western city before his election to the United States Congress (002-114). His honorific "the Honourable" — by which Colville recalls reading of him in the press (003-128) — reflects his service as a member of the House of Representatives. After completing his term in Congress, he established a residence in Washington, D.C., living in the "elegant mansion" described by the newspapers (002-114). He married Lina Ridgely, a young American woman then travelling in Europe, at some point after the events surrounding Colville's youthful rejection in Florence. The couple had one daughter, Effie, who was likely born during or shortly after his congressional term. Mr. Bowen died at some point before the novel's opening; at the time of the main action, Mrs. Bowen has been living abroad in Florence long enough that Effie is of school age and attending a Swiss boarding school at Vevay (003-011).
