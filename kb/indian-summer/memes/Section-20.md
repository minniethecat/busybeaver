[020-001] [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]] rose. "Excuse me a moment; I will be back directly. Don't get

[020-002] up, please," she said, and prevented him with a quick withdrawal to

[020-003] another room, which left upon his sense the impression of elegant grace,

[020-004] and a smile and sunny glance. But neither had any warmth in it.

[020-005] [[theodore-colville|Colville]] heaved an involuntary sigh. "Do you feel very much used up?" he

[020-006] asked [[imogene-graham|Imogene]].

[020-007] "Not at all," she laughed. "Do you?"

[020-008] "Not in the least. My veglione hasn't ended yet. I'm still practically

[020-009] at the Pergola. It's easy to keep a thing of that sort up if you don't

[020-010] sleep after you get home."

[020-011] "Didn't you sleep? I expected to lie awake a long time thinking it over;

[020-012] but I dropped asleep at once. I suppose I was very tired. I didn't even

[020-013] dream."

[020-014] "You must have slept hard. You're pretty apt to dream when you're

[020-015] waking."

[020-016] "How do you know?"

[020-017] "Ah, I've noticed when you've been talking to me. Better not! It's a bad

[020-018] habit; it gives you false views of things. I used----"

[020-019] "But you mustn't say you _used!_ That's forbidden now. Remember your

[020-020] promise!"

[020-021] "My promise? What promise?"

[020-022] "Oh, if you've forgotten already."

[020-023] "I remember. But that was last night."

[020-024] "No, no! It was for all time. Why should dreams be so very misleading? I

[020-025] think there's ever so much in dreams. The most wonderful thing is the

[020-026] way you make people talk in dreams. It isn't strange that you should

[020-027] talk yourself, but that other people should say this and that when you

[020-028] aren't at all expecting what they say."

[020-029] "That's when you're sleeping. But when you're waking, you make people

[020-030] say just what you want. And that's why day dreams are so bad. If you

[020-031] make people say what you want, they probably don't mean it."

[020-032] "Don't you think so?"

[020-033] "Half the time. Do you ever have day dreams?" he asked Effie, pressing

[020-034] her cheek against his own.

[020-035] "I don't know what they are," she murmured, with a soft little note of

[020-036] polite regret for her ignorance, if possibly it incommoded him.

[020-037] "You will by and by," he said, "and then you must look out for them.

[020-038] They're particularly bad in this air. I had one of them in [[florence|Florence]] once

[020-039] that lasted three months."

[020-040] "What was it about?" asked the child.

[020-041] Imogene involuntarily bent forward.

[020-042] "Ah, I can't tell you now. She's trying to hear us."

[020-043] "No, no," protested the girl, with a laugh. "I was thinking of something

[020-044] else."

[020-045] "Oh, we know her, don't we?" he said to the child, with a playful appeal

[020-046] to that passion for the joint possession of a mystery which all children

[020-047] have.

[020-048] "We might whisper it," she suggested.

[020-049] "No; better wait for some other time." They were sitting near a table

[020-050] where a pencil and some loose leaves of paper lay. He pulled his chair a

[020-051] little closer, and, with the child still upon his knee, began to

[020-052] scribble and sketch at random. "Ah, there's [[san-miniato|San Miniato]]," he said, with

[020-053] a glance from the window. "Must get its outline in. You've heard how

[020-054] there came to be a church up there? No? Well, it shows the sort of man

[020-055] San Miniato really was. He was one of the early Christians, and he gave

[020-056] the poor pagans a great deal of trouble. They first threw him to the

[020-057] wild beasts in the amphitheatre, but the moment those animals set eyes

[020-058] on him they saw it would be of no use; they just lay down and died. Very

[020-059] well, then; the pagans determined to see what effect the axe would have

[020-060] upon San Miniato: but as soon as they struck off his head he picked it

[020-061] up, set it back on his shoulders again, waded across the [[arno-river|Arno]], walked up

[020-062] the hill, and when he came to a convenient little oratory up there he

[020-063] knelt down and expired. Isn't that a pretty good story? It's like

[020-064] fairies, isn't it?"

[020-065] "Yes," whispered the child.

[020-066] "What nonsense!" said Imogene. "You made it up."

[020-067] "Oh, did I? Perhaps I built the church that stands there to commemorate

[020-068] the fact. It's all in the history of Florence. Not in all histories;

[020-069] some of them are too proud to put such stories in, but I'm going to put

[020-070] every one I can find into the history I'm writing for Effie. San Miniato

[020-071] was beheaded where the church of Santa Candida stands now, and he walked

[020-072] all that distance."

[020-073] "Did he have to die when he got to the oratory?" asked the child, with

[020-074] gentle regret.

[020-075] "It appears so," said Colville, sketching. "He would have been dead by

[020-076] this time, anyway, you know."

[020-077] "Yes," she reluctantly admitted.

[020-078] "I never quite like those things, either, Effie," he said, pressing her

[020-079] to him. "There were people cruelly put to death two or three thousand

[020-080] years ago that I can't help feeling would be alive yet if they had been

[020-081] justly treated. There are a good many fairy stories about Florence;

[020-082] perhaps they used to be true stories; the truth seems to die out of

[020-083] stories after a while, simply because people stop believing them. Saint

[020-084] Ambrose of Milan restored the son of his host to life when he came down

[020-085] here to dedicate the Church of San Giovanni. Then there was another

[020-086] saint, San Zenobi, who worked a very pretty miracle after he was dead.

[020-087] They were carrying his body from the Church of San Giovanni to the

[020-088] Church of Santa Reparata, and in Piazza San Giovanni his bier touched a

[020-089] dead elm-tree that stood there, and the tree instantly sprang into leaf

[020-090] and flower, though it was in the middle of the winter. A great many

[020-091] people took the leaves home with them, and a marble pillar was put up

[020-092] there, with a cross and an elm-tree carved on it. Oh, the case is very

[020-093] well authenticated."

[020-094] "I shall really begin to think you believe such things," said Imogene.

[020-095] "Perhaps you _are_ a Catholic."

[020-096] Mrs. Bowen returned to the room, and sat down.

[020-097] "There's another fairy story, prettier yet," said Colville, while the

[020-098] little girl drew a long deep breath of satisfaction and expectation.

[020-099] "You've heard of the Buondelmonti?" he asked Imogene.

[020-100] "Oh, it seems to me as if I'd had _nothing_ but the Buondelmonti dinned

[020-101] into me since I came to Florence!" she answered in lively despair.

[020-102] "Ah, this happened some centuries before the Buondelmonte you've been

[020-103] bored with was born. This was Giovanni Gualberto of the Buondelmonti,

[020-104] and he was riding along one day in 1003, near the Church of San Miniato,

[020-105] when he met a certain man named Ugo, who had killed one of his brothers.

[020-106] Gualberto stopped and drew his sword; Ugo saw no other chance of escape,

[020-107] and he threw himself face downward on the ground, with his arms

[020-108] stretched out in the form of the cross. 'Gualberto, remember Jesus

[020-109] Christ, who died upon the cross praying for His enemies.' The story says

[020-110] that these words went to Gualberto's heart; he got down from his horse,

[020-111] and in sign of pardon lifted his enemy and kissed and embraced him. Then

[020-112] they went together into the church, and fell on their knees before the

[020-113] figure of Christ upon the cross, and the figure bowed its head in sign

[020-114] of approval and pleasure in Gualberto's noble act of Christian piety."

[020-115] "Beautiful!" murmured the girl; the child only sighed.

[020-116] "Ah, yes; it's an easy matter to pick up one's head from the ground, and

[020-117] set it back on one's shoulders, or to bring the dead to life, or to make

[020-118] a tree put forth leaves and flowers in midwinter; but to melt the heart

[020-119] of a man with forgiveness in the presence of his enemy--that's a

[020-120] different thing; _that's_ no fairy story; that's a real miracle; and I

[020-121] believe this one happened--it's so impossible."

[020-122] "Oh yes, it must have happened," said the girl.

[020-123] "Do you think it's so very hard to forgive, then?" asked Mrs. Bowen

[020-124] gravely.

[020-125] "Oh, not for ladies," replied Colville.

[020-126] She flushed, and her eyes shone when she glanced at him.

[020-127] "I'm sorry to put you down," he said to the child; "but I can't take you

[020-128] with me, and I must be going."

[020-129] Mrs. Bowen did not ask him to stay to lunch; he thought afterward that

[020-130] she might have relented as far as that but for the last little thrust,

[020-131] which he would better have spared.

[020-132] "Effie dear," said her mother, when the door closed upon Colville,

[020-133] "don't you think you'd better lie down a while? You look so tired."

[020-134] "Shall I lie down on the sofa here?"

[020-135] "No, on your bed."

[020-136] "Well."

[020-137] "I'll go with you, Effie," said Imogene, "and see that you're nicely

[020-138] tucked in."

[020-139] When she returned alone, Mrs. Bowen was sitting where she had left her,

[020-140] and seemed not to have moved. "I think Effie will drop off to sleep,"

[020-141] she said; "she seems drowsy." She sat down, and after a pensive moment

[020-142] continued, "I wonder what makes Mr. Colville seem so gloomy."

[020-143] "Does he seem gloomy?" asked Mrs. Bowen unsympathetically.

[020-144] "No, not gloomy exactly. But different from last night. I wish people

[020-145] could always be the same! He was so gay and full of spirits; and now

[020-146] he's so self-absorbed. He thinks you're offended with him, Mrs. Bowen."

[020-147] "I don't think he was very much troubled about it. I only thought he was

[020-148] flighty from want of sleep. At your age you don't mind the loss of a

[020-149] night."

[020-150] "Do you think Mr. Colville seems so very old?" asked Imogene anxiously.

[020-151] Mrs. Bowen appeared not to have heard her. She went to the window and

[020-152] looked out. When she came back, "Isn't it almost time for you to have a

[020-153] letter from home?" she asked.

[020-154] "Why, no. I had one from mother day before yesterday. What made you

[020-155] think so?"

[020-156] "Imogene," interrupted Mrs. Bowen, with a sudden excitement which she

[020-157] tried to control, but which made her lips tremble, and break a little

[020-158] from her restraint, "you know that I am here in the place of your

[020-159] mother, to advise you and look after you in every way?"

[020-160] "Why, yes, Mrs. Bowen," cried the girl, in surprise.

[020-161] "It's a position of great [[social-duty|responsibility]] in regard to a young lady. I

[020-162] can't have anything to reproach myself with afterward."

[020-163] "No."

[020-164] "Have I always been kind to you, and considerate of your rights and your

[020-165] freedom? Have I ever interfered with you in any way that you think I

[020-166] oughtn't?"

[020-167] "What an idea! You've been loveliness itself, Mrs. Bowen!"

[020-168] "Then I want you to listen to me, and answer me frankly, and not suspect

[020-169] my motives."

[020-170] "Why, how _could_ I do that?"

[020-171] "Never mind!" cried Mrs. Bowen impatiently, almost angrily. "People

[020-172] can't help their suspicions! Do you think [[mr-morton|Mr. Morton]] cares for you?"

[020-173] The girl hung her head.

[020-174] "Imogene, answer me!"

[020-175] "I don't know," answered Imogene coldly; "but if you're troubled about

[020-176] that, Mrs. Bowen, you needn't be; I don't care anything for Mr. Morton."

[020-177] "If I thought you were becoming interested in any one, it would be my

[020-178] duty to write to your mother and tell her."

[020-179] "Of course; I should expect you to do it."

[020-180] "And if I saw you becoming interested in any one in a way that I thought

[020-181] would make you unhappy, it would be my duty to warn you."

[020-182] "Yes."

[020-183] "Of course, I don't mean that any one would knowingly try to make you

[020-184] unhappy?"

[020-185] "No."

[020-186] "Men don't go about nowadays trying to break girls' hearts. But very

[020-187] good men can be thoughtless and selfish."

[020-188] "Yes; I understand that," said Imogene, in a falling accent.

[020-189] "I don't wish to prejudice you against any one. I should consider it

[020-190] very wrong and wicked. Besides, I don't care to interfere with you to

[020-191] that degree. You are old enough to see and judge for yourself."

[020-192] Imogene sat silent, passing her hand across the front of her dress. The

[020-193] clock ticked audibly from the mantel.
