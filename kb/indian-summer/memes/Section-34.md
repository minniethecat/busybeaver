[034-001] "Oh," she murmured. "Do you think it is [[sacrifice-and-renunciation|self-sacrifice]] for me to give

[034-002] myself to you? It's self-glorification! You don't understand--I haven't

[034-003] told you what I mean, or else I've told it in such a way that I've made

[034-004] it hateful to you. Do you think I don't care for you except to be

[034-005] something to you? I'm not so generous as that. You are all the world to

[034-006] me. If I take myself back from you, as you say, what shall I do with

[034-007] myself?"

[034-008] "Has it come to that?" asked [[theodore-colville|Colville]]. He sat down again with her, and

[034-009] this time he put his arm around her and drew her to him, but it seemed

[034-010] to him he did it as if she were his child. "I was going to tell you just

[034-011] now that each of us lived to himself in this world, and that no one

[034-012] could hope to enter into the life of another and complete it. But now I

[034-013] see that I was partly wrong. We two are bound together, [[imogene-graham|Imogene]], and

[034-014] whether we become all in all or nothing to each other, we can have no

[034-015] separate fate."

[034-016] The girl's eyes kindled with rapture. "Then let us never speak of it

[034-017] again. I was going to say something, but now I won't say it."

[034-018] "Yes, say it."

[034-019] "No; it will make you think that I am anxious on my own account about

[034-020] appearances before people."

[034-021] "You poor child, I shall never think you are anxious on your own account

[034-022] about anything. What were you going to say?"

[034-023] "Oh, nothing! It was only--are you invited to the Phillipses' fancy

[034-024] ball?"

[034-025] "Yes," said Colville, silently making what he could of the diversion, "I

[034-026] believe so."

[034-027] "And are you going--did you mean to go?" she asked timidly.

[034-028] "Good heavens, no! What in the world should I do at another fancy ball?

[034-029] I walked about with the airy grace of a bull in a china shop at the last

[034-030] one."

[034-031] Imogene did not smile. She faintly sighed. "Well, then, I won't go

[034-032] either."

[034-033] "Did you intend to go?"

[034-034] "Oh no!"

[034-035] "Why, of course you did, and it's very right you should. Did you want me

[034-036] to go?"

[034-037] "It would bore you."

[034-038] "Not if you're there." She gave his hand a grateful pressure. "Come,

[034-039] I'll go, of course, Imogene. A fancy ball to please you is a very

[034-040] different thing from a fancy ball in the abstract."

[034-041] "Oh, what nice things you say! Do you know, I always admired your

[034-042] compliments? I think they're the most charming compliments in the

[034-043] world."

[034-044] "I don't think they're half so pretty as yours; but they're more

[034-045] sincere."

[034-046] "No, honestly. They flatter, and at the same time they make fun of the

[034-047] flattery a little; they make a person feel that you like them, even

[034-048] while you laugh at them."

[034-049] "They appear to be rather an intricate kind of compliment--sort of

[034-050] _salsa agradolce_ affair--tutti frutti style--species of moral

[034-051] mayonnaise."

[034-052] "No--be quiet! You know what I mean. What were we talking about? Oh! I

[034-053] was going to say that the most fascinating thing about you always was

[034-054] that ironical way of yours."

[034-055] "Have I an ironical way? You were going to tell me something more about

[034-056] the fancy ball."

[034-057] "I don't care for it. I would rather talk about you."

[034-058] "And I prefer the ball. It's a fresher topic--to me."

[034-059] "Very well, then. But this I will say. No matter how happy you should

[034-060] be, I should always want you to keep that tone of persiflage. You've no

[034-061] idea how perfectly intoxicating it is."

[034-062] "Oh yes, I have. It seems to have turned the loveliest and wisest head

[034-063] in the world."

[034-064] "Oh, do you really think so? I would give anything if you did."

[034-065] "What?"

[034-066] "Think I was pretty," she pleaded, with full eyes. "Do you?"

[034-067] "No, but I think you are wise. Fifty per cent, of truth--it's a large

[034-068] average in compliments. What are you going to wear?"

[034-069] "Wear? Oh! At the ball! Something Egyptian, I suppose. It's to be an

[034-070] Egyptian ball. Didn't you understand that?"

[034-071] "Oh yes. But I supposed you could go in any sort of dress."

[034-072] "You can't. You must go in some Egyptian character."

[034-073] "How would Moses do? In the bulrushes, you know. You could be Pharaoh's

[034-074] daughter, and recognise me by my three hats. And toward the end of the

[034-075] evening, when I became very much bored, I could go round killing

[034-076] Egyptians."

[034-077] "No, no. Be serious. Though I like you to joke, too. I shall always want

[034-078] you to joke. Shall you, always?"

[034-079] "There may be emergencies when I shall fail--like family prayers, and

[034-080] grace before meat, and dangerous sickness."

[034-081] "Why, of course. But I mean when we're together, and there's no reason

[034-082] why you shouldn't?"

[034-083] "Oh, at such times I shall certainly joke."

[034-084] "And before people, too! I won't have them saying that it's sobered

[034-085] you--that you used to be very gay, and now you're cross, and never say

[034-086] anything."

[034-087] "I will try to keep it up sufficiently to meet the public demand."

[034-088] "And I shall want you to joke me, too. You must satirise me. It does

[034-089] more to show me my faults than anything else, and it will show other

[034-090] people how perfectly submissive I am, and how I think everything you do

[034-091] is just right."

[034-092] "If I were to beat you a little in company, don't you think it would

[034-093] serve the same purpose?"

[034-094] "No, no; be serious."

[034-095] "[[About]] joking?"

[034-096] "No, about me. I know that I'm very intense, and you must try to correct

[034-097] that tendency in me."

[034-098] "I will, with pleasure. Which of my tendencies are you going to

[034-099] correct?"

[034-100] "You have none."

[034-101] "Well, then, neither have you. I'm not going to be outdone in

[034-102] civilities."

[034-103] "Oh, if people could only hear you talk in this light way, and then know

[034-104] what _I_ know!"

[034-105] Colville broke out into a laugh at the deep sigh which accompanied these

[034-106] words. As a whole, the thing was grotesque and terrible to him, but

[034-107] after a habit of his, he was finding a strange pleasure in its details.

[034-108] "No, no," she pleaded. "Don't laugh. There are girls that would give

[034-109] their eyes for it."

[034-110] "As pretty eyes as yours?"

[034-111] "Do you think they're nice?"

[034-112] "Yes, if they were not so mysterious."

[034-113] "Mysterious?"

[034-114] "Yes, I feel that your eyes can't really be as honest as they look. That

[034-115] was what puzzled me about them the first night I saw you."

[034-116] "No--did it, really?"

[034-117] "I went home saying to myself that no girl could be so sincere as that

[034-118] Miss Graham seemed."

[034-119] "Did you say that?"

[034-120] "Words to that effect."

[034-121] "And what do you think now?"

[034-122] "Ah, I don't know. You had better go as the Sphinx."

[034-123] Imogene laughed in simple gaiety of heart.

[034-124] "How far we've got from the ball!" she said, as if the remote excursion

[034-125] were a triumph. "What shall we really go as?"

[034-126] "Isis and Osiris."

[034-127] "Weren't they gods of some kind?"

[034-128] "Little one-horse deities--not very much."

[034-129] "It won't do to go as gods of any kind. They're always failures. People

[034-130] expect too much of them."

[034-131] "Yes," said Colville. "That's human nature under all circumstances. But

[034-132] why go to an Egyptian ball at all?"

[034-133] "Oh, we must go. If we both stayed away it would make talk at once, and

[034-134] my object is to keep people in the dark till the very last moment. Of

[034-135] course it's unfortunate your having told [[mrs-amsden|Mrs. Amsden]] that you were going

[034-136] away, and then telling her just after you came back with me that you

[034-137] were going to stay. But it can't be helped now. And I don't really care

[034-138] for it. But don't you see why I want you to go to all these things?"

[034-139] "All these things?"

[034-140] "Yes, everything you're invited to after this. It's not merely for a

[034-141] blind as regards ourselves now, but if they see that you're very fond of

[034-142] all sorts of gaieties, they will see that you are--they will

[034-143] understand----"

[034-144] There was no need for her to complete the sentence. Colville rose.

[034-145] "Come, come, my dear child," he said, "why don't you end all this at

[034-146] once? I don't blame you. Heaven knows I blame no one but myself! I ought

[034-147] to have the strength to break away from this mistake, but I haven't. I

[034-148] couldn't bear to see you suffer from pain that I should give you even

[034-149] for your good. But do it yourself, Imogene, and for pity's sake don't

[034-150] forbear from any notion of sparing me. I have no wish except for your

[034-151] happiness, and now I tell you clearly that no appearance we can put on

[034-152] before the world will deceive the world. At the end of all our trouble I

[034-153] shall still be forty----"

[034-154] She sprang to him and put her hand over his mouth. "I know what you're

[034-155] going to say, and I won't let you say it, for you've promised over and

[034-156] over again not to speak of that any more. Oh, do you think I care for

[034-157] the world, or what it will think or say?"

[034-158] "Yes, very much."

[034-159] "That shows how little you understand me. It's because I wish to _defy_

[034-160] the world--"

[034-161] "Imogene! Be as honest with yourself as you are with me."

[034-162] "I _am_ honest."

[034-163] "Look me in the eyes, then."

[034-164] She did so for an instant, and then hid her face on his shoulder.

[034-165] "You silly girl," he said. "What is it you really do wish?"

[034-166] "I wish there was no one in the world but you and me."

[034-167] "Ah, you'd find it very crowded at times," said Colville sadly. "Well,

[034-168] well," he added, "I'll go to your fandangoes, because you want me to

[034-169] go."

[034-170] "That's all I wished you to say," she replied, lifting her head, and

[034-171] looking him radiantly in the face. "I don't want you to go at all! I

[034-172] only want you to promise that you'll come here every night that you're

[034-173] invited out, and read to [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]] and me."

[034-174] "Oh, I can't do that," said Colville; "I'm too fond of society. For

[034-175] example, I've been invited to an Egyptian fancy ball, and I couldn't

[034-176] think of giving that up."

[034-177] "Oh, how delightful you are! They couldn't any of them talk like you."

[034-178] He had learned to follow the processes of her thought now. "Perhaps they

[034-179] can when they come to my age."

[034-180] "There!" she exclaimed, putting her hand on his mouth again, to remind

[034-181] him of another broken promise. "Why can't you give up the Egyptian

[034-182] ball?"

[034-183] "Because I expect to meet a young lady there--a very beautiful young

[034-184] lady."

[034-185] "But how shall you know her if she's disguised?"

[034-186] "Why, I shall be disguised too, you know."

[034-187] "Oh, what delicious nonsense you _do_ talk! Sit down here and tell me

[034-188] what you are going to wear."

[034-189] She tried to pull him back to the sofa. "What character shall you go

[034-190] in?"

[034-191] "No, no," he said, resisting the gentle traction. "I can't; I have

[034-192] urgent business down-town."

[034-193] "Oh! Business in _[[florence|Florence]]!"_

[034-194] "Well, if I stayed, I should tell you what disguise I'm going to the

[034-195] ball in."

[034-196] "I knew it was that. What do you think would be a good character for

[034-197] me?"

[034-198] "I don't know. The serpent of old Nile would be pretty good for you."

[034-199] "Oh, I know you don't think it!" she cried fondly. She had now let him

[034-200] take her hand, and he stood holding it at arm's-length. [[effie-bowen|Effie Bowen]] came

[034-201] into the room. "Good-bye," said Imogene, with an instant assumption of

[034-202] society manner.

[034-203] "Good-bye," said Colville, and went out.

[034-204] "Oh, Mr. Colville!" she called, before he got to the outer door.

[034-205] "Yes," he said, starting back.

[034-206] She met him midway of the dim corridor. "Only to--" She put her arms

[034-207] about his neck and sweetly kissed him.

[034-208] Colville went out into the sunlight feeling like some strange, newly

[034-209] invented kind of scoundrel--a rascal of such recent origin and

[034-210] introduction that he had not yet had time to classify himself and

[034-211] ascertain the exact degree of his turpitude. The task employed his

[034-212] thoughts all that day, and kept him vibrating between an instinctive

[034-213] conviction of monstrous wickedness and a logical and well-reasoned

[034-214] perception that he had all the facts and materials for a perfectly good

[034-215] conscience. He was the betrothed lover of this poor child, whose

[034-216] affection he could not check without a degree of brutality for which

[034-217] only a better man would have the courage. When he thought of perhaps

[034-218] refusing her caresses, he imagined the shock it would give her, and the

[034-219] look of grief and mystification that would come into her eyes, and he

[034-220] found himself incapable of that cruel rectitude. He knew that these were

[034-221] the impulses of a white and loving soul; but at the end of all his

[034-222] argument they remained a terror to him, so that he lacked nothing but

[034-223] the will to fly from Florence and shun her altogether till she had heard

[034-224] from her family. This, he recalled, with bitter self-reproach was what

[034-225] had been his first inspiration; he had spoken of it to Mrs. Bowen, and

[034-226] it had still everything in its favour except that it was impossible.
