[029-001] "That is not true, and you needn't say it to spare me. You are going

[029-002] away because [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]] said something to you about me."

[029-003] "Not quite that," returned [[theodore-colville|Colville]] gently.

[029-004] "No; it was something that she said to me about you. But it's the same

[029-005] thing. It makes no difference. I ask you not to go for that."

[029-006] "Do you know what you are saying, [[imogene-graham|Imogene]]?"

[029-007] "Yes."

[029-008] Colville waited a long moment. "Then, I thank you, you dear girl, and I

[029-009] am going to-morrow, all the same. But I shan't forget this; whatever my

[029-010] life is to be, this will make it less unworthy and less unhappy. If it

[029-011] could buy anything to give you joy, to add some little grace to the good

[029-012] that must come to you, I would give it. Some day you'll meet the young

[029-013] fellow whom you're to make immortal, and you must tell him of an old

[029-014] fellow who knew you afar off, and understood how to worship you for an

[029-015] angel of pity and unselfishness. Ah, I hope he'll understand, too!

[029-016] Good-bye." If he was to fly, that was the sole instant. He took her

[029-017] hand, and said again, "Good-bye." And then he suddenly cried, "Imogene,

[029-018] do you wish me to stay?"

[029-019] "Yes!" said the girl, pouring all the intensity of her face into that

[029-020] whisper.

[029-021] "Even if there had been nothing said to make me go away--should you

[029-022] still wish me to stay?"

[029-023] "Yes."

[029-024] He looked her in the starry, lucid eyes, where a divine fervour

[029-025] deepened. He sighed in nerveless perplexity; it was she who had the

[029-026] courage.

[029-027] "It's a mistake! You mustn't! I am too old for you! It would be a wrong

[029-028] and a cruelty! Yes, you must let me go, and forget me. I have been to

[029-029] blame. If Mrs. Bowen has blamed me, she was right--I deserved it; I

[029-030] deserved all she could say against me."

[029-031] "She never said anything against you. Do you think I would have let her?

[029-032] No; it was I that said it, and I blamed you. It was because I thought

[029-033] that you were--you were--"

[029-034] "Trifling with you? How could you think that?"

[029-035] "Yes, I know now how it was, and it makes you seem all the grander to

[029-036] me. Did you think I cared for your being older than I was? I never cared

[029-037] for it--I never hardly thought of it after the very first. I tried to

[029-038] make you understand that, and how it hurt me to have you speak of it.

[029-039] Don't you think that I could see how good you were? Do you suppose that

[029-040] all I want is to be happy? I don't care for that--I despise it, and I

[029-041] always hate myself for seeking my own pleasure, if I find myself doing

[029-042] it. I have seen enough of life to know what _that_ comes to! And what

[029-043] hurt me worst of all was that you seemed to believe that I cared for

[029-044] nothing but amusing myself, when I wished to be something better,

[029-045] higher! It's nothing whether you are of my age or not, if--if--you care

[029-046] for me."

[029-047] "Imogene!"

[029-048] "All that I ask is to be with you, and try to make you forget what's

[029-049] been sad in your life, and try to be of use to you in whatever you are

[029-050] doing, and I shall be prouder and gladder of that than anything that

[029-051] people _call_ happiness."

[029-052] Colville stood holding her hand, while she uttered these ideas and

[029-053] incoherent repetitions of them, with a deep sense of powerlessness. "If

[029-054] I believed that I could keep you from regretting this--"

[029-055] "What should I regret? I won't let you depreciate yourself--make

[029-056] yourself out not good enough for the best. Oh, I know how it happened!

[029-057] But now you shall never think of it again. No; I will not let you. That

[029-058] is the only way you could make me regret anything."

[029-059] "I am going to stay," said Colville. "But on my own terms. I will be

[029-060] bound to you, but you shall not be bound to me."

[029-061] "You doubt me! I would rather have you go! No; stay. And let me prove to

[029-062] you how wrong you are. I mustn't ask more than that. Only give me the

[029-063] chance to show you how different I am from what you think--how different

[029-064] you are, too."

[029-065] "Yes. But you must be free."

[029-066] "Well."

[029-067] "What are they doing so long there?" asked [[mrs-amsden|Mrs. Amsden]] of Effie, putting

[029-068] her glasses to her eyes. "I can't see."

[029-069] "They are just holding hands," said the child, with an easy satisfaction

[029-070] in the explanation, which perhaps the old lady did not share. "He always

[029-071] holds my hand when he is with me."

[029-072] "Does he, indeed?" exclaimed Mrs. Amsden, with a cackle. She added,

[029-073] "That's very polite of him, isn't it? You must be a great favourite with

[029-074] Mr. Colville. You will miss him when he's gone."

[029-075] "Yes. He's very nice."

[029-076] Colville and Imogene returned, coming slowly across the loose, neglected

[029-077] grass toward the old woman's seat. She rose as they came up.

[029-078] "You don't seem to have succeeded so well in getting flowers for Miss

[029-079] Graham as for the other ladies. But perhaps you didn't find her

[029-080] favourite over there. What is your favourite flower, Miss Graham? Don't

[029-081] say you have none! I didn't know that I preferred scarlet anemones. Were

[029-082] there no forget-me-nots over there in the grass?"

[029-083] "There was no occasion for them," answered Colville.

[029-084] "You always did make such pretty speeches!" said the old lady. "And they

[029-085] have such an orphic character, too; you can interpret them in so many

[029-086] different ways. Should you mind saying just what you meant by that one?"

[029-087] "Yes, very much," replied Colville.

[029-088] The old lady laughed with cheerful resignation. She would as lief report

[029-089] that reply of his as another. Even more than a man whom she could

[029-090] entangle in his speech she liked a man who could slip through the toils

[029-091] with unfailing ease. Her talk with such a man was the last consolation

[029-092] which remained to her from a life of harmless coquetries.

[029-093] "I will refer it to Mrs. Bowen," she said. "She is a very wise woman,

[029-094] and she used to know you a great while ago."

[029-095] "If you like, I will do it for you, Mrs. Amsden. I'm going to see her."

[029-096] "To renew your adieux? Well, why not? Parting is such sweet sorrow! And

[029-097] if I were a young man I would go to say good-bye to Mrs. Bowen as often

[029-098] as she would let me. Now tell me honestly, Mr. Colville, did you ever

[029-099] see such an exquisite, perfect _creature_?"

[029-100] "Oh, that's asking a good deal."

[029-101] "What?"

[029-102] "To tell you a thing honestly. How did you come here, Mrs. Amsden?"

[029-103] "In Mrs. Bowen's carriage. I sent it round from the Pitti entrance to

[029-104] the Porta Romana. It's waiting there now, I suppose."

[029-105] "I thought you had been corrupted, somehow. Your zeal is

[029-106] carriage-bought. It _is_ a delightful vehicle. Do you think you could

[029-107] give me a lift home in it?"

[029-108] "Yes, indeed. I've always a seat for you in my carriage. To Hotel

[029-109] d'Atene?"

[029-110] "No, to [[palazzo-pinti|Palazzo Pinti]]."

[029-111] "This is deliciously mysterious," said Mrs. Amsden, drawing her shawl up

[029-112] about her shoulders, which, if no longer rounded, had still a charming

[029-113] droop. One realises in looking at such old ladies that there are women

[029-114] who could manage their own skeletons winningly. She put up her glasses,

[029-115] which were an old-fashioned sort, held to the nose by a handle, and

[029-116] perused the different persons of the group. "Mr. Colville concealing an

[029-117] inward trepidation under a bold front; Miss Graham agitated but firm;

[029-118] the child as much puzzled as the old woman. I feel that we are a very

[029-119] interesting group--almost dramatic."

[029-120] "Oh, call us a passage from a modern novel," suggested Colville, "if

[029-121] you're in the romantic mood. One of Mr. James's."

[029-122] "Don't you think we ought to be rather more of the great world for that?

[029-123] I hardly feel up to Mr. James. I should have said Howells. Only nothing

[029-124] happens in that case!"

[029-125] "Oh, very well; that's the most comfortable way. If it's only Howells,

[029-126] there's no reason why I shouldn't go with Miss Graham to show her the

[029-127] view of [[florence|Florence]] from the cypress grove up yonder."

[029-128] "No; he's very particular when he's on Italian ground," said Mrs.

[029-129] Amsden, rising. "You must come another time with Miss Graham, and bring

[029-130] Mrs. Bowen. It's quite time we were going home."

[029-131] The light under the limbs of the trees had begun to grow more liquid.

[029-132] The currents of warm breeze streaming through the cooler body of the air

[029-133] had ceased to ruffle the lakelet round the fountain, and the naiads rode

[029-134] their sea-horses through a perfect calm. A damp, pierced with the fresh

[029-135] odour of the water and of the springing grass, descended upon them. The

[029-136] saunterers through the different paths and alleys were issuing upon the

[029-137] main avenues, and tending in gathering force toward the gate.

[029-138] They found Mrs. Bowen's carriage there, and drove first to her house,

[029-139] beyond which Mrs. Amsden lived in a direct line. On the way Colville

[029-140] kept up with her the bantering talk that they always carried on

[029-141] together, and found in it a respite from the formless future pressing

[029-142] close upon him. He sat with Effie on the front seat, and he would not

[029-143] look at Imogene's face, which, nevertheless, was present to some inner

[029-144] vision. When the porter opened the iron gate below and rang Mrs. Bowen's

[029-145] bell, and Effie sprang up the stairs before them to give her mother the

[029-146] news of Mr. Colville's coming, the girl stole her hand into his.

[029-147] "Shall you--tell her?"

[029-148] "Of course. She must know without an instant's delay."

[029-149] "Yes, yes; that is right. Oh!--Shall I go with you?"

[029-150] "Yes; come!"

[029-151] XV

[029-152] Mrs. Bowen came in to them, looking pale and pain-worn, as she did that

[029-153] evening when she would not let Colville go away with the other

[029-154] tea-taking callers to whom she had made her headache an excuse. The

[029-155] eyelids which she had always a little difficulty in lifting were heavy

[029-156] with suffering, and her pretty smile had an effect of very great

[029-157] remoteness. But there was no consciousness of anything unusual or

[029-158] unexpected in his presence expressed in her looks or manner. Colville

[029-159] had meant to take Imogene by the hand and confront Mrs. Bowen with an

[029-160] immediate declaration of what had happened; but he found this

[029-161] impossible, at least in the form of his intention; he took, instead, the

[029-162] hand of conventional welcome which she gave him, and he obeyed her in

[029-163] taking provisionally the seat to which she invited him. At the same time

[029-164] the order of his words was dispersed in that wonder, whether she

[029-165] suspected anything, with which he listened to her placid talk about the

[029-166] weather; she said she had thought it was a chilly day outdoors; but her

[029-167] headaches always made her very sensitive.

[029-168] "Yes," said Colville, "I supposed it was cold myself till I went out,

[029-169] for I woke with a tinge of rheumatism." He felt a strong desire to

[029-170] excuse, to justify what had happened, and he went on, with a painful

[029-171] sense of Imogene's eyes bent in bewildered deference upon him. "I

[029-172] started out for a walk with [[mr-waters|Mr. Waters]], but I left him after we got

[029-173] across the [[ponte-vecchio|Ponte Vecchio]]; he went up to look at the Michelangelo

[029-174] bastions, and I strolled over to [[boboli-gardens|the Boboli]] Gardens--where I found your

[029-175] young people."

[029-176] He had certainly brought himself to the point, but he seemed actually

[029-177] further from it than at first, and he made a desperate plunge, trying at

[029-178] the same time to keep something of his habitual nonchalance. "But that

[029-179] doesn't account for my being here. Imogene accounts for that. She has

[029-180] allowed me to stay in Florence."

[029-181] Mrs. Bowen could not turn paler than her headache had left her, and she

[029-182] now underwent no change of complexion. But her throat was not clear

[029-183] enough to say to the end, "Allowed you to stay in--" The trouble in her

[029-184] throat arrested her again.
