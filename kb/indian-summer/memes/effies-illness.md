## Summary

Effie Bowen's illness begins during the masked Carnival ball at the Teatro della Pergola and extends through the days that follow, precipitating the first serious rupture in the social harmony of the Palazzo Pinti circle. At the veglione, the well-disciplined child voices an uncharacteristic complaint -- "It seems to me that mamma is getting all the good of the veglione" (018-039) -- a plaintive murmur that signals her physical discomfort even before the crisis unfolds. When the party scatters on the dance floor and Mrs. Bowen departs with the ailing Effie, a doctor is summoned. Mrs. Bowen receives Colville on his return with a chilling economy:

> She made short, quiet answers to all his eager inquiries. Most probably it was some childish indisposition; Effie had been faint. No, he need not go for the doctor. Mr. Waters had called the doctor, who had just gone away. There was nothing else that he could do for her. She dropped her eyes, and in everything but words dismissed him. (019-121--019-126)

The illness proves more than momentary. The next day, Effie appears "a little pale" and willingly lets Colville take her on his knee, laying "her languid head on his shoulder" (019-188--019-189). By evening, the concern deepens into a quiet domestic tableau:

> "Effie dear," said her mother, when the door closed upon Colville, "don't you think you'd better lie down a while? You look so tired."
> "Shall I lie down on the sofa here?"
> "No, on your bed." (020-132--020-135)

She drifts toward sleep on a sofa in the drawing room, described as "drowsy" by her mother: "I think Effie will drop off to sleep," she said; "she seems drowsy" (020-140--020-141). Over the following days Colville anxiously inquires after her, and by the time of the painter's reception she has recovered sufficiently that the question "And was the little one well?" is met with "Very much better" (022-074--022-075).

## Context

The illness occurs during the Florentine Carnival season, immediately following the veglione that Mrs. Bowen had attended only reluctantly. From the start, the veglione represented a transgression of the social caution that had governed Mrs. Bowen's conduct: she had acknowledged that "a great many people would be shocked if they knew I went" (024-133--024-135). Effie's sudden faintness transforms a social misadventure into a domestic crisis. The doctor's reassurance -- the illness is "some childish indisposition" (019-122--019-123) -- does nothing to dispel the emotional damage already done. The episode forces Mrs. Bowen to leave the theatre without her protegee Imogene or Colville, leaving the two to return home alone through the Carnival streets, an impropriety that Mrs. Bowen's subsequent coldness makes unmistakably clear. The illness thus arrives at precisely the moment when the tensions latent in the household's arrangement -- romantic rivalry, the claims of youth versus middle age, the conflict between American propriety and European license -- have been brought to the surface by the veglione. It provides Mrs. Bowen with both a genuine maternal obligation and a convenient shield behind which she can withdraw from Colville, setting the terms for the estrangement that will dominate the novel's middle chapters.

## Participants

- **Effie Bowen** -- The young daughter of Mrs. Bowen, whose sudden illness at the veglione triggers the crisis. Her faintness (019-123), pallor (019-188), and drowsiness (020-141) are the objective facts of the episode, but her illness also functions symbolically: the household's emotional equilibrium, like the child herself, has been unsettled.
- **Lina Bowen** -- Effie's mother, who departs the veglione early to tend to her sick child (019-143). Her maternal devotion is genuine, but her subsequent coldness toward Colville uses Effie's illness as the occasion for a strategic withdrawal. When Colville arrives the next day, she dismisses him with "short, quiet answers" and feigns hearing Effie call to avoid saying goodbye (019-121, 019-128).
- **Theodore Colville** -- The protagonist, whose anxious inquiries after Effie -- "I will come round in the morning. I must know how Effie is" (019-134) -- reveal both his genuine affection for the child and his uneasy conscience about the evening's events. His delayed visit to Palazzo Pinti, motivated partly by social vanity, is shadowed by the question of whether "Effie's indisposition was no more serious than it appeared" (022-119).
- **Imogene Graham** -- Mrs. Bowen's young protegee, who helps tuck Effie into bed (020-137) and remains in the drawing room while Effie sleeps. Her presence during the illness's aftermath underscores the triangular tension: she is both the cause of Mrs. Bowen's displeasure and the unwitting rival for Colville's attention.
- **Mr. Waters** -- The elderly American minister who assists Mrs. Bowen at the veglione, calls the doctor for Effie (019-124), and later, during Colville's visit, inquires "And was the little one well?" (022-074) -- a brief exchange that bridges the domestic sphere of the illness with the philosophical conversations that drive the novel's moral inquiry.

## Aftermath / Consequences

Effie's illness fractures the Palazzo Pinti household at a decisive moment. It provides the immediate pretext for Mrs. Bowen's coldness toward Colville on the morning after the veglione -- a coldness she sharpens into devastating irony when she remarks, "As I told Imogene, young people will be young people; and I knew how fond you were of dancing" (019-181--019-182), classifying Colville not as the mature guardian he imagines himself but as a participant in the youthful follies he ought to have restrained.

The illness also deepens Colville's moral unease. He arrives at Palazzo Pinti "with the feeling that he was defying Mrs. Bowen" (019-137), yet the visible evidence of Effie's indisposition -- her pallor, her languor -- disarms him. The narrator observes that Mrs. Bowen's maternal excuse is unimpeachable: "it was natural that she should excuse herself, and should wish to be with her sick child: she had done really nothing" (019-143). But, as the narrator notes with characteristic psychological acuity, "when a woman has done nothing she fills the soul of the man whose conscience troubles him with an instinctive apprehension" (019-143). Colville finds himself in the position of one who has wronged a woman who has given him no grounds for complaint -- a predicament far more unsettling than open conflict.

In the larger architecture of the novel, Effie's illness serves as the first in a sequence of physical crises -- followed later by Colville's carriage accident and brain-fever -- that dismantle the illusions sustaining the romantic triangle. The illness exposes the fragility of the household's emotional arrangements, demonstrating that the polite surface of expatriate social life can be shattered by the simplest of domestic emergencies. Effie's recovery by chapter 22 restores outward calm, but the damage done to the trust between Colville and Mrs. Bowen will require the far more severe ordeal of the carriage accident and its aftermath to repair.
