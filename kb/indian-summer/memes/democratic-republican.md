# The Democratic-Republican

## Description

The Democratic-Republican was the leading daily newspaper in [[des-vaches|Des Vaches]], Indiana, and the center of [[theodore-colville|Theodore Colville]]'s professional life for fifteen years. It was formed by the merger of two rival papers: the Des Vaches *Republican*, which Colville's elder brother had unexpectedly and almost involuntarily become the owner of, and the *Wabash Valley Democrat*, which the brother subsequently purchased, "uniting them under the name of the *Democrat-Republican*" (001-129--001-132). Colville, who had come to Des Vaches as an architect but found no demand for his profession, was drawn into the newspaper business by his brother and eventually "bought his brother out and became sole owner and editor of the *Democrat-Republican*" (001-150--001-151). Under his stewardship the paper grew into a respected publication, sustained by the "constant excitements and its endless variety" of newspaper life (001-152), and Colville became "the object of a pleasing popularity" in the community (001-150).

## Role in the Novel

The Democratic-Republican never appears directly in the novel's Florentine scenes, but it exerts a gravitational pull on [[theodore-colville|Colville]]'s consciousness throughout the narrative. The paper represents the working life Colville abandoned -- the "occupation of which he had bereaved himself" and which he missed "keenly and at every moment" (001-164--001-165). His departure from [[des-vaches|Des Vaches]] "sprang from disgust" (001-039) after a political humiliation: he permitted his name to be used as a congressional candidate by the opposing party and was "defeated by a tremendous majority" (001-053--001-055), a "mortification for Colville which his pride could not brook" (001-059). He sold the paper contemptuously and fled to Italy.

The newspaper is the anchor of the novel's central geographical and existential tension. When Colville stands on the [[ponte-vecchio]] in the opening scene, his mind immediately substitutes the American landscape: the [[arno-river|Arno]] becomes the Wabash, and the Florentine bridge becomes the Main Street Bridge at Des Vaches (001-030--001-032). This substitution is rooted in the loss of the paper -- the loss of purpose, of industry, of the "real world" of American provincial journalism. Colville "had lived long in a community where every one was intensely occupied, and he unconsciously paid to Des Vaches the tribute of feeling that an objectless life was disgraceful to a man" (001-161--001-163). The Democratic-Republican is thus the absent presence that defines Colville's midlife crisis: the work that gave his life meaning, the institution he abandoned in disgust, and the measure against which his Florentine idleness is judged. This pattern of loss and displacement connects the paper to the novel's exploration of [[expatriate-identity]] and the psychological cost of abandoning one's vocation.

## Historical Basis

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| Model | Small-town Midwestern daily newspapers, late 19th century |
| Predecessors | *Republican* and *Wabash Valley Democrat* — rival partisan organs |
| Merger pattern | Consolidation of party-press into commercial daily, typical of the Gilded Age |
| Author's knowledge | Howells worked as newspaper editor/reporter in small-town Ohio |
| Literary precedent | Howells explored small-town journalism earlier in *A Modern Instance* (1882) |
| Novel function | Represents the earnest, industrious provincial world the expatriate flees and misses |

The Democratic-Republican is a fictional creation, but it is modelled on the small-town daily newspapers that proliferated across the American Midwest in the late nineteenth century. These papers -- often formed by the merger of partisan organs (Democratic and Republican sheets combining into a single commercial enterprise) -- were the backbone of Gilded Age provincial journalism. Howells himself had deep firsthand knowledge of this world: as a young man he worked as a newspaper editor and reporter in small-town Ohio before moving to Boston and New York. His novel *A Modern Instance* (1882) had already explored the moral texture of small-town journalism. The merger that creates the Democratic-Republican -- two rival partisan papers united under a single masthead -- reflects a historical reality of the period, when the economics of newspaper publishing drove consolidation and the old party-press model gave way to the commercially minded local daily. In its function within the novel, the Democratic-Republican represents the earnest, industrious, provincial world of American work that the expatriate flees -- and secretly misses.

## Key Scenes

### The Founding of the Paper (Chapter I)

The origin of the Democratic-Republican is recounted in the novel's extended flashback, which establishes [[theodore-colville|Colville]]'s accidental transformation from architect to newspaperman.

> His brother had been seven years at Des Vaches, where he had lands, and a lead-mine, and a scheme for a railroad, and had lately added a daily newspaper to his other enterprises. He had, in fact, added two newspapers; for having unexpectedly and almost involuntarily become the owner of the Des Vaches *Republican*, the fancy of building up a great local journal seized him, and he bought the *Wabash Valley Democrat*, uniting them under the name of the *Democrat-Republican*. (001-124--001-132)

### Colville Takes Over (Chapter I)

Colville's ascent from reluctant recruit to proprietor is narrated as a gradual, almost unconscious process -- a man drifting into the identity that would define his adult life.

> He learned in time to relish the humorous intimacy of the life about him. He found himself the object of a pleasing popularity. He bought his brother out and became sole owner and editor of the *Democrat-Republican*. He had never any great newspaper-ability, but by fairly intelligent and diligent application he made his paper a respectable property. (001-147--001-152)

### The Political Defeat (Chapter I)

The event that drives Colville from [[des-vaches|Des Vaches]] -- and ultimately back to [[florence]] -- is a political miscalculation centered on the newspaper's influence. This episode is the novel's foundational wound, the [[colville-political-disgrace]] that sends him into his Florentine exile.

> After the political defeat, which he felt was the most colourable expression of the general disapproval, he had sold the *Democrat-Republican* at a handsome profit, and had returned to Italy after an absence of seventeen years. His purpose was to resume his studies of the Gothic architecture. (001-154--001-157)
