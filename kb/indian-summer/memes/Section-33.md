[033-001] "No," said [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]], putting her hand to her head with a gesture she

[033-002] had; "that would be quite unnecessary. It would be ridiculous under the

[033-003] circumstances. I have thought of it, and I have decided that the

[033-004] American way is the best."

[033-005] "Very well, then," said [[imogene-graham|Imogene]], with the air of summing up; "then the

[033-006] only question is whether we shall make it known or not to other people."

[033-007] This point seemed to give Mrs. Bowen greater pause than any. She was a

[033-008] long time silent, and [[theodore-colville|Colville]] saw that Imogene was beginning to chafe

[033-009] at her indecision. Yet he did not see the moment to intervene in a

[033-010] debate in which he found himself somewhat ludicrously ignored, as if the

[033-011] affair were solely the concern of these two women, and none of his.

[033-012] "Of course, Mrs. Bowen," said the girl haughtily, "if it will be

[033-013] disagreeable to you to have it known----"

[033-014] Mrs. Bowen blushed delicately--a blush of protest and of generous

[033-015] surprise, or so it seemed to Colville. "I was not thinking of myself,

[033-016] Imogene. I only wish to consider you. And I was thinking whether, at

[033-017] this distance from home, you wouldn't prefer to have your family's

[033-018] approval before you made it known."

[033-019] "I am sure of their approval. Father will do what mother says, and she

[033-020] has always said that she would never interfere with me in--in--such a

[033-021] thing."

[033-022] "Perhaps you would like all the more, then, to show her the deference of

[033-023] waiting for her consent."

[033-024] Imogene started as if stopped short in swift career; it was not hard for

[033-025] Colville to perceive that she saw for the first time the reverse side of

[033-026] a magnanimous impulse. She suddenly turned to him.

[033-027] "I think Mrs. Bowen is right," he said gravely, in answer to the eyes of

[033-028] Imogene. He continued, with a flicker of his wonted mood: "You must

[033-029] consider me a little in the matter. I have some small shreds of

[033-030] self-respect about me somewhere, and I would rather not be put in the

[033-031] attitude of defying your family, or ignoring them."

[033-032] "No," said Imogene, in the same effect of arrest.

[033-033] "When it isn't absolutely necessary," continued Colville. "Especially as

[033-034] you say there will be no opposition."

[033-035] "Of course," Imogene assented; and in fact what he said was very just,

[033-036] and he knew it; but he could perceive that he had suffered loss with

[033-037] her. A furtive glance at Mrs. Bowen did not assure him that he had made

[033-038] a compensating gain in that direction, where, indeed, he had no right to

[033-039] wish for any.

[033-040] "Well, then," the girl went on, "it shall be so. We will wait. It will

[033-041] only be waiting. I ought to have thought of you before; I make a bad

[033-042] beginning," she said tremulously. "I supposed I was thinking of you; but

[033-043] I see that I was only thinking of myself." The tears stood in her eyes.

[033-044] Mrs. Bowen, quite overlooked in this apology, slipped from the room.

[033-045] "Imogene!" said Colville, coming toward her.

[033-046] She dropped herself upon his shoulder. "Oh, why, why, why am I so

[033-047] miserable?"

[033-048] "Miserable, Imogene!" he murmured, stroking her beautiful hair.

[033-049] "Yes, yes! Utterly miserable! It must be because I'm unworthy of

[033-050] you--unequal every way. If you think so, cast me off at once. Don't be

[033-051] weakly merciful!"

[033-052] The words pierced his heart. "I would give the world to make you happy,

[033-053] my child!" he said, with perfidious truth, and a sigh that came from the

[033-054] bottom of his soul. "Sit down here by me," he said, moving to the sofa;

[033-055] and with whatever obscure sense of [[social-duty|duty]] to her innocent self-abandon, he

[033-056] made a space between them, and reduced her embrace to a clasp of the

[033-057] hand she left with him. "Now tell me," he said, "what is it makes you

[033-058] unhappy?"

[033-059] "Oh, I don't know," she answered, drying her averted eyes. "I suppose I

[033-060] am overwrought from not sleeping, and from thinking how we should

[033-061] arrange it all."

[033-062] "And now that it's all arranged, can't you be cheerful again?"

[033-063] "Yes."

[033-064] "You're satisfied with the way we've arranged it? Because if--"

[033-065] "Oh, perfectly--perfectly!" She hastily interrupted. "I wouldn't have it

[033-066] otherwise. Of course," she added, "it wasn't very pleasant having some

[033-067] one else suggest what I ought to have thought of myself, and seem more

[033-068] delicate about you than I was."

[033-069] "Some one else?"

[033-070] "You know! Mrs. Bowen."

[033-071] "Oh! But I couldn't see that she was anxious to spare me. It occurred to

[033-072] me that she was concerned about your family."

[033-073] "It led up to the other! it's all the same thing."

[033-074] "Well, even in that case, I don't see why you should mind it. It was

[033-075] certainly very friendly of her, and I know that she has your interest at

[033-076] heart entirely."

[033-077] "Yes; she knows how to make it seem so."

[033-078] Colville hesitated in bewilderment. "Imogene!" he cried at last, "I

[033-079] don't understand this. Don't you think Mrs. Bowen likes you?"

[033-080] "She detests me."

[033-081] "Oh, no, no, no! That's too cruel an error. You mustn't think that. I

[033-082] can't let you. It's morbid. I'm sure that she's devotedly kind and good

[033-083] to you."

[033-084] "Being kind and good isn't liking. I know what she thinks. But of course

[033-085] I can't expect to convince you of it; no one else could see it."

[033-086] "No!" said Colville, with generous fervour. "Because it doesn't exist

[033-087] and you mustn't imagine it. You are as sincerely and unselfishly

[033-088] regarded in this house as you could be in your own home. I'm sure of

[033-089] that. I know Mrs. Bowen. She has her little worldlinesses and

[033-090] unrealities of manner, but she is truth and loyalty itself. She would

[033-091] rather die than be false, or even unfair. I knew her long ago--"

[033-092] "Yes," cried the girl, "long before you knew me!"

[033-093] "And I know her to be the soul of honour," said Colville, ignoring the

[033-094] childish outburst. "Honour--like a man's," he added. "And, Imogene, I

[033-095] want you to promise me that you'll not think of her any more in that

[033-096] way. I want you to think of her as faithful and loving to you, for she

[033-097] is so. Will you do it?"

[033-098] Imogene did not answer him at once. Then she turned upon him a face of

[033-099] radiant [[sacrifice-and-renunciation|self-abnegation]]. "I will do anything you tell me. Only tell me

[033-100] things to do."

[033-101] The next time he came he again saw Mrs. Bowen alone before Imogene

[033-102] appeared. The conversation was confined to two sentences.

[033-103] "Mr. Colville," she said, with perfectly tranquil point, while she

[033-104] tilted a shut book to and fro on her knee, "I will thank you not to

[033-105] defend me."

[033-106] Had she overheard? Had Imogene told her? He answered, in a fury of

[033-107] resentment for her ingratitude that stupefied him. "I will never speak

[033-108] of you again."

[033-109] Now they were enemies; he did not know how or why, but he said to

[033-110] himself, in the bitterness of his heart, that it was better so; and when

[033-111] Imogene appeared, and Mrs. Bowen vanished, as she did without another

[033-112] word to him, he folded the girl in a vindictive embrace.

[033-113] "What is the matter?" she asked, pushing away from him.

[033-114] "With me?"

[033-115] "Yes; you seem so excited."

[033-116] "Oh, nothing," he said, shrinking from the sharpness of that scrutiny in

[033-117] a woman's eyes, which, when it begins the perusal of a man's soul,

[033-118] astonishes and intimidates him; he never perhaps becomes able to endure

[033-119] it with perfect self-control. "I suppose a slight degree of excitement

[033-120] in meeting you may be forgiven me." He smiled under the unrelaxed

[033-121] severity of her gaze.

[033-122] "Was Mrs. Bowen saying anything about me?"

[033-123] "Not a word," said Colville, glad of getting back to the firm truth

[033-124] again, even if it were mere literality.

[033-125] "We have made it up," she said, her scrutiny changing to a lovely appeal

[033-126] for his approval. "What there was to make up."

[033-127] "Yes?"

[033-128] "I told her what you had said. And now it's all right between us, and

[033-129] you mustn't be troubled at that any more. I did it to please you."

[033-130] She seemed to ask him with the last words whether she really had pleased

[033-131] him, as if something in his aspect suggested a doubt; and he hastened to

[033-132] reassure her. "That was very good of you. I appreciate it highly. It's

[033-133] extremely gratifying."

[033-134] She broke into a laugh of fond derision. "I don't believe you really

[033-135] cared about it, or else you're not thinking about it now. Sit down here;

[033-136] I want to tell you of something I've thought out." She pulled him to the

[033-137] sofa, and put his arm about her waist, with a simple fearlessness and

[033-138] matter-of-course promptness that made him shudder. He felt that he ought

[033-139] to tell her not to do it, but he did not quite know how without wounding

[033-140] her. She took hold of his hand and drew his lax arm taut. Then she

[033-141] looked up into his eyes, as if some sense of his misgiving had conveyed

[033-142] itself to her, but she did not release her hold of his hand.

[033-143] "Perhaps we oughtn't, if we're not engaged?" she suggested, with such

[033-144] utter trust in him as made his heart quake.

[033-145] "Oh," he sighed, from a complexity of feeling that no explanation could

[033-146] wholly declare, "we're engaged enough for that, I suppose."

[033-147] "I'm glad you think so," she answered innocently. "I knew you wouldn't

[033-148] let me if it were not right." Having settled the question, "Of course,"

[033-149] she continued, "we shall all do our best to keep our secret; but in

[033-150] spite of everything it may get out. Do you see?"

[033-151] "Well?"

[033-152] "Well, of course it will make a great deal of remark."

[033-153] "Oh yes; you must be prepared for that, Imogene," said Colville, with as

[033-154] much gravity as he could make comport with his actual position.

[033-155] "I am prepared for it, and prepared to despise it," answered the girl.

[033-156] "I shall have no trouble except the fear that you will mind it." She

[033-157] pressed his hand as if she expected him to say something to this.

[033-158] "I shall never care for it," he said, and this was true enough. "My only

[033-159] care will be to keep you from regretting. I have tried from the first to

[033-160] make you see that I was very much older than you. It would be miserable

[033-161] enough if you came to see it too late."

[033-162] "I have never seen it, and I never shall see it, because there's no such

[033-163] difference between us. It isn't the years that make us young or old--who

[033-164] is it says that? No matter, it's true. And I want you to believe it. I

[033-165] want you to feel that I am your youth--the youth you were robbed

[033-166] of--given back to you. Will you do it? Oh, if you could, I should be the

[033-167] happiest girl in the world." Tears of fervour dimmed the beautiful eyes

[033-168] which looked into his. "Don't speak!" she hurried on. "I won't let you

[033-169] till I have said it all. It's been this idea, this hope, with me

[033-170] always--ever since I knew what happened to you here long ago--that you

[033-171] might go back in my life and take up yours where it was broken off; that

[033-172] I might make your life what it would have been--complete your destiny--"

[033-173] Colville wrenched himself loose from the hold that had been growing more

[033-174] tenderly close and clinging. "And do you think I could be such a vampire

[033-175] as to let you? Yes, yes; I have had my dreams of such a thing; but I see

[033-176] now how hideous they were. You shall make no such sacrifice to me. You

[033-177] must put away the fancies that could never be fulfilled, or if by some

[033-178] infernal magic they could, would only bring sorrow to you and shame to

[033-179] me. God forbid! And God forgive me, if I have done or said anything to

[033-180] put this in your head! And thank God it isn't too late yet for you to

[033-181] take yourself back."
