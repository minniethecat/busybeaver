# Mr. Waters

> The Rev. Mr. Waters is a former Unitarian minister from a small New England village who, following the example of Ralph Waldo Emerson, renounced his pulpit and retired to Florence to devote himself to study — above all, the life and legacy of Savonarola. He functions as the novel's quiet sage: a figure of gentle, undogmatic moral clarity whose presence offers Theodore Colville both companionship and an implicit standard of integrity against which the protagonist's convoluted romantic entanglements are measured.

## Role

Mr. Waters serves as the novel's moral center and spiritual anchor — a **supporting** figure whose function is less to drive the plot than to illuminate it. He is the elderly clergyman Mrs. Bowen introduces Colville to at her reception (005-102), and he becomes, in the weeks that follow, Colville's most valued Florentine interlocutor. Unlike Colville, who is caught in a "baffled destiny, an arrested fate" (011-118), Waters has made peace with his renunciations: he surrendered his ministry when Unitarian doctrine "thinning out into paler doubt was no longer tenable with him" (015-082), and he lives in contented poverty in a modest lodging overlooking the Piazza San Marco, sustained by intellectual passion and everyday acts of gentle benevolence. As Imogene Graham puts it, placing him in implicit contrast with the charming but unsettled Colville: "Mr. Waters is good" (008-150). That goodness — undramatic, unassuming, entirely authentic — makes him the ethical touchstone of the novel.

## Character Analysis

Waters is introduced obliquely, through the impression he makes on others. Imogene Graham, who is quick to judge and drawn to worldly charm, finds him unexpectedly compelling: "Mr. Waters's manner is what I call _truly_ complimentary. He always talks to you as if he expected you to be interested in serious matters, and as if you were his intellectual equal" (006-124 to 006-126). The key word is "truly" — where Colville's flattery carries an ironic edge, Waters's respect is unfeigned. He treats a young woman as a mind, not as a spectacle; he is, Imogene adds with wonder, "so _happy_ here in Florence! He gives you the impression of feeling every breath he breathes here a privilege" (006-126 to 006-128). This capacious gratitude — for serious conversation, for the Florentine light, for a book on Savonarola held in the hand "with a simple relish as if it were something whose pleasantness was sensible to the touch" (011-100 to 011-101) — marks Waters as the rare character in Howells's fiction who has achieved what others merely seek: a life of undivided purpose.

Physically, Waters is "short and slight, with a youthful eagerness in his face surviving on good terms with the grey locks that fell down his temples from under the brim of his soft felt hat" (011-023 to 011-026). That phrase "surviving on good terms" is telling: age has not extinguished his curiosity, and his "boyish sweetness" blends with "a sort of appreciative shrewdness" (011-026 to 011-028). He retains the alertness of the born scholar, a man who can open a newly borrowed book and begin reading it at once, "already as remote as the fifteenth century through the magic of the book" (011-102 to 011-103). Colville, watching him, feels both admiration and unease — for he himself is not yet ready, he suspects, to turn his back so completely on "life, active life, life of his own day" (011-105 to 011-106).

Waters's intellectual life centers on Savonarola, the Florentine reformer whose puritan zeal and public execution in 1498 make him an unlikely object of study for a mild New England Unitarian. But Waters's interest is characteristically generous and self-reflective: "No two minds could well have been further apart than the New England minister and the Florentine monk, and no two souls nearer together, as Colville recognised with a not irreverent smile" (015-047 to 015-049). Waters is drawn to Savonarola's failure — "it's one of the things in history that I've found it hardest to accept" — and seeks to "justify" it by understanding the point at which "a man's virtues become mischievous and intolerable" (011-086 to 011-088). This inquiry doubles as a meditation on his own renunciation: Waters, who walked away from his pulpit rather than preach a creed he could no longer endorse, is implicitly asking whether his own puritanism, like Savonarola's, was "dreary and ugly" (011-077) — and whether quiet withdrawal was the better course.

Perhaps Waters's most penetrating quality is his gentle but uncanny perceptiveness about character. When Colville visits him in Piazza San Marco, Waters remarks on "our friends at Palazzo Pinti" and refers to "such a beautiful young creature" (015-123 to 015-124). Colville naturally assumes he means Imogene Graham. Waters quietly corrects him: "I meant Mrs. Bowen" (015-126). The exchange is a small masterpiece of misdirection: Colville — and the reader — have been trained by the conventions of romance to equate "beautiful young creature" with the nubile Imogene. Waters sees past convention to the deeper beauty of Lina Bowen, the widow who has weathered loss, raised a child alone, and maintained her grace and integrity. When Colville protests that Imogene "is certainly a beautiful girl," Waters concedes only that "she is very crude yet" — and then delivers an observation that reverberates through the entire novel: "You never can know what sort of nature a young girl has. Her nature depends so much upon that of the man whose fate she shares" (015-139 to 015-140). This insight — that a young woman's character is not fixed but shaped by the quality of the man who loves her — is at once the most radical and the most conservative idea in the book. It places an enormous burden on male moral responsibility, and it provides Colville with the first glimmer of what his pursuit of Imogene would actually entail.

Waters's biography is sketched economically but vividly in chapter 15. He had filled "the sort of sacerdotal office" — a phrase that captures the awkward, sui generis quality of the American pastorate — "in the little deeply book-clubbed New England village where he had outlived most of his flock" (015-078 to 015-079). The verb "outlived" is mournful and matter-of-fact. When "even the pale Unitarianism thinning out into paler doubt was no longer tenable with him" (015-082 to 015-083), he rose in the midst of his dwindled congregation — "the surviving dyspeptics and consumptives" (015-080) — and, following Emerson's precedent, asked to be released. His people raised a subscription and sent him to Florence, where he lives in "the poverty which can always be decent in Italy" (015-040). His days are spent studying Savonarola, taking Italian lessons from his landlady's daughter, and frequenting the reading-room at Vieusseux's. The green-grocer reveres him as "a foreign gentleman of saintly amiability and incomprehensible preferences" (015-060 to 015-061). His American gospel — "there is no other gospel. That is the gospel" (015-105) — is the optimistic faith that things "will all come right in time" (015-103), a creed he holds with a serenity that is never smug, because it has been earned through renunciation rather than inherited through privilege.

## Relationships

- **Theodore Colville** — Colville is drawn to Waters as a model of what a life after disappointment might look like. Waters's intellectual serenity simultaneously inspires and unnerves him: "He considered Mr. Waters's grey hairs, and determined to be wiser" (011-094). Waters, in turn, perceives the dangers of Colville's position before Colville himself does — his remark about Mrs. Bowen's beauty being the deeper kind is a tacit warning about the folly of Colville's pursuit of Imogene. Their friendship is one of the quiet consolations of the novel: two middle-aged American men in Florence, one at peace and one still seeking it.

- **Lina Bowen** — Waters admires Mrs. Bowen with a perceptiveness that Colville initially lacks. His description of her as "beautiful" rather than merely pretty, and as a "young creature" despite being Colville's contemporary, signals an eye for character that penetrates beyond surface charm (015-126 to 015-129). Mrs. Bowen, for her part, values Waters enough to introduce him to Colville at her reception (005-102), suggesting she recognizes a kindred seriousness in both men.

- **Imogene Graham** — Imogene finds Waters "lovely" and is charmed by his manner of treating her as an intellectual equal (006-123 to 006-126). But Waters is not charmed back in any reciprocal sense: he finds her "very crude yet" (015-131) and his theory that a young girl's nature depends upon the man whose fate she shares carries an implicit critique of Colville's romantic project. Imogene's interest in Waters is itself revealing — she responds to the very qualities (seriousness, sincerity, intellectual engagement) that Colville can only simulate through irony.

- **Savonarola** — Though not a character in the novel's present action, Savonarola is Waters's constant intellectual companion and spiritual double. Waters's study of the Florentine reformer — particularly his effort to understand where virtue becomes "excessive" and "mischievous" (011-086 to 011-087) — is the intellectual expression of his own life's renunciation of the pulpit. Colville recognizes the bond between the two figures as a paradox of affinity across difference: "No two minds could well have been further apart than the New England minister and the Florentine monk, and no two souls nearer together" (015-047 to 015-049).

## Key Scenes

### Introduction at Mrs. Bowen's Reception (005-067 to 005-105)

Colville falls into conversation with a "friendly old gentleman" (005-067) who proves to be, like himself, an American skeptical of the international-novel stereotype of the brash countryman. They discuss American girls — the "hard materialism" that can afflict them, and the "unsuspecting, unsophisticated freedom" that is preferable (005-093 to 005-098). Only after this natural accord is established does Mrs. Bowen approach and make the formal introduction: "Then I'll introduce you to Rev. Mr. Waters" (005-102). The delayed naming is characteristic of Howells's technique: the man's quality must be felt before his title is known.

> "I'm bound to say that I don't find our countrymen so aggressive, so loud, as our international novelists would make out. I haven't met any of their peculiar heroines as yet, sir." (005-077 to 005-079)

### The Reading-Room at Vieusseux's (011-015 to 011-106)

Colville encounters Waters at Vieusseux's, the social and intellectual crossroads of the foreign colony in Florence. Their exchange over Savonarola and Villari's biography reveals the intellectual range of Waters's mind and the personal stakes of his scholarship: he is fascinated by "justifying his failure to myself" (011-075 to 011-076). The scene also captures the physical presence of the old scholar — his youthful eagerness, his near-sighted fumbling for his library card, the way he opens a new book with tactile pleasure.

> "It is very interesting to inquire just at what point a man's virtues become mischievous and intolerable." (011-087 to 011-088)

### The Lodging in Piazza San Marco (015-039 to 015-114)

Chapter 15 provides the most sustained portrait of Waters's daily life. His room is bare but for books, with a writing table, a sofa-bed, and windows looking onto the church and convent of San Marco. We learn of his simple habits: a morning purchase of radishes and butter from the green-grocer (015-062 to 015-065), Italian lessons from his landlady's daughter (015-052 to 015-054), and the strange American dish of fried stockfish and potatoes that the trattoria waiters attempt for him on Sundays (015-058 to 015-059). The chapter also contains Waters's quietly subversive remark to Colville about Mrs. Bowen's beauty, and his theory of the relationship between a young woman's nature and the man she marries.

> "Ah," said the old man gently, "I wish they hadn't introduced the nun! But I suppose they can't help signalising their escape from the domination of the Church on all occasions. It's a natural reaction. It will all come right in time." (015-100 to 015-103)

### The "Beautiful Young Creature" Exchange (015-121 to 015-146)

Waters, with an almost clinical gentleness, undermines Colville's conventional assumption that "beautiful young creature" must refer to Imogene. The correction — "I meant Mrs. Bowen" — is delivered "quietly" (015-126), without a hint of reproof, yet it reframes the entire romantic geometry of the novel. Waters then articulates his theory: young girls like Imogene are "crude" because their natures are still unformed, dependent upon "the man whose fate she shares" (015-140). The passage is a touchstone for the novel's deepest concerns about love, responsibility, and the ethical weight of romantic choice.

> "You never can know what sort of nature a young girl has. Her nature depends so much upon that of the man whose fate she shares." (015-139 to 015-140)

## Quotations

- "There is no other gospel. That is the gospel." (015-105) — Waters's reply when Colville calls his optimism "the true American gospel."

- "No two minds could well have been further apart than the New England minister and the Florentine monk, and no two souls nearer together." (015-047 to 015-049) — Colville's reflection on Waters and Savonarola.

- "I find my ancestral puritanism particularly appealed to by the puritanism of Savonarola." (011-071 to 011-072) — Waters on his intellectual kinship with the reformer.
