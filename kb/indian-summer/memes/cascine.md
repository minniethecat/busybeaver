# Cascine

## Description

The Cascine is the great public park of Florence, stretching westward along the north bank of the Arno River. In *Indian Summer*, Howells introduces it in the novel's celebrated opening scene, as Theodore Colville gazes out from the Ponte Vecchio at the swollen river and traces its course downstream:

> There must already have been rain in the mountains, for the yellow torrent that seethed and swirled around the piers of the bridge was swelling momently on the wall of the Lung' Arno, and rolling a threatening flood toward the Cascine, where it lost itself under the ranks of the poplars that seemed to file across its course, and let their delicate tops melt into the pallor of the low horizon. (001-020)

The park is defined by these poplars — regimented lines of trees that mark the western limit of the city's visual field. When identified to Effie Bowen, the Cascine is given its simplest, most direct definition: "The Cascine you remember — the park where we were driving — that clump of woods there" (002-061). It sits at the far end of the Lung' Arno vista, visible from the city's central bridges, "quite down by the Cascine" (002-061) where the iron bridge crosses the river.

Colville's first approach to the Cascine in the narrative establishes its atmospheric character in winter: "The sun danced on the river, and bathed the long line of pale buff and grey houses that followed its curve, and ceased in the mist of leafless tree-tops where the Cascine began" (011-123). The park in January and February is a place of "leafless tree-tops" and "the mist" — a subdued landscape that suits the novel's autumnal mood. Later, in spring, it bursts into colour, as Effie Bowen reports: "all full of daisies to-day — perfectly powdered with them. And in under the trees there were millions of violets and crow's-feet" (037-143).

Physically, the Cascine is traversed by avenues and a canal, with gates at its city end, a road along the river, and a "bosky stretch" (012-133) of wooded interior — a space large enough to accommodate driving, strolling, and the quiet dramas of expatriate life. The park extends far enough that one may drive "quite down to the end of the Cascine" (038-093), where a monument to a young Indian prince stands — a detail that underscores the park's international associations in a city of travellers and sojourners.

## Significance in the Novel

The Cascine functions as more than scenic background in *Indian Summer*; it is the outdoor theatre of the expatriate social world, a place where characters perform, observe, and are observed. Howells establishes it early as one of the two great Florentine parks — "the Cascine or the Boboli Gardens" (011-154) — the twin poles of outdoor leisure for the foreign colony. When Colville asks, "Does one still come for one's health to the Cascine?" (011-152), he invokes a custom that was central to the Anglo-American experience of Florence in the nineteenth century: the daily promenade, a ritual mixing of health, fashion, and social reconnaissance.

The Cascine is the setting for the novel's first extended conversation between Colville and Imogene Graham. Colville encounters her and Effie Bowen at the Cascine gates, and what follows — a walk through the park, a litany of literary enthusiasms from the girl, and Colville's gently deflating responses — initiates the central romantic entanglement of the novel. Imogene's later account of the meeting to Mrs. Bowen captures both the innocence of the encounter and the vigilance that surrounds it: "We got out to walk in the Cascine, and we saw him coming in at the gate" (013-133). Mrs. Bowen's sharp follow-up — "Was there anybody you knew in the Cascine?" (013-139) — reveals the park's social dimension: one does not walk in the Cascine unseen, and every encounter carries a potential audience.

The park also serves as a measure of time and change. When Colville drives with Mrs. Bowen to the Cascine in the novel's later chapters, their conversation turns to reminiscence — "talking of the old times in the Cascine, twenty years before" (038-096). The park endures while the people who walk in it age, marry, or depart. As Colville reflects: "I remember, when I think, / That my youth was half divine" (012-041 to 012-044). The Cascine, for him, is the landscape of lost youth — a place he visited as a young man with "young ladies" (011-154), and to which he now returns in middle age, acutely aware of the distance between then and now.

The park's literary associations are also teased out on its paths. Colville reflects with "forlorn irony" that Imogene must have believed they were "talking literature in the Cascine" (013-104) during their walk — a phrase that simultaneously captures the girl's youthful ambition for intellectual conversation, Colville's knowing distance from his own youthful pretensions, and the park's role as a space where such pretensions can be performed. The Cascine is where talk happens — of books, of art, of love — and where the quality of that talk reveals the age and temperament of the talkers.

## Key Scenes

### The First Encounter — Colville Meets Imogene at the Cascine Gates (Chapter 11)

Colville, having fled the Gabinetto Vieusseux in a crisis of purpose, walks toward the Cascine and encounters Imogene and Effie Bowen at the park gates. The scene establishes the park as the social crossroads of expatriate Florence and launches the novel's central relationship:

> A carriage passed before him through the Cascine gates, and drove down the road next the river. He followed, and when it had got a little way it stopped at the roadside, and a lady and little girl alighted, who looked about and caught sight of him, and then obviously waited for him to come up with them. It was Imogene and Effie Bowen. (011-133 to 011-134)

The encounter at the gates precipitates Colville's wistful question about the fountain of youth:

> "Does one still come for one's health to the Cascine? When I was in Florence before, there was no other place if one went to look for it with young ladies — the Cascine or the Boboli Gardens. Do they keep the fountain of youth turned on here during the winter still?" (011-152 to 011-154)

### The Statues and Heine's Story (Chapter 12)

Walking deeper into the park, Colville and Imogene pause at a crossroad where weathered statues stand. The moment prompts Colville to recall Heine's story of a boy who kisses a garden statue, a tale that merges art, memory, and the passage of time:

> They had come to a point where some moss-grown, weather-beaten statues stood at the corners of the road that traversed the bosky stretch between the avenues of the Cascine. "Ah, how beautiful they are!" he said, halting, and giving himself to the rapture that a blackened garden statue imparts to one who beholds it from the vantage-ground of sufficient years and experience. (012-133)

Colville's meditation on the statues — beautiful precisely because they are blackened, moss-grown, and weather-beaten — is a miniature of the novel's larger concern with the beauty that only age and experience can perceive. The young Imogene, for all her enthusiasm, cannot yet see what he sees.

### The Drive with Mrs. Bowen (Chapter 38)

In a late turn toward intimacy, Mrs. Bowen asks Colville to drive with her in the Cascine. The excursion — without Imogene, who makes an excuse — marks a crucial shift in the novel's romantic geometry. The drive reaches the park's far end, where they pause at an unexpected monument:

> They drove quite down to the end of the Cascine, and got out there to admire the gay monument, with the painted bust, of the poor young Indian prince who died in Florence. They strolled all about, talking of the old times in the Cascine, twenty years before; and walking up the road beside the canal, while the carriage slowly followed, they stopped to enjoy the peasants lying asleep in the grass on the other bank. (038-093 to 038-096)

The scene gathers wildflowers, peasants asleep in the grass, and the quiet of a spring afternoon — a setting of almost pastoral simplicity in which the most serious relationship of the novel can begin to crystallize. The monument to the Indian prince — a foreigner who died far from home — resonates with the novel's themes of expatriation and mortality.

### The Daisies and Violets — Effie's Report (Chapter 37)

Effie Bowen's excited account of the Cascine in spring provides the novel's most vivid botanical detail and the child's perspective on the park. Her description — daisies "perfectly powdered" and "millions of violets and crow's-feet" under the trees (037-143) — captures the renewal of the season and the simple pleasure the Cascine affords to those who do not walk in it burdened by adult preoccupations.

## Real-World Counterpart

The Cascine of *Indian Summer* is the real Parco delle Cascine, Florence's largest public park, which stretches for approximately three kilometres along the north bank of the Arno from the Piazza Vittorio Veneto (near the city centre) to the Ponte all'Indiano. Originally a Medici hunting estate and dairy farm (the name derives from *cascina*, meaning farmhouse or dairy), it was opened to the public in the late eighteenth century under Grand Duke Pietro Leopoldo and became, by the 1880s — the period of Howells's novel — the city's principal promenade and carriage-drive, particularly favoured by the Anglo-American expatriate community. The park's layout, with its long avenues of poplars and plane trees, its canal, its statues, and its riverside road, remains substantially as Howells described it. The "gay monument, with the painted bust, of the poor young Indian prince who died in Florence" (038-093) is the Monumento all'Indiano, a late-nineteenth-century memorial to Rajaram Chhatrapati, the Maharaja of Kolhapur, who died in Florence in 1870 while returning from London to India — a real landmark that confirms Howells's documentary precision. Today the park continues to serve Florentines and visitors as a place for walking, cycling, and open-air recreation, though the formal carriage-drive culture of the nineteenth-century expatriate colony has long since vanished.
