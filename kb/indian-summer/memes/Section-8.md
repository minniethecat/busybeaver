[008-001] "No," said [[imogene-graham|Miss Graham]], with a shake of the head that expressed her

[008-002] resolution to speak the whole truth if she died for it, "not even in

[008-003] Santa Maria Novella."

[008-004] "What a wonderful old place it is! That curious façade, with the dials

[008-005] and its layers of black and white marble soaked golden-red in a hundred

[008-006] thousand sunsets! That exquisite grand portal!" He gesticulated with the

[008-007] hand that the tea-cup left free, to suggest form and measurement as

[008-008] artists do. "Then the inside! The great Cimabue, with all that famous

[008-009] history on its back--the first divine Madonna by the first divine

[008-010] master, carried through the streets in a triumph of art and religion!

[008-011] Those frescoes of Ghirlandajo's with real Florentine faces and figures

[008-012] in them, and all lavished upon the eternal twilight of that choir--but I

[008-013] suppose that if the full day were let in on them, once, they would

[008-014] vanish like ghosts at cock-crow! You must be sure to see the Spanish

[008-015] chapel; and the old cloister itself is such a pathetic place. There's a

[008-016] boys' school, as well as a military college, in the suppressed convent

[008-017] now, and the colonnades were full of boys running and screaming and

[008-018] laughing and making a joyful racket; it was so much more sorrowful than

[008-019] silence would have been there. One of the little scamps came up to me,

[008-020] and the young monk that was showing me round, and bobbed us a mocking

[008-021] bow and bobbed his hat off; then they all burst out laughing again and

[008-022] raced away, and the monk looked after them and said, so sweetly and

[008-023] wearily, 'They're at their diversions: we must have patience.' There are

[008-024] only twelve monks left there; all the rest are scattered and gone." He

[008-025] gave his cup to Miss Graham for more tea.

[008-026] "Don't you think," she asked, drawing it from the samovar, "that it is

[008-027] very sad having the convents suppressed?"

[008-028] "It was very sad having slavery abolished--for some people," suggested

[008-029] [[theodore-colville|Colville]]; he felt the unfairness of the point he had made.

[008-030] "Yes," sighed Miss Graham.

[008-031] Colville stood stirring his second cup of tea, when the _portière_

[008-032] parted, and showed [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]] wistfully pausing on the threshold. Her

[008-033] face was pale, but she looked extremely pretty there.

[008-034] "Ah, come in, Mrs. Bowen!" he called gaily to her. "I won't give you

[008-035] away to the other people. A cup of tea will do you good."

[008-036] "Oh, I'm a great deal better," said Mrs. Bowen, coming forward to give

[008-037] him her hand. "I heard your voice, and I couldn't resist looking in."

[008-038] "That was very kind of you," said Colville gratefully: and her eyes met

[008-039] his in a glance that flushed her face a deep red. "You find me here--_I_

[008-040] don't know why!--in my character of old family friend, doing my best to

[008-041] make life a burden to the young ladies."

[008-042] "I wish you would stay to a family dinner with us," said Mrs. Bowen, and

[008-043] Miss Graham brightened in cordial support of the hospitality. "Why can't

[008-044] you?"

[008-045] "I don't know, unless it's because I'm a humane person, and have some

[008-046] consideration for your headache."

[008-047] "Oh, that's all gone," said Mrs. Bowen. "It was one of those convenient

[008-048] headaches--if you ever had them, you'd know--that go off at sunset."

[008-049] "But you'd have another to-morrow."

[008-050] "No, I'm safe for a whole fortnight from another."

[008-051] "Then you leave me without an excuse, and I was just wishing I had

[008-052] none," said Colville.

[008-053] After dinner Mrs. Bowen sent Effie to bed early to make up for the late

[008-054] hours of the night before, but she sat before the fire with Miss Graham

[008-055] rather late, talking Colville over, when he was gone.

[008-056] "He's very puzzling to me," said Miss Graham. "Sometimes you think he's

[008-057] nothing but an old cynic, from his talk, and then something so sweet and

[008-058] fresh comes out that you don't know what to do. Don't you think he has

[008-059] really a very poetical mind, and that he's putting all the rest on?"

[008-060] "I think he likes to make little effects," said Mrs. Bowen judiciously.

[008-061] "He always did, rather."

[008-062] "Why, was he like this when he was young?"

[008-063] "I don't consider him very old now."

[008-064] "No, of course not. I meant when you knew him before." Miss Graham had

[008-065] some needlework in her hand; Mrs. Bowen, who never even pretended to

[008-066] work at that kind of thing, had nothing in hers but the feather screen.

[008-067] "He is old, compared with you, Imogene; but you'll find, as you live

[008-068] along, that your contemporaries are always young. Mr. Colville is very

[008-069] much improved. He used to be painfully shy, but he put on a bold front,

[008-070] and now the bold front seems to have become a second nature with him."

[008-071] "I like it," said Miss Graham, to her needle.

[008-072] "Yes; but I suspect he's still shy, at heart. He used to be very

[008-073] sentimental, and was always talking Ruskin. I think if he hadn't talked

[008-074] Ruskin so much, Jenny Milbury might have treated him better. It was very

[008-075] priggish in him."

[008-076] "Oh, I can't imagine Mr. Colville's being priggish!"

[008-077] "He's very much improved. He used to be quite a sloven in his dress; you

[008-078] know how very slovenly most American gentlemen are in their dress, at

[008-079] any rate. I think that influenced her against him too."

[008-080] "He isn't slovenly now," suggested Miss Graham.

[008-081] "Oh no; he's quite swell," said Mrs. Bowen, depriving the adjective of

[008-082] slanginess by the refinement of her tone.

[008-083] "Well," said Miss Graham, "I don't see how you could have endured her

[008-084] after that. It was atrocious."

[008-085] "It was better for her to break with him, if she found out she didn't

[008-086] love him, than to marry him. That," said Mrs. Bowen, with a depth of

[008-087] feeling uncommon for her, "would have been a thousand times worse."

[008-088] "Yes, but she ought to have found out before she led him on so far."

[008-089] "Sometimes girls can't. They don't know themselves; they think they're

[008-090] in love when they're not. She was very impulsive, and of course she was

[008-091] flattered by it; he was so intellectual. But at last she found that she

[008-092] couldn't bear it, and she had to tell him so."

[008-093] "Did she ever say why she didn't love him?"

[008-094] "No; I don't suppose she could. The only thing I remember her saying was

[008-095] that he was 'too much of a mixture.'"

[008-096] "What _did_ she mean by that?"

[008-097] "I don't know exactly."

[008-098] "Do you think he's insincere?"

[008-099] "Oh no. Perhaps she meant that he wasn't single-minded."

[008-100] "Fickle?"

[008-101] "No. He certainly wasn't that in her case."

[008-102] "Undecided?"

[008-103] "He was decided enough with her--at last."

[008-104] Imogene dropped the hopeless quest, "How can a man ever stand such a

[008-105] thing?" she sighed.

[008-106] "He stood it very nobly. That was the best thing about it; he took it in

[008-107] the most delicate way. She showed me his letter. There wasn't a word or

[008-108] a hint of reproach in it; he seemed to be anxious about nothing but her

[008-109] feeling badly for him. Of course he couldn't help showing that he was

[008-110] mortified for having pursued her with attentions that were disagreeable

[008-111] to her; but that was delicate too. Yes, it was a very large-minded

[008-112] letter."

[008-113] "It was shocking in her to show it."

[008-114] "It wasn't very nice. But it was a letter that any girl might have been

[008-115] proud to show."

[008-116] "Oh, she _couldn't_ have done it to gratify her vanity!"

[008-117] "Girls are very queer, my dear," said Mrs. Bowen, as if the fact were an

[008-118] abstraction. She mused upon the flat of her screen, while Miss Graham

[008-119] plied her needle in silence.

[008-120] The latter spoke first. "Do you think he was very much broken by it?"

[008-121] "You never can tell. He went out west then, and there he has stayed ever

[008-122] since. I suppose his life would have been very different if nothing of

[008-123] the kind had happened. He had a great deal of talent. I always thought I

[008-124] should hear of him in some way."

[008-125] "Well, it was a heartless, shameless thing! I don't see how you can

[008-126] speak of it so leniently as you do, Mrs. Bowen. It makes all sorts of

[008-127] coquetry and flirtation more detestable to me than ever. Why, it has

[008-128] ruined his life!"

[008-129] "Oh, he was young enough then to outlive it. After all, they were a boy

[008-130] and girl."

[008-131] "A boy and girl! How old were they?"

[008-132] "He must have been twenty-three or four, and she was twenty."

[008-133] "My age! Do you call that being a girl?"

[008-134] "She was old enough to know what she was about," said Mrs. Bowen justly.

[008-135] Imogene fell back in her chair, drawing out her needle the full length

[008-136] of its thread, and then letting her hand fall. "I don't know. It seems

[008-137] as if I never should be grown up, or anything but a child. Yes, when I

[008-138] think of the way young men talk, they do seem boys. Why can't they talk

[008-139] like Mr. Colville? I wish I could talk like him. It makes you forget how

[008-140] old and plain he is."

[008-141] She remained with her eyelids dropped in an absent survey of her sewing,

[008-142] while Mrs. Bowen regarded her with a look of vexation, impatience,

[008-143] resentment, on the last refinement of these emotions, which she banished

[008-144] from her face before Miss Graham looked up and said, with a smile "How

[008-145] funny it is to see Effie's infatuation with him! She can't take her eyes

[008-146] off him for a moment, and she follows him round the room so as not to

[008-147] lose a word he is saying. It was heroic of her to go to bed without a

[008-148] murmur before he left to-night."

[008-149] "Yes, she sees that he is good," said Mrs. Bowen.

[008-150] "Oh, she sees that he's something very much more. [[mr-waters|Mr. Waters]] is good."

[008-151] Miss Graham had the best of the argument, and so Mrs. Bowen did not

[008-152] reply.

[008-153] "I feel," continued the young girl, "as if it were almost a shame to

[008-154] have asked him to go to that silly dancing party with us. It seems as if

[008-155] we didn't appreciate him. I think we ought to have kept him for high

[008-156] aesthetic occasions and historical researches."

[008-157] "Oh, I don't think Mr. Colville was very deeply offended at being asked

[008-158] to go with us."

[008-159] "No," said Imogene, with another sigh, "he didn't seem so. I suppose

[008-160] there's always an undercurrent of sadness--of tragedy--in everything for

[008-161] him."

[008-162] "I don't suppose anything of the kind," cried Mrs. Bowen gaily. "He's

[008-163] had time enough to get over it."

[008-164] "Do people _ever_ get over such things?"

[008-165] "Yes--men."

[008-166] "It must be because he was young, as you say. But if it had happened

[008-167] _now_?"

[008-168] "Oh, it _couldn't_ happen now. He's altogether too cool and

[008-169] calculating."

[008-170] "Do you think he's cool and calculating?"

[008-171] "No. He's too old for a [[romantic-disillusionment|broken heart]]--a new one."

[008-172] "Mrs. Bowen," demanded the girl solemnly, "could _you_ forgive yourself

[008-173] for such a thing if you had done it?"

[008-174] "Yes, perfectly well, if I wasn't in love with him."

[008-175] "But if you'd made him _think_ you were?" pursued the girl breathlessly.

[008-176] "If I were a flirt--yes."

[008-177] "_I_ couldn't," said Imogene, with tragic depth.

[008-178] "Oh, be done with your intensities, and go to bed, Imogene," said Mrs.

[008-179] Bowen, giving her a playful push.

[008-180] VI

[008-181] It was so long since Colville had been at a dancing party that Mrs.

[008-182] Bowen's offer to take him to [[madame-uccelli|Madame Uccelli]]'s had first struck his sense

[008-183] of the ludicrous. Then it had begun to flatter him; it implied that he

[008-184] was still young enough to dance if he would, though he had stipulated

[008-185] that they were not to expect anything of the kind from him. He liked

[008-186] also the notion of being seen and accepted in Florentine society as the

[008-187] old friend of Mrs. Bowen's, for he had not been long in discovering that

[008-188] her position in [[florence|Florence]] was, among the foreign residents, rather

[008-189] authoritative. She was one of the very few Americans who were asked to

[008-190] Italian houses, and Italian houses lying even beyond the neutral ground

[008-191] of English-speaking intermarriages. She was not, of course, asked to the

[008-192] great Princess Strozzi ball, where the Florentine nobility appeared in

[008-193] the mediaeval pomp--the veritable costumes--of their ancestors; only a

[008-194] rich American banking family went, and their distinction was spoken of

[008-195] under the breath; but any glory short of this was within Mrs. Bowen's

[008-196] reach. So an old lady who possessed herself of Colville the night before

[008-197] had told him, celebrating Mrs. Bowen at length, and boasting of her

[008-198] acceptance among the best English residents, who, next after the

[008-199] natives, seem to constitute the social ambition of Americans living in

[008-200] Italian cities.
