# Mrs. Fleming

## Role

Mrs. Fleming is a minor, largely off-stage character in William Dean Howells's *Indian Summer* (1886). She belongs to the American expatriate community residing in Florence, where she functions as a chaperone to Imogene Graham and as a hostess of one of the season's notable social events. Though she never directly speaks a line of dialogue in the narrative, her presence — conveyed through the glimpses of "the top of her bonnet" from an adjoining room (023-045, 023-115) — provides the choreographic framework for a pivotal scene of romantic tension between Theodore Colville and Imogene Graham in Chapter 23. She later hosts a ball of sufficient importance to be referenced as a calendared fixture of Florentine social life (036-151), marking her as a central node in the expatriate social network within which the novel's central drama unfolds.

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Fleming receives no direct physical description or psychological characterization in the novel; she exists almost entirely as a social function — the chaperone, the hostess — rather than as a rendered individual. When Howells does give her a physical presence, it is metonymic and deliberately marginal: she is visible only as "the top of her bonnet still in the next room" (023-046, 023-115). This synecdochic treatment aligns with the novel's realist aesthetic — the story is focalized through Colville's consciousness, and a married American matron outside his romantic and intellectual interests simply does not occupy his attention. Yet her structural importance to the novel's social architecture is undeniable.

The fact that Mrs. Fleming is entrusted with chaperoning Imogene Graham — a young, unmarried American woman of marriageable age — at a public picture exhibition (023-017, 023-018) signals that she is regarded within the Florentine American colony as a respectable, trustworthy figure. Chaperonage was a serious social responsibility in the nineteenth-century expatriate context, carrying implicit guarantees about a young woman's propriety and safety. That Imogene's guardians — effectively Mrs. Bowen — permit her to attend the exhibition under Mrs. Fleming's care indicates the older woman's established standing within the community.

When the conversation between Colville and Imogene grows emotionally fraught — Imogene accused Colville of amusing himself with her, and he demanded to know the source of the accusation — the distressed girl's instinctive response is to wish for Mrs. Fleming's return: "Oh, I wish Mrs. Fleming——" (023-111). The broken sentence suggests that Mrs. Fleming represents safety, normalcy, and social rescue for a young woman who has ventured beyond her depth. Colville himself recognizes this social logic and, with "freezing _hauteur_," delivers Imogene back to the vicinity of Mrs. Fleming's bonnet before making his exit (023-114, 023-115). Mrs. Fleming thus serves, however passively, as the social anchor that prevents the scene from capsizing entirely into scandal.

The ball that Mrs. Fleming hosts — "Mrs. Fleming's ball" (036-151) — is mentioned in Chapter 36 as a fixture of the Florentine social calendar. Colville, exhausted by the emotional turmoil of his engagement to Imogene, declares that he will sleep twenty-four hours in order to "be fresh for Mrs. Fleming's ball" (036-151). The ball represents the glittering surface of expatriate life beneath which the novel's emotional crisis runs its course. Imogene's declaration that she will not attend (036-152) signals the extent of her personal distress — she is prepared to withdraw even from the principal social events of the season. That the ball proceeds regardless underscores Mrs. Fleming's role as a provider of the stable social infrastructure that Howells's characters both rely upon and chafe against.

## Relationships

- **Mr. Fleming (husband)**: Mrs. Fleming is married to Mr. Fleming, a fellow member of the American expatriate community in Florence. Their household is sufficiently established and respectable to host one of the season's notable balls (036-151) and to lend social credibility to the young women under Mrs. Fleming's chaperonage. Mr. Fleming remains entirely unnamed and undescribed within the narrative, known only through his wife's activities.

- **Imogene Graham (charge / protégée)**: Mrs. Fleming serves as chaperone to Imogene Graham, the novel's young female lead. At the picture exhibition in Chapter 23, Imogene attends under Mrs. Fleming's escort (023-017, 023-018). Imogene's desperate appeal for Mrs. Fleming's presence during her painful exchange with Colville — "Oh, I wish Mrs. Fleming——" (023-111) — reveals the chaperone's role as a source of emotional and social security for the inexperienced young woman. Their relationship, though never dramatized directly, is one of the novel's functional social bonds.

- **Theodore Colville (social acquaintance)**: Colville interacts with Mrs. Fleming only as a social fixture rather than as an individual. At the exhibition, he leads Imogene away from Mrs. Fleming's designated spot, joking about the conventions of chaperonage — "The chaperone is never there when you come back with the _protégée_" (023-047) — and later delivers Imogene back to the vicinity of Mrs. Fleming's bonnet when the conversation turns uncomfortable (023-114, 023-115). He later anticipates her ball with weary enthusiasm (036-151). Mrs. Fleming serves, in Colville's social calculus, as a reliable piece of the Florentine social furniture.

- **Mrs. Bowen (social peer)**: Though never shown in direct interaction, Mrs. Fleming and Mrs. Bowen move within the same American expatriate circle in Florence. Mrs. Bowen permits Imogene to attend the picture exhibition under Mrs. Fleming's care rather than accompanying her personally, suggesting a degree of social trust between the two women. When Colville seeks Mrs. Bowen's help after the exhibition incident, he does so at Palazzo Pinti — Mrs. Bowen's residence — rather than through Mrs. Fleming, drawing a subtle line between social acquaintance (Fleming) and intimate friendship (Bowen).

- **The Florentine Expatriate Community**: Mrs. Fleming and her household are embedded in the broader American and Anglo-American social world of Florence that provides the novel's backdrop. Her ball (036-151) is a gathering point for this community, paralleling Mrs. Bowen's Thursday receptions and the various parties, exhibitions, and theatrical outings that structure the expatriate social calendar. Along with the Inglehart family and other married couples, the Flemings represent the stable, conventional domesticity that contrasts with the romantic instability of the novel's central triangle.

## Key Scenes

### 1. The Picture Exhibition — Chaperone in Absentia (Chapter 23)

The most extended window into Mrs. Fleming's role comes during the picture exhibition in Chapter 23. When Colville approaches Imogene and inquires after Mrs. Bowen, the girl's reply immediately establishes Mrs. Fleming's function:

> "No," answered the girl, still very lifelessly; "I came with Mrs. Fleming." She looked about the room as if not to look at him. (023-017, 023-018)

Mrs. Fleming's physical absence from the immediate scene — she has left Imogene at a window — enables the charged private conversation between Colville and Imogene that follows. When the pair return from viewing the pictures, Mrs. Fleming remains tantalizingly out of reach:

> When they had made the round of the rooms Mrs. Fleming was not at the window where she had left Imogene; the girl detected the top of her bonnet still in the next room. (023-044, 023-045)

This visual detail — only "the top of her bonnet" visible — is Mrs. Fleming's sole physical description in the entire novel, and it exemplifies Howells's technique of conveying social presence through metonymic fragments.

### 2. The Crisis and Return — Chaperone as Refuge (Chapter 23)

When the conversation between Colville and Imogene reaches a breaking point over her implicit accusation that he has been amusing himself with her, Imogene's instinct is to wish for the chaperone's protective presence:

> "I don't know—yes. That is, not——Oh, I wish Mrs. Fleming——" (023-111)

Colville responds with cold formality, returning her to the social safety that Mrs. Fleming represents:

> "Let me take you to Mrs. Fleming," said Colville, with freezing _hauteur_; and led the way where the top of Mrs. Fleming's bonnet still showed itself. (023-114, 023-115)

The repetition of "the top of Mrs. Fleming's bonnet" as the visual landmark by which Colville navigates back to social propriety underscores how completely Mrs. Fleming functions as a positional marker rather than a dramatic character — she is the fixed point in the social geography to which endangered _protégées_ may be returned.

### 3. The Ball — Social Infrastructure (Chapter 36)

By Chapter 36, Mrs. Fleming's ball has become a fixture on the Florentine social calendar, significant enough to be invoked as a deadline for emotional recovery:

> "I am going to sleep twenty-four hours. Then I shall be fresh for Mrs. Fleming's ball." (036-151)

Colville's weary determination to attend, juxtaposed with Imogene's flat refusal — "I'm not going" (036-152) — illustrates how the Flemings' social event serves as a backdrop against which the novel's deepening romantic crisis is measured. The ball, hosted by Mrs. Fleming and her husband, represents the ongoing round of expatriate sociability that continues regardless of private emotional catastrophe — a hallmark of Howells's realist vision of the relationship between individual tragedy and social routine.
