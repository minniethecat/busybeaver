[041-001] [[theodore-colville|Colville]] spoke to [[imogene-graham|Imogene]], who withdrew her eyes from it with a sigh,

[041-002] after long brooding upon the scene. "You can do nothing with it, I see."

[041-003] "With what?"

[041-004] "The landscape. It's too full of every possible interest. What a history

[041-005] is written all over it, public and private! If you don't take it simply

[041-006] like any other landscape, it becomes an oppression. It's well that

[041-007] tourists come to Italy so ignorant, and keep so. Otherwise they couldn't

[041-008] live to get home again; the past would crush them."

[041-009] Imogene scrutinised him as if to extract some personal meaning from his

[041-010] words, and then turned her head away. The clergyman addressed him with

[041-011] what was like a respectful toleration of the drolleries of a gifted but

[041-012] eccentric man, the flavour of whose talk he was beginning to taste.

[041-013] "You don't really mean that one shouldn't come to Italy as well informed

[041-014] as possible?"

[041-015] "Well, I did," said Colville, "but I don't."

[041-016] The young man pondered this, and Imogene started up with an air of

[041-017] rescuing them from each other--as if she would not let [[mr-morton|Mr. Morton]] think

[041-018] Colville trivial or Colville consider the clergyman stupid, but would do

[041-019] what she could to take their minds off the whole question. Perhaps she

[041-020] was not very clear as to how this was to be done; at any rate she did

[041-021] not speak, and [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]] came to her support, from whatever motive of

[041-022] her own. It might have been from a sense of the injustice of letting Mr.

[041-023] Morton suffer from the complications that involved herself and the

[041-024] others. The affair had been going very hitchily ever since they started,

[041-025] with the burden of the conversation left to the two men and that

[041-026] helpless girl; if it were not to be altogether a failure she must

[041-027] interfere.

[041-028] "Did you ever hear of Gratiano when you were in Venice?" she asked Mr.

[041-029] Morton.

[041-030] "Is he one of their new water-colourists?" returned the young man. "I

[041-031] heard they had quite a school there now."

[041-032] "No," said Mrs. Bowen, ignoring her failure as well as she could; "he

[041-033] was a famous talker; he loved to speak an infinite deal of nothing more

[041-034] than any man in Venice."

[041-035] "An ancestor of mine, Mr. Morton," said Colville; "a poor, honest man,

[041-036] who did his best to make people forget that the ladies were silent.

[041-037] Thank you, Mrs. Bowen, for mentioning him. I wish he were with us

[041-038] to-day."

[041-039] The young man laughed. "Oh, in the _Merchant of Venice_!"

[041-040] "No other," said Colville.

[041-041] "I confess," said Mrs. Bowen, "that I _am_ rather stupid this morning. I

[041-042] suppose it's the softness of the air; it's been harsh and irritating so

[041-043] long. It makes me drowsy."

[041-044] "Don't mind _us_," returned Colville. "We will call you at important

[041-045] points." They were driving into a village at which people stop sometimes

[041-046] to admire the works of art in its church. "Here, for example, is--What

[041-047] place is this?" he asked of the coachman.

[041-048] "San Domenico."

[041-049] "I should know it again by its beggars." Of all ages and sexes they

[041-050] swarmed round the carriage, which the driver had instinctively slowed to

[041-051] oblige them, and thrust forward their hands and hats. Colville gave

[041-052] Effie his small change to distribute among them, at sight of which they

[041-053] streamed down the street from every direction. Those who had received

[041-054] brought forward the halt and blind, and did not scruple to propose being

[041-055] rewarded for this service. At the same time they did not mind his

[041-056] laughing in their faces; they laughed too, and went off content, or as

[041-057] nearly so as beggars ever are. He buttoned up his pocket as they drove

[041-058] on more rapidly. "I am the only person of no principle--except Effie--in

[041-059] the carriage, and yet I am at this moment carrying more blessings out of

[041-060] this village than I shall ever know what to do with. Mrs. Bowen, I know,

[041-061] is regarding me with severe disapproval. She thinks that I ought to have

[041-062] sent the beggars of San Domenico to [[florence|Florence]], where they would all be

[041-063] shut up in the Pia Casa di Ricovero, and taught some useful occupation.

[041-064] It's terrible in Florence. You can walk through Florence now and have no

[041-065] appeal made to your better nature that is not made at the appellant's

[041-066] risk of imprisonment. When I was there before, you had opportunities of

[041-067] giving at every turn."

[041-068] "You can send a cheque to the Pia Casa," said Mrs. Bowen.

[041-069] "Ah, but what good would that do me? When I give I want the pleasure of

[041-070] it; I want to see my beneficiary cringe under my bounty. But I've tried

[041-071] in vain to convince you that the world has gone wrong in other ways. Do

[041-072] you remember the one-armed man whom we used to give to on the Lung'

[041-073] [[arno-river|Arno]]? That persevering sufferer has been repeatedly arrested for

[041-074] mendicancy, and obliged to pay a fine out of his hard earnings to escape

[041-075] being sent to your Pia Casa."

[041-076] Mrs. Bowen smiled, and said, Was he living yet? in a pensive tone of

[041-077] reminiscence. She was even more than patient of Colville's nonsense. It

[041-078] seemed to him that the light under her eyelids was sometimes a grateful

[041-079] light. Confronting Imogene and the young man whose hopes of her he was

[041-080] to destroy at the first opportunity, the lurid moral atmosphere which he

[041-081] breathed seemed threatening to become a thing apparent to sense, and to

[041-082] be about to blot the landscape. He fought it back as best he could, and

[041-083] kept the hovering cloud from touching the earth by incessant effort. At

[041-084] times he looked over the side of the carriage, and drew secretly a long

[041-085] breath of fatigue. It began to be borne in upon him that these ladies

[041-086] were using him ill in leaving him the burden of their entertainment. He

[041-087] became angry, but his heart softened, and he forgave them again, for he

[041-088] conjectured that he was the cause of the cares that kept them silent. He

[041-089] felt certain that the affair had taken some new turn. He wondered if

[041-090] Mrs. Bowen had told Imogene what she had demanded of him. But he could

[041-091] only conjecture and wonder in the dreary undercurrent of thought that

[041-092] flowed evenly and darkly on with the talk he kept going. He made the

[041-093] most he could of the varying views of Florence which the turns and

[041-094] mounting levels of the road gave him. He became affectionately grateful

[041-095] to the young clergyman when he replied promptly and fully, and took an

[041-096] interest in the objects or subjects he brought up.

[041-097] Neither Mrs. Bowen nor Imogene was altogether silent. The one helped on

[041-098] at times wearily, and the other broke at times from her abstraction.

[041-099] Doubtless the girl had undertaken too much in insisting upon a party of

[041-100] pleasure with her mind full of so many things, and doubtless Mrs. Bowen

[041-101] was sore with a rankling resentment at her insistence, and vexed at

[041-102] herself for having yielded to it. If at her time of life and with all

[041-103] her experience of it, she could not rise under this inner load, Imogene

[041-104] must have been crushed by it.

[041-105] Her starts from the dreamy oppression, if that were what kept her

[041-106] silent, took the form of aggression, when she disagreed with Colville

[041-107] about things he was saying, or attacked him for this or that thing which

[041-108] he had said in times past. It was an unhappy and unamiable

[041-109] self-assertion, which he was not able to compassionate so much when she

[041-110] resisted or defied Mrs. Bowen, as she seemed seeking to do at every

[041-111] point. Perhaps another would not have felt it so; it must have been

[041-112] largely in his consciousness; the young clergyman seemed not to see

[041-113] anything in these bursts but the indulgence of a gay caprice, though his

[041-114] laughing at them did not alleviate the effect to Colville, who, when he

[041-115] turned to Mrs. Bowen for her alliance, was astonished with a prompt

[041-116] snub, unmistakable to himself, however imperceptible to others.

[041-117] He found what diversion and comfort he could in the party of children

[041-118] who beset them at a point near the town, and followed the carriage,

[041-119] trying to sell them various light and useless trifles made of

[041-120] straw--fans, baskets, parasols, and the like. He bought recklessly of

[041-121] them and gave them to Effie, whom he assured, without the applause of

[041-122] the ladies, and with the grave question of the young clergyman, that the

[041-123] vendors were little Etruscan girls, all at least twenty-five hundred

[041-124] years old. "It's very hard to find any Etruscans under that age; most of

[041-125] the grown-up people are three thousand."

[041-126] The child humoured his extravagance with the faith in fable which

[041-127] children are able to command, and said, "Oh, tell me about them!" while

[041-128] she pushed up closer to him, and began to admire her presents, holding

[041-129] them up before her, and dwelling fondly upon them one by one.

[041-130] "Oh, there's very little to tell," answered Colville. "They're mighty

[041-131] close people, and always keep themselves very much _to_ themselves. But

[041-132] wouldn't you like to see a party of Etruscans of all ages, even down to

[041-133] little babies only eleven or twelve hundred years old, come driving into

[041-134] an American town? It would make a great excitement, wouldn't it?"

[041-135] "It would be splendid."

[041-136] "Yes; we would give them a collation in the basement of the City Hall,

[041-137] and drive them out to the cemetery. The Americans and Etruscans are very

[041-138] much alike in that--they always show you their tombs."

[041-139] "Will they in [[fiesole|Fiesole]]?"

[041-140] "How you always like to burrow into the past!" interrupted Imogene.

[041-141] "Well, it's rather difficult burrowing into the future," returned

[041-142] Colville defensively. Accepting the challenge, he added: "Yes, I should

[041-143] really like to meet a few Etruscans in Fiesole this morning. I should

[041-144] feel as if I'd got amongst my contemporaries at last; they would

[041-145] understand me."

[041-146] The girl's face flushed. "Then no one else can understand you?"

[041-147] "Apparently not. I am the great American _incompris_."

[041-148] "I'm sorry for you," she returned feebly; and, in fact, sarcasm was not

[041-149] her strong point.

[041-150] When they entered the town they found the Etruscans preoccupied with

[041-151] other visitors, whom at various points in the quaint little piazza they

[041-152] surrounded in dense groups, to their own disadvantage as guides and

[041-153] beggars and dealers in straw goods. One of the groups reluctantly

[041-154] dispersed to devote itself to the new arrivals, and these then perceived

[041-155] that it was a party of artists, scattered about and sketching, which had

[041-156] absorbed the attention of the population. Colville went to the

[041-157] restaurant to order lunch, leaving the ladies to the care of Mr. Morton.

[041-158] When he came back he found the carriage surrounded by the artists, who

[041-159] had turned out to be the Inglehart boys. They had walked up to Fiesole

[041-160] the afternoon before, and they had been sketching there all the morning.

[041-161] With the artist's indifference to the conventional objects of interest,

[041-162] they were still ignorant of what ought to be seen in Fiesole by

[041-163] tourists, and they accepted Colville's proposition to be of his party in

[041-164] going the rounds of the Cathedral, the Museum, and the view from that

[041-165] point of the wall called the Belvedere. They found that they had been at

[041-166] the Belvedere before without knowing that it merited particular

[041-167] recognition, and some of them had made sketches from it--of bits of

[041-168] architecture and landscape, and of figure amongst the women with straw

[041-169] fans and baskets to sell, who thronged round the whole party again, and

[041-170] interrupted the prospect. In the church they differed amongst themselves

[041-171] as to the best bits for study, and Colville listened in whimsical

[041-172] despair to the enthusiasm of their likings and dislikings. All that was

[041-173] so far from him now; but in the Museum, which had only a thin interest

[041-174] based upon a small collection of art and archeology, he suffered a real

[041-175] affliction in the presence of a young Italian couple, who were probably

[041-176] plighted lovers. They went before a grey-haired pair, who might have

[041-177] been the girl's father and mother, and they looked at none of the

[041-178] objects, though they regularly stopped before them and waited till their

[041-179] guide had said his say about them. The girl, clinging tight to the young

[041-180] man's arm, knew nothing but him; her mouth and eyes were set in a

[041-181] passionate concentration of her being upon him, and he seemed to walk in

[041-182] a dream of her. From time to time they peered upon each other's faces,

[041-183] and then they paused, rapt and indifferent to all besides.
