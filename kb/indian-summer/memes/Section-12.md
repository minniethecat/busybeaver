[012-001] "Should you have thought," she asked, turning her face back toward him,

[012-002] "that it would be so hot in the sun to-day? _Oh_, that beautiful river!

[012-003] How it twists and writhes along! Do you remember that sonnet of

[012-004] Longfellow's--the one he wrote in Italian about the [[ponte-vecchio|Ponte Vecchio]], and

[012-005] the [[arno-river|Arno]] twisting like a dragon underneath it? They say that Hawthorne

[012-006] used to live in a villa just behind the hill over there; we're going to

[012-007] look it up as soon as the weather is settled. Don't you think his books

[012-008] are perfectly fascinating?"

[012-009] "Yes," said [[theodore-colville|Colville]]; "only I should want a good while to say it."

[012-010] "_I_ shouldn't!" retorted the girl. "When you've said fascinating,

[012-011] you've said everything. There's no other word for them. Don't you like

[012-012] to talk about the books you've read?"

[012-013] "I would if I could remember the names of the characters. But I get them

[012-014] mixed up."

[012-015] "Oh, _I_ never do! I remember the least one of them, and all they do and

[012-016] say."

[012-017] "I used to."

[012-018] "It seems to me you _used_ to do everything."

[012-019] "It seems to me as if I did."

[012-020] "'I remember, when I think,

[012-021] That my youth was half divine.'"

[012-022] "Oh, Tennyson--yes! _He's_ fascinating. Don't you think he's

[012-023] fascinating?"

[012-024] "Very," said Colville. He was wondering whether this were the kind of

[012-025] talk that he thought was literary when he was a young fellow.

[012-026] "How perfectly weird the 'Vision of Sin' is!" [[imogene-graham|Imogene]] continued. "Don't

[012-027] you like _weird_ things?"

[012-028] "Weird things?" Colville reflected. "Yes; but I don't see very much in

[012-029] them any more. The fact is, they don't seem to come to anything in

[012-030] particular."

[012-031] "Oh, _I_ think they do! I've had dreams that I've lived on for days. Do

[012-032] you ever have prophetic dreams?"

[012-033] "Yes; but they never come true. When they do, I know that I didn't have

[012-034] them."

[012-035] "What _do_ you mean?"

[012-036] "I mean that we are all so fond of the marvellous that we can't trust

[012-037] ourselves about any experience that seems supernatural. If a ghost

[012-038] appeared to me I should want him to prove it by at least two other

[012-039] reliable, disinterested witnesses before I believed my own account of

[012-040] the matter."

[012-041] "Oh!" cried the girl, half puzzled, half amused. "Then of course you

[012-042] don't believe in ghosts?"

[012-043] "Yes; I expect to be one myself some day. But I'm in no hurry to mingle

[012-044] with them."

[012-045] Imogene smiled vaguely, as if the talk pleased her, even when it mocked

[012-046] the fancies and whims which, after so many generations that have

[012-047] indulged them, she was finding so fresh and new in her turn.

[012-048] "Don't you like to walk by the side of a river?" she asked, increasing

[012-049] her eager pace a little. "I feel as if it were bearing me along."

[012-050] "I feel as if I were carrying it," said Colville. "It's as fatiguing as

[012-051] walking on railroad ties."

[012-052] "Oh, that's too bad!" cried the girl. "How can you be so prosaic? Should

[012-053] you ever have believed that the sun could be so hot in January? And look

[012-054] at those ridiculous green hillsides over the river there! Don't you like

[012-055] it to be winter when it _is_ winter?"

[012-056] She did not seem to have expected anything from Colville but an

[012-057] impulsive acquiescence, but she listened while he defended the mild

[012-058] weather. "I think it's very well for Italy," he said. "It has always

[012-059] seemed to me--that is, it seems to me now for the first time, but one

[012-060] has to begin the other way--as if the seasons here had worn themselves

[012-061] out like the turbulent passions of the people. I dare say the winter was

[012-062] much fiercer in the times of the Bianchi and Neri."

[012-063] "Oh, how delightful! Do you really believe that?"

[012-064] "No, I don't know that I do. But I shouldn't have much difficulty in

[012-065] proving it, I think, to the sympathetic understanding."

[012-066] "I wish you would prove it to mine. It sounds so pretty, I'm sure it

[012-067] must be true."

[012-068] "Oh, then, it isn't necessary. I'll reserve my arguments for Mrs.

[012-069] Bowen."

[012-070] "You had better. She isn't at all romantic. She says it's very well for

[012-071] me she isn't--that her being matter-of-fact lets me be as romantic as I

[012-072] like."

[012-073] "Then [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]] isn't as romantic as she would like to be if she hadn't

[012-074] charge of a romantic young lady?"

[012-075] "Oh, I don't say that. Dear me! I'd no idea it _could_ be so hot in

[012-076] January." As they strolled along beside the long hedge of laurel, the

[012-077] carriage slowly following them at a little distance, the sun beat strong

[012-078] upon the white road, blotched here and there with the black irregular

[012-079] shadows of the ilexes. The girl undid the pelisse across her breast,

[012-080] with a fine impetuosity, and let it swing open as she walked. She

[012-081] stopped suddenly. "Hark! What bird was that?"

[012-082] "'It was the nightingale, and not the lark,'" suggested Colville lazily.

[012-083] "Oh, _don't_ you think _Romeo and Juliet_ is divine?" demanded Imogene,

[012-084] promptly dropping the question of the bird.

[012-085] "I don't know about Romeo," returned Colville, "but it's sometimes

[012-086] occurred to me that Juliet was rather forth-putting."

[012-087] "You _know_ she wasn't. It's my favourite play. I could go every night.

[012-088] It's perfectly amazing to me that they can play anything else."

[012-089] "You would like it five hundred nights in the year, like _Hazel Kirke_?

[012-090] That would be a good deal of Romeo, not to say Juliet."

[012-091] "They ought to do it out of respect to Shakespeare. Don't you like

[012-092] Shakespeare?"

[012-093] "Well, I've seen the time when I preferred Alexander Smith," said

[012-094] Colville evasively.

[012-095] "Alexander Smith? Who in the world is Alexander Smith?"

[012-096] "How recent you are! Alexander Smith was an immortal who flourished

[012-097] about the year 1850."

[012-098] "That was before I was born. How could I remember him? But I don't feel

[012-099] so very recent for all that."

[012-100] "Neither do I, this morning," said Colville. "I was up at one of

[012-101] Pharaoh's balls last night, and I danced too much."

[012-102] He gave Imogene a droll glance, and then bent it upon Effie's discreet

[012-103] face. The child dropped her eyes with a blush like her mother's, having

[012-104] first sought provisional counsel of Imogene, who turned away. He rightly

[012-105] inferred that they all had been talking him over at breakfast, and he

[012-106] broke into a laugh which they joined in, but Imogene said nothing in

[012-107] recognition of the fact.

[012-108] With what he felt to be haste for his relief she said, "Don't you hate

[012-109] to be told to read a book?"

[012-110] "I used to--quarter of a century ago," said Colville, recognising that

[012-111] this was the way young people talked, even then.

[012-112] "Used to?" she repeated. "Don't you now?"

[012-113] "No; I'm a great deal more tractable now. I always say that I shall get

[012-114] the book out of the library. I draw the line at buying. I still hate to

[012-115] buy a book that people recommend."

[012-116] "What kind of books do you like to buy?"

[012-117] "Oh, no kind. I think we ought to get all our books out of the library."

[012-118] "Do you never like to talk in earnest?"

[012-119] "Well, not often," said Colville. "Because, if you do, you can't say

[012-120] with a good conscience afterward that you were only in fun."

[012-121] "Oh! And do you always like to talk so that you can get out of things

[012-122] afterward?"

[012-123] "No. I didn't say that, did I?"

[012-124] "Very nearly, I should think."

[012-125] "Then I'm glad I didn't quite."

[012-126] "I like people to be outspoken--to say everything they think," said the

[012-127] girl, regarding him with a puzzled look.

[012-128] "Then I foresee that I shall become a favourite," answered Colville. "I

[012-129] say a great deal more than I think."

[012-130] She looked at him again with envy, with admiration, qualifying her

[012-131] perplexity. They had come to a point where some moss-grown,

[012-132] weather-beaten statues stood at the corners of the road that traversed

[012-133] the bosky stretch between the avenues of the [[cascine|Cascine]]. "Ah, how beautiful

[012-134] they are!" he said, halting, and giving himself to the rapture that a

[012-135] blackened garden statue imparts to one who beholds it from the

[012-136] vantage-ground of sufficient years and experience.

[012-137] "Do you remember that story of Heine's," he resumed, after a moment, "of

[012-138] the boy who steals out of the old castle by moonlight, and kisses the

[012-139] lips of the garden statue, fallen among the rank grass of the ruinous

[012-140] parterres? And long afterward, when he looks down on the sleep of the

[012-141] dying girl where she lies on the green sofa, it seems to him that she

[012-142] and that statue are the same?"

[012-143] "Oh!" deeply sighed the young girl. "No, I never read it. Tell me what

[012-144] it is. I _must_ read it."

[012-145] "The rest is all talk--very good talk; but I doubt whether it would

[012-146] interest you. He goes on to talk of a great many things---of the way

[012-147] Bellini spoke French, for example. He says it was bloodcurdling,

[012-148] horrible, cataclysmal. He brought out the poor French words and broke

[012-149] them upon the wheel, till you thought the whole world must give way with

[012-150] a thunder-crash. A dead hush reigned in the room; the women did not know

[012-151] whether to faint or fly; the men looked down at their pantaloons, and

[012-152] tried to realise what they had on."

[012-153] "Oh, how perfectly delightful! how shameful!" cried the girl. "I _must_

[012-154] read it. What is it in? What is the name of the story?"

[012-155] "It isn't a story," said Colville. "Did you ever see anything lovelier

[012-156] than these statues?"

[012-157] "No," said Imogene. "_Are_ they good?"

[012-158] "They are much better than good--they are the very worst rococo."

[012-159] "What makes you say they are beautiful, then?"

[012-160] "Why, don't you see? They commemorate youth, gaiety, brilliant, joyous

[012-161] life. That's what that kind of statues was made for--to look on at rich,

[012-162] young, beautiful people and their gallantries; to be danced before by

[012-163] fine ladies and gentlemen playing at shepherd and shepherdesses; to be

[012-164] driven past by marcheses and contessinas flirting in carriages; to be

[012-165] hung with scarfs and wreaths; to be parts of eternal _fétes champêtres_.

[012-166] Don't you see how bored they look? When I first came to Italy I should

[012-167] have detested and ridiculed their bad art; but now they're

[012-168] exquisite--the worse, the better."

[012-169] "I don't know what in the world you _do_ mean," said Imogene, laughing

[012-170] uneasily.

[012-171] "Mrs. Bowen would. It's a pity Mrs. Bowen isn't here with us. Miss

[012-172] Effie, if I lift you up to one of those statues, will you kindly ask it

[012-173] if it doesn't remember a young American signor who was here just before

[012-174] the French Revolution? I don't believe it's forgotten me."

[012-175] "No, no," said Imogene. "It's time we were walking back. Don't you like

[012-176] Scott!" she added. "I should think you would if you like those romantic

[012-177] things. I used to like Scott so _much_. When I was fifteen I wouldn't

[012-178] read anything but Scott. Don't you like Thackeray? Oh, he's so

[012-179] _cynical_! It's perfectly delightful."

[012-180] "Cynical?" repeated Colville thoughtfully. "I was looking into _The

[012-181] Newcomes_ the other day, and I thought he was rather sentimental."

[012-182] "Sentimental! Why, what an idea! That is the strangest thing I ever

[012-183] heard of. Oh!" she broke in upon her own amazement, "don't you think

[012-184] Browning's 'Statue and the Bust' is splendid? [[mr-morton|Mr. Morton]] read it to

[012-185] us--to Mrs. Bowen, I mean."

[012-186] Colville resented this freedom of Mr. Morton's, he did not know just

[012-187] why; then his pique was lost in sarcastic recollection of the time when

[012-188] he too used to read poems to ladies. He had read that poem to Lina

[012-189] Ridgely and the other one.

[012-190] "Mrs. Bowen asked him to read it," Imogene continued.

[012-191] "Did she?" asked Colville pensively.

[012-192] "And then we discussed it afterward. We had a long discussion. And then

[012-193] he read us the 'Legend of Pornic,' and we had a discussion about that.

[012-194] Mrs. Bowen says it was real gold they found in the coffin; but I think

[012-195] it was the girl's 'gold hair.' I don't know which Mr. Morton thought.

[012-196] Which do you? Don't you think the 'Legend of Pornic' is splendid?"
