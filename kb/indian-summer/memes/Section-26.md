[026-001] "It's been a long day," [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]] admitted. She did not ask [[imogene-graham|Imogene]] why

[026-002] she could not sleep, perhaps because she knew already, and was too

[026-003] honest to affect ignorance.

[026-004] The girl dropped into a chair opposite her, and began to pull her

[026-005] fingers through the long tangle of her hair, while she drew her breath

[026-006] in sighs that broke at times on her lips; some tears fell down her

[026-007] cheeks unheeded. "Mrs. Bowen," she said, at length, "I should like to

[026-008] know what right we have to drive any one from [[florence|Florence]]? I should think

[026-009] people would call it rather a high-handed proceeding if it were known."

[026-010] Mrs. Bowen met this feebleness promptly. "It isn't likely to be known.

[026-011] But we are not driving Mr. [[theodore-colville|Colville]] away."

[026-012] "He is going."

[026-013] "Yes; he said he would go."

[026-014] "Don't you believe he will go?"

[026-015] "I believe he will do what he says."

[026-016] "He has been very kind to us all; he has been as _good!_"

[026-017] "No one feels that more than I," said Mrs. Bowen, with a slight tremor

[026-018] in her voice. She faltered a moment. "I can't let you say those things

[026-019] to me, Imogene."

[026-020] "No; I know it's wrong. I didn't know what I was saying. Oh, I wish I

[026-021] could tell what I ought to do! I wish I could make up my mind. Oh, I

[026-022] can't let him go--_so_. I--I don't know what to think any more. Once it

[026-023] was clear, but now I'm not sure; no, I'm not sure."

[026-024] "Not sure about what?"

[026-025] "I think I am the one to go away, if any one."

[026-026] "You know you can't go away," said Mrs. Bowen, with weary patience.

[026-027] "No, of course not. Well, I shall never see any one like him."

[026-028] Mrs. Bowen made a start in her chair, as if she had no longer the power

[026-029] to remain quiet, but only placed herself a little more rigidly in it.

[026-030] "No," the girl went on, as if uttering a hopeless reverie. "He made

[026-031] every moment interesting. He was always thinking of us--he never thought

[026-032] of himself. He did as much for Effie as for any one; he tried just as

[026-033] hard to make himself interesting to her. He was unselfish. I have seen

[026-034] him at places being kind to the stupidest people. You never caught him

[026-035] choosing out the stylish or attractive ones, or trying to shine at

[026-036] anybody's expense. Oh, he's a true gentleman--I shall always say it. How

[026-037] delicate he was, never catching you up, or if you said a foolish thing,

[026-038] trying to turn it against you. No, never, never, never! Oh dear! And

[026-039] now, what can he think of me? Oh, how frivolous and fickle and selfish

[026-040] he must think me!"

[026-041] "Imogene!" Mrs. Bowen cried out, but quelled herself again.

[026-042] "Yes," pursued the girl, in the same dreary monotone, "he thinks I

[026-043] couldn't appreciate him because he was old. He thinks that I cared for

[026-044] his not being handsome! Perhaps--perhaps----" She began to catch her

[026-045] breath in the effort to keep back the sobs that were coming. "Oh, I

[026-046] can't bear it! I would rather die than let him think it--such a thing as

[026-047] that!" She bent her head aside, and cried upon the two hands with which

[026-048] she clutched the top of her chair.

[026-049] Mrs. Bowen sat looking at her distractedly. From time to time she seemed

[026-050] to silence a word upon her lips, and in fact she did not speak.

[026-051] Imogene lifted her head at last, and softly dried her eyes. Then, as she

[026-052] pushed her handkerchief back into the pocket of her robe, "What sort of

[026-053] looking girl was that other one?"

[026-054] "That other one?"

[026-055] "Yes; you know what I mean: the one who behaved so badly to him before."

[026-056] "Imogene!" said Mrs. Bowen severely, "this is nonsense, and I can't let

[026-057] you go on so. I might pretend not to know what you mean; but I won't do

[026-058] that; and I tell you that there is no sort of likeness--of

[026-059] comparison----"

[026-060] "No, no," wailed the girl, "there _is_ none. I feel that. She had

[026-061] nothing to warn her--he hadn't suffered then; he was young; he was able

[026-062] to bear it--you said it yourself, Mrs. Bowen. But now--_now_, what will

[026-063] he do? He could make fun of that, and not hate her so much, because she

[026-064] didn't know how much harm she was doing. But I did; and what can he

[026-065] think of me?"

[026-066] Mrs. Bowen looked across the barrier between them, that kept her from

[026-067] taking Imogene into her arms, and laughing and kissing away her craze,

[026-068] with cold dislike, and only said, "You know whether you've really

[026-069] anything to accuse yourself of, Imogene. I can't and won't consider Mr.

[026-070] Colville in the matter; I _didn't_ consider him in what I said to-day.

[026-071] And I tell you again that I will not interfere with you in the slightest

[026-072] degree beyond appearances and the [[social-duty|responsibility]] I feel to your mother.

[026-073] And it's for you to know your own mind. You are old enough. I will do

[026-074] what you say. It's for you to be sure that you wish what you say."

[026-075] "Yes," said Imogene huskily, and she let an interval that was long to

[026-076] them both elapse before she said anything more. "Have I always done what

[026-077] you thought best, Mrs. Bowen?"

[026-078] "Yes, I have never complained of you."

[026-079] "Then why can't you tell me now what you think best?"

[026-080] "Because there is nothing to be done. It is all over."

[026-081] "But if it were not, would you tell me?"

[026-082] "No."

[026-083] "Why?"

[026-084] "Because I--couldn't."

[026-085] "Then I take back my promise not to write to Mr. Colville. I am going to

[026-086] ask him to stay."

[026-087] "Have you made up your mind to that, Imogene?" asked Mrs. Bowen, showing

[026-088] no sign of excitement, except to take a faster hold of her own wrists

[026-089] with the slim hands in which she had caught them.

[026-090] "Yes."

[026-091] "You know the position it places you in?"

[026-092] "What position?"

[026-093] "Has he offered himself to you?"

[026-094] "No!" the girl's face blazed.

[026-095] "Then, after what's passed, this is the same as offering yourself to

[026-096] him."

[026-097] Imogene turned white. "I must write to him, unless you forbid me."

[026-098] "Certainly I shall not forbid you." Mrs. Bowen rose and went to her

[026-099] writing-desk. "But if you have fully made up your mind to this step, and

[026-100] are ready for the consequences, whatever they are----" She stopped,

[026-101] before sitting down, and looked back over her shoulder at Imogene.

[026-102] "Yes," said the girl, who had also risen.

[026-103] "Then I will write to Mr. Colville for you, and render the proceeding as

[026-104] little objectionable as possible."

[026-105] Imogene made no reply. She stood motionless while Mrs. Bowen wrote.

[026-106] "Is this what you wished?" asked the latter, offering the sheet:----

[026-107] "Dear Mr. Colville,--I have reasons for wishing to recall my consent to

[026-108] your going away. Will you not come and lunch with us to-morrow, and try

[026-109] to forget everything that has passed during a few days?

[026-110] "Yours very sincerely,

[026-111] "Evalina Bowen."

[026-112] "Yes, that will do," gasped Imogene.

[026-113] Mrs. Bowen rang the bell for the porter, and stood with her back to the

[026-114] girl, waiting for him at the salon door. He came after a delay that

[026-115] sufficiently intimated the lateness of the hour. "This letter must go at

[026-116] once to the [[hotel-datene|Hotel d'Atene]]," said Mrs. Bowen peremptorily.

[026-117] "You shall be served," said the porter, with fortitude.

[026-118] As Mrs. Bowen turned, Imogene ran toward her with clasped hands. "Oh,

[026-119] how merciful--how good----"

[026-120] Mrs. Bowen shrank back. "Don't touch me, Imogene, please!"

[026-121] It was her letter which Colville found on his table and read by the

[026-122] struggling light of his newly acquired candle. Then he sat down and

[026-123] replied to it.

[026-124] "Dear Mrs. Bowen,--I know that you mean some sort of kindness by me, and

[026-125] I hope you will not think me prompted by any poor resentment in

[026-126] declining to-morrow's lunch. I am satisfied that it is best for me to

[026-127] go; and I am ashamed not to be gone already. But a ridiculous accident

[026-128] has kept me, and when I came in and found your note I was just going to

[026-129] write and ask your patience with my presence in Florence till Monday

[026-130] morning.

[026-131] "Yours sincerely, THEODORE COLVILLE."

[026-132] He took his note down to the porter, who had lain down again in his

[026-133] little booth, but sprang up with a cheerful request to be commanded.

[026-134] Colville consulted him upon the propriety of sending the note to Palazzo

[026-135] Pinti at once, and the porter, with his head laid in deprecation upon

[026-136] one of his lifted shoulders, owned that it was perhaps the very least

[026-137] little bit in the world late.

[026-138] "Send it the first thing in the morning, then," said Colville.

[026-139] Mrs. Bowen received it by the servant who brought her coffee to the

[026-140] room, and she sent it without any word to Imogene. The girl came

[026-141] instantly back with it. She was fully dressed, as if she had been up a

[026-142] long time, and she wore a very plain, dull dress, in which one of her

[026-143] own sex might have read the expression of a potential self-devotion.

[026-144] "It's just as I wish it, Mrs. Bowen," she said, in a low key of

[026-145] impassioned resolution. "_Now_, my conscience is at rest. And you have

[026-146] done this for me, Mrs. Bowen!" She stood timidly with the door in her

[026-147] hand, watching Mrs. Bowen's slight smile; then, as if at some sign in

[026-148] it, she flew to the bed and kissed her, and so fled out of the room

[026-149] again.

[026-150] Colville slept late, and awoke with a vague sense of self-reproach,

[026-151] which faded afterward to such poor satisfaction as comes to us from the

[026-152] consciousness of having made the best of a bad business; some pangs of

[026-153] softer regret mixed with this. At first he felt a stupid obligation to

[026-154] keep indoors, and he really did not go out till after lunch. The

[026-155] sunshine had looked cold from his window, and with the bright fire which

[026-156] he found necessary in his room, he fancied a bitterness in the gusts

[026-157] that caught up the dust in the piazza, and blew it against the line of

[026-158] cabs on the other side; but when he got out into the weather he found

[026-159] the breeze mild and the sun warm. The streets were thronged with people,

[026-160] and at all the corners there were groups of cloaked and overcoated

[026-161] talkers, soaking themselves full of the sunshine. The air throbbed, as

[026-162] always, with the sound of bells, but it was a mellower and opener sound

[026-163] than before, and looking at the purple bulk of one of those hills which

[026-164] seem to rest like clouds at the end of each avenue in Florence, Colville

[026-165] saw that it was clear of snow. He was going up through Via Cavour to

[026-166] find [[mr-waters|Mr. Waters]] and propose a walk, but he met him before he had got

[026-167] half-way to San Marco.

[026-168] The old man was at a momentary stand-still, looking up at the Riccardi

[026-169] Palace, and he received Colville with apparent forgetfulness of anything

[026-170] odd in his being still in Florence. "Upon the whole," he said, without

[026-171] preliminary of any sort, as Colville turned and joined him in walking

[026-172] on, "I don't know any homicide that more distinctly proves the futility

[026-173] of assassination as a political measure than that over yonder." He

[026-174] nodded his head sidewise toward the palace as he shuffled actively along

[026-175] at Colville's elbow.

[026-176] "You might say that the moment when Lorenzino killed Alessandro was the

[026-177] most auspicious for a deed of that kind. The Medici had only recently

[026-178] been restored; Alessandro was the first ruler in Florence, who had worn

[026-179] a title; no more reckless, brutal, and insolent tyrant ever lived, and

[026-180] his right, even such as the Medici might have, to play the despot was

[026-181] involved in the doubt of his origin; the heroism of the great siege

[026-182] ought still to have survived in the people who withstood the forces of

[026-183] the whole German Empire for fifteen months; it seems as if the taking

[026-184] off of that single wretch should have ended the whole Medicean

[026-185] domination; but there was not a voice raised to second the homicide's

[026-186] appeal to the old love of liberty in Florence. The Medici party were

[026-187] able to impose a boy of eighteen upon the most fiery democracy that ever

[026-188] existed, and to hunt down and destroy Alessandro's murderer at their

[026-189] leisure. No," added the old man thoughtfully, "I think that the friends

[026-190] of progress must abandon assassination as invariably useless. The

[026-191] trouble was not that Alessandro was alive, but that Florence was dead.

[026-192] Assassination always comes too early or too late in any popular

[026-193] movement. It may be," said Mr. Waters, with a carefulness to do justice

[026-194] to assassination which made Colville smile, "that the modern scientific

[026-195] spirits may be able to evolve something useful from the principle, but

[026-196] considering the enormous abuses and perversions to which it is liable, I

[026-197] am very doubtful of it--very doubtful."
