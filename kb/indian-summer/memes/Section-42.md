[042-001] The young painters had their jokes about it; even [[mr-morton|Mr. Morton]] smiled, and

[042-002] [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]] recognised it. But [[imogene-graham|Imogene]] did not smile; she regarded the

[042-003] lovers with an interest in them scarcely less intense than their

[042-004] interest in each other; and a cold perspiration of question broke out on

[042-005] [[theodore-colville|Colville]]'s forehead. Was that her ideal of what her own engagement

[042-006] should be? Had she expected him to behave in that way to her, and to

[042-007] accept from her a devotion like that girl's? How bitterly he must have

[042-008] disappointed her! It was so impossible to him that the thought of it

[042-009] made him feel that he must break all ties which bound him to anything

[042-010] like it. And yet he reflected that the time was when he could have been

[042-011] equal to that, and even more.

[042-012] After lunch the painters joined them again, and they all went together

[042-013] to visit the ruins of the Roman theatre and the stretch of Etruscan wall

[042-014] beyond it. The former seems older than the latter, whose huge blocks of

[042-015] stone lie as firmly and evenly in their courses as if placed there a

[042-016] year ago; the turf creeps to the edge at top, and some small trees nod

[042-017] along the crest of the wall, whose ancient face, clean and bare, looks

[042-018] sternly out over a vast prospect, now young and smiling in the first

[042-019] delight of spring. The piety or interest of the community, which guards

[042-020] the entrance to the theatre by a fee of certain centesimi, may be

[042-021] concerned in keeping the wall free from the grass and vines which are

[042-022] stealing the half-excavated arena back to forgetfulness and decay; but

[042-023] whatever agency it was, it weakened the appeal that the wall made to the

[042-024] sympathy of the spectators.

[042-025] They could do nothing with it; the artists did not take their

[042-026] sketch-blocks from their pockets. But in the theatre, where a few broken

[042-027] columns marked the place of the stage, and the stone benches of the

[042-028] auditorium were here and there reached by a flight of uncovered steps,

[042-029] the human interest returned.

[042-030] "I suspect that there is such a thing as a ruin's being too old," said

[042-031] Colville. "Our Etruscan friends made the mistake of building their wall

[042-032] several thousand years too soon for our purpose."

[042-033] "Yes," consented the young clergyman. "It seems as if our own race

[042-034] became alienated from us through the mere effect of time, don't you

[042-035] think, sir? I mean, of course, terrestrially."

[042-036] The artists looked uneasy, as if they had not counted upon anything of

[042-037] this kind, and they began to scatter about for points of view. Effie got

[042-038] her mother's leave to run up and down one of the stairways, if she would

[042-039] not fall. Mrs. Bowen sat down on one of the lower steps, and Mr. Morton

[042-040] took his place respectfully near her.

[042-041] "I wonder how it looks from the top?" Imogene asked this of Colville,

[042-042] with more meaning than seemed to belong to the question properly.

[042-043] "There is nothing like going to see," he suggested. He helped her up,

[042-044] giving her his hand from one course of seats to another. When they

[042-045] reached the point which commanded the best view of the whole, she sat

[042-046] down, and he sank at her feet, but they did not speak of the view.

[042-047] "Theodore, I want to tell you something," she said abruptly. "I have

[042-048] heard from home."

[042-049] "Yes?" he replied, in a tone in which he did his best to express a

[042-050] readiness for any fate.

[042-051] "Mother has telegraphed. She is coming out. She is on her way now. She

[042-052] will be here very soon."

[042-053] Colville did not know exactly what to say to these passionately

[042-054] consecutive statements. "Well?" he said at last.

[042-055] "Well"--she repeated his word--"what do you intend to do?"

[042-056] "Intend to do in what event?" he asked, lifting his eyes for the first

[042-057] time to the eyes which he felt burning down upon him.

[042-058] "If she should refuse?"

[042-059] Again he could not command an instant answer, but when it came it was a

[042-060] fair one. "It isn't for me to say what I shall do," he replied gravely.

[042-061] "Or, if it is, I can only say that I will do whatever you wish."

[042-062] "Do _you_ wish nothing?"

[042-063] "Nothing but your happiness."

[042-064] "Nothing but my happiness!" she retorted. "What is my happiness to me?

[042-065] Have I ever sought it?"

[042-066] "I can't say," he answered; "but if I did not think you would find it--"

[042-067] "I shall find it, if ever I find it, in yours," she interrupted. "And

[042-068] what shall you do if my mother will not consent to our engagement?"

[042-069] The experienced and sophisticated man--for that in no ill way was what

[042-070] Colville was--felt himself on trial for his honour and his manhood by

[042-071] this simple girl, this child. He could not endure to fall short of her

[042-072] ideal of him at that moment, no matter what error or calamity the

[042-073] fulfilment involved. "If you feel sure that you love me, Imogene, it

[042-074] will make no difference to me what your mother says. I would be glad of

[042-075] her consent; I should hate to go counter to her will; but I know that I

[042-076] am good enough man to be true and keep you all my life the first in all

[042-077] my thoughts, and that's enough for me. But if you have any fear, any

[042-078] doubt of yourself, now is the time--"

[042-079] Imogene rose to her feet as in some turmoil of thought or emotion that

[042-080] would not suffer her to remain quiet.

[042-081] "Oh, keep still!" "Don't get up yet!" "Hold on a minute, please!" came

[042-082] from the artists in different parts of the theatre, and half a dozen

[042-083] imploring pencils were waved in the air.

[042-084] "They are sketching you," said Colville, and she sank compliantly into

[042-085] her seat again.

[042-086] "I have no doubt for myself--no," she said, as if there had been no

[042-087] interruption.

[042-088] "Then we need have no anxiety in meeting your mother," said Colville,

[042-089] with a light sigh, after a moment's pause. "What makes you think she

[042-090] will be unfavourable?"

[042-091] "I don't think that; but I thought--I didn't know but--"

[042-092] "What?"

[042-093] "Nothing, now." Her lips were quivering; he could see her struggle for

[042-094] self-control, but he could not see it unmoved.

[042-095] "Poor child!" he said, putting out his hand toward her.

[042-096] "Don't take my hand; they're all looking," she begged.

[042-097] He forbore, and they remained silent and motionless a little while,

[042-098] before she had recovered herself sufficiently to speak again.

[042-099] "Then we are promised to each other, whatever happens," she said.

[042-100] "Yes."

[042-101] "And we will never speak of this again. But there is one thing. Did Mrs.

[042-102] Bowen ask you to tell Mr. Morton of our engagement?"

[042-103] "She said that I ought to do so."

[042-104] "And did you say you would?"

[042-105] "I don't know. But I suppose I ought to tell him."

[042-106] "I don't wish you to!" cried the girl.

[042-107] "You don't wish me to tell him?"

[042-108] "No; I will not have it!"

[042-109] "Oh, very well; it's much easier not. But it seems to me that it's only

[042-110] fair to him."

[042-111] "Did you think of that yourself?" she demanded fiercely.

[042-112] "No," returned Colville, with sad self-recognition. "I'm afraid I'm not

[042-113] apt to think of the comforts and rights of other people. It was Mrs.

[042-114] Bowen who thought of it."

[042-115] "I knew it!"

[042-116] "But I must confess that I agreed with her, though I would have

[042-117] preferred to postpone it till we heard from your family." He was

[042-118] thoughtfully silent a moment; then he said, "But if their decision is to

[042-119] have no weight with us, I think he ought to be told at once."

[042-120] "Do you think that I am flirting with him?"

[042-121] "Imogene!" exclaimed Colville reproachfully.

[042-122] "That's what you imply; that's what she implies."

[042-123] "You're very unjust to Mrs. Bowen, Imogene."

[042-124] "Oh, you always defend her! It isn't the first time you've told me I was

[042-125] unjust to her."

[042-126] "I don't mean that you are willingly unjust, or could be so, to any

[042-127] living creature, least of all to her. But I--we--owe her so much; she

[042-128] has been so patient."

[042-129] "What do we owe her? How has she been patient?"

[042-130] "She has overcome her dislike to me."

[042-131] "Oh, indeed!"

[042-132] "And--and I feel under obligation to her for--in a thousand little ways;

[042-133] and I should be glad to feel that we were acting with her approval; I

[042-134] should like to please her."

[042-135] "You wish to tell Mr. Morton?"

[042-136] "I think I ought."

[042-137] "To please Mrs. Bowen! Tell him, then! You always cared more to please

[042-138] her than me. Perhaps you stayed in [[florence|Florence]] to please her!"

[042-139] She rose and ran down the broken seats and ruined steps so recklessly

[042-140] and yet so sure-footedly that it seemed more like a flight than a pace

[042-141] to the place where Mrs. Bowen and Mr. Morton were talking together.

[042-142] Colville followed as he could, slowly and with a heavy heart. A good

[042-143] thing develops itself in infinite and unexpected shapes of good; a bad

[042-144] thing into manifold and astounding evils. This mistake was whirling away

[042-145] beyond his recall in hopeless mazes of error. He saw this generous young

[042-146] spirit betrayed by it to ignoble and unworthy excess, and he knew that

[042-147] he and not she was to blame.

[042-148] He was helpless to approach her, to speak with her, to set her right,

[042-149] great as the need of that was, and he could see that she avoided him.

[042-150] But their relations remained outwardly undisturbed. The artists brought

[042-151] their sketches for inspection and comment, and, without speaking to each

[042-152] other, he and Imogene discussed them with the rest.

[042-153] When they started homeward the painters said they were coming a little

[042-154] way with them for a send-off, and then going back to spend the night in

[042-155] [[fiesole|Fiesole]]. They walked beside the carriage, talking with Mrs. Bowen and

[042-156] Imogene, who had taken their places, with Effie between them, on the

[042-157] back seat; and when they took their leave, Colville and the young

[042-158] clergyman, who had politely walked with them, continued on foot a little

[042-159] further, till they came to the place where the highway to Florence

[042-160] divided into the new road and the old. At this point it steeply overtops

[042-161] the fields on one side, which is shored up by a wall some ten or twelve

[042-162] feet deep; and here round a sharp turn of the hill on the other side

[042-163] came a peasant driving a herd of the black pigs of the country.

[042-164] Mrs. Bowen's horses were, perhaps, pampered beyond the habitual

[042-165] resignation of Florentine horses to all manner of natural phenomena;

[042-166] they reared at sight of the sable crew, and backing violently uphill,

[042-167] set the carriage across the road, with its hind wheels a few feet from

[042-168] the brink of the wall. The coachman sprang from his seat, the ladies and

[042-169] the child remained in theirs as if paralysed.

[042-170] Colville ran forward to the side of the carriage. "Jump, Mrs. Bowen!

[042-171] jump, Effie! Imogene--"

[042-172] The mother and the little one obeyed. He caught them in his arms and set

[042-173] them down. The girl sat still, staring at him with reproachful, with

[042-174] disdainful eyes.

[042-175] He leaped forward to drag her out; she shrank away, and then he flew to

[042-176] help the coachman, who had the maddened horses by the bit.

[042-177] "Let go!" he heard the young clergyman calling to him; "she's safe!" He

[042-178] caught a glimpse of Imogene, whom Mr. Morton had pulled from the other

[042-179] side of the carriage. He struggled to free his wrist from the curb-bit

[042-180] chain of the horse, through which he had plunged it in his attempt to

[042-181] seize the bridle. The wheels of the carriage went over the wall; he felt

[042-182] himself whirled into the air, and then swung ruining down into the

[042-183] writhing and crashing heap at the bottom of the wall.

[042-184] XXI

[042-185] When Colville came to himself his first sensation was delight in the

[042-186] softness and smoothness of the turf on which he lay. Then the strange

[042-187] colour of the grass commended itself to his notice, and presently he

[042-188] perceived that the thing under his head was a pillow, and that he was in

[042-189] bed. He was supported in this conclusion by the opinion of the young man

[042-190] who sat watching him a little way off, and who now smiled cheerfully at

[042-191] the expression in the eyes which Colville turned inquiringly upon him.
