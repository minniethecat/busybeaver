[022-001] "Ah, but if you become personally interested in the problems, it's as

[022-002] bad as if you hadn't the materials of judgment left; you're prejudiced.

[022-003] Besides, I doubt my youthfulness very much."

[022-004] "You are fifty, I presume?" suggested [[mr-waters|Mr. Waters]], in a leading way.

[022-005] "Not very near--only too near," laughed [[theodore-colville|Colville]]. "I'm forty-one."

[022-006] "You are younger than I supposed. But I remember now that at your age I

[022-007] had the same feeling which you intimate. It seemed to me then that I had

[022-008] really passed the bound which separates us from the further possibility

[022-009] of youth. But I've lived long enough since to know that I was mistaken.

[022-010] At forty, one has still a great part of youth before him--perhaps the

[022-011] richest and sweetest part. By that time the turmoil of ideas and

[022-012] sensations is over; we see clearly and feel consciously. We are in a

[022-013] sort of quiet in which we peacefully enjoy. We have enlarged our

[022-014] perspective sufficiently to perceive things in their true proportion and

[022-015] relation; we are no longer tormented with the lurking fear of death,

[022-016] which darkens and embitters our earlier years; we have got into the

[022-017] habit of life; we have often been ailing and we have not died. Then we

[022-018] have time enough behind us to supply us with the materials of reverie

[022-019] and reminiscence; the terrible solitude of inexperience is broken; we

[022-020] have learned to smile at many things besides the fear of death. We ought

[022-021] also to have learned pity and patience. Yes," the old man concluded, in

[022-022] cheerful self-corroboration, "it is a beautiful age."

[022-023] "But it doesn't look so beautiful as it is," Colville protested. "People

[022-024] in that rosy prime don't produce the effect of garlanded striplings upon

[022-025] the world at large. The women laugh at us; they think we are fat old

[022-026] fellows; they don't recognise the slender and elegant youth that resides

[022-027] in our unwieldy bulk."

[022-028] "You take my meaning a little awry. Besides, I doubt if even the ground

[022-029] you assume is tenable. If a woman has lived long enough to be truly

[022-030] young herself, she won't find a man at forty either decrepit or

[022-031] grotesque. He can even make himself youthful to a girl of thought and

[022-032] imagination."

[022-033] "Yes," Colville assented, with a certain discomfort.

[022-034] "But to be truly young at forty," resumed Mr. Waters, "a man should be

[022-035] already married."

[022-036] "Yes?"

[022-037] "I sometimes feel," continued the old man, "that I made a mistake in

[022-038] yielding to a disappointment that I met with early in life, and in not

[022-039] permitting myself the chance of retrieval. I have missed a beautiful and

[022-040] consoling experience in my devotion to a barren regret."

[022-041] Colville said nothing, but he experienced a mixed feeling of amusement,

[022-042] of repulsion, and of curiosity at this.

[022-043] "We are put into the world to be of it. I am more and more convinced of

[022-044] that. We have scarcely a right to separate ourselves from the common lot

[022-045] in any way. I justify myself for having lived alone only as a widower

[022-046] might. I--lost her. It was a great while ago."

[022-047] "Yes," said Colville, after the pause which ensued; "I agree with you

[022-048] that one has no right to isolate himself, to refuse his portion of the

[022-049] common lot; but the effects of even a rebuff may last so long that one

[022-050] has no heart to put out his hand a second time--for a second rap over

[022-051] the knuckles. Oh, I know how trivial it is in the retrospect, and how

[022-052] what is called a disappointment is something to be humbly grateful for

[022-053] in most cases; but for a while it certainly makes you doubtful whether

[022-054] you were ever really intended to share the common lot." He was aware of

[022-055] an insincerity in his words; he hoped that it might not be perceptible,

[022-056] but he did not greatly care.

[022-057] Mr. Waters took no notice of what he had been saying. He resumed from

[022-058] another point. "But I should say that it would be unwise for a man of

[022-059] mature life to seek his happiness with one much younger than himself. I

[022-060] don't deny that there are cases in which the disparity of years counts

[022-061] for little or nothing, but generally speaking, people ought to be as

[022-062] equally mated in age as possible. They ought to start with the same

[022-063] advantages of ignorance. A young girl can only live her life through a

[022-064] community of feeling, an equality of inexperience in the man she gives

[022-065] her heart to. If he is tired of things that still delight her, the

[022-066] chances of unhappiness are increased."

[022-067] "Yes, that's true," answered Colville gravely. "It's apt to be a mistake

[022-068] and a wrong."

[022-069] "Oh, not always--not always," said the old minister. "We mustn't look at

[022-070] it in that way quite. Wrongs are of the will." He seemed to lapse into a

[022-071] greater intimacy of feeling with Colville. "Have you seen [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]]

[022-072] to-day? Or--ah! true! I think you told me."

[022-073] "No," said Colville. "Have we spoken of her? But I have seen her."

[022-074] "And was the little one well?"

[022-075] "Very much better."

[022-076] "Pretty creatures, both of them," said the minister, with as fresh a

[022-077] pleasure in his recognition of the fact as if he had not said nearly the

[022-078] same thing once before, "You've noticed the very remarkable resemblance

[022-079] between mother and daughter?"

[022-080] "Oh yes."

[022-081] "There is a gentleness in Mrs. Bowen which seems to me the last

[022-082] refinement of a gracious spirit," suggested Mr. Waters. "I have never

[022-083] met any lady who reconciled more exquisitely what is charming in society

[022-084] with what is lovely in nature."

[022-085] "Yes," said Colville. "Mrs. Bowen always had that gentle manner. I used

[022-086] to know her here as a girl a great while ago."

[022-087] "Did you? I wonder you allowed her to become Mrs. Bowen."

[022-088] This sprightliness of Mr. Waters amused Colville greatly. "At that time

[022-089] I was preoccupied with my great mistake, and I had no eyes for Mrs.

[022-090] Bowen."

[022-091] "It isn't too late yet," said Mr. Waters, with open insinuation.

[022-092] A bachelor of forty is always flattered by any suggestion of marriage;

[022-093] the suggestion that a beautiful and charming woman would marry him is

[022-094] too much for whatever reserves of modesty and wisdom he may have stored

[022-095] up Colville took leave of the old minister in better humour with himself

[022-096] than he had been for forty-eight hours, or than he had any very good

[022-097] reason for being now.

[022-098] Mr. Waters came with him to the head of the stairs and held up the lamp

[022-099] for him to see. The light fell upon the white locks thinly straggling

[022-100] from beneath his velvet skull-cap, and he looked like some mediaeval

[022-101] scholar of those who lived and died for learning in [[florence|Florence]] when

[022-102] letters were a passion there almost as strong as love.

[022-103] The next day Colville would have liked to go at once and ask about

[022-104] Effie, but upon the whole he thought he would not go till after he had

[022-105] been at the reception where he was going in the afternoon. It was an

[022-106] artist who was giving the reception; he had a number of pictures to

[022-107] show, and there was to be tea. There are artists and artists. This

[022-108] painter was one who had a distinct social importance. It was felt to be

[022-109] rather a nice thing to be asked to his reception; one was sure at least

[022-110] to meet the nicest people.

[022-111] This reason prevailed with Colville so far as it related to Mrs. Bowen,

[022-112] whom he felt that he would like to tell he had been there. He would

[022-113] speak to her of this person and that--very respected and recognised

[022-114] social figures,--so that she might see he was not the outlaw, the

[022-115] Bohemian, he must sometimes have appeared to her. It would not be going

[022-116] too far to say that something like an obscure intention to show himself

[022-117] the next Sunday at the English chapel, where Mrs. Bowen went, was not

[022-118] forming itself in his mind. As he went along it began to seem not

[022-119] impossible that she would be at the reception. If [[effies-illness|Effie's indisposition]]

[022-120] was no more serious than it appeared yesterday, very probably Mrs. Bowen

[022-121] would be there. He even believed that he recognised her carriage among

[022-122] those which were drawn up in front of the old palace, under the

[022-123] painter's studio windows.

[022-124] There were a great number of people of the four nationalities that

[022-125] mostly consort in Italy. There were English and Americans and Russians

[022-126] and the sort of Italians resulting from the native intermarriages with

[022-127] them; here and there were Italians of pure blood, borderers upon the

[022-128] foreign life through a literary interest, or an artistic relation, or a

[022-129] matrimonial intention; here and there, also, the large stomach of a

[022-130] German advanced the bounds of the new empire and the new ideal of [[social-duty|duty]].

[022-131] There were no Frenchmen; one may meet them in more strictly Italian

[022-132] assemblages, but it is as if the sorrows and uncertainties of France in

[022-133] these times discouraged them from the [[cosmopolitan-society|international society]] in which

[022-134] they were always an infrequent element. It is not, of course, imaginable

[022-135] that as Frenchmen they have doubts of their merits, but that they have

[022-136] their misgivings as to the intelligence of others. The language that

[022-137] prevailed was English--in fact, one heard no other,--and the tea which

[022-138] our civilisation carries everywhere with it steamed from the cups in all

[022-139] hands. This beverage, in fact, becomes a formidable factor in the life

[022-140] of a Florentine winter. One finds it at all houses, and more or less

[022-141] mechanically drinks it.

[022-142] "I am turning out a terrible tea toper," said Colville, stirring his cup

[022-143] in front of the old lady whom his relations to the ladies at Palazzo

[022-144] Pinti had interested so much. "I don't think I drink less than ten cups

[022-145] a day; seventy cups a week is a low average for me. I'm really beginning

[022-146] to look down at my boots a little anxiously."

[022-147] [[mrs-amsden|Mrs. Amsden]] laughed. She had not been in America for forty years, but

[022-148] she liked the American way of talking better than any other. "Oh, didn't

[022-149] you hear about Inglehart when he was here? He was so good-natured that

[022-150] he used to drink all the tea people offered him, and then the young

[022-151] ladies made tea for him in his studio when they went to look at his

[022-152] pictures. It almost killed him. By the time spring came he trembled so

[022-153] that the brush flew out of his hands when he took it up. He had to hurry

[022-154] off to Venice to save his life. It's just as bad at the Italian houses;

[022-155] they've learned to like tea."

[022-156] "When I was here before, they never offered you anything but coffee,"

[022-157] said Colville. "They took tea for medicine, and there was an old joke

[022-158] that I thought I should die of, I heard it so often about the Italian

[022-159] that said to the English woman when she offered him tea, '_Grazie; sto

[022-160] bene_.'"

[022-161] "Oh, that's all changed now."

[022-162] "Yes; I've seen the tea, and I haven't heard the joke."

[022-163] The flavour of Colville's talk apparently encouraged his companion to

[022-164] believe that he would like to make fun of their host's paintings with

[022-165] her; but whether he liked them, or whether he was principled against

[022-166] that sort of return for hospitality, he chose to reply seriously to some

[022-167] ironical lures she threw out.

[022-168] "Oh, if you're going to be good," she exclaimed, "I shall have nothing

[022-169] more to say to you. Here comes Mr. Thurston; I can make _him_ abuse the

[022-170] pictures. There! You had better go away to a young lady I see alone over

[022-171] yonder, though I don't know what you will do with _one_ alone." She

[022-172] laughed and shook her head in a way that had once been arch and lively,

[022-173] but that was now puckery and infirm--it is affecting to see these things

[022-174] in women--and welcomed the old gentleman who came up and superseded

[022-175] Colville.
