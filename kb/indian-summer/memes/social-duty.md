# Social Duty

## Definition

In *Indian Summer*, social duty functions as the ethical framework through which characters navigate their obligations to one another — particularly in the charged romantic triangle of Theodore Colville, Mrs. Bowen, and Imogene Graham. Social duty in the novel encompasses the expectations of propriety, the obligations owed by a hostess to her young charge, by an older man to a younger woman, and by individuals to the social conventions that govern expatriate Florentine society. It is a concept that binds characters in competing claims, creating the "divided duty" (007-146) that Colville himself identifies as a recurring dilemma. Social duty is not presented simply as external constraint; it becomes internalised, frequently entangled with self-interest, such that characters struggle to distinguish genuine moral obligation from the rationalisations of personal desire. The novel's treatment of social duty is distinctive in its refusal to purify the concept: duty is always alloyed with human frailty, vanity, and longing.

## Context in the Novel

The theme of social duty is most intensely explored through Mrs. Bowen's role as Imogene Graham's guardian in Florence. As a widow serving *in loco parentis* for the young American girl, Mrs. Bowen is bound by duty to protect Imogene — yet this duty is complicated by her own suppressed feelings for Colville. When she tells him, "I am trying to do my duty to Imogene, and when that is ended, all is ended" (032-106), she articulates a position in which duty serves as both moral anchor and emotional defense. Her later assertion — "I know what my duty to her is, and I will fulfil it. No matter how distasteful it is to either of us" (032-125) — reveals social duty as an imperative that overrides personal comfort, yet the very vehemence of the declaration betrays the emotional cost. The duty she performs for Imogene is inseparable from the performance of indifference toward Colville.

Colville's own relationship to social duty is more conflicted. Throughout the novel, he oscillates between recognising his obligations — "I feel that I must in some sort protect her from herself" (032-135) — and evading them through irony and self-deprecation. His sense of duty toward Imogene is shaded by his awareness of the age difference between them and his own uncertain affections. When Imogene attempts to cast their relationship as a restorative sacrifice, offering to give him back his lost youth, Colville recoils: "You shall make no such sacrifice to me. You must put away the fancies that could never be fulfilled" (033-176). Here, social duty manifests as the older man's obligation to refuse a younger woman's self-abnegation — a refusal that is itself a form of sacrifice, since it means relinquishing the consolation she offers. The novel traces how this "obscure sense of duty" (033-055) operates below the level of conscious reasoning, shaping Colville's physical as well as moral posture.

The theme extends beyond the central triangle to the broader expatriate society. Characters are constantly negotiating the "respective duties of chaperone and *protégée*" (023-057), and Colville himself is brought to a moment of clarity when, after receiving Imogene's letter of apology for her accusing words, "his duty was clear; he must go to Mrs. Bowen" (023-160). This crystallisation of duty — the recognition of a clear course of action — is rare in the novel, and even here it is complicated by Colville's awareness that his noble purpose contains "always some admixture, however slight, of less noble motive" (023-160). The novel's treatment of social duty thus resists simple moral clarity; duty is always alloyed with self-interest, with the petty satisfactions of being in the right.

Obligation also figures in the novel's quieter interpersonal moments. Colville feels "a stupid obligation" (026-153) in the wake of his engagement to Imogene, an obligation that burdens without enlightening. Later, Imogene confesses to feeling "under obligation to her" (042-132) — referring to Mrs. Bowen — in a thousand small ways, revealing how social duty creates webs of indebtedness that complicate already difficult relationships. These obligations function less as moral guides than as sources of ambivalence, pulling characters in contradictory directions.

In the novel's climactic scene, the language of social duty reaches its fullest expression. Mrs. Bowen confesses that she failed in her duty to Imogene because she could not act without feeling she was also acting for herself: "I ought to have broken it off; I ought to have been frank with her; it was my duty; but I couldn't without feeling that I was acting for myself too, and I would not submit to that degradation" (046-146). The paradox is devastating: the purity of her moral ideal — that a dutiful act must be perfectly disinterested — paralyses her, preventing her from acting at all. She would rather suffer the guilt of inaction than the "degradation" of mixed motives. Colville's response — his acknowledgment of "how high and pure your ideals of duty are, and how heroically, angelically, you have struggled to fulfil them, broken and borne down by my clumsy and stupid selfishness from the start" (046-179) — transforms social duty from a social convention into a tragic moral ideal, one that Mrs. Bowen has pursued at the cost of her own happiness.

## Key Passages

> "I am trying to do my duty to Imogene, and when that is ended, all is ended. There is no way now for you to please me — as you call it — except to keep her from regretting what she has done." ... "I know what my duty to her is, and I will fulfil it. No matter how distasteful it is to either of us, you must come here as before. The house is as free to you as ever — freer." (032-106 to 032-125)

In this charged exchange between Mrs. Bowen and Colville, duty serves simultaneously as a moral compass and a protective barrier. Mrs. Bowen's insistence on doing her duty to Imogene functions as a way of maintaining distance from Colville while preserving the social forms that their circle expects. The phrase "when that is ended, all is ended" carries a double meaning: the end of her duty will also mark the end of their connection, which she frames as an inevitability rather than a loss. The passage demonstrates how social duty in *Indian Summer* operates as a language for managing emotional proximity — it permits intimacy within prescribed limits while foreclosing the possibility of more.

> ...with whatever obscure sense of duty to her innocent self-abandon, he made a space between them, and reduced her embrace to a clasp of the hand she left with him... "You shall make no such sacrifice to me. You must put away the fancies that could never be fulfilled, or if by some infernal magic they could, would only bring sorrow to you and shame to me. God forbid! And God forgive me, if I have done or said anything to put this in your head!" (033-055 to 033-176)

Colville's "obscure sense of duty" operates here as an impulse toward physical and emotional restraint — an instinct to create distance between himself and Imogene's "innocent self-abandon." The duty is obscure precisely because it is felt rather than reasoned, a product of conscience rather than calculation. His outburst against Imogene's proposed sacrifice reveals the paradox at the heart of social duty in the novel: the refusal of another's sacrifice is itself a form of self-sacrifice, since Colville must reject the very consolation that Imogene offers him. The religious language ("God forbid! And God forgive me") elevates the moment beyond social convention into the register of moral horror.

> But now his duty was clear; he must go to Mrs. Bowen. In the noblest human purpose there is always some admixture, however slight, of less noble motive, and Colville was not without the willingness to see whatever embarrassment she might feel when he showed her the letter, and to invoke her finest tact to aid him in re-assuring the child. (023-160)

This passage captures the novel's characteristic irony about social duty. The clarity of Colville's duty — the recognition that he must involve Mrs. Bowen in resolving the misunderstanding with Imogene — is immediately undercut by the narrator's observation about the admixture of less noble motives. Colville's duty is genuine, but so is his petty satisfaction at the prospect of witnessing Mrs. Bowen's embarrassment. The passage refuses to purify duty of its human impurities, insisting instead on the complex entanglement of obligation and self-interest. Duty, for Howells, is never simply duty — it is always also something else.

> "I ought to have broken it off; I ought to have been frank with her; it was my duty; but I couldn't without feeling that I was acting for myself too, and I would not submit to that degradation. No! I would rather have died." ... "I understand how high and pure your ideals of duty are, and how heroically, angelically, you have struggled to fulfil them, broken and borne down by my clumsy and stupid selfishness from the start." (046-146 to 046-179)

In the novel's emotional climax, Mrs. Bowen's confession and Colville's response elevate social duty to a tragic principle. Mrs. Bowen's paralysis — her inability to act on her duty because she cannot disentangle it from self-interest — is the novel's most acute analysis of the psychology of moral obligation. The threefold repetition of "I ought to have" conveys the weight of accumulated failure, while the defiant "No! I would rather have died" reveals the extremity of her commitment to a standard of pure disinterestedness. Colville's tribute — "how high and pure your ideals of duty are" — acknowledges that duty can be a quality of character even when it fails to produce right action. The passage transforms what might have been merely a social convention into an existential ideal, measured not by success but by the intensity of the struggle.

## Related Concepts

- **Self-Sacrifice** — Closely tied to social duty throughout the novel; characters frame their renunciations as duties owed to others, yet the novel consistently interrogates whether such sacrifices are truly selfless or disguised forms of self-assertion. Imogene's insistence that giving herself to Colville is "self-glorification" rather than self-sacrifice (034-001) exemplifies this ambiguity.
- **Moral Responsibility** — The broader ethical framework within which social duty operates. Colville's recognition that he is "answerable for all this, and that no one else is" (039-183) shifts the locus of duty from social expectation to individual conscience, complicating the novel's treatment of obligation.
- **Middle Age and Youth** — Social duty in *Indian Summer* is inflected by the age difference between Colville and Imogene, which generates specific obligations: the older party's duty to protect the younger from a mistake, even at the cost of the younger's short-term happiness (033-176).
- **Appearance and Reality** — The performance of social duty — maintaining the appearance of propriety — is continually contrasted with the reality of characters' inner lives. Mrs. Bowen's duty to maintain proper social forms coexists with her private emotional turmoil, and the novel explores the cost of sustaining this division.
