[009-001] It interested him to find that some geographical distinctions which are

[009-002] fading at home had quite disappeared in [[florence|Florence]]. When he was there

[009-003] before, people from quite small towns in the East had made pretty Lina

[009-004] Ridgely and her friend feel the disadvantage of having come from the

[009-005] Western side of an imaginary line; he had himself been at the pains

[009-006] always to let people know, at the American watering-places where he

[009-007] spent his vacations, that though presently from [[des-vaches|Des Vaches]], Indiana, he

[009-008] was really born in Rhode Island; but in Florence it was not at all

[009-009] necessary. He found in [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]]'s house people from Denver, Chicago,

[009-010] St. Louis, Boston, New York, and Baltimore, all meeting as of apparently

[009-011] the same civilisation, and whether Mrs. Bowen's own origin was, like

[009-012] that of the Etruscan cities, lost in the mists of antiquity, or whether

[009-013] she had sufficiently atoned for the error of her birth by subsequent

[009-014] residence in the national capital and prolonged sojourn in New York, it

[009-015] seemed certainly not to be remembered against her among her Eastern

[009-016] acquaintance. The time had been when the fact that [[imogene-graham|Miss Graham]] came from

[009-017] Buffalo would have gone far to class her with the animal from which her

[009-018] native city had taken its name; but now it made no difference, unless it

[009-019] was a difference in her favour. The English spoke with the same vague

[009-020] respect of Buffalo and of Philadelphia; and to a family of real

[009-021] Bostonians [[theodore-colville|Colville]] had the courage to say simply that he lived in Des

[009-022] Vaches, and not to seek to palliate the truth in any sort. If he wished

[009-023] to prevaricate at all, it was rather to attribute himself to Mrs.

[009-024] Bowen's city in Ohio.

[009-025] She and Miss Graham called for him with her carriage the next night,

[009-026] when it was time to go to [[madame-uccelli|Madame Uccelli]]'s.

[009-027] "This gives me a very patronised and effeminate feeling," said Colville,

[009-028] getting into the odorous dark of the carriage, and settling himself upon

[009-029] the front seat with a skill inspired by his anxiety not to tear any of

[009-030] the silken spreading white wraps that inundated the whole interior.

[009-031] "Being come for by ladies!" They both gave some nervous joyful laughs,

[009-032] as they found his hand in the obscurity, and left the sense of a gloved

[009-033] pressure upon it. "Is this the way you used to do in Vesprucius, Mrs.

[009-034] Bowen?"

[009-035] "Oh no, indeed!" she answered. "The young gentlemen used to find out

[009-036] whether I was going, and came for me with a hack, and generally, if the

[009-037] weather was good, we walked home."

[009-038] "That's the way we still do in Des Vaches. Sometimes, as a tremendous

[009-039] joke, the ladies come for us in leap-year. How do you go to balls in

[009-040] Buffalo, Miss Graham? Or, no; I withdraw the embarrassing question."

[009-041] Some gleams from the street lamps, as they drove along, struck in

[009-042] through the carriage windows, and flitted over the ladies' faces and

[009-043] were gone again. "Ah! this is very trying. Couldn't you stop him at the

[009-044] next corner, and let me see how radiant you ladies really are? I may be

[009-045] in very great danger; I'd like to know just how much."

[009-046] "It wouldn't be of any use," cried the young girl gaily. "We're all

[009-047] wrapped up, and you couldn't form any idea of us. You must wait, and let

[009-048] us burst upon you when we come out of the dressing-room at Madame

[009-049] Uccelli's."

[009-050] "But then it may be too late," he urged. "Is it very far?"

[009-051] "Yes," said Mrs. Bowen. "It's ridiculously far. It's outside the Roman

[009-052] Gate. I don't see why people live at that distance."

[009-053] "In order to give the friends you bring the more pleasure of your

[009-054] company, Mrs. Bowen."

[009-055] "Ah! that's very well. But you're not logical."

[009-056] "No," said Colville; "you can't be logical and complimentary at the same

[009-057] time. It's too much to ask. How delicious your flowers are!" The ladies

[009-058] each had a bouquet in her hand, which she was holding in addition to her

[009-059] fan, the edges of her cloak, and the skirt of her train.

[009-060] "Yes," said Mrs. Bowen; "we are so much obliged to you for them."

[009-061] "Why, I sent you _no flowers_," said Colville, startled into untimely

[009-062] earnest.

[009-063] "Didn't you?" triumphed Mrs. Bowen. "I thought gentlemen always sent

[009-064] flowers to ladies when they were going to a ball with them. They used

[009-065] to, in Columbus."

[009-066] "And in Buffalo they always do," Miss Graham added.

[009-067] "Ah! they don't in Des Vaches," said Colville. They tried to mystify him

[009-068] further about the bouquets; they succeeded in being very gay, and in

[009-069] making themselves laugh a great deal. Mrs. Bowen was even livelier than

[009-070] the young girl.

[009-071] Her carriage was one of the few private equipages that drove up to

[009-072] Madame Uccelli's door; most people had not even come in a _remise_, but,

[009-073] after the simple Florentine fashion, had taken the little cabs, which

[009-074] stretched in a long line up and down the way; the horses had let their

[009-075] weary heads drop, and were easing their broken knees by extending their

[009-076] forelegs while they drowsed; the drivers, huddled in their great-coats,

[009-077] had assembled around the doorway to see the guests alight, with that

[009-078] amiable, unenvious interest of the Italians in the pleasure of others.

[009-079] The deep sky glittered with stars; in the corner of the next villa

[009-080] garden the black plumes of some cypresses blotted out their space among

[009-081] them.

[009-082] "_Isn't_ it Florentine?" demanded Mrs. Bowen, giving the hand which

[009-083] Colville offered in helping her out of the carriage a little vivid

[009-084] pressure, full of reminiscence and confident sympathy. A flush of youth

[009-085] warmed his heart; he did not quail even when the porter of the villa

[009-086] intervened between her and her coachman, whom she was telling when to

[009-087] come back, and said that the carriages were ordered for three o'clock.

[009-088] "Did you ever sit up so late as that in Des Vaches?" asked Miss Graham

[009-089] mischievously.

[009-090] "Oh yes; I was editor of a morning paper," he explained. But he did not

[009-091] like the imputation of her question.

[009-092] Madame Uccelli accepted him most hospitably among her guests when he was

[009-093] presented. She was an American who had returned with her Italian husband

[009-094] to Italy, and had long survived him in the villa which he had built with

[009-095] her money. Such people grow very queer with the lapse of time. Madame

[009-096] Uccelli's character remained inalienably American, but her manners and

[009-097] customs had become largely Italian; without having learned the language

[009-098] thoroughly, she spoke it very fluently, and its idioms marked her

[009-099] Philadelphia English. Her house was a menagerie of all the

[009-100] nationalities; she was liked in Italian society, and there were many

[009-101] Italians; English-speaking Russians abounded; there were many genuine

[009-102] English, Germans, Scandinavians, Protestant Irish, American Catholics,

[009-103] and then Americans of all kinds. There was a superstition of her

[009-104] exclusiveness among her compatriots, but one really met every one there

[009-105] sooner or later; she was supposed to be a convert to the religion of her

[009-106] late husband, but no one really knew what religion she was of, probably

[009-107] not even Madame Uccelli herself. One thing you were sure of at her

[009-108] house, and that was a substantial supper; it is the example of such

[009-109] resident foreigners which has corrupted the Florentines, though many

[009-110] native families still hold out against it.

[009-111] The dancing was just beginning, and the daughter of Madame Uccelli, who

[009-112] spoke both English and Italian much better than her mother, came forward

[009-113] and possessed herself of Miss Graham, after a polite feint of pressing

[009-114] Mrs. Bowen to let her find a partner for her.

[009-115] Mrs. Bowen cooed a gracious refusal, telling Fanny Uccelli that she knew

[009-116] very well that she never danced now. The girl had not much time for

[009-117] Colville; she welcomed him, but she was full of her business of starting

[009-118] the dance, and she hurried away without asking him whether she should

[009-119] introduce him to some lady for the quadrille that was forming. Her

[009-120] mother, however, asked him if he would not go out and get himself some

[009-121] tea, and she found a lady to go with him to the supper-room. This lady

[009-122] had daughters whom apparently she wished to supervise while they were

[009-123] dancing, and she brought Colville back very soon. He had to stand by the

[009-124] sofa where she sat till Madame Uccelli found him and introduced him to

[009-125] another mother of daughters. Later he joined a group formed by the

[009-126] father of one of the dancers and the non-dancing husband of a dancing

[009-127] wife. Their conversation was perfunctory; they showed one another that

[009-128] they had no pleasure in it.

[009-129] Presently the father went to see how his daughter looked while dancing;

[009-130] the husband had evidently no such curiosity concerning his wife; and

[009-131] Colville went with the father, and looked at Miss Graham. She was very

[009-132] beautiful, and she obeyed the music as if it were her breath; her face

[009-133] was rapt, intense, full of an unsmiling delight, which shone in her dark

[009-134] eyes, glowing like low stars. Her _abandon_ interested Colville, and

[009-135] then awed him; the spectacle of that young, unjaded capacity for

[009-136] pleasure touched him with a profound sense of loss. Suddenly Imogene

[009-137] caught sight of him, and with the coming of a second look in her eyes

[009-138] the light of an exquisite smile flashed over her face. His heart was in

[009-139] his throat.

[009-140] "_Your_ daughter?" asked the fond parent at his elbow. "That is mine

[009-141] yonder in red."

[009-142] Colville did not answer, nor look at the young lady in red. The dance

[009-143] was ceasing; the fragments of those kaleidoscopic radiations were

[009-144] dispersing themselves; the tormented piano was silent.

[009-145] The officer whom Imogene had danced with brought her to Mrs. Bowen, and

[009-146] resigned her with the regulation bow, hanging his head down before him

[009-147] as if submitting his neck to the axe. She put her hand in Colville's

[009-148] arm, where he stood beside Mrs. Bowen. "Oh, _do_ take me to get

[009-149] something to eat!"

[009-150] In the supper-room she devoured salad and ices with a childish joy in

[009-151] them. The place was jammed, and she laughed from her corner at

[009-152] Colville's struggles in getting the things for her and bringing them to

[009-153] her. While she was still in the midst of an ice, the faint note of the

[009-154] piano sounded. "Oh, they're beginning again. It's the Lancers!" she

[009-155] said, giving him the plate back. She took his arm again; she almost

[009-156] pulled him along on their return.

[009-157] "Why don't _you_ dance?" she demanded mockingly.

[009-158] "I would if you'd let me dance with you."

[009-159] "Oh, that's impossible! I'm engaged ever so many deep." She dropped his

[009-160] arm instantly at sight of a young Englishman who seemed to be looking

[009-161] for her. This young Englishman had a zeal for dancing that was

[009-162] unsparing; partners were nothing to him except as a means of dancing;

[009-163] his manner expressed a supreme contempt for people who made the

[009-164] slightest mistake, who danced with less science or less conscience than

[009-165] himself. "I've been looking for you," he said, in a tone of cold rebuke,

[009-166] without looking at her. "We've been waiting."

[009-167] Colville wished to beat him, but Imogene took his rebuke meekly, and

[009-168] murmured some apologies about not hearing the piano before. He hurried

[009-169] her off without recognising Colville's existence in any way.

[009-170] The undancing husband of the dancing wife was boring himself in a

[009-171] corner; Colville decided that the chances with him were better than with

[009-172] the fond father, and joined him, just as a polite officer came up and

[009-173] entreated him to complete a set. "Oh, I never danced in my life," he

[009-174] replied; and then he referred the officer to Colville. "Don't _you_

[009-175] dance?"

[009-176] "I used to dance," Colville began, while the officer stood looking

[009-177] patiently at him. This was true. He used to dance the Lancers, too, and

[009-178] very badly, seventeen years before. He had danced it with [[miss-ridgely|Lina Ridgely]]

[009-179] and the other one, Mrs. Milbury. His glance wandered to the vacant place

[009-180] on the floor; it was the same set which Miss Graham was in; she smiled

[009-181] and beckoned derisively. A vain and foolish ambition fired him. "Oh yes,

[009-182] I can dance a little," he said.
