[017-001] It was an intimacy; and society in [[florence|Florence]] is not blind, and especially

[017-002] it is not dumb. The old lady who had celebrated [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]] to him the

[017-003] first night at [[palazzo-pinti|Palazzo Pinti]] led a life of active questions as to what

[017-004] was the supreme attraction to [[theodore-colville|Colville]] there, and she referred her doubt

[017-005] to every friend with whom she drank tea. She philosophised the situation

[017-006] very scientifically, and if not very conclusively, how few are the

[017-007] absolute conclusions of science upon any point!

[017-008] "He is a bachelor, and there is a natural affinity between bachelors and

[017-009] widows--much more than if he were a widower too. If he were a widower I

[017-010] should say it was undoubtedly mademoiselle. If he were a little _bit_

[017-011] younger, I should have no doubt it was madame; but men of that age have

[017-012] such an ambition to marry young girls! I suppose that they think it

[017-013] proves they are not so very old, after all. And certainly he isn't too

[017-014] old to marry. If he were wise--which he probably isn't, if he's like

[017-015] other men in such matters--there wouldn't be any question about Mrs.

[017-016] Bowen. Pretty creature! And so much sense! Too much for him. Ah, my

[017-017] dear, how we are wasted upon that sex!"

[017-018] Mrs. Bowen herself treated the affair with masterly frankness. More than

[017-019] once in varying phrase, she said: "You are very good to give us so much

[017-020] of your time, Mr. Colville, and I won't pretend I don't know it. You're

[017-021] helping me out with a very hazardous experiment. When I undertook to see

[017-022] [[imogene-graham|Imogene]] through a winter in Florence, I didn't reflect what a very gay

[017-023] time girls have at home, in Western towns especially. But I haven't

[017-024] heard her breathe Buffalo once. And I'm sure it's doing her a great deal

[017-025] of good here. She's naturally got a very good mind; she's very ambitious

[017-026] to be cultivated. She's read a good deal, and she's anxious to know

[017-027] history and art; and your advice and criticism are the greatest possible

[017-028] advantage to her."

[017-029] "Thank you," said Colville, with a fine, remote dissatisfaction. "I

[017-030] supposed I was merely enjoying myself."

[017-031] He had lately begun to haunt his banker's for information in regard to

[017-032] the Carnival balls, with the hope that something might be made out of

[017-033] them.

[017-034] But either there were to be no great Carnival balls, or it was a mistake

[017-035] to suppose that his banker ought to know about them. Colville went

[017-036] experimentally to one of the people's balls at a minor theatre, which he

[017-037] found advertised on the house walls. At half-past ten the dancing had

[017-038] not begun, but the masks were arriving; young women in gay silks and

[017-039] dirty white gloves; men in women's dresses, with enormous hands; girls

[017-040] as pages; clowns, pantaloons, old women, and the like. They were all

[017-041] very good-humoured; the men, who far outnumbered the women, danced

[017-042] contentedly together. Colville liked two cavalry soldiers who waltzed

[017-043] with each other for an hour, and then went off to a battery on

[017-044] exhibition in the pit, and had as much electricity as they could hold.

[017-045] He liked also two young citizens who danced together as long as he

[017-046] stayed, and did not leave off even for electrical refreshment. He came

[017-047] away at midnight, pushing out of the theatre through a crowd of people

[017-048] at the door, some of whom were tipsy. This certainly would not have done

[017-049] for the ladies, though the people were civilly tipsy.

[017-050] IX

[017-051] The next morning Paolo, when he brought up Colville's breakfast, brought

[017-052] the news that there was to be a veglione at the [[teatro-della-pergola|Pergola Theatre]]. This

[017-053] news revived Colville's courage. "Paolo," he said, "you ought to open a

[017-054] banking-house." Paolo was used to being joked by foreigners who could

[017-055] not speak Italian very well; he smiled as if he understood.

[017-056] The banker had his astute doubts of Paolo's intelligence; the banker in

[017-057] Europe doubts all news not originating in his house; but after a day or

[017-058] two the advertisements in the newspapers carried conviction even to the

[017-059] banker.

[017-060] When Colville went to the ladies with news of the veglione, he found

[017-061] that they had already heard of it. "Should you like to go?" he asked

[017-062] Mrs. Bowen.

[017-063] "I don't know. What do you think?" she asked in turn.

[017-064] "Oh, it's for you to do the thinking. I only know what I want."

[017-065] Imogene said nothing, while she watched the internal debate as it

[017-066] expressed itself in Mrs. Bowen's face.

[017-067] "People go in boxes," she said thoughtfully; "but you would feel that a

[017-068] box wasn't the same thing exactly?"

[017-069] "_We_ went on the floor," suggested Colville.

[017-070] "It was very different then. And, besides, Mrs. Finlay had absolutely

[017-071] _no_ sense of propriety." When a woman has explicitly condemned a given

[017-072] action, she apparently gathers courage for its commission under a little

[017-073] different conditions. "Of course, if we went upon the floor, I shouldn't

[017-074] wish it to be known at all, though foreigners can do almost anything

[017-075] they like."

[017-076] "Really," said Colville, "when it comes to that, I don't see any harm in

[017-077] it."

[017-078] "And you say go?"

[017-079] "I say whatever you say."

[017-080] Mrs. Bowen looked from him to Imogene. "I don't either," she said

[017-081] finally, and they understood that she meant the harm which he had not

[017-082] seen.

[017-083] "Which of us has been so good as to deserve this?" asked Colville.

[017-084] "Oh, you have all been good," she said. "We shall go in masks and

[017-085] dominoes," she continued. "Nothing will happen, and who should know us

[017-086] if anything did?" They had received tickets to the great Borghese ball,

[017-087] which is still a fashionable and desired event of the Carnival to

[017-088] foreigners in Florence; but their preconceptions of the veglione threw

[017-089] into the shade the entertainment which the gentlemen of Florence offered

[017-090] to favoured sojourners.

[017-091] "Come," said Mrs. Bowen, "you must go with us and help us choose our

[017-092] dominoes."

[017-093] A prudent woman does not do an imprudent thing by halves. Effie was to

[017-094] be allowed to go to the veglione too, and she went with them to the shop

[017-095] where they were to hire their dominoes. It would be so much more fun,

[017-096] Mrs. Bowen said, to choose the dresses in the shop than to have them

[017-097] sent home for you to look at. Effie was to be in black; Imogene was to

[017-098] have a light blue domino, and Mrs. Bowen chose a purple one; even where

[017-099] their faces were not to be seen they considered their complexions in

[017-100] choosing the colours. If you happened to find a friend, and wanted to

[017-101] unmask, you would not want to look horrid. The shop people took the

[017-102] vividest interest in it all, as if it were a new thing to them, and

[017-103] these were the first foreigners they had ever served with masks and

[017-104] dominoes. They made Mrs. Bowen and Imogene go into an inner room and

[017-105] come out for the mystification of Colville, hulking about in the front

[017-106] shop with his mask and domino on.

[017-107] "Which is which?" the ladies both challenged him, in the mask's

[017-108] conventional falsetto, when they came out.

[017-109] With a man's severe logic he distinguished them according to their

[017-110] silks, but there had been time for them to think of changing, and they

[017-111] took off their masks to laugh in his face.

[017-112] They fluttered so airily about among the pendent masks and dominoes,

[017-113] from which they shook a ghostly perfume of old carnivals, that his heart

[017-114] leaped.

[017-115] "Ah, you'll never be so fascinating again!" he cried. He wanted to take

[017-116] them in his arms, they were both so delicious; a man has still only that

[017-117] primitive way of expressing his supreme satisfaction in women. "Now,

[017-118] which am I?" he demanded of them, and that made them laugh again. He had

[017-119] really put his arm about Effie.

[017-120] "Do you think you will know your papa at the veglione?" asked one of the

[017-121] shop-women, with a mounting interest in the amiable family party.

[017-122] They all laughed; the natural mistake seemed particularly droll to

[017-123] Imogene.

[017-124] "Come," cried Mrs. Bowen; "it's time we should be going."

[017-125] That was true; they had passed so long a time in the shop that they did

[017-126] not feel justified in seriously attempting to beat down the price of

[017-127] their dresses. They took them at the first price. The woman said with

[017-128] reason that it was Carnival, and she could get her price for the things.

[017-129] They went to the veglione at eleven, the ladies calling for Colville, as

[017-130] before, in Mrs. Bowen's carriage. He felt rather sheepish, coming out of

[017-131] his room in his mask and domino, but the corridors of the hotel were

[017-132] empty, and for the most part dark; there was no one up but the porter,

[017-133] who wished him a pleasant time in as matter-of-fact fashion as if he

[017-134] were going out to an evening party in his dress coat. His spirits

[017-135] mounted in the atmosphere of adventure which the ladies diffused about

[017-136] them in the carriage; [[effie-bowen|Effie Bowen]] laughed aloud when he entered, in

[017-137] childish gaiety of heart.

[017-138] The narrow streets roared with the wheels of cabs and carriages coming

[017-139] and going; the street before the theatre was so packed that it was some

[017-140] time before they could reach the door. Masks were passing in and out;

[017-141] the nervous joy of the ladies expressed itself in a deep-drawn quivering

[017-142] sigh. Their carriage door was opened by a servant of the theatre, who

[017-143] wished them a pleasant veglione, and the next moment they were in the

[017-144] crowded vestibule, where they paused a moment, to let Imogene and Effie

[017-145] really feel that they were part of a masquerade.

[017-146] "Now, keep all together," said Mrs. Bowen, as they passed through the

[017-147] inner door of the vestibule, and the brilliantly lighted theatre flashed

[017-148] its colours and splendours upon them. The floor of the pit had been

[017-149] levelled to that of the stage, which, stripped of the scenic apparatus,

[017-150] opened vaster spaces for the motley crew already eddying over it in the

[017-151] waltz. The boxes, tier over tier, blazed with the light of candelabra

[017-152] which added their sparkle to that of the gas jets.

[017-153] "You and Effie go before," said Mrs. Bowen to Imogene. She made them

[017-154] take hands like children, and mechanically passed her own hand through

[017-155] Colville's arm.

[017-156] A mask in red from head to foot attached himself to the party, and began

[017-157] to make love to her in excellent pantomime.

[017-158] Colville was annoyed. He asked her if he should tell the fellow to take

[017-159] himself off.

[017-160] "Not on any account!" she answered. "It's perfectly delightful. It

[017-161] wouldn't be the veglione without it. Did you ever see such good acting?"

[017-162] "I don't think it's remarkable for anything but its fervour," said

[017-163] Colville.

[017-164] "I should like to see you making love to some lady," she rejoined

[017-165] mischievously.

[017-166] "I will make love to you, if you like," he said, but he felt in an

[017-167] instant that his joke was in bad taste.

[017-168] They went the round of the theatre. "That is Prince Strozzi, Imogene,"

[017-169] said Mrs. Bowen, leaning forward to whisper to the girl. She pointed out

[017-170] other people of historic and aristocratic names in the boxes, where

[017-171] there was a democracy of beauty among the ladies, all painted and

[017-172] powdered to the same marquise effect.

[017-173] On the floor were gentlemen in evening dress, without masks, and here

[017-174] and there ladies waltzing, who had masks but no dominoes. But for the

[017-175] most part people were in costume; the theatre flushed and flowered in

[017-176] gay variety of tint that teased the eye with its flow through the dance.

[017-177] Mrs. Bowen had circumscribed the adventure so as to exclude dancing from

[017-178] it. Imogene was not to dance. One might go to the veglione and look on

[017-179] from a box; if one ventured further and went on the floor, decidedly one

[017-180] was not to dance.

[017-181] This was thoroughly understood beforehand, and there were to be no

[017-182] petitions or murmurs at the theatre. They found a quiet corner, and sat

[017-183] down to look on.
