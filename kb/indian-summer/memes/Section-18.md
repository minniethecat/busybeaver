[018-001] The mask in red followed, and took his place at a little distance,

[018-002] where, whenever [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]] looked that way, he continued to protest his

[018-003] passion.

[018-004] "You're sure he doesn't bore you?" suggested [[theodore-colville|Colville]].

[018-005] "No, indeed. He's very amusing."

[018-006] "Oh, all right!"

[018-007] The waltz ceased; the whirling and winding confusion broke into an

[018-008] irregular streaming hither and thither, up and down. They began to pick

[018-009] out costumes and characters that interested them. Clowns in white, with

[018-010] big noses, and harlequins in their motley, with flat black masks,

[018-011] abounded. There were some admirable grasshoppers in green, with long

[018-012] antennae quivering from their foreheads. Two or three Mephistos reddened

[018-013] through the crowd. Several knights in armour got about with difficulty,

[018-014] apparently burdened by their greaves and breastplates.

[018-015] A group of leaping and dancing masks gathered around a young man in

[018-016] evening dress, with long hair, who stood leaning against a pillar near

[018-017] them, and who underwent their mockeries with a smile of patience, half

[018-018] amused, half tormented.

[018-019] When they grew tired of baiting him, and were looking about for other

[018-020] prey, the red mask redoubled his show of devotion to Mrs. Bowen, and the

[018-021] other masks began to flock round and approve.

[018-022] "Oh, now," she said, with a little embarrassed laugh, in which there was

[018-023] no displeasure, "I think you may ask him to go away. But don't be harsh

[018-024] with him," she added, at a brusque movement which Colville made toward

[018-025] the mask.

[018-026] "Oh, why should I be harsh with him? We're not rivals." This was not in

[018-027] good taste either, Colville felt. "Besides, I'm an Italian too," he

[018-028] said, to retrieve himself. He made a few paces toward the mask, and said

[018-029] in a low tone, with gentle suggestion, "Madame finds herself a little

[018-030] incommoded."

[018-031] The mask threw himself into an attitude of burlesque despair, bowed low

[018-032] with his hand on his heart, in token of submission, and vanished into

[018-033] the crowd. The rest dispersed with cries of applause.

[018-034] "How very prettily you did it, both of you!" said Mrs. Bowen. "I begin

[018-035] to believe you are an Italian, Mr. Colville. I shall be afraid of you."

[018-036] "You weren't afraid of him."

[018-037] "Oh, he was a real Italian."

[018-038] "It seems to me that mamma is getting all the good of the veglione,"

[018-039] said Effie, in a plaintive murmur. The well-disciplined child must have

[018-040] suffered deeply before she lifted this seditious voice.

[018-041] "Why, so I am, Effie," answered her mother, "and I don't think it's fair

[018-042] myself. What shall we do about it?"

[018-043] "I should like something to eat," said the child.

[018-044] "So should I," said Colville. "That's reparation your mother owes us

[018-045] all. Let's make her take us and get us something. Wouldn't you like an

[018-046] ice, [[imogene-graham|Miss Graham]]?"

[018-047] "Yes, an _ice_," said Imogene, with an effect of adding, "Nothing more

[018-048] for worlds," that made Colville laugh. She rose slowly, like one in a

[018-049] dream, and cast a look as impassioned as a look could be made through a

[018-050] mask on the scene she was leaving behind her. The band was playing a

[018-051] waltz again, and the wide floor swam with circling couples.

[018-052] The corridor where the tables were set was thronged with people, who

[018-053] were drinking beer and eating cold beef and boned turkey and slices of

[018-054] huge round sausages. "Oh, how _can_ they?" cried the girl, shuddering.

[018-055] "I didn't know you were so ethereal-minded about these things," said

[018-056] Colville. "I thought you didn't object to the salad at Madame

[018-057] Uccelli's."

[018-058] "Oh, but at the veglione!" breathed the girl for all answer. He laughed

[018-059] again, but Mrs. Bowen did not laugh with him; he wondered why.

[018-060] When they returned to their corner in the theatre they found a mask in a

[018-061] black domino there, who made place for them, and remained standing near.

[018-062] They began talking freely and audibly, as English-speaking people

[018-063] incorrigibly do in Italy, where their tongue is all but the language of

[018-064] the country.

[018-065] "Really," said Colville, "I think I shall stifle in this mask. If you

[018-066] ladies will do what you can to surround me and keep me secret, I'll take

[018-067] it off a moment."

[018-068] "I believe I will join you, Mr. Colville," said the mask near them. He

[018-069] pushed up his little visor of silk, and discovered the mild, benignant

[018-070] features of [[mr-waters|Mr. Waters]].

[018-071] "Bless my soul!" cried Colville.

[018-072] Mrs. Bowen was apparently too much shocked to say anything.

[018-073] "You didn't expect to meet me here?" asked the old man, as if otherwise

[018-074] it should be the most natural thing in the world. After that they could

[018-075] only unite in suppressing their astonishment. "It's extremely

[018-076] interesting," he went on, "extremely! I've been here ever since the

[018-077] exercises began, and I have not only been very greatly amused, but

[018-078] greatly instructed. It seems to me the key to a great many anomalies in

[018-079] the history of this wonderful people."

[018-080] If Mr. Waters took this philosophical tone about the Carnival, it was

[018-081] not possible for Colville to take any other.

[018-082] "And have you been able to divine from what you have seen here," he

[018-083] asked gravely, "the grounds of [[girolamo-savonarola|Savonarola]]'s objection to the Carnival?"

[018-084] "Not at all," said the old man promptly. "I have seen nothing but the

[018-085] most harmless gaiety throughout the evening."

[018-086] Colville hung his head. He remembered reading once in a passage from

[018-087] Swedenborg, that the most celestial angels had scarcely any power of

[018-088] perceiving evil.

[018-089] "Why aren't you young people dancing?" asked Mr. Waters, in a cheerful

[018-090] general way, of Mrs. Bowen's party.

[018-091] Colville was glad to break the silence. "Mrs. Bowen doesn't approve of

[018-092] dancing at vegliones."

[018-093] "No?--why not?" inquired the old man, with invincible simplicity.

[018-094] Mrs. Bowen smiled her pretty, small smile below her mask.

[018-095] "The company is apt to be rather mixed," she said quietly.

[018-096] "Yes," pursued Mr. Waters; "but you could dance with one another. The

[018-097] company seems very well behaved."

[018-098] "Oh, quite so," Mrs. Bowen assented.

[018-099] "Shortly after I came," said Mr. Waters, "one of the masks asked me to

[018-100] dance. I was really sorry that my age and traditions forbade my doing

[018-101] so. I tried to explain, but I'm afraid I didn't make myself quite

[018-102] clear."

[018-103] "Probably it passed for a joke with her," said Colville, in order to say

[018-104] something.

[018-105] "Ah, very likely; but I shall always feel that my impressions of the

[018-106] Carnival would have been more definite if I could have danced. Now, if I

[018-107] were a young man like you----"

[018-108] Imogene turned and looked at Colville through the eye-holes of her mask;

[018-109] even in that sort of isolation he thought her eyes expressed surprise.

[018-110] "It never occurred to you before that I was a young man," he suggested

[018-111] gravely.

[018-112] She did not reply.

[018-113] After a little interval, "Imogene," asked Mrs. Bowen, "would you like to

[018-114] dance?"

[018-115] Colville was astonished. "The veglione has gone to your head, Mrs.

[018-116] Bowen," he tacitly made his comment. She had spoken to Imogene, but she

[018-117] glanced at him as if she expected him to be grateful to her for this

[018-118] stroke of liberality.

[018-119] "What would be the use?" returned the girl.

[018-120] Colville rose. "After my performance in the Lancers, I can't expect you

[018-121] to believe me; but I really do know how to waltz." He had but to extend

[018-122] his arms, and she was hanging upon his shoulder, and they were whirling

[018-123] away through a long orbit of delight to the girl.

[018-124] "Oh, why have you let me do you such injustice?" she murmured intensely.

[018-125] "I never shall forgive myself."

[018-126] "It grieved me that you shouldn't have divined that I was really a

[018-127] magnificent dancer in disguise, but I bore it as best I could," said

[018-128] Colville, really amused at her seriousness. "Perhaps you'll find out

[018-129] after a while that I'm not an old fellow either, but only a 'Lost

[018-130] Youth.'"

[018-131] "Hush," she said; "I don't like to hear you talk so."

[018-132] "How?"

[018-133] "[[About]]--age!" she answered. "It makes me feel----- Don't to-night!"

[018-134] Colville laughed. "It isn't a fact that my blinking is going to change

[018-135] materially. You had better make the most of me as a [[the-past|lost youth]]. I'm old

[018-136] enough to be two of them."

[018-137] She did not answer, and as they wound up and down through the other

[018-138] orbing couples, he remembered the veglione of seventeen years before,

[018-139] when he had dreamed through the waltz with the girl who jilted him; she

[018-140] was very docile and submissive that night; he believed afterward that if

[018-141] he had spoken frankly then, she would not have refused him. But he had

[018-142] veiled his passion in words and phrases that, taken in themselves, had

[018-143] no meaning--that neither committed him nor claimed her. He could not

[018-144] help it; he had not the courage at any moment to risk the loss of her

[018-145] for ever, till it was too late, till he must lose her.

[018-146] "Do you believe in pre-existence?" he demanded of Imogene.

[018-147] "Oh yes!" she flashed back. "This very instant it was just as if I had

[018-148] been here before, long ago."

[018-149] "Dancing with me?"

[018-150] "With you? Yes--yes--I think so."

[018-151] He had lived long enough to know that she was making herself believe

[018-152] what she said, and that she had not lived long enough to know this.

[018-153] "Then you remember what I said to you--tried to say to you--that night?"

[018-154] Through one of those psychological juggles which we all practise with

[018-155] ourselves at times, it amused him, it charmed him, to find her striving

[018-156] to realise this past.

[018-157] "No; it was so long ago? What was it?" she whispered dreamily.

[018-158] A turn of the waltz brought them near Mrs. Bowen; her mask seemed to

[018-159] wear a dumb reproach.

[018-160] He began to be weary; one of the differences between youth and later

[018-161] life is that the latter wearies so soon of any given emotion.

[018-162] "Ah, I can't remember, either! Aren't you getting rather tired of the

[018-163] waltz and me?"

[018-164] "Oh no; go on!" she deeply murmured. "Try to remember."

[018-165] The long, pulsating stream of the music broke and fell. The dancers

[018-166] crookedly dispersed in wandering lines. She took his arm; he felt her

[018-167] heart leap against it; those innocent, trustful throbs upbraided him. At

[018-168] the same time his own heart beat with a sort of fond, protecting

[018-169] tenderness; he felt the witchery of his power to make this young,

[018-170] radiant, and beautiful creature hang flattered and bewildered on his

[018-171] talk; he liked the compassionate worship with which his tacit confidence

[018-172] had inspired her, even while he was not without some satirical sense of

[018-173] the crude sort of heart-broken hero he must be in the fancy of a girl of

[018-174] her age.

[018-175] "Let us go and walk in the corridor a moment," he said. But they walked

[018-176] there till the alluring melancholy music of the waltz began again. In a

[018-177] mutual caprice, they rejoined the dance.

[018-178] It came into his head to ask, "Who is _he_?" and as he had got past

[018-179] denying himself anything, he asked it.

[018-180] "He? What he?"

[018-181] "He that Mrs. Bowen thought might object to your seeing the Carnival?"

[018-182] "Oh!--oh yes! That was the not impossible he."

[018-183] "Is that all?"

[018-184] "Yes."

[018-185] "Then he's not even the not improbable he?"

[018-186] "No, indeed."

[018-187] They waltzed in silence. Then, "Why did you ask me that?" she murmured.

[018-188] "I don't know. Was it such a strange question?"

[018-189] "I don't know. You ought to."

[018-190] "Yes, if it was wrong, I'm old enough to know better."

[018-191] "You promised not to say 'old' any more."

[018-192] "Then I suppose I mustn't. But you mustn't get me to ignore it, and then

[018-193] laugh at me for it."

[018-194] "Oh!" she reproached him, "you think I could do that?"

[018-195] "You could if it was you who were here with me once before."

[018-196] "Then I know I wasn't."

[018-197] Again they were silent, and it was he who spoke first. "I wish you would

[018-198] tell me why you object to the interdicted topic?"

[018-199] "Because--because I like every time to be perfect in itself."

[018-200] "Oh! And this wouldn't be perfect in itself if I were--not so young as

[018-201] some people?"
