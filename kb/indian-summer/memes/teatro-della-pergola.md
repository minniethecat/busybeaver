## Description

The Teatro della Pergola is a historic theatre in Florence that serves as the setting for the veglione, the Carnival masked ball that forms the dramatic and emotional centerpiece of the novel. Howells depicts the theatre as a grand, multi-level space with boxes overlooking a wide floor used for dancing.

The floor is thronged with masked revelers in elaborate costumes. Clowns in white with big noses, harlequins in motley, grasshoppers in green, and Mephistos and knights in armor fill the space (018-009–018-013). The air grows thick with dust, and the lights burned through it as through a fog (019-041), creating an atmosphere festive and disorienting.

The theatre also features corridors lined with tables where attendees drink beer and eat cold beef, boned turkey, and slices of huge round sausages (018-052–018-053). A vestibule provides respite from the heat. The orchestra plays waltzes, and the wide floor swam with circling couples (018-051).

## Significance in the Novel

The veglione at the Pergola represents the novel's pivotal turning point. Central romantic tensions among Colville, Mrs. Bowen, and Imogene Graham are acted out under the liberating cover of masks and Carnival license.

Colville learns of the veglione from Paolo, who brings the news with his breakfast (017-052). Mrs. Bowen reluctantly permits Imogene to dance, stretching a point of propriety she would not ordinarily concede (018-053–018-062). Colville and Imogene waltz together, their intimacy deepened by the masked anonymity and by Colville's memories of a veglione seventeen years earlier (018-049–018-051).

The evening reaches a crisis when Effie Bowen falls ill (019-123) and Mrs. Bowen must leave, while Colville and Imogene remain lost in their own absorption. This produces a lasting rupture between Colville and Mrs. Bowen.

Imogene later confesses that she forced Mrs. Bowen to attend, calling herself the most selfish girl in the world (024-140). The Pergola functions as both a real Florentine landmark and a symbolic space where ordinary social constraints are suspended.

## Real-World Counterpart

The Teatro della Pergola is a real and historically significant theatre in Florence, located on Via della Pergola. Built in 1656 under the patronage of the Accademia degli Immobili, it is one of the oldest opera houses in Italy.

It is considered the first theatre in Europe to feature the now-standard arrangement of tiered boxes stacked in a horseshoe shape around the stage. The theatre was an active venue for opera and theatrical productions in the 1880s, when *Indian Summer* is set, and its Carnival veglioni were a well-known feature of Florentine social life.

Howells, who lived in Florence as American consul during the 1860s and maintained close ties to the city, would have known the Pergola firsthand. The theatre continues to operate today as a performing arts venue, now managed by the Fondazione Teatro della Toscana.
