[037-001] "They appear to like the formalist too, as well as the form," said

[037-002] [[theodore-colville|Colville]], with scorn not obviously necessary.

[037-003] "Oh yes; they must have everything in the concrete," said the old

[037-004] gentleman cheerfully.

[037-005] "I wonder where [[mr-morton|Mr. Morton]] met [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]] first," said Colville.

[037-006] "Here, I think. I believe he had letters to her. Before you came I used

[037-007] often to meet him at her house. I think she has helped him with money at

[037-008] times."

[037-009] "Isn't that rather an unpleasant idea?"

[037-010] "Yes; it's disagreeable. And it places the ministry in a dependent

[037-011] attitude. But under our system it's unavoidable. Young men devoting

[037-012] themselves to the ministry frequently receive gifts of money."

[037-013] "I don't like it," cried Colville.

[037-014] "They don't feel it as others would. I didn't myself. Even at present I

[037-015] may be said to be living on charity. But sometimes I have fancied that

[037-016] in Mr. Morton's case there might be peculiarly mitigating

[037-017] circumstances."

[037-018] "What do you mean?"

[037-019] "When I met him first at Mrs. Bowen's I used to think that it was Miss

[037-020] Graham in whom he was interested----"

[037-021] "I can assure you," interrupted Colville, "that she was never interested

[037-022] in him."

[037-023] "Oh no; I didn't suppose that," returned the old man tranquilly. "And

[037-024] I've since had reason to revise my opinion. I think he is interested in

[037-025] Mrs. Bowen."

[037-026] "Mrs. Bowen! And you think that would be a mitigating circumstance in

[037-027] his acceptance of money from her? If he had the spirit of a man at all,

[037-028] it would make it all the more revolting."

[037-029] "Oh no, oh no," softly pleaded [[mr-waters|Mr. Waters]]. "We must not look at these

[037-030] things too romantically. He probably reasons that she would give him all

[037-031] her money if they were married."

[037-032] "But he has no right to reason in that way," retorted Colville, with

[037-033] heat. "They are not married; it's ignoble and unmanly for him to count

[037-034] upon it. It's preposterous. She must be ten years older than he."

[037-035] "Oh, I don't say that they're to be married," Mr. Waters replied. "But

[037-036] these disparities of age frequently occur in marriage. I don't like

[037-037] them, though sometimes I think the evil is less when it is the wife who

[037-038] is the elder. We look at youth and age in a gross, material way too

[037-039] often. Women remain young longer than men. They keep their youthful

[037-040] sympathies; an old woman understands a young girl. Do you--or do

[037-041] I--understand a young man?"

[037-042] Colville laughed harshly. "It isn't _quite_ the same thing, Mr. Waters.

[037-043] But yes; I'll admit, for the sake of argument, that I don't understand

[037-044] young men. I'll go further, and say that I don't like them; I'm afraid

[037-045] of them. And you wouldn't think," he added abruptly, "that it would be

[037-046] well for me to marry a girl twenty years younger than myself."

[037-047] The old man glanced up at him with innocent slyness. "I prefer always to

[037-048] discuss these things in an impersonal way."

[037-049] "But you can't discuss them impersonally with me; I'm engaged to Miss

[037-050] Graham. Ever since you first found me here after I told you I was going

[037-051] away I have wished to tell you this, and this seems as good a time as

[037-052] any--or as bad." The defiance faded from his voice, which dropped to a

[037-053] note of weary sadness. "Yes, we're engaged--or shall be, as soon as she

[037-054] can hear from her family. I wanted to tell you because it seemed somehow

[037-055] your due, and because I fancied you had a friendly interest in us both."

[037-056] "Yes, that is true," returned Mr. Waters. "I wish you joy." He went

[037-057] through the form of offering his hand to Colville, who pressed it with

[037-058] anxious fervour.

[037-059] "I confess," he said, "that I feel the risks of the affair. It's not

[037-060] that I have any dread for my own part; I have lived my life, such as it

[037-061] is. But the child is full of fancies about me that can't be fulfilled.

[037-062] She dreams of restoring my youth somehow, of retrieving the past for me,

[037-063] of avenging me at her own cost for an unlucky love affair that I had

[037-064] here twenty years ago. It's pretty of her, but it's terribly

[037-065] pathetic--it's tragic. I know very well that I'm a middle-aged man, and

[037-066] that there's no more youth for me. I'm getting grey, and I'm getting

[037-067] fat; I wouldn't be young if I could; it's a bore. I suppose I could keep

[037-068] up an illusion of youthfulness for five or six years more; and then if I

[037-069] could be quietly chloroformed out of the way, perhaps it wouldn't have

[037-070] been so very bad."

[037-071] "I have always thought," said Mr. Waters dreamily, "that a good deal

[037-072] might be said for abbreviating hopeless suffering. I have known some

[037-073] very good people advocate its practice by science."

[037-074] "Yes," answered Colville. "Perhaps I've presented that point too

[037-075] prominently. What I wished you to understand was that I don't care for

[037-076] myself; that I consider only the happiness of this young girl that's

[037-077] somehow--I hardly know how--been put in my keeping. I haven't forgotten

[037-078] the talks that we've had heretofore on this subject, and it would be

[037-079] affectation and bad taste in me to ignore them. Don't be troubled at

[037-080] anything you've said; it was probably true, and I'm sure it was sincere.

[037-081] Sometimes I think that the kindest--the least cruel--thing I could do

[037-082] would be to break with her, to leave her. But I know that I shall do

[037-083] nothing of the kind; I shall drift. The child is very dear to me. She

[037-084] has great and noble qualities; she's supremely unselfish; she loves me

[037-085] through her mistaken pity, and because she thinks she can sacrifice

[037-086] herself to me. But she can't. Everything is against that; she doesn't

[037-087] know how, and there is no reason why. I don't express it very well. I

[037-088] think nobody clearly understands it but Mrs. Bowen, and I've somehow

[037-089] alienated her."

[037-090] He became aware that his [[sacrifice-and-renunciation|self-abnegation]] was taking the character of

[037-091] self-pity, and he stopped.

[037-092] Mr. Waters seemed to be giving the subject serious attention in the

[037-093] silence that ensued. "There is this to be remembered," he began, "which

[037-094] we don't consider in our mere speculations upon any phase of human

[037-095] affairs; and that is the wonderful degree of amelioration that any given

[037-096] difficulty finds in the realisation. It is the anticipation, not the

[037-097] experience, that is the trial. In a case of this kind, facts of

[037-098] temperament, of mere association, of union, work unexpected mitigations;

[037-099] they not only alleviate, they allay. You say that she cherishes an

[037-100] illusion concerning you: well, with women, nothing is so indestructible

[037-101] as an illusion. Give them any chance at all, and all the forces of their

[037-102] nature combine to preserve it. And if, as you say, she is so dear to

[037-103] you, that in itself is almost sufficient. I can well understand your

[037-104] misgivings, springing as they do from a sensitive conscience; but we may

[037-105] reasonably hope that they are exaggerated. Very probably there will not

[037-106] be the rapture for her that there would be if--if you were younger; but

[037-107] the chances of final happiness are great--yes, very considerable. She

[037-108] will learn to appreciate what is really best in you, and you already

[037-109] understand her. Your love for her is the key to the future. Without

[037-110] that, of course----"

[037-111] "Oh, of course," interrupted Colville hastily. Every touch of this

[037-112] comforter's hand had been a sting; and he parted with him in that

[037-113] feeling of utter friendlessness involving a man who has taken counsel

[037-114] upon the confession of half his trouble.

[037-115] Something in Mrs. Bowen's manner when he met her next made him think

[037-116] that perhaps [[imogene-graham|Imogene]] had been telling her of the sympathy he had

[037-117] expressed for her ill-health. It was in the evening, and Imogene and Mr.

[037-118] Morton were looking over a copy of _The Marble Faun_, which he had

[037-119] illustrated with photographs at Rome. Imogene asked Colville to look at

[037-120] it too, but he said he would examine it later; he had his opinion of

[037-121] people who illustrated _The Marble Faun_ with photographs; it surprised

[037-122] him that she seemed to find something novel and brilliant in the idea.

[037-123] [[effie-bowen|Effie Bowen]] looked round where she was kneeling on a chair beside the

[037-124] couple with the book, and seeing Colville wandering neglectedly about

[037-125] before he placed himself, she jumped down and ran and caught his hand.

[037-126] "Well, what now?" he asked, with a dim smile, as she began to pull him

[037-127] toward the sofa. When he should be expelled from [[palazzo-pinti|Palazzo Pinti]] he would

[037-128] really miss the worship of that little thing. He knew that her impulse

[037-129] had been to console him for his exclusion from the pleasures that

[037-130] Imogene and Mr. Morton were enjoying.

[037-131] "Nothing. Just talk," she said, making him fast in a corner of the sofa

[037-132] by crouching tight against him.

[037-133] "What about? [[About]] which is the pleasantest season?"

[037-134] "Oh no; we've talked about that so often. Besides, of course you'd say

[037-135] spring, now that it's coming on so nicely."

[037-136] "Do you think I'm so changeable as that? Haven't I always said winter

[037-137] when this question of the seasons was up? And I say it now. Shan't you

[037-138] be awfully sorry when you can't have a pleasant little fire on the

[037-139] hearth like this any more?"

[037-140] "Yes; I know. But it's very nice having the flowers, too. The grass was

[037-141] all full of daisies to-day--perfectly powdered with them."

[037-142] "To-day? Where?"

[037-143] "At the [[cascine|Cascine]]. And in under the trees there were millions of violets

[037-144] and crow's-feet. Mr. Morton helped me to get them for mamma and Imogene.

[037-145] And we stayed so long that when we drove home the daisies had all shut

[037-146] up, and the little pink leaves outside made it look like a field of red

[037-147] clover. Are you never going there any more?"

[037-148] Mrs. Bowen came in. From the fact that there was no greeting between her

[037-149] and Mr. Morton, Colville inferred that she was returning to the room

[037-150] after having already been there. She stood a moment, with a little

[037-151] uncertainty, when she had shaken hands with him, and then dropped upon

[037-152] the sofa beyond Effie. The little girl ran one hand through Colville's

[037-153] arm, and the other through her mother's, and gripped them fast. "Now I

[037-154] have got you both," she triumphed, and smiled first into her face, and

[037-155] then into his.

[037-156] "Be quiet, Effie," said her mother, but she submitted.

[037-157] "I hope you're better for your drive to-day, Mrs. Bowen. Effie has been

[037-158] telling me about it."

[037-159] "We stayed out a long time. Yes, I think the air did me good; but I'm

[037-160] not an invalid, you know."

[037-161] "Oh no."

[037-162] "I'm feeling a little fagged. And the weather was tempting. I suppose

[037-163] you've been taking one of your long walks."

[037-164] "No, I've scarcely stirred out. I usually feel like going to meet the

[037-165] spring a little more than half-way; but this year I don't, somehow."

[037-166] "A good many people are feeling rather languid, I believe," said Mrs.

[037-167] Bowen.

[037-168] "I hope you'll get away from [[florence|Florence]]," said Colville.

[037-169] "Oh," she returned, with a faint flush, "I'm afraid Imogene exaggerated

[037-170] that a little." She added, "You are very good."

[037-171] She was treating him more kindly than she had ever done since that

[037-172] Sunday afternoon when he came in with Imogene to say that he was going

[037-173] to stay. It might be merely because she had worn out her mood of

[037-174] severity, as people do, returning in good-humour to those with whom they

[037-175] were offended, merely through the reconciling force of time. She did not

[037-176] look at him, but this was better than meeting his eye with that

[037-177] interceptive glance. A strange peace touched his heart. Imogene and the

[037-178] young clergyman at the table across the room were intent on the book

[037-179] still; he was explaining and expatiating, and she listening. Colville

[037-180] saw that he had a fine head, and an intelligent, handsome, gentle face.

[037-181] When he turned again to Mrs. Bowen it was with the illusion that she had

[037-182] been saying something; but she was, in fact, sitting mute, and her face,

[037-183] with its bright colour, showed pathetically thin.
