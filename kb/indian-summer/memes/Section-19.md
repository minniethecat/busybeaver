[019-001] "I didn't mean that. No; but if you didn't mention it, no one else would

[019-002] think of it or care for it."

[019-003] "Did any one ever accuse you of flattering, [[imogene-graham|Miss Graham]]?"

[019-004] "Not till now. And you are unjust."

[019-005] "Well, I withdraw the accusation."

[019-006] "And will you ever pretend such a thing again?"

[019-007] "Oh, never!"

[019-008] "Then I have your promise."

[019-009] The talk was light word-play, such as depends upon the talker's own mood

[019-010] for its point or its pointlessness. Between two young people of equal

[019-011] years it might have had meanings to penetrate, to sigh over, to

[019-012] question. [[theodore-colville|Colville]] found it delicious to be pursued by the ingenuous

[019-013] fervour of this young girl, eager to vindicate her sincerity in

[019-014] prohibiting him from his own ironical depreciation. Apparently, she had

[019-015] a sentimental mission of which he was the object; he was to be convinced

[019-016] that he was unnecessarily morbid; he was to be cheered up, to be kept in

[019-017] heart.

[019-018] "I must believe in you after this," he said, with a smile which his mask

[019-019] hid.

[019-020] "Thanks," she breathed. It seemed to him that her hand closed

[019-021] convulsively upon his in their light clasp.

[019-022] The pressure sent a real pang to his heart. It forced her name from his

[019-023] lips. "Imogene! Ah, I've no right to call you that."

[019-024] "Yes."

[019-025] "From this out I promise to be twenty years younger. But no one is to

[019-026] know it but you. Do you think you will know it? I shouldn't like to keep

[019-027] the secret to myself altogether."

[019-028] "No; I will help you. It shall be _our_ secret."

[019-029] She gave a low laugh of delight. He convinced himself that she had

[019-030] entered into the light spirit of banter in which he believed that he was

[019-031] talking.

[019-032] The music ceased again. He whirled her to the seat where he had left

[019-033] [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]]. She was not there, nor the others.

[019-034] Colville felt the meanness of a man who has betrayed his trust, and his

[019-035] self-contempt was the sharper because the trust had been as tacit and

[019-036] indefinite as it was generous. The effect of Mrs. Bowen's absence was as

[019-037] if she had indignantly flown, and left him to the consequences of his

[019-038] treachery.

[019-039] He sat down rather blankly with Imogene to wait for her return; it was

[019-040] the only thing they could do.

[019-041] It had grown very hot. The air was thick with dust. The lights burned

[019-042] through it as through a fog.

[019-043] "I believe I will take off my mask," she said. "I can scarcely breathe."

[019-044] "No, no," protested Colville; "that won't do."

[019-045] "I feel faint," she gasped.

[019-046] His heart sank. "Don't," he said incoherently. "Come with me into the

[019-047] vestibule, and get a breath of air."

[019-048] He had almost to drag her through the crowd, but in the vestibule she

[019-049] revived, and they returned to their place again. He did not share the

[019-050] easy content with which she recognised the continued absence of Mrs.

[019-051] Bowen.

[019-052] "Why they must be lost. But isn't it perfect sitting here and watching

[019-053] the maskers?"

[019-054] "Perfect," said Colville distractedly.

[019-055] "Don't you like to make romances about the different ones?"

[019-056] It was on Colville's tongue to say that he had made all the romances he

[019-057] wished for that evening, but he only answered, "Oh, very."

[019-058] "Poor Mrs. Bowen," laughed the girl. "It will be such a joke on her,

[019-059] with her punctilious notions, getting lost from her _protégée_ at a

[019-060] [[the-veglione|Carnival ball]]! I shall tell every one."

[019-061] "Oh no, don't," said Colville, in horror that big mask scarcely

[019-062] concealed.

[019-063] "Why not?"

[019-064] "It wouldn't be at all the thing."

[019-065] "Why, are you becoming [[europeanisation|Europeanised]] too?" she demanded. "I thought you

[019-066] went in for all sorts of unconventionalities. Recollect your promise.

[019-067] You must be as impulsive as I am."

[019-068] Colville, staring anxiously about in every direction, made for the first

[019-069] time the reflection that most young girls probably conform to the

[019-070] proprieties without in the least knowing why.

[019-071] "Do you think," he asked, in desperation, "that you would be afraid to

[019-072] be left here a moment while I went about in the crowd and tried to find

[019-073] them?"

[019-074] "Not at all," she said. But she added, "Don't be gone long."

[019-075] "Oh no," he answered, pulling off his mask. "Be sure not to move from

[019-076] here on any account."

[019-077] He plunged into the midst of the crowd that buffeted him from side to

[019-078] side as he struck against its masses. The squeaking and gibbering masks

[019-079] mocked in their falsetto at his wild-eyed, naked face thrusting hither

[019-080] and thither among them.

[019-081] "I saw your lady wife with another gentleman," cried one of them, in a

[019-082] subtle misinterpretation of the cause of his distraction.

[019-083] The throng had immensely increased; the clowns and harlequins ran

[019-084] shrieking up and down, and leaped over one another's heads.

[019-085] It was useless. He went back to Imogene with a heart-sickening fear that

[019-086] she too might have vanished.

[019-087] But she was still there.

[019-088] "You ought to have come sooner," she said gaily. "That red mask has been

[019-089] here again. He looked as if he wanted to make love to _me_ this time.

[019-090] But he didn't. If you'd been here you might have asked him where Mrs.

[019-091] Bowen was."

[019-092] Colville sat down. He had done what he could to mend the matter, and the

[019-093] time had come for philosophical submission. It was now his [[social-duty|duty]] to keep

[019-094] up Miss Graham's spirits. They were both Americans, and from the

[019-095] national standpoint he was simply the young girl's middle-aged bachelor

[019-096] friend. There was nothing in the situation for him to beat his breast

[019-097] about.

[019-098] "Well, all that we can do is to wait for them," he said.

[019-099] "Oh yes," she answered easily. "They'll be sure to come back in the

[019-100] course of time."

[019-101] They waited a half-hour, talking somewhat at random, and still the

[019-102] others did not come. But the red mask came again. He approached

[019-103] Colville, and said politely--

[019-104] "_La signora è partita._"

[019-105] "The lady gone?" repeated Colville, taking this to be part of the red

[019-106] mask's joke.

[019-107] "_La bambina pareva poco lene._"

[019-108] "The little one not well?" echoed Colville again, rising. "Are you

[019-109] joking?"

[019-110] The mask made a deep murmur of polite deprecation. "I am not capable of

[019-111] such a thing in a serious affair. Perhaps you know me?" he said, taking

[019-112] off his mask, and in further sign of good faith he gave the name of a

[019-113] painter sufficiently famous in [[florence|Florence]].

[019-114] "I beg your pardon, and thank you," said Colville. He had no need to

[019-115] speak to Imogene--, her hand was already trembling on his arm.

[019-116] They drove home in silence through the white moonlight of the streets,

[019-117] filled everywhere with the gay voices and figures of the Carnival.

[019-118] Mrs. Bowen met them at the door of her apartment, and received them with

[019-119] a manner that justly distributed the responsibility and penalty for

[019-120] their escapade. Colville felt that a meaner spirit would have wreaked

[019-121] its displeasure upon the girl alone. She made short, quiet answers to

[019-122] all his eager inquiries. Most probably it was some childish

[019-123] indisposition; Effie had been faint. No, he need not go for the doctor.

[019-124] [[mr-waters|Mr. Waters]] had called the doctor, who had just gone away. There was

[019-125] nothing else that he could do for her. She dropped her eyes, and in

[019-126] everything but words dismissed him. She would not even remain with him

[019-127] till he could decently get himself out of the house. She left Imogene to

[019-128] receive his adieux, feigning that she heard Effie calling.

[019-129] "I'm--I'm very sorry," faltered the girl, "that we didn't go back to her

[019-130] at once."

[019-131] "Yes; I was to blame," answered the humiliated hero of her Carnival

[019-132] dream. The clinging regret with which she kept his hand at parting

[019-133] scarcely consoled him for what had happened.

[019-134] "I will come round in the morning," he said. "I must know how Effie is."

[019-135] "Yes; come."

[019-136] X

[019-137] Colville went to [[palazzo-pinti|Palazzo Pinti]] next day with the feeling that he was

[019-138] defying Mrs. Bowen. Upon a review of the facts he could not find himself

[019-139] so very much to blame for the occurrences of the night before, and he

[019-140] had not been able to prove to his reason that Mrs. Bowen had resented

[019-141] his behaviour. She had not made a scene of any sort when he came in with

[019-142] Imogene; it was natural that she should excuse herself, and should wish

[019-143] to be with her sick child: she had done really nothing. But when a woman

[019-144] has done nothing she fills the soul of the man whose conscience troubles

[019-145] him with an instinctive apprehension. There is then no safety, his

[019-146] nerves tell him, except in bringing the affair, whatever it is, to an

[019-147] early issue--in having it out with her. Colville subdued the cowardly

[019-148] impulse of his own heart, which would have deceived him with the

[019-149] suggestion that Mrs. Bowen might be occupied with Effie, and it would be

[019-150] better to ask for Miss Graham. He asked for Mrs. Bowen, and she came in

[019-151] directly.

[019-152] She smiled in the usual way, and gave her hand, as she always did; but

[019-153] her hand was cold, and she looked tired, though she said Effie was quite

[019-154] herself again, and had been asking for him. "Imogene has been telling

[019-155] her about your adventure last night, and making her laugh."

[019-156] If it had been Mrs. Bowen's purpose to mystify him, she could not have

[019-157] done it more thoroughly than by this bold treatment of the affair. He

[019-158] bent a puzzled gaze upon her. "I'm glad any of you have found it

[019-159] amusing," he said;--"I confess that I couldn't let myself off so lightly

[019-160] in regard to it." She did not reply, and he continued: "The fact is, I

[019-161] don't think I behaved very well. I abused your kindness to Miss Graham."

[019-162] "Abused my kindness to Miss Graham?"

[019-163] "Yes. When you allowed her to dance at the veglione, I ought to have

[019-164] considered that you were stretching a point. I ought to have taken her

[019-165] back to you very soon, instead of tempting her to go and walk with me in

[019-166] the corridor."

[019-167] "Yes," said Mrs. Bowen. "So it was you who proposed it? Imogene was

[019-168] afraid that she had. What exemplary young people you are! The way each

[019-169] of you confesses and assumes all the blame would leave the severest

[019-170] chaperone without a word."

[019-171] Her gaiety made Colville uncomfortable. He said gravely, "What I blame

[019-172] myself most for is that I was not there to be of use to you when

[019-173] Effie----"

[019-174] "Oh, you mustn't think of that at all. Mr. Waters was most efficient. My

[019-175] admirer in the red mask was close at hand, and between them they got

[019-176] Effie out without the slightest disturbance. I fancy most people thought

[019-177] it was a Carnival joke. Please don't think of that again."

[019-178] Nothing could be politer than all this.

[019-179] "And you won't allow me to punish myself for not being there to give you

[019-180] even a moral support?"

[019-181] "Certainly not. As I told Imogene, young people will be young people;

[019-182] and I knew how fond you were of dancing."

[019-183] Though it pierced him, Colville could not help admiring the neatness of

[019-184] this thrust. "I didn't know you were so ironical, Mrs. Bowen."

[019-185] "Ironical? Not at all."

[019-186] "Ah! I see I'm not forgiven."

[019-187] "I'm sure I don't know what you mean."

[019-188] Imogene and Effie came in. The child was a little pale, and willingly

[019-189] let him take her on his knee, and lay her languid head on his shoulder.

[019-190] The girl had not aged overnight like himself and Mrs. Bowen; she looked

[019-191] as fresh and strong as yesterday.

[019-192] "Miss Graham," said Colville, "if a person to whom you had done a deadly

[019-193] wrong insisted that you hadn't done any wrong at all, should you

[019-194] consider yourself forgiven?"

[019-195] "It would depend upon the person," said the girl, with innocent

[019-196] liveliness, recognising the extravagance in his tone.

[019-197] "Yes," he said, with an affected pensiveness, "so very much depends upon

[019-198] the person in such a case."
