# Europeanisation

## Definition

Europeanisation in *Indian Summer* designates the cultural process by which American characters — especially those who have lived for extended periods abroad — absorb European manners, social conventions, and child-rearing philosophies, and in doing so shed qualities that the novel treats as distinctively American: spontaneity, liberty, and an unselfconscious relation to nature and social life. The term appears most explicitly as a charge, a playful but pointed accusation: "Oh, you're Europeanised" (006-152). Yet the charge, once levelled, proves impossible to confine to a single target. It boomerangs: the accuser is accused in turn, and the novel reveals Europeanisation not as a fixed condition that some characters possess and others lack, but as a continuum of cultural transformation along which all the American characters are positioned.

The concept is not merely descriptive but evaluative. To be "Europeanised" is to have lost something — the "wild, sweet liberty which once made American girlhood a long rapture" (003-026 to 003-027) — and to have gained something else: refinement, propriety, a system of child-rearing that protects young women from the dangers of unchecked freedom. Howells declines to resolve the question of whether this exchange represents progress or decline, allowing the novel's characters to embody different positions on the spectrum and leaving the reader to negotiate among them.

## Context in the Novel

The charge of Europeanisation is first levelled by Theodore Colville against Lina Bowen during an evening discussion of American girlhood. Colville, the middle-aged newspaperman returned to Florence after seventeen years, articulates an ideal he associates with the American past: "the old national ideal of girlish liberty as wide as the continent, as fast as the Mississippi" (006-150 to 006-151). Mrs. Bowen, who has raised her daughter Effie in Europe under a regime of strict maternal supervision, withstands him "with delicate firmness" (006-152). Her reply is unapologetic: "I certainly prefer the European plan of bringing up girls. I shouldn't think of letting a daughter of mine have the freedom I had" (006-153 to 006-155). The exchange is structurally important because it refuses to treat the European alternative as a simple corruption of American innocence. Mrs. Bowen has known the frontier liberty herself — she was raised with it — and has deliberately rejected it. Her Europeanisation is a considered choice, not ignorance or affectation.

The novel's third chapter provides the most vivid dramatisation of Europeanised child-rearing in practice. When Colville asks the young Effie Bowen a conversational question, she answers "with a quick look of question at her mother, and checking herself in a possibly indiscreet smile" (003-015 to 003-017). Colville is amused "to see the child referring even this point of propriety to her mother" (003-018 to 003-019), and his amusement quickly darkens into elegy as he catalogues all the freedoms Effie will never know: the buggy rides, the unchaperoned picnics, the lingering at the gate with youthful escorts (003-020 to 003-027). The passage makes visible the mechanism of Europeanisation as a daily discipline of self-censorship and maternal reference — Effie has been trained to submit impulse to the filter of propriety, and the training has become instinct.

The charge of Europeanisation is turned back on Colville himself in Chapter 19, during the Carnival ball episode. When Colville, separated from Mrs. Bowen's party, urges discretion upon Imogene Graham, she demands: "Why, are you becoming Europeanised too? I thought you went in for all sorts of unconventionalities" (019-065 to 019-066). Imogene's accusation reveals the instability of the concept: the man who charged Mrs. Bowen with Europeanisation is now himself charged with the same transformation. Colville's immediate reflection — "that most young girls probably conform to the proprieties without in the least knowing why" (019-069 to 019-070) — suggests that Europeanisation may be less a conscious adoption of foreign values than an unconscious absorption of ambient social norms, operating below the level of articulated belief.

The novel extends the concept beyond child-rearing and manners to encompass a broader cultural contrast between American and European relationships to nature and authority. Imogene Graham, standing by the Arno, complains: "They do pen you up so from it in Europe!" (013-008), articulating a characteristically American desire for unmediated access to the natural world. This spatial constraint — being "penned up" — serves as a metaphor for the broader Europeanisation process: the replacement of American expansiveness with European enclosure, of frontier openness with Old World containment. Conversely, the novel also explores the limits of European tradition for understanding distinctively American figures. Mr. Waters, the ex-minister who has renounced his calling, cannot be understood "through European tradition" (015-077) — his very existence, combining sacerdotal office with radical doubt and personal freedom, requires an American interpretive frame.

## Key Passages

> They entered into a discussion which is always fruitful with Americans — the discussion of American girlhood, and Colville contended for the old national ideal of girlish liberty as wide as the continent, as fast as the Mississippi. Mrs. Bowen withstood him with delicate firmness. "Oh," he said, "you're Europeanised." "I certainly prefer the European plan of bringing up girls," she replied steadfastly. "I shouldn't think of letting a daughter of mine have the freedom I had." (006-148 to 006-155)

This passage is the novel's most explicit naming of the concept and its most concentrated dramatisation of the cultural conflict it encodes. Colville spatialises American identity through geographical imagery — the continent, the Mississippi — making the frontier the enabling condition of girlish liberty. Mrs. Bowen's reply replaces Colville's romantic nationalism with the practical language of child-rearing: "the European plan." Her use of "steadfastly" and her personal testimony — she has had "the freedom" and has chosen not to give it to her daughter — make her Europeanisation a position earned through experience rather than adopted through imitation.

> It amused him to see the child referring even this point of propriety to her mother, and his thoughts idled off to what Mrs. Bowen's own untrammelled girlhood must have been in her Western city. For her daughter there were to be no buggy rides, or concerts, or dances at the invitation of young men; no picnics, free and unchaperoned as the casing air; no sitting on the steps at dusk with callers who never dreamed of asking for her mother; no lingering at the gate with her youthful escort home from the ball — nothing of that wild, sweet liberty which once made American girlhood a long rapture. (003-018 to 003-027)

Colville's elegiac catalogue of denied freedoms is the novel's most lyrical rendering of what Europeanisation costs. Each item in the list — the buggy ride, the unchaperoned picnic, the dusk conversation on the steps — is drawn from the texture of American small-town courtship, and the accumulation builds toward the paragraph's climactic phrase: "wild, sweet liberty." The word "once" is the sentence's operative term, marking the temporal rupture at the heart of the novel's treatment of Europeanisation: the liberty it laments belongs irrecoverably to the past. Colville's "suppressed sigh for the inevitable change" (003-029 to 003-030) acknowledges that Europeanisation is not a reversible choice but a historical movement.

> "Why, are you becoming Europeanised too?" she demanded. "I thought you went in for all sorts of unconventionalities. Recollect your promise. You must be as impulsive as I am." Colville, staring anxiously about in every direction, made for the first time the reflection that most young girls probably conform to the proprieties without in the least knowing why. (019-065 to 019-070)

This passage reverses the direction of the accusation, revealing Europeanisation as a condition that can be attributed to anyone who invokes propriety as a guide to conduct. Imogene's linking of Europeanisation with conventionality — and of American identity with impulsiveness and unconventionality — mirrors Colville's own earlier logic, now turned against him. Colville's reflection that young girls "conform to the proprieties without in the least knowing why" extends the concept beyond conscious cultural preference into the realm of unconscious socialisation, suggesting that Europeanisation operates as much through absorbed habit as through deliberate adoption.

> "I don't want to throw stones into it, but to get near it — to get near to any bit of nature. They do pen you up so from it in Europe!" (013-006 to 013-008)

Imogene's exclamation articulates the spatial and experiential dimension of Europeanisation. The phrase "pen you up" evokes enclosure, constraint, and the regulation of access — a European landscape that is parcelled, owned, and mediated, contrasting with the American ideal of open, unmediated access to nature. The complaint extends the Europeanisation concept beyond manners and child-rearing to encompass a fundamental difference in how the two cultures structure the human relationship to the natural world.

## Related Concepts

- **American Girlhood** — The primary site where Europeanisation anxiety is dramatised and debated; the question of whether American girls should be raised according to the European plan or the old national ideal of frontier liberty is the novel's central vehicle for exploring the costs and benefits of Europeanisation.
- **Expatriate Identity** — Europeanisation is a specific form of the broader identity transformation that American characters undergo through extended European residence; the expatriate condition creates the circumstances under which Europeanisation becomes possible and visible.
- **Youth and Age** — The Europeanisation debate is inflected by generational position: the older characters debate what kind of cultural inheritance the younger generation should receive, and Colville's middle-aged perspective — nostalgic for the old American ideal yet attracted to European refinement — is constitutive of his ambivalent relationship to the concept.
- **Social Duty** — The propriety framework that Europeanised characters adopt — the deference to convention, the submission of impulse to social form — overlaps with the novel's broader treatment of duty and moral obligation, raising the question of whether Europeanisation represents a genuine ethical refinement or a surrender of American moral independence.
