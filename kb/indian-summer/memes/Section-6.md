[006-001] "Well, yes, I suppose I do. Other people."

[006-002] "No; but don't you like it for yourself?"

[006-003] "I can't tell; I haven't been through anything worth speaking of yet."

[006-004] [[imogene-graham|Miss Graham]] looked at him dubiously, but pursued with ardour: "Why, just

[006-005] getting back to [[florence|Florence]], after not having been here for so long--I

[006-006] should think it would be so romantic. Oh dear! I wish I were here for

[006-007] the second time."

[006-008] "I'm afraid you wouldn't like it so well," said [[theodore-colville|Colville]]. "I wish I were

[006-009] here for the first time. There's nothing like the first time in

[006-010] everything."

[006-011] "Do you really think so?"

[006-012] "Well, there's nothing like the first time in Florence."

[006-013] "Oh, I can't imagine it. I should think that recalling the old emotions

[006-014] would be perfectly fascinating."

[006-015] "Yes, if they'd come when you do call them. But they're as

[006-016] contrary-minded as spirits from the vasty deep. I've been shouting

[006-017] around here for my old emotions all day, and I haven't had a responsive

[006-018] squeak."

[006-019] "Oh!" cried Miss Graham, staring full-eyed at him. "How delightful!"

[006-020] [[effie-bowen|Effie Bowen]] turned away her pretty little head and laughed, as if it

[006-021] might not be quite kind to laugh at a person's joke to his face.

[006-022] Stimulated by their appreciation, Colville went on with more nonsense.

[006-023] "No; the only way to get at your old emotions in regard to Florence is

[006-024] to borrow them from somebody who's having them fresh. What do _you_

[006-025] think about Florence, Miss Graham?"

[006-026] "I? I've been here two months."

[006-027] "Then it's too late?"

[006-028] "No, I don't know that it is. I keep feeling the strangeness all the

[006-029] time. But I can't tell you. It's very different from Buffalo, I can

[006-030] assure you."

[006-031] "Buffalo? I can imagine the difference. And it's not altogether to the

[006-032] disadvantage of Buffalo."

[006-033] "Oh, have you been there?" asked Miss Graham, with a touching little

[006-034] eagerness. "Do you know anybody in Buffalo?"

[006-035] "Some of the newspaper men; and I pass through there once a year on my

[006-036] way to New York--or used to. It's a lively place."

[006-037] "Yes, it is," sighed Miss Graham fondly.

[006-038] "Do the girls of Buffalo still come out at night and dance by the light

[006-039] of the moon?"

[006-040] "What!"

[006-041] "Ah, I see," said Colville, peering at her under his thoughtfully

[006-042] knitted brows, "you do belong to another era. You don't remember the old

[006-043] negro minstrel song."

[006-044] "No," said Miss Graham. "I can only remember the end of the war."

[006-045] "How divinely young!" said Colville. "Well," he added, "I wish that

[006-046] French lady could have overheard us, Miss Graham. I think she would have

[006-047] changed her mind about Americans striking the note of personality in

[006-048] their talk."

[006-049] "Oh!" exclaimed the girl reproachfully, after a moment of swift

[006-050] reflection and recognition, "I don't see how you could let me do it! You

[006-051] don't suppose that I should have talked so with every one? It was

[006-052] because you were another American, and such an old friend of Mrs.

[006-053] Bowen's."

[006-054] "That is what I shall certainly tell the French lady if she attacks me

[006-055] about it," said Colville. He glanced carelessly toward the end of the

[006-056] room, and saw [[mr-morton|the young clergyman]] taking leave of [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]]; all the

[006-057] rest of the company were gone. "Bless me!" he said, "I must be going."

[006-058] Mrs. Bowen had so swiftly advanced upon him that she caught the last

[006-059] words. "Why?" she asked.

[006-060] "Because it's to-morrow, I suspect, and the invitation was for one day

[006-061] only."

[006-062] "It was a season ticket," said Mrs. Bowen, with gay hospitality, "and it

[006-063] isn't to-morrow for half an hour yet. I can't think of letting you go.

[006-064] Come up to the fire, all, and let's sit down by it. It's at its very

[006-065] best."

[006-066] Effie looked a pretty surprise and a pleasure in this girlish burst from

[006-067] her mother, whose habitual serenity made it more striking in contrast,

[006-068] and she forsook Miss Graham's hand and ran forward and disposed the

[006-069] easy-chairs comfortably about the hearth.

[006-070] Colville and Mrs. Bowen suddenly found themselves upon those terms which

[006-071] often succeed a long separation with people who have felt kindly toward

[006-072] each other at a former meeting and have parted friends: they were much

[006-073] more intimate than they had supposed themselves to be, or had really any

[006-074] reason for being.

[006-075] "Which one of your guests do you wish me to offer up, Mrs. Bowen?" he

[006-076] asked, from the hollow of the arm-chair, not too low, which he had sunk

[006-077] into. With Mrs. Bowen in a higher chair at his right hand, and Miss

[006-078] Graham intent upon him from the sofa on his left, a sense of delicious

[006-079] satisfaction filled him from head to foot. "There isn't one I would

[006-080] spare if you said the word."

[006-081] "And there isn't one I want destroyed, I'm sorry to say," answered Mrs.

[006-082] Bowen. "Don't you think they were all very agreeable?"

[006-083] "Yes, yes; agreeable enough--agreeable enough, I suppose. But they

[006-084] stayed too long. When I think we might have been sitting here for the

[006-085] last half-hour, if they'd only gone sooner, I find it pretty hard to

[006-086] forgive them."

[006-087] Mrs. Bowen and Miss Graham exchanged glances above his head--a glance

[006-088] which demanded, "Didn't I tell you?" for a glance that answered, "Oh, he

[006-089] _is_!" Effie Bowen's eyes widened; she kept them fastened upon Colville

[006-090] in silent worship.

[006-091] He asked who were certain of the company that he had noticed, and Mrs.

[006-092] Bowen let him make a little fun of them: the fun was very good-natured.

[006-093] He repeated what the German had said about the worldly ambition of

[006-094] [[american-girlhood|American girls]]; but she would not allow him so great latitude in this.

[006-095] She said they were no worldlier than other girls. Of course, they were

[006-096] fond of society, and some of them got a little spoiled. But they were in

[006-097] no danger of becoming too conventional.

[006-098] Colville did not insist. "I missed the military to-night, Mrs. Bowen,"

[006-099] he said. "I thought one couldn't get through an evening in Florence

[006-100] without officers?"

[006-101] "We have them when there is dancing," returned Mrs. Bowen.

[006-102] "Yes, but they don't know anything but dancing," Miss Graham broke in.

[006-103] "I like some one who can talk something besides compliments."

[006-104] "You are very peculiar, you know, Imogene," urged Mrs. Bowen gently. "I

[006-105] don't think our young men at home do much better in conversation, if you

[006-106] come to that, though."

[006-107] "Oh, _young_ men, yes! They're the same everywhere. But here, even when

[006-108] they're away along in the thirties, they think that girls can only enjoy

[006-109] flattery. _I_ should like a gentleman to talk to me without a single

[006-110] word or look to show that he thought I was good-looking."

[006-111] "Ah, how could he be?" Colville insinuated, and the young girl coloured.

[006-112] "I mean, if I were pretty. This everlasting adulation is insulting."

[006-113] "Mr. Morton doesn't flatter," said Mrs. Bowen thoughtfully, turning the

[006-114] feather screen she held at her face, now edgewise, now flatwise, toward

[006-115] Colville.

[006-116] "Oh no," owned Miss Graham. "He's a clergyman."

[006-117] Mrs. Bowen addressed herself to Colville. "You must go to hear him some

[006-118] day. He's very interesting, if you don't mind his being rather Low

[006-119] Church."

[006-120] Colville was going to pretend to an advanced degree of ritualism; but it

[006-121] occurred to him that it might be a serious matter to Mrs. Bowen, and he

[006-122] asked instead who was the [[mr-waters|Rev. Mr. Waters]].

[006-123] "Oh, isn't he lovely?" cried Miss Graham. "There, Mrs. Bowen! Mr.

[006-124] Waters's manner is what I call _truly_ complimentary. He always talks to

[006-125] you as if he expected you to be interested in serious matters, and as if

[006-126] you were his intellectual equal. And he's so _happy_ here in Florence!

[006-127] He gives you the impression of feeling every breath he breathes here a

[006-128] privilege. You ought to hear him talk about [[girolamo-savonarola|Savonarola]], Mr. Colville."

[006-129] "Well," said Colville, "I've heard a great many people talk about

[006-130] Savonarola, and I'm rather glad he talked to me about American girls."

[006-131] "American girls!" uttered Miss Graham, in a little scream. "Did Mr.

[006-132] Waters talk to you about _girls_?"

[006-133] "Yes. Why not? He was probably in love with one once."

[006-134] "Mr. Waters?" cried the girl. "What nonsense!"

[006-135] "Well, then, with some old lady. Would you like that better?"

[006-136] Miss Graham looked at Mrs. Bowen for permission, as it seemed, and then

[006-137] laughed, but did not attempt any reply to Colville.

[006-138] "You find even that incredible of such pyramidal antiquity," he resumed.

[006-139] "Well, it _is_ hard to believe. I told him what that German said, and we

[006-140] agreed beautifully about another type of American girl which we said we

[006-141] preferred."

[006-142] "Oh! What could it be?" demanded Miss Graham.

[006-143] "Ah, it wouldn't be so easy to say right off-hand," answered Colville

[006-144] indolently.

[006-145] Mrs. Bowen put her hand under the elbow of the arm holding her screen.

[006-146] "I don't believe I should agree with you so well," she said, apparently

[006-147] with a sort of didactic intention.

[006-148] They entered into a discussion which is always fruitful with

[006-149] Americans--the discussion of American girlhood, and Colville contended

[006-150] for the old national ideal of girlish liberty as wide as the continent,

[006-151] as fast as the Mississippi. Mrs. Bowen withstood him with delicate

[006-152] firmness. "Oh," he said, "you're [[europeanisation|Europeanised]]."

[006-153] "I certainly prefer the European plan of bringing up girls," she replied

[006-154] steadfastly. "I shouldn't think of letting a daughter of mine have the

[006-155] freedom I had."

[006-156] "Well, perhaps it will come right in the next generation, then; she will

[006-157] let her daughter have the freedom she hadn't."

[006-158] "Not if I'm alive to prevent it," cried Mrs. Bowen.

[006-159] Colville laughed. "Which plan do you prefer, Miss Graham?"

[006-160] "I don't think it's quite the same now as it used to be," answered the

[006-161] girl evasively.

[006-162] "Well, then, all I can say is that if I had died before this chance, I

[006-163] had lived a blessed time. I perceive more and more that I'm obsolete.

[006-164] I'm in my dotage; I prattle of the good old times, and the new spirit of

[006-165] the age flouts me. Miss Effie, do you prefer the Amer----"

[006-166] "No, thank you," said her mother quickly.

[006-167] "Effie is out of the question. It's time you were in bed, Effie."

[006-168] The child came with instant submissiveness and kissed her mother

[006-169] good-night; she kissed Miss Graham, and gave her hand to Colville. He

[006-170] held it a moment, letting her pull shyly away from him, while he lolled

[006-171] back in his chair, and laughed at her with his sad eyes. "It's past the

[006-172] time _I_ should be in bed, my dear, and I'm sitting up merely because

[006-173] there's nobody to send me. It's not that I'm really such a very bad boy.

[006-174] Good night. Don't put me into a disagreeable dream; put me into a nice

[006-175] one." The child bridled at the mild pleasantry, and when Colville

[006-176] released her hand she suddenly stooped forward and kissed him.

[006-177] "You're so _funny_!" she cried, and ran and escaped beyond the

[006-178] _portière_.

[006-179] Mrs. Bowen stared in the same direction, but not with severity. "Really,

[006-180] Effie has been carried a little beyond herself."

[006-181] "Well," said Colville, "that's _one_ conquest since I came to Florence.

[006-182] And merely by being funny! When I was in Florence before, Mrs. Bowen,"

[006-183] he continued, after a moment, "there were two ladies here, and I used to

[006-184] go about quite freely with either of them. They were both very pretty,

[006-185] and we were all very young. Don't you think it was charming?" Mrs. Bowen

[006-186] coloured a lovely red, and smiled, but made no other response. "Florence

[006-187] has changed very much for the worse since that time. There used to be a

[006-188] pretty flower-girl, with a wide-flapping straw hat, who flung a heavy

[006-189] bough full of roses into my lap when she met me driving across the

[006-190] Carraja bridge. I spent an hour looking for that girl to-day, and

[006-191] couldn't find her. The only flower-girl I could find was a fat one of

[006-192] fifty, who kept me fifteen minutes in Via Tornabuoni while she was

[006-193] fumbling away at my button-hole, trying to poke three second-hand

[006-194] violets and a sickly daisy into it. Ah, youth! youth! I suppose a young

[006-195] fellow could have found that other flower-girl at a glance; but _my_ old

[006-196] eyes! No, we belong, each of us, to our own generation. Mrs. Bowen," he

[006-197] said, with a touch of tragedy--whether real or affected, he did not well

[006-198] know himself--in his hardiness, "what has become of Mrs. Pilsbury?"
