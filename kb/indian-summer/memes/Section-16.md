[016-001] "Awfully," said [[theodore-colville|Colville]], going to the window with her, and putting his

[016-002] arm on Effie's shoulder, where she knelt in a chair looking out. "What

[016-003] have you seen?"

[016-004] "Oh, only two Spanish students with mandolins," said [[imogene-graham|Imogene]]; "but you

[016-005] can see they're _beginning_ to come."

[016-006] "They'll stop now," murmured Effie, with gentle disappointment; "it's

[016-007] commencing to rain."

[016-008] "Oh, too bad!" wailed the young girl. But just then two mediaeval

[016-009] men-at-arms came in sight, carrying umbrellas. "Isn't that too

[016-010] delicious? Umbrellas and chain-armour!"

[016-011] "You can't expect them to let their chain-armour get rusty," said

[016-012] Colville. "You ought to have been with me--minstrels in scale-armour,

[016-013] Florentines of [[girolamo-savonarola|Savonarola]]'s times, nuns, clowns, demons, fairies--no end

[016-014] to them."

[016-015] "It's very well saying we ought to have been with you; but we can't go

[016-016] anywhere alone."

[016-017] "I didn't say alone," said Colville. "Don't you think [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]] would

[016-018] trust you with me to see these Carnival beginnings?" He had not meant at

[016-019] all to do anything of this kind, but that had not prevented his doing

[016-020] it.

[016-021] "How do we know, when she hasn't been asked?" said Imogene, with a touch

[016-022] of burlesque dolor, such as makes a dignified girl enchanting, when she

[016-023] permits it to herself. She took Effie's hand in hers, the child having

[016-024] faced round from the window, and stood smoothing it, with her lovely

[016-025] head pathetically tilted on one side.

[016-026] "What haven't I been asked yet?" demanded Mrs. Bowen, coming lightly

[016-027] toward them from a door at the side of the _salon_. She gave her hand to

[016-028] Colville with the prettiest grace, and a cordiality that brought a flush

[016-029] to her cheek. There had really been nothing between them but a little

[016-030] unreasoned coolness, if it were even so much as that; say rather a

[016-031] dryness, aggravated by time and absence, and now, as friends do, after a

[016-032] thing of that kind, they were suddenly glad to be good to each other.

[016-033] "Why, you haven't been asked how you have been this long time," said

[016-034] Colville.

[016-035] "I have been wanting to tell you for a whole week," returned Mrs. Bowen,

[016-036] seating the rest and taking a chair for herself. "Where have you been?"

[016-037] "Oh, shut up in my cell at [[hotel-datene|Hotel d'Atene]], writing a short history of the

[016-038] Florentine people for Miss Effie."

[016-039] "Effie, take Mr. Colville's hat," said her mother. "We're going to make

[016-040] you stay to lunch," she explained to him.

[016-041] "Is that so?" he asked, with an effect of polite curiosity.

[016-042] "Yes." Imogene softly clapped her hands, unseen by Mrs. Bowen, for

[016-043] Colville's instruction that all was going well. If it delights women to

[016-044] pet an undangerous friend of our sex, to use him like one of themselves,

[016-045] there are no words to paint the soft and flattered content with which

[016-046] his spirit purrs under their caresses. "You must have nearly finished

[016-047] the history," added Mrs. Bowen.

[016-048] "Well, I could have finished it," said Colville, "if I had only begun

[016-049] it. You see, writing a short history of the Florentine people is such

[016-050] quick work that you have to be careful how you actually put pen to

[016-051] paper, or you're through with it before you've had any fun out of it."

[016-052] "I think Effie will like to read that kind of history," said her mother.

[016-053] The child hung her head, and would not look at Colville; she was still

[016-054] shy with him; his absence must have seemed longer to a child, of course.

[016-055] At lunch they talked of the Carnival sights that had begun to appear. He

[016-056] told of his call upon [[mr-waters|Mr. Waters]], and of the old minister's purpose to

[016-057] see all he could of the Carnival in order to judge intelligently of

[016-058] Savonarola's opposition to it.

[016-059] "Mr. Waters is a very good man," said Mrs. Bowen, with the air of not

[016-060] meaning to approve him quite, nor yet to let any notion of his be made

[016-061] fun of in her presence. "But for my part I wish there were not going to

[016-062] be any Carnival; the city will be in such an uproar for the next two

[016-063] weeks."

[016-064] "O Mrs. Bowen!" cried Imogene reproachfully; Effie looked at her mother

[016-065] in apparent anxiety lest she should be meaning to put forth an

[016-066] unquestionable power and stop the Carnival.

[016-067] "The last Carnival, I thought there was never going to be any end to it;

[016-068] I was so glad when Lent came."

[016-069] "Glad when _Lent_ came!" breathed Imogene, in astonishment; but she

[016-070] ventured upon nothing more insubordinate, and Colville admired to see

[016-071] this spirited girl as subject to Mrs. Bowen as her own child. There is

[016-072] no reason why one woman should establish another woman over her, but

[016-073] nearly all women do it in one sort or another, from love of a voluntary

[016-074] submission, or from a fear of their own ignorance, if they are younger

[016-075] and more inexperienced than their lieges. Neither the one passion nor

[016-076] the other seems to reduce them to a like passivity as regards their

[016-077] husbands. They must apparently have a fetish of their own sex. Colville

[016-078] could see that Imogene obeyed Mrs. Bowen not only as a _protégée_ but as

[016-079] a devotee.

[016-080] "Oh, I suppose _you_ will have to go through it all," said Mrs. Bowen,

[016-081] in reward of the girl's acquiescence.

[016-082] "You're rather out of the way of it up here," said Colville. "You had

[016-083] better let me go about with the young ladies--if you can trust them to

[016-084] the care of an old fellow like me."

[016-085] "Oh, I don't think you're so very old, at all times," replied Mrs.

[016-086] Bowen, with a peculiar look, whether indulgent or reproachful he could

[016-087] not quite make out.

[016-088] But he replied, boldly, in his turn: "I have certainly my moments of

[016-089] being young still; I don't deny it. There's always a danger of their

[016-090] occurrence."

[016-091] "I was thinking," said Mrs. Bowen, with a graceful effect of not

[016-092] listening, "that you would let me go too. It would be quite like old

[016-093] times."

[016-094] "Only too much honour and pleasure," returned Colville, "if you will

[016-095] leave out the old times. I'm not particular about having them along."

[016-096] Mrs. Bowen joined in laughing at the joke, which they had to themselves.

[016-097] "I was only consulting an explicit abhorrence of yours in not asking you

[016-098] to go at first," he explained.

[016-099] "Oh yes; I understand that."

[016-100] The excellence of the whole arrangement seemed to grow upon Mrs. Bowen.

[016-101] "Of course," she said, "Imogene ought to see all she can of the

[016-102] Carnival. She may not have another chance, and perhaps if she had, _he_

[016-103] wouldn't consent."

[016-104] "I'll engage to get _his_ consent," said the girl. "What I was afraid of

[016-105] was that I couldn't get yours, Mrs. Bowen."

[016-106] "Am I so severe as that?" asked Mrs. Bowen softly.

[016-107] "Quite," replied Imogene.

[016-108] "Perhaps," thought Colville, "it isn't always silent submission."

[016-109] For no very good reason that any one could give, the Carnival that year

[016-110] was not a brilliant one. Colville's party seemed to be always meeting

[016-111] the same maskers on the street, and the maskers did not greatly increase

[016-112] in numbers. There were a few more of them after nightfall, but they were

[016-113] then a little more bacchanal, and he felt it was better that the ladies

[016-114] had gone home by that time. In the pursuit of the tempered pleasure of

[016-115] looking up the maskers he was able to make the reflection that their

[016-116] fantastic and vivid dresses sympathised in a striking way with the

[016-117] architecture of the city, and gave him an effect of [[florence|Florence]] which he

[016-118] could not otherwise have had. There came by and by a little attempt at a

[016-119] _corso_ in Via Cerratani and Via Tornabuoni. There were some masks in

[016-120] carriages, and from one they actually threw plaster _confetti_; half a

[016-121] dozen bare-legged boys ran before and beat one another with bladders,

[016-122] Some people, but not many, watched the show from the windows, and the

[016-123] footways were crowded.

[016-124] Having proposed that they should see the Carnival together, Colville had

[016-125] made himself responsible for it to the Bowen household. Imogene said,

[016-126] "Well is this the famous Carnival of Florence?"

[016-127] "It certainly doesn't compare with the Carnival last year," said Mrs.

[016-128] Bowen.

[016-129] "Your reproach is just, Mrs. Bowen," he acknowledged. "I've managed it

[016-130] badly. But you know I've been out of practice a great while there in Des

[016-131] Vaches."

[016-132] "Oh, poor Mr. Colville!" cried Imogene. "He isn't altogether to blame."

[016-133] "I don't know," said Mrs. Bowen, humouring the joke in her turn. "It

[016-134] seems to me that if he had consulted us a little earlier, he might have

[016-135] done better."

[016-136] He drove home with the ladies, and Mrs. Bowen made him stay to tea. As

[016-137] if she felt that he needed to be consoled for the failure of his

[016-138] Carnival, she was especially indulgent with him. She played to him on

[016-139] the piano some of the songs that were in fashion when they were in

[016-140] Florence together before.

[016-141] Imogene had never heard them; she had heard her mother speak of them.

[016-142] One or two of them were negro songs, such as very pretty young ladies

[016-143] used to sing without harm to themselves or offence to others; but

[016-144] Imogene decided that they were rather rowdy. "Dear me, Mrs. Bowen! Did

[016-145] _you_ sing such songs? You wouldn't let Effie!"

[016-146] "No, I wouldn't let Effie. The times are changed. I wouldn't let Effie

[016-147] go to the theatre alone with a young gentleman."

[016-148] "The times are changed for the worse," Colville began. "What harm ever

[016-149] came to a young man from a young lady's going alone to the theatre with

[016-150] him?"

[016-151] He stayed till the candles were brought in, and then went away only

[016-152] because, as he said, they had not asked him to stay to dinner.

[016-153] He came nearly every day, upon one pretext or another, and he met them

[016-154] oftener than that at the teas and on the days of other ladies in

[016-155] Florence; for he was finding the busy idleness of the life very

[016-156] pleasant, and he went everywhere. He formed the habit of carrying

[016-157] flowers to the [[palazzo-pinti|Palazzo Pinti]], excusing himself on the ground that they

[016-158] were so cheap and so abundant as to be impersonal. He brought violets to

[016-159] Effie and roses to Imogene; to Mrs. Bowen he always brought a bunch of

[016-160] the huge purple anemones which grow so abundantly all winter long about

[016-161] Florence. "I wonder why _purple_ anemones?" he asked her one day in

[016-162] presenting them to her.

[016-163] "Oh, it is quite time I should be wearing purple," she said gently.

[016-164] "Ah, Mrs. Bowen!" he reproached her. "Why do I bring purple violets to

[016-165] Miss Effie?"

[016-166] "You must ask Effie!" said Mrs. Bowen, with a laugh.

[016-167] After that he stayed away forty-eight hours, and then appeared with a

[016-168] bunch of the red anemones, as large as tulips, which light up the meadow

[016-169] grass when it begins to stir from its torpor in the spring. "They grew

[016-170] on purpose to set me right with you," he said, "and I saw them when I

[016-171] was in the country."

[016-172] It was a little triumph for him, which she celebrated by putting them in

[016-173] a vase on her table, and telling people who exclaimed over them that

[016-174] they were some Mr. Colville gathered in the country. He enjoyed his

[016-175] privileges at her house with the futureless satisfaction of a man. He

[016-176] liked to go about with the Bowens; he was seen with the ladies driving

[016-177] and walking, in most of their promenades. He directed their visits to

[016-178] the churches and the galleries; he was fond of strolling about with

[016-179] Effie's daintily-gloved little hand in his. He took her to Giocosa's and

[016-180] treated her to ices; he let her choose from the confectioner's prettiest

[016-181] caprices in candy; he was allowed to bring the child presents in his

[016-182] pockets. Perhaps he was not as conscientious as he might have been in

[016-183] his behaviour with the little girl. He did what he could to spoil her,

[016-184] or at least to relax the severity of the training she had received; he

[016-185] liked to see the struggle that went on in the mother's mind against

[016-186] this, and then the other struggle with which she overcame her opposition

[016-187] to it. The worst he did was to teach Effie some picturesque Western

[016-188] phrases, which she used with innocent effectiveness; she committed the

[016-189] crimes against convention which he taught her with all the conventional

[016-190] elegance of her training. The most that he ever gained for her were some

[016-191] concessions in going out in weather that her mother thought unfit, or

[016-192] sitting up for half-hours after her bed-time. He ordered books for her

[016-193] from Goodban's, and it was Colville now, and not the [[mr-morton|Rev. Mr. Morton]],

[016-194] who read poetry aloud to the ladies on afternoons when Mrs. Bowen gave

[016-195] orders that she and Miss Graham should be denied to all other comers.
