[031-001] "Do you think he wouldn't like it?"

[031-002] "Yes."

[031-003] "Well, it would become him. How old do you suppose he is, [[imogene-graham|Imogene]]?

[031-004] Seventy-five?"

[031-005] "What an idea!" cried the girl fiercely. "He's forty-one."

[031-006] "I didn't know they had those little jiggering lines at the corners of

[031-007] their eyes so quick. But forty-one is pretty old, isn't it? Is Mr.

[031-008] Waters--"

[031-009] "Effie," said her mother's voice at the door behind her, "will you ring

[031-010] for Giovanni, and tell him to bring me a cup of coffee in here?" She

[031-011] spoke from the _portière_ of the _salotto_.

[031-012] "Yes, mamma. I'll bring it to you myself."

[031-013] "Thank you, dear," [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]] called from within.

[031-014] The little girl softly pressed her hands together. "I _hope_ she'll let

[031-015] me stay up! I feel so excited, and I hate to lie and think so long

[031-016] before I get to sleep. Couldn't you just hint a little to her that I

[031-017] might stay up? It's Sunday night."

[031-018] "I can't, Effie," said Imogene. "I oughtn't to interfere with any of

[031-019] your mother's rules."

[031-020] The child sighed submissively and took the coffee that Giovanni brought

[031-021] to her. She and Imogene went into the _salotto_ together. Mrs. Bowen was

[031-022] at her writing-desk. "You can bring the coffee here, Effie," she said.

[031-023] "Must I go to bed at once, mamma?" asked the child, setting the cup

[031-024] carefully down.

[031-025] The mother looked distractedly up from her writing. "No; you may sit up

[031-026] a while," she said, looking back to her writing.

[031-027] "How long, mamma?" pleaded the little girl.

[031-028] "Oh, till you're sleepy. It doesn't matter _now_."

[031-029] She went on writing; from time to time she tore up what she had written.

[031-030] Effie softly took a book from the table, and perching herself on a

[031-031] stiff, high chair, bent over it and began to read.

[031-032] Imogene sat by the hearth, where a small fire was pleasant in the indoor

[031-033] chill of an Italian house, even after so warm a day as that had been.

[031-034] She took some large beads of the strand she wore about her neck into her

[031-035] mouth, and pulled at the strand listlessly with her hand while she

[031-036] watched the fire. Her eyes wandered once to the child.

[031-037] "What made you take such an uncomfortable chair, Effie?"

[031-038] Effie shut her book over her hand. "It keeps me wakeful longer," she

[031-039] whispered, with a glance at her mother from the corner of her eye.

[031-040] "I don't see why any one should wish to be wakeful," sighed the girl.

[031-041] When Mrs. Bowen tore up one of her half-written pages Imogene started

[031-042] nervously forward, and then relapsed again into her chair. At last Mrs.

[031-043] Bowen seemed to find the right phrases throughout, and she finished

[031-044] rather a long letter, and read it over to herself. Then she said,

[031-045] without leaving her desk, "Imogene, I've been trying to write to your

[031-046] mother. Will you look at this?"

[031-047] She held the sheet over her shoulder, and Imogene came languidly and

[031-048] took it; Mrs. Bowen dropped her face forward on the desk, into her

[031-049] hands, while Imogene was reading.

[031-050] "FLORENCE, _March_ 10, 18--

[031-051] "Dear Mrs. Graham,--I have some very important news to give you in

[031-052] regard to Imogene, and as there is no way of preparing you for it, I

[031-053] will tell you at once that it relates to her marriage.

[031-054] "She has met at my house a gentleman whom I knew in [[florence|Florence]] when I was

[031-055] here before, and of whom I never knew anything but good. We have seen

[031-056] him very often, and I have seen nothing in him that I could not approve.

[031-057] He is Mr. [[theodore-colville|Theodore Colville]], of [[des-vaches|Prairie des Vaches]], Indiana, where he

[031-058] was for many years a newspaper editor; but he was born somewhere in New

[031-059] England. He is a very cultivated, interesting man; and though not

[031-060] exactly a society man, he is very agreeable and refined in his manners.

[031-061] I am sure his character is irreproachable, though he is not a member of

[031-062] any church. In regard to his means I know nothing whatever, and can only

[031-063] infer from his way of life that he is in easy circumstances.

[031-064] "The whole matter has been a surprise to me, for Mr. Colville is some

[031-065] twenty-one or two years older than Imogene, who is very young in her

[031-066] feelings for a girl of her age. If I could have realised anything like a

[031-067] serious attachment between them sooner, I would have written before.

[031-068] Even now I do not know whether I am to consider them engaged or not. No

[031-069] doubt Imogene will write you more fully.

[031-070] "Of course I would rather not have had anything of the kind happen while

[031-071] Imogene was under my charge, though I am sure that you will not think I

[031-072] have been careless or imprudent about her. I interfered as far as I

[031-073] could, at the first moment I could, but it appears that it was then too

[031-074] late to prevent what has followed.--Yours sincerely, EVALINA BOWEN."

[031-075] Imogene read the letter twice over, and then she said, "Why isn't he a

[031-076] society man?"

[031-077] Probably Mrs. Bowen expected this sort of approach. "I don't think a

[031-078] society man would have undertaken to dance the Lancers as he did at

[031-079] Madam Uccelli's," she answered patiently, without lifting her head.

[031-080] Imogene winced, but "I should despise him if he were merely a society

[031-081] man," she said. "I have seen enough of them. I think it's better to be

[031-082] intellectual and good."

[031-083] Mrs. Bowen made no reply, and the girl went on. "And as to his being

[031-084] older, I don't see what difference it makes. If people are in sympathy,

[031-085] then they are of the same age, no difference how much older than one the

[031-086] other is. I have always heard that." She urged this as if it were a

[031-087] question.

[031-088] "Yes," said Mrs. Bowen.

[031-089] "And how should his having been a newspaper editor be anything against

[031-090] him?"

[031-091] Mrs. Bowen lifted her face and stared at the girl in astonishment. "Who

[031-092] said it was against him?"

[031-093] "You hint as much. The whole letter is against him."

[031-094] "Imogene!"

[031-095] "Yes! Every word! You make him out perfectly detestable. I don't know

[031-096] why you should hate _him_, He's done everything he could to satisfy

[031-097] you."

[031-098] Mrs. Bowen rose from her desk, putting her hand to her forehead, as if

[031-099] to soften a shock of headache that her change of posture had sent there.

[031-100] "I will leave the letter with you, and you can send it or not as you

[031-101] think best. It's merely a formality, my writing to your mother. Perhaps

[031-102] you'll see it differently in the morning. Effie!" she called to the

[031-103] child, who with her book shut upon her hand had been staring at them and

[031-104] listening intently. "It's time to go to bed now."

[031-105] When Effie stood before the glass in her mother's room, and Mrs. Bowen

[031-106] was braiding her hair and tying it up for the night, she asked ruefully,

[031-107] "What's the matter with Imogene, mamma?"

[031-108] "She isn't very happy to-night."

[031-109] "You don't seem very happy either," said the child, watching her own

[031-110] face as it quivered in the mirror. "I should think that now Mr.

[031-111] Colville's concluded to stay, we would all be happy again. But we don't

[031-112] seem to. We're--we're perfectly demoralised!" It was one of the words

[031-113] she had picked up from Colville.

[031-114] The quivering face in the glass broke in a passion of tears, and Effie

[031-115] sobbed herself to sleep.

[031-116] Imogene sat down at Mrs. Bowen's desk, and pushing her letter away,

[031-117] began to write.

[031-118] "FLORENCE, _March_ 10, 18--.

[031-119] "DEAR MOTHER,---I inclose a letter from Mrs. Bowen which will tell you

[031-120] better than I can what I wish to tell. I do not see how I can add

[031-121] anything that would give you more of an idea of him, or less, either. No

[031-122] person can be put down in cold black and white, and not seem like a mere

[031-123] inventory. I do not suppose you expected me to become engaged when you

[031-124] sent me out to Florence, and, as Mrs. Bowen says, I don't know whether I

[031-125] am engaged or not. I will leave it entirely to Mr. Colville; if he says

[031-126] we are engaged, we are. I am sure he will do what is best. I only know

[031-127] that he was going away from Florence because he thought I supposed he

[031-128] was not in earnest, and I asked him to stay.

[031-129] "I am a good deal excited to-night, and cannot write very clearly. But I

[031-130] will write soon again, and more at length.

[031-131] "Perhaps something will be decided by that time. With much love to

[031-132] father,

[031-133] "Your affectionate daughter,

[031-134] "Imogene."

[031-135] She put this letter into an envelope with Mrs. Bowen's, and leaving it

[031-136] unsealed to show her in the morning, she began to write again. This time

[031-137] she wrote to a girl with whom she had been on terms so intimate that

[031-138] when they left school they had agreed to know each other by names

[031-139] expressive of their extremely confidential friendship, and to address

[031-140] each other respectively as Diary and Journal. They were going to write

[031-141] every day, if only a line or two; and at the end of a year they were to

[031-142] meet and read over together the records of their lives as set down in

[031-143] these letters. They had never met since, though it was now three years

[031-144] since they parted, and they had not written since Imogene came abroad;

[031-145] that is, Imogene had not answered the only letter she had received from

[031-146] her friend in Florence. This friend was a very serious girl, and had

[031-147] wished to be a minister, but her family would not consent, or even

[031-148] accept the compromise of studying medicine, which she proposed, and she

[031-149] was still living at home in a small city of central New York. Imogene

[031-150] now addressed her--

[031-151] "DEAR DIARY,--You cannot think how far away the events of this day have

[031-152] pushed the feelings and ideas of the time when I agreed to write to you

[031-153] under this name. Till now it seems to me as if I had not changed in the

[031-154] least thing since we parted, and now I can hardly know myself for the

[031-155] same person. O dear Di! something very wonderful has come into my life,

[031-156] and I feel that it rests with me to make it the greatest blessing to

[031-157] myself and others, or the greatest misery. If I prove unworthy of it or

[031-158] unequal to it, then I am sure that nothing but wretchedness will come of

[031-159] it.

[031-160] "I am engaged--yes!--and to a man more than twice my own age. It is so

[031-161] easy to tell you this, for I know that your large-mindedness will

[031-162] receive it very differently from most people, and that you will see it

[031-163] as I do. He is the noblest of men, though he tries to conceal it under

[031-164] the light, ironical manner with which he has been faithful to a cruel

[031-165] disappointment. It was here in Florence, twenty years ago, that a

[031-166] girl--I am ashamed to call her a girl--trifled with the priceless

[031-167] treasure that has fallen to me, and flung it away. You, Di, will

[031-168] understand how I was first fascinated with the idea of trying to atone

[031-169] to him here for all the wrong he had suffered. At first it was only the

[031-170] vaguest suggestion--something like what I had read in a poem or a

[031-171] novel--that had nothing to do with me personally, but it grew upon me

[031-172] more and more the more I saw of him, and felt the witchery of his light,

[031-173] indifferent manner, which I learned to see was tense with the anguish he

[031-174] had suffered. She had killed his youth; she had spoiled his life: if I

[031-175] could revive them, restore them! It came upon me like a great flash of

[031-176] light at last, and as soon as this thought took possession of me, I felt

[031-177] my whole being elevated and purified by it, and I was enabled to put

[031-178] aside with contempt the selfish considerations that had occurred to me

[031-179] at first. At first the difference between our ages was very shocking to

[031-180] me; for I had always imagined it would be some one young; but when this

[031-181] light broke upon me, I saw that _he_ was young, younger even than I, as

[031-182] a man is at the same age with a girl. Sometimes with my experiences, the

[031-183] fancies and flirtations that every one has and _must_ have, however one

[031-184] despises them, I felt so _old_ beside him; for he had been true to one

[031-185] love all his life, and he had not wavered for a moment. If I could make

[031-186] him forget it, if I could lift every feather's weight of sorrow from his

[031-187] breast, if I could help him to complete the destiny, grand and beautiful

[031-188] as it would have been, which another had arrested, broken off--don't you

[031-189] see, Di dear, how rich my reward would be?
