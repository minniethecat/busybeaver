[021-001] "I will not have it left to me!" cried [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]]. "It is hard enough,

[021-002] at any rate. Do you think I like to speak to you?"

[021-003] "No."

[021-004] "Of course it makes me seem inhospitable, and distrustful, and

[021-005] detestable."

[021-006] "I never thought of accusing you," said the girl, slowly lifting her

[021-007] eyes.

[021-008] "I will never, never speak to you of it again," said Mrs. Bowen, "and

[021-009] from this time forth I insist upon your feeling just as free as if I

[021-010] hadn't spoken." She trembled upon the verge of a sob, from which she

[021-011] repelled herself.

[021-012] [[imogene-graham|Imogene]] sat still, with a sort of serious, bewildered look.

[021-013] "You shall have every proper opportunity of meeting any one you like."

[021-014] "Oh yes."

[021-015] "And I shall be only too gl-glad to take back everything!"

[021-016] Imogene sat motionless and silent. Mrs. Bowen broke out again with a

[021-017] sort of violence; the years teach us something of self-control, perhaps,

[021-018] but they weaken and unstring the nerves. In this opposition of silence

[021-019] to silence, the woman of the world was no match for the inexperienced

[021-020] girl.

[021-021] "Have you nothing to say, Imogene?"

[021-022] "I never thought of him in that way at all. I don't know what to say

[021-023] yet. It--confuses me. I--I can't imagine it. But if you think that he is

[021-024] trying to amuse himself----"

[021-025] "I never said that!"

[021-026] "No, I know it."

[021-027] "He likes to make you talk, and to talk with you. But he is perfectly

[021-028] idle here, and--there is too much difference, every way. The very good

[021-029] in him makes it the worse. I suppose that after talking with him every

[021-030] one else seems insipid."

[021-031] "Yes."

[021-032] Mrs. Bowen rose and ran suddenly from the room.

[021-033] Imogene remained sitting cold and still.

[021-034] No one had been named since they spoke of [[mr-morton|Mr. Morton]].

[021-035] XI

[021-036] [[theodore-colville|Colville]] had not done what he meant in going to Mrs. Bowen's; in fact,

[021-037] he had done just what he had not meant to do, as he distinctly perceived

[021-038] in coming away. It was then that in a luminous retrospect he discovered

[021-039] his motive to have been a wish to atone to her for behaviour that must

[021-040] have distressed her, or at least to explain it to her. She had not let

[021-041] him do this at once; an instant willingness to hear and to condone was

[021-042] not in a woman's nature; she had to make him feel, by the infliction of

[021-043] a degree of punishment, that she had suffered. But before she ended she

[021-044] had made it clear that she was ready to grant him a tacit pardon, and he

[021-045] had answered with a silly sarcasm the question that was to have led to

[021-046] peace. He could not help seeing that throughout the whole Carnival

[021-047] adventure she had yielded her cherished reluctances to please him, to

[021-048] show him that she was not stiff or prudish, to convince him that she

[021-049] would not be a killjoy through her devotion to conventionalities which

[021-050] she thought he despised. He could not help seeing that he had abused her

[021-051] delicate generosity, insulted her subtle concessions. He strolled along

[021-052] down the [[arno-river|Arno]], feeling flat and mean, as a man always does after a

[021-053] contest with a woman in which he has got the victory; our sex can

[021-054] preserve its self-respect only through defeat in such a case. It gave

[021-055] him no pleasure to remember that the glamour of the night before seemed

[021-056] still to rest on Imogene unbroken; that, indeed, was rather an added

[021-057] pain. He surprised himself in the midst of his poignant reflections by a

[021-058] yawn. Clearly the time was past when these ideal troubles could keep him

[021-059] awake, and there was, after all, a sort of brutal consolation in the

[021-060] fact. He was forty-one years old, and he was sleepy, whatever capacity

[021-061] for suffering remained to him. He went to his hotel to catch a little

[021-062] nap before lunch. When he woke it was dinner-time. The mists of slumber

[021-063] still hung about him, and the events of the last forty-eight hours

[021-064] showed vast and shapelessly threatening through them.

[021-065] When the drama of the _table d'hôte_ reached its climax of roast

[021-066] chestnuts and butter, he determined to walk over to San Marco and pay a

[021-067] visit to [[mr-waters|Mr. Waters]]. He found the old minister from Haddam East Village,

[021-068] Massachusetts, Italianate outwardly in almost ludicrous degree. He wore

[021-069] a fur-lined overcoat indoors; his feet, cased in thick woollen shoes,

[021-070] rested on a strip of carpet laid before his table; a man who had lived

[021-071] for forty years in the pungent atmosphere of an air-tight stove,

[021-072] succeeding a quarter of a century of roaring hearth fires, contented

[021-073] himself with the spare heat of a scaldino, which he held his clasped

[021-074] hands over in the very Italian manner; the lamp that cast its light on

[021-075] the book open before him was the classic _lucerna_, with three beaks,

[021-076] fed with olive oil. He looked up at his visitor over his spectacles,

[021-077] without recognising him, till Colville spoke. Then, after their

[021-078] greeting, "Is it snowing heavily?" he asked.

[021-079] "It isn't snowing at all. What made you think that?"

[021-080] "Perhaps I was drowsing over my book and dreamed it. We become very

[021-081] strange and interesting studies to ourselves as we live along."

[021-082] He took up the metaphysical consideration with the promptness of a man

[021-083] who has no small-talk, and who speaks of the mind and soul as if they

[021-084] were the gossip of the neighbourhood.

[021-085] "At times the forty winters that I passed in Haddam East Village seem

[021-086] like an alien experience, and I find myself pitying the life I lived

[021-087] there quite as if it were the life of some one else. It seems incredible

[021-088] that men should still inhabit such climates."

[021-089] "Then you're not homesick for Haddam East Village?"

[021-090] "Ah! for the good and striving souls there, yes; especially the souls of

[021-091] some women there. They used to think that it was I who gave them

[021-092] consolation and spiritual purpose, but it was they who really imparted

[021-093] it. Women souls--how beautiful they sometimes are! They seem truly like

[021-094] angelic essences. I trust that I shall meet them somewhere some time,

[021-095] but it will never be in Haddam East Village. Yes, I must have been

[021-096] dreaming when you came in. I thought that I was by my fire there, and

[021-097] all round over the hills and in the streets the snow was deep and

[021-098] falling still. "How distinctly," he said, closing his eyes, as artists do

[021-099] in looking at a picture, "I can see the black wavering lines of the walls

[021-100] in the fields sinking into the drifts! the snow billowed over the graves

[021-101] by the church where I preached! the banks of snow around the houses! the

[021-102] white desolation everywhere! I ask myself at times if the people are

[021-103] still there. Yes, I feel as blessedly remote from that terrible winter

[021-104] as if I had died away from it, and were in the weather of heaven."

[021-105] "Then you have no reproach for feeble-spirited fellow-citizens who

[021-106] abandon their native climate and come to live in Italy?"

[021-107] The old man drew his fur coat closer about him and shrugged his

[021-108] shoulders in true Florentine fashion. "There may be something to say

[021-109] against those who do so in the heyday of life, but I shall not be the

[021-110] one to say it. The race must yet revert in its decrepitude, as I have in

[021-111] mine, to the climates of the South. Since I have been in Italy I have

[021-112] realised what used to occur to me dimly at home--the cruel disproportion

[021-113] between the end gained and the means expended in reclaiming the savage

[021-114] North. Half the human endeavour, half the human suffering, would have

[021-115] made the whole South Protestant and the whole East Christian, and our

[021-116] civilisation would now be there. No, I shall never go back to New

[021-117] England. New England? New Ireland----New Canada! Half the farms in

[021-118] Haddam are in the hands of our Irish friends, and the labour on the rest

[021-119] is half done by French Canadians. That is all right and well. New

[021-120] England must come to me here, by way of the great middle West and the

[021-121] Pacific coast."

[021-122] Colville smiled at the Emersonian touch, but he said gravely, "I can

[021-123] never quite reconcile myself to the thought of dying out of my own

[021-124] country."

[021-125] "Why not? It is very unimportant where one dies. A moment after your

[021-126] breath is gone you are in exile for ever--or at home for ever."

[021-127] Colville sat musing upon this phase of Americanism, as he had upon many

[021-128] others. At last he broke the silence they had both let fall, far away

[021-129] from the topic they had touched.

[021-130] "Well," he asked, "how did you enjoy the veglione?"

[021-131] "Oh, I'm too old to go to such places for pleasure," said the minister

[021-132] simply. "But it was very interesting, and certainly very striking:

[021-133] especially when I went back, toward daylight, after seeing Mrs. Bowen

[021-134] home."

[021-135] "Did you go back?" demanded Colville, in some amaze.

[021-136] "Oh yes. I felt that my experience was incomplete without some knowledge

[021-137] of how the Carnival ended at such a place."

[021-138] "Oh! And do you still feel that [[girolamo-savonarola|Savonarola]] was mistaken?"

[021-139] "There seemed to be rather more boisterousness toward the close, and, if

[021-140] I might judge, the excitement grew a little unwholesome. But I really

[021-141] don't feel myself very well qualified to decide. My own life has been

[021-142] passed in circumstances so widely different that I am at a certain

[021-143] disadvantage."

[021-144] "Yes," said Colville, with a smile; "I daresay the Carnival at Haddam

[021-145] East Village was quite another tiling."

[021-146] The old man smiled responsively. "I suppose that some of my former

[021-147] parishioners might have been scandalised at my presence at a Carnival

[021-148] ball, had they known the fact merely in the abstract; but in my letters

[021-149] home I shall try to set it before them in an instructive light. I should

[021-150] say that the worst thing about such a scene of revelry would be that it

[021-151] took us too much out of our inner quiet. But I suppose the same remark

[021-152] might apply to almost any form of social entertainment."

[021-153] "Yes."

[021-154] "But human nature is so constituted that some means of expansion must be

[021-155] provided, or a violent explosion takes place. The only question is what

[021-156] means are most innocent. I have been looking about," added the old man

[021-157] quietly, "at the theatres lately."

[021-158] "Have you?" asked Colville, opening his eyes, in suppressed surprise.

[021-159] "Yes; with a view to determining the degree of harmless amusement that

[021-160] may be derived from them. It's rather a difficult question. I should be

[021-161] inclined to say, however, that I don't think the ballet can ever be

[021-162] instrumental for good."

[021-163] Colville could not deny himself the pleasure of saying, "Well, not the

[021-164] highest, I suppose."

[021-165] "No," said Mr. Waters, in apparent unconsciousness of the irony. "But I

[021-166] think the Church has made a mistake in condemning the theatre _in toto_.

[021-167] It appears to me that it might always have countenanced a certain order

[021-168] of comedy, in which the motive and plot are unobjectionable. Though I

[021-169] don't deny that there are moods when all laughter seems low and unworthy

[021-170] and incompatible with the most advanced state of being. And I confess,"

[021-171] he went on, with a dreamy thoughtfulness, "that I have very great

[021-172] misgivings in regard to tragedy. The glare that it throws upon the play

[021-173] of the passions--jealousy in its anguish, revenge glutting itself, envy

[021-174] eating its heart, hopeless love--their nakedness is terrible. The terror

[021-175] may be salutary; it may be very mischievous. I am afraid that I have

[021-176] left some of my inquiries till it is too late. I seem to have no longer

[021-177] the materials of judgment left in me. If I were still a young man like

[021-178] you----"

[021-179] "Am I still a young man?" interrupted Colville sadly.

[021-180] "You are young enough to respond to the appeals that sometimes find me

[021-181] silent. If I were of your age I should certainly investigate some of

[021-182] these interesting problems."
