[024-001] [[theodore-colville|Colville]] said all this looking down, in a fashion he had. When he looked

[024-002] up he saw a severity in [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]]'s pretty face, such as he had not

[024-003] seen there before.

[024-004] "I didn't know she had been writing to you, but I know that you are

[024-005] talking of [[imogene-graham|Imogene]]. She told me what she had said to you yesterday, and

[024-006] I blamed her for it, but I'm not sure that it wasn't best."

[024-007] "Oh, indeed!" said Colville. "Perhaps you can tell me who put the idea

[024-008] into her head?"

[024-009] "Yes; I did."

[024-010] A dead silence ensued, in which the fragments of the situation broken by

[024-011] these words revolved before Colville's thought with kaleidoscopic

[024-012] variety, and he passed through all the phases of anger, resentment,

[024-013] wounded self-love, and accusing shame.

[024-014] At last, "I suppose you had your reasons," he said simply.

[024-015] "I am in her mother's place here," she replied, tightening the grip of

[024-016] one little hand upon another, where she held them laid against the side

[024-017] of her waist.

[024-018] "Yes, I know that," said Colville; "but what reason had you to warn her

[024-019] against me as a person who was amusing himself with her? I don't like

[024-020] the phrase; but she seems to have got it from you; I use it at third

[024-021] hand."

[024-022] "I don't like the phrase either; I didn't invent it."

[024-023] "You used it."

[024-024] "No; it wasn't I who used it. I should have been glad to use another, if

[024-025] I could," said Mrs. Bowen, with perfect steadiness.

[024-026] "Then you mean to say that you believe I've been trifling with the

[024-027] feelings of this child?"

[024-028] "I mean to say nothing. You are very much older; and she is a romantic

[024-029] girl, very extravagant. You have tried to make her like you."

[024-030] "I certainly have. I have tried to make [[effie-bowen|Effie Bowen]] like me too."

[024-031] Mrs. Bowen passed this over in serenity that he felt was not far from

[024-032] contempt.

[024-033] He gave a laugh that did not express enjoyment.

[024-034] "You have no right to laugh!" she cried, losing herself a little, and so

[024-035] making her first gain upon him.

[024-036] "It appears not. Perhaps you will tell me what I am to do about this

[024-037] letter?"

[024-038] "That is for you to decide." She recovered herself, and lost ground with

[024-039] him in proportion.

[024-040] "I thought perhaps that since you were able to judge my motives so

[024-041] clearly, you might be able to advise me."

[024-042] "I don't judge your motives," Mrs. Bowen began. She added suddenly, as

[024-043] if by an after-thought, "I don't think you had any."

[024-044] "I'm obliged to you."

[024-045] "But you are as much to blame as if you had."

[024-046] "And perhaps I'm as much to blame as if I had really wronged somebody?"

[024-047] "Yes."

[024-048] "It's rather paradoxical. You don't wish me to see her any more?"

[024-049] "I haven't any wish about it; you must not _say_ that I have," said Mrs.

[024-050] Bowen, with dignity.

[024-051] Colville smiled. "May I _ask_ if you have?"

[024-052] "Not for myself."

[024-053] "You put me on very short allowance of conjecture."

[024-054] "I will not let you trifle with the matter!" she cried. "You have made

[024-055] me speak, when a word, a look, ought to have been enough. Oh, I didn't

[024-056] think you had the miserable vanity to wish it!"

[024-057] Colville stood thinking a long time and she waiting. "I see that

[024-058] everything is at an end. I am going away from [[florence|Florence]]. Good-bye, Mrs.

[024-059] Bowen." He approached her, holding out his hand. But if he expected to

[024-060] be rewarded for this, nothing of the kind happened. She shrank swiftly

[024-061] back.

[024-062] "No, no. You shall not touch me."

[024-063] He paused a moment, gazing keenly at her face, in which, whatever other

[024-064] feeling showed, there was certainly no fear of him. Then with a slight

[024-065] bow he left the room.

[024-066] Mrs. Bowen ran from it by another door, and shut herself into her own

[024-067] room. When she returned to the salotto, Imogene and Effie were just

[024-068] coming in. The child went to lay aside her hat and sacque; the girl,

[024-069] after a glance at Mrs. Bowen's face, lingered inquiringly.

[024-070] "Mr. Colville came here with your letter, Imogene."

[024-071] "Yes," said Imogene faintly. "Do you think I oughtn't to have written

[024-072] it?"

[024-073] "Oh, it makes no difference now. He is going away from Florence."

[024-074] "Yes?" breathed the girl.

[024-075] "I spoke openly with him."

[024-076] "Yes?"

[024-077] "I didn't spare him. I made him think I hated and despised him."

[024-078] Imogene was silent. Then she said, "I know that whatever you have done,

[024-079] you have acted for the best."

[024-080] "Yes, I have a right that you should say that--I have a right that you

[024-081] should always say it. I think he has behaved very foolishly, but I don't

[024-082] blame him----"

[024-083] "No! I was to blame."

[024-084] "I don't _know_ that he was to blame, and I won't let you think he was."

[024-085] "Oh, he is the best man in the world!"

[024-086] "He gave up at once; he didn't try to defend himself. It's nothing for

[024-087] you to lose a friend at your age; but at mine----"

[024-088] "I _know_ it, Mrs. Bowen."

[024-089] "And I wouldn't even shake hands with him when he was going; I----"

[024-090] "Oh, I don't see how you could be so hard!" cried Imogene. She put up

[024-091] her hands to her face, and broke into tears. Mrs. Bowen watched her, dry

[024-092] eyed, with her lips parted, and an intensity of question in her face.

[024-093] "Imogene," she said at last, "I wish you to promise me one thing."

[024-094] "Yes."

[024-095] "Not to write to Mr. Colville again."

[024-096] "No, no; indeed I won't, Mrs. Bowen!" The girl came up to kiss her; Mrs.

[024-097] Bowen turned her cheek.

[024-098] Imogene was going from the room, when Mrs. Bowen spoke again. "But I

[024-099] wish you to promise me this only because you don't feel sure of yourself

[024-100] about him. If you care for him--if you think you care for him--then I

[024-101] leave you perfectly free."

[024-102] The girl looked up, scared. "No, no; I'd rather you wouldn't leave me

[024-103] free--you mustn't; I shouldn't know what to do."

[024-104] "Very well, then," said Mrs. Bowen.

[024-105] They both waited a moment, as if each were staying for the other to

[024-106] speak. Then Imogene asked, "Is he--going soon?"

[024-107] "I don't know," said Mrs. Bowen. "Why should he want to delay? He had

[024-108] better go at once. And I hope he will go home--as far from Florence as

[024-109] he can. I should think he would _hate_ the place."

[024-110] "Yes," said the girl, with a quivering sigh; "it must be hateful to

[024-111] him." She paused, and then she rushed on with bitter self-reproach. "And

[024-112] I--_I_ have helped to make it so! O Mrs. Bowen, perhaps it's _I_ who

[024-113] have been trifling with _him_. Trying to make him believe--no, not trying

[024-114] to do that, but letting him see that I sympathised--Oh, do you think I

[024-115] have?"

[024-116] "You know what you have been doing, Imogene," said Mrs. Bowen, with the

[024-117] hardness it surprises men to know women use with each other, they seem

[024-118] such tender creatures in the abstract. "You have no need to ask me."

[024-119] "No, no."

[024-120] "As you say, I warned you from the first."

[024-121] "Oh yes; you did."

[024-122] "I couldn't do more than hint; it was too much to expect----"

[024-123] "Oh, yes, yes."

[024-124] "And if you couldn't take my hints, I was helpless."

[024-125] "Yes; I see it."

[024-126] "I was only afraid of saying too much, and all through that miserable

[024-127] veglione business I was trying to please you and him, because I was

[024-128] afraid I _had_ said too much--gone too far. I wanted to show you that I

[024-129] disdained to be suspicious, that I was ashamed to suppose that a girl of

[024-130] your age could care for the admiration of a man of his."

[024-131] "Oh, I didn't care for his admiration. I admired _him_--and pitied him."

[024-132] Mrs. Bowen apparently would not be kept now from saying all that had

[024-133] been rankling in her breast. "I didn't approve of going to the veglione.

[024-134] A great many people would be shocked if they knew I went; I wouldn't at

[024-135] all like to have it known. But I was not going to have him thinking that

[024-136] I was severe with you, and wanted to deny you any really harmless

[024-137] pleasure."

[024-138] "Oh, who could think that? You're only too good to me. You see," said

[024-139] the girl, "what a return I have made for your trust. I knew you didn't

[024-140] want to go to the veglione. If I hadn't been the most selfish girl in

[024-141] the world I wouldn't have let you. But I did. I _forced_ you to go, and

[024-142] then, after we got there, I seized every advantage, and abused your

[024-143] kindness till I wonder I didn't sink through the floor. Yes; I ought to

[024-144] have refused to dance--if I'd had a spark of generosity or gratitude I

[024-145] would have done it; and I ought to have come straight back to you the

[024-146] instant the waltz was done. And now see what has come of it! I've made

[024-147] you think he was trifling with me, and I've made him think that I'm a

[024-148] false and hollow-hearted thing."

[024-149] "You know best what you have done, Imogene," said Mrs. Bowen, with a

[024-150] smiling tearfulness that was somehow very bitter. She rose from the

[024-151] sofa, as if to indicate that there was no more to be said, and Imogene,

[024-152] with a fresh burst of grief, rushed away to her own room.

[024-153] She dropped on her knees beside her bed, and stretched out her arms upon

[024-154] it, an image of that desolation of soul which, when we are young, seems

[024-155] limitless, but which in later life we know has comparatively narrow

[024-156] bounds beyond the clouds that rest so blackly around us.

[024-157] XII

[024-158] In his room Colville was devouring as best he might the chagrin with

[024-159] which he had come away from [[palazzo-pinti|Palazzo Pinti]], while he packed his trunk for

[024-160] departure. Now that the thing was over, the worst was past. Again he

[024-161] observed that his emotions had no longer the continuity that the

[024-162] emotions of his youth possessed. As he remembered, a painful or pleasant

[024-163] impression used to last indefinitely; but here he was with this

[024-164] humiliating affair hot in his mind, shrugging his shoulders with a sense

[024-165] of relief, almost a sense of escape. Does the soul really wear out with

[024-166] the body? The question flitted across his mind as he took down a pair of

[024-167] trousers, and noticed that they were considerably frayed about the feet;

[024-168] he determined to give them to Paolo, and this reminded him to ring for

[024-169] Paolo, and send word to the office that he was going to take the evening

[024-170] train for Rome.

[024-171] He went on packing, and putting away with the different garments the

[024-172] unpleasant thoughts that he knew he should be sure to unpack with them

[024-173] in Rome; but they would then have less poignancy.

[024-174] For the present he was doing the best he could, and he was not making

[024-175] any sort of pretences. When his trunk was locked he kindled himself a

[024-176] fire, and sat down before it to think of Imogene. He began with her, but

[024-177] presently it seemed to be Mrs. Bowen that he was thinking of; then he

[024-178] knew he was dropping off to sleep by the manner in which their two ideas

[024-179] mixed. The fatigues and excitements of the week had been great, but he

[024-180] would not give way; it was too disgraceful.

[024-181] Some one rapped at his door. He called out "_Avanti_!" and he would have

[024-182] been less surprised to see either of those ladies than Paolo with the

[024-183] account he had ordered to be made out. It was a long, pendulous,

[024-184] minutely itemed affair, such as the traveller's recklessness in candles

[024-185] and firewood comes to in the books of the Continental landlord, and it

[024-186] almost swept the floor when its volume was unrolled. But it was not the

[024-187] sum-total that dismayed Colville when he glanced at the final figure;

[024-188] that, indeed, was not so very great, with all the items; it was the

[024-189] conviction, suddenly flashing upon him, that he had not money enough by

[024-190] him to pay it. His watch, held close to the fire, told him that it was

[024-191] five o'clock; the banks had been closed an hour, and this was Saturday

[024-192] afternoon.
