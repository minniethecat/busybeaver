# Loggia dei Lanzi

## Description

The Loggia dei Lanzi is the open-air sculpture gallery that occupies the south side of Florence's Piazza della Signoria, directly adjacent to the Palazzo Vecchio — "the old palace" — and the Uffizi Gallery. Though Howells never names the Loggia directly in *Indian Summer*, the landmark is an inseparable part of the piazza his characters traverse on two notable occasions. The Piazza della Signoria is introduced when Colville and Mr. Waters "came out into the Piazza della Signoria, suddenly, as one always seems to do, upon the rise of the old palace and the leap of its tower into the blue air" (027-069 to 027-070). The Loggia, with its three wide arches open to the piazza, stands to the right of the Palazzo Vecchio, forming the architectural link between the medieval palace and the Uffizi's long arcaded wing. Beneath its vaulted ceiling, a collection of monumental Renaissance and classical sculptures — including Benvenuto Cellini's bronze *Perseus with the Head of Medusa* and Giambologna's *Rape of the Sabine Women* — have been displayed since the sixteenth century, making the Loggia effectively an outdoor museum open to all who cross the square.

The Loggia forms part of the architectural ensemble that Colville later describes with affectionate familiarity when he walks "down Via Tornabuoni across through Porta Rosso and the Piazza della Signoria and the Uffizzi" (046-012 to 046-013), noting — with his characteristic blend of irony and fondness — how "all the statues loafing about in their shirt sleeves, and the objects of interest stretching and yawning round, and having a good rest after their winter's work" (046-014 to 046-016). The personification of the statuary — sculptures relaxing after the tourist season — captures a distinctively Florentine intimacy with its public art that the Loggia embodies: sculpture not confined to a museum but present in the open air, part of the city's daily life. The Loggia's three great arches and the raised platform that keeps its treasures just above the level of the piazza create a transitional space — neither fully interior nor fully exterior — that mirrors the condition of the novel's expatriate characters, suspended as they are between their American origins and their Italian present.

## Significance in the Novel

The Loggia dei Lanzi, as an integral element of the Piazza della Signoria, participates in the novel's treatment of Florence's public spaces as places where history, art, and the present moment converge. When Colville and Mr. Waters enter the piazza in Chapter 27, the Loggia's arcade — filled with its famous sculptures — silently witnesses their conversation about Savonarola and martyrdom. The "history of all Florence is there, with memories of every great time in bronze or marble" (027-071 to 027-072), and the Loggia's collection provides concrete instances of this observation: classical heroes, Renaissance masterpieces, and Medici lions all share the space beneath its arches, embodying the layered strata of Florentine civilisation.

In Chapter 46, the Loggia's sculptures become part of Colville's domestic vision of Florence. His description of "all the statues loafing about in their shirt sleeves" (046-014) playfully demystifies the city's monumental art, transforming it from objects of reverent tourism into familiar presences — neighbours, almost — with whom one shares the rhythm of the seasons. This casual relationship with high art is characteristic of Colville's hard-won Florentine identity: he is no longer a tourist but a resident who can speak of Renaissance masterpieces as if they were acquaintances encountered on a Sunday stroll. The Loggia, with its permanently installed sculptures exposed to sun and rain and the gaze of passersby, is the perfect embodiment of this peculiarly Florentine collapse of distance between the monumental and the everyday.

The Loggia also stands in telling juxtaposition to Savonarola's execution site, marked in the piazza's paving just before its steps. While the bronze plaque commemorates intolerance and martyrdom — "the martyr who hangs for ever from the gibbet over the quenchless fire in the midst" (027-073 to 027-074) — the Loggia's sculptures testify to the civic humanism of Renaissance Florence, the same city that burned the friar. The spatial proximity of these two memorials — one to violence, the other to beauty — enacts the paradox of Florentine history that Howells's novel continually explores: a city capable of producing both Savonarola's pyre and Cellini's Perseus.

## Key Scenes

### Emerging into the Piazza — Colville and Mr. Waters (Chapter 27)

Colville and Mr. Waters, walking from the Duomo, suddenly find themselves in the Piazza della Signoria, the Loggia's arches framing the scene to their right:

> They had been walking down the Via Calzioli from the Duomo, and now they came out into the Piazza della Signoria, suddenly, as one always seems to do, upon the rise of the old palace and the leap of its tower into the blue air. The history of all Florence is there, with memories of every great time in bronze or marble, but the supreme presence is the martyr who hangs for ever from the gibbet over the quenchless fire in the midst. (027-069 to 027-074)

The "memories of every great time in bronze or marble" (027-072) evoke not only the statuary of the piazza generally but specifically the Loggia's collection — the most concentrated assembly of monumental sculpture in the square — standing in silent witness as the two men pass through.

> They walked out of the Piazza and down to the Lung' Arno, through the corridor of the Uffizzi, where the illustrious Florentines stand in marble under the arches, all reconciled and peaceful and equal at last. (027-086 to 027-088)

The route from the Piazza through the Uffizi corridor necessarily skirts the Loggia, whose own marble residents — classical gods and Renaissance heroes — form the architectural and thematic prelude to the effigies of illustrious Florentines that line the gallery passage.

### Statues in Their Shirt Sleeves — Colville's Domestic Florence (Chapter 46)

Near the novel's conclusion, Colville describes to Effie and Mrs. Bowen his walk through a tourist-free Florence, and the Loggia's sculptures become emblems of a city at ease with its own beauty:

> I didn't see a Baedeker on the whole way up here, and I walked down Via Tornabuoni across through Porta Rosso and the Piazza della Signoria and the Uffizzi. You've no idea how comfortable and home-like it was — all the statues loafing about in their shirt sleeves, and the objects of interest stretching and yawning round, and having a good rest after their winter's work. (046-012 to 046-016)

The comic personification encapsulates Colville's transformation: the man who once found Florence's beauty "insufferable" (001-026) now finds the city's statuary companionable. The Loggia's sculptures, perpetually on display yet immune to the seasons of tourism, embody the Florence he has learned — at last — to call home.

## Real-World Counterpart

The Loggia dei Lanzi (also known as the Loggia della Signoria or Loggia dell'Orcagna) is a fourteenth-century open-air sculpture gallery on the south side of Florence's Piazza della Signoria, adjacent to the Palazzo Vecchio and perpendicular to the Uffizi Gallery. Built between 1376 and 1382 to a design attributed to Simone Talenti and Benci di Cione (though once believed to be by Orcagna), the Loggia originally served as a platform for civic ceremonies and public assemblies. Its name derives from the *Lanzichenecchi* (German mercenary landsknechts) billeted there by Cosimo I de' Medici in the sixteenth century. Since the Medici period, the Loggia has housed an extraordinary collection of outdoor sculpture, including Benvenuto Cellini's *Perseus with the Head of Medusa* (1545--1554) — one of the most celebrated bronze statues of the Renaissance — Giambologna's *Rape of the Sabine Women* (1579--1583) and *Hercules and the Centaur Nessus* (1599), and a group of ancient Roman statues. By the time of Howells's *Indian Summer*, the Loggia was an established stop on the Grand Tour and a fixture of every Baedeker — precisely the kind of monument that Colville, in his posture of world-weary familiarity, would have known well. Howells, who had lived in Italy as American consul in Venice from 1861 to 1865 and travelled extensively in Tuscany, would have passed through the Loggia on many occasions; his characters' movements through the Piazza della Signoria — walking from the Duomo to the Lungarno, or from Via Tornabuoni through to the Uffizi — necessarily skirt the Loggia's arches and pass within sight of its celebrated sculptures.
