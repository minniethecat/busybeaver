# Expatriate Identity

## Definition

Expatriate identity in *Indian Summer* refers to the complex negotiation of American selfhood experienced by characters who live for extended periods in Europe, particularly Italy. The concept encompasses both the conscious performance of national identity — characters explicitly identifying themselves as "American" — and the unconscious cultural transformation that European residence produces, captured in the novel's recurring charge of being "Europeanised." For Howells's Americans in Florence, identity is not a fixed national inheritance but a contested, relational category negotiated through daily social interactions with Europeans and fellow expatriates.

## Context in the Novel

Theodore Colville, the novel's protagonist, returns to Florence after a seventeen-year absence, having previously lived there as a young man. His return places him at the intersection of two cultural worlds: he is an American by birth and temperament, yet deeply attached to Italian life and landscape. The novel opens with Colville standing on the Ponte Vecchio, the quintessential Florentine setting, immediately establishing the expatriate's position — physically in Europe, culturally American (001-011).

Colville's expatriate identity is marked by ambiguity from the outset. His departure for Europe is publicly framed as a health measure — "the fiction that he was going to Europe for his health, impaired by his journalistic labours" (001-076) — while his private purpose is artistic and professional. This doubleness characterises expatriate identity throughout the novel: the European sojourn is simultaneously a departure from American life and a deeper engagement with American self-understanding, achieved through distance and contrast.

The expatriate community in Florence is depicted as a distinct social formation, neither fully American nor European. At Mrs. Bowen's salon, "the company seemed to be mostly Americans, but there were a good many evident English also," with Italians and Germans completing the multinational assembly (005-050). Within this cosmopolitan setting, national identity becomes a salient category of self-presentation and mutual evaluation — characters continually assess whether someone is "English or American" (005-052), and national stereotypes provide the material for social performance.

The novel's central charge of becoming "Europeanised" crystallises the anxiety at the heart of expatriate identity. When Colville accuses Mrs. Bowen of being "Europeanised" for preferring "the European plan of bringing up girls" (006-152 to 006-153), the accusation carries both personal and cultural weight. To be Europeanised is to have lost something essentially American — the "wild, sweet liberty which once made American girlhood a long rapture" (003-026 to 003-027) — and the charge recurs throughout the novel as characters navigate the boundary between cultural adaptation and cultural betrayal. By the novel's later sections, the question is turned back on Colville himself: "Why, are you becoming Europeanised too?" (019-065).

## Key Passages

> They entered into a discussion which is always fruitful with Americans — the discussion of American girlhood, and Colville contended for the old national ideal of girlish liberty as wide as the continent, as fast as the Mississippi. Mrs. Bowen withstood him with delicate firmness. "Oh," he said, "you're Europeanised." (006-148 to 006-152)

This exchange encapsulates the novel's treatment of expatriate identity as a site of ideological conflict. Colville's invocation of "the old national ideal" — spatialised through references to the continent and the Mississippi — positions American identity as a frontier liberty that European residence threatens to erode. Mrs. Bowen's "delicate firmness" suggests that her Europeanised position is not a surrender but a considered alternative, complicating any simple equation of national identity with moral virtue.

> He spoke with that fond, that appealing patriotism, which expresses so much to the sympathetic foreigner in Italy: the sense of great and painful uncertainty of Italy's future through the complications of diplomacy, the memory of her sufferings in the past, the spirit of quiet and inexhaustible patience for trials to come. (005-030 to 005-034)

This passage articulates the expatriate's distinctive perspective: the "sympathetic foreigner" perceives Italy's national experience with a clarity and empathy that may be unavailable to natives. The expatriate position enables a form of cosmopolitan sympathy, yet the very phrase "sympathetic foreigner" underscores the unbridgeable distance between the observer and the observed.

> Colville found that he had no longer the youthful sense of any sort of lastingness in his sojourn, but he still turned a deaf ear to the question of his return. The thought of it annoyed him; he did not like the notion of going back to America, and taking up the dull course of existence he had dropped there. (001-153 to 001-155)

Colville's reluctance to return to America — the "dull course of existence" awaiting him there — reveals the existential dimension of expatriate identity. Europe is not merely a geographical location but a psychic space of freedom from American social constraints. The expatriate's refusal to "go back" is a refusal of a particular version of American selfhood.

> "That interests me as an American," said Colville. "But why do you want a republic in Italy?" (004-046 to 004-047)

In this brief exchange with an Italian gentleman, Colville explicitly foregrounds his national identity as the interpretive frame through which he engages with Italian politics. The self-identification as "an American" is not incidental but constitutive of his perspective — he is interested *as* an American, his curiosity mediated by his national identity even as he immerses himself in Italian political discourse.

## Related Concepts

- **Middle Age and Youth** — Expatriate identity in the novel is inflected by age: Colville's return to Florence as a middle-aged man differs fundamentally from his youthful residence, and the expatriate experience changes meaning across the life course.
