# Mrs. Amsden

> Mrs. Amsden is an elderly American woman who belongs to the long-established Florentine expatriate society in *Indian Summer*. Having not set foot in America for forty years (022-147), she serves as a chaperone to younger members of the American circle and provides a sharp-eyed, cheerfully irreverent counterpoint to the novel's more earnest romantic entanglements. Though a minor character in terms of plot function, she is one of Howells's most vividly drawn comic presences: an old lady who has learned to "manage [her] own skeletons winningly" (029-114) and who finds in witty conversation the "last consolation" of "a life of harmless coquetries" (029-092).

## Role

Mrs. Amsden functions as a **minor** character whose primary narrative role is practical — she chaperones Imogene Graham at evening parties when Mrs. Bowen is indisposed (035-109) — and tonal: her bantering exchanges with Theodore Colville provide the novel with some of its lightest, most ironic moments. She is first introduced indirectly, when Imogene Graham describes her to Colville as "the loveliest old lady here in Florence, who's lived here thirty years, and she's always going back and never getting back, and she's so homesick she doesn't know what to do" (005-141 to 005-143). When she appears in person in the Boboli Gardens (028-079), she immediately establishes herself as a veteran of social skirmishing, greeting Colville with a mock-reproach about his closed eyes in church and drawing him into the kind of spirited verbal play in which both evidently delight. As a long-term resident who occupies a secure place in Florentine society without being central to it, she embodies a particular type of expatriate existence: permanently displaced yet thoroughly acclimated, detached enough to observe the romantic dramas around her with amused curiosity, but still sufficiently engaged to serve as their facilitator through her chaperonage.

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Amsden's most striking quality is her wit — a dry, self-possessed humour that makes her one of the few characters in the novel capable of meeting Colville on his own ironic ground. When she encounters him in the Boboli Gardens, ostensibly by chance, she immediately accuses him of lying in wait: "Are you sure that you didn't know I was to go by here at exactly half-past four?" (028-138 to 028-139). The jest is double-edged: it teases Colville while also acknowledging, with the self-deprecation that is the privilege of age, that an assignation with *her* is the last thing he would plan. She presses the mock-flirtation further when she spots the red anemone in his hand: "Yes, and with a single red anemone in your hand, so that I should know you without being obliged to put on my spectacles" (028-142 to 028-143). Colville's reply — "You divine everything, Mrs. Amsden" (028-144) — is given with the flower itself, a gesture that crystallises their relationship: an elaborate, mutually enjoyable game of gallantry without stakes.

Her physical description is handled with characteristic Howellsian economy and a touch of genuine pathos. When Colville first sees her in the Boboli, she is "the old lady" with "the child by the hand" (028-081). But the novel's most memorable description of her comes later, as she draws her shawl about "her shoulders, which, if no longer rounded, had still a charming droop" — prompting the narrator's reflection that "One realises in looking at such old ladies that there are women who could manage their own skeletons winningly" (029-111 to 029-114). The phrase is a small masterpiece of double vision: it acknowledges the ravages of age even as it insists on the persistence of grace. Mrs. Amsden's eyeglass — "an old-fashioned sort, held to the nose by a handle" (029-113) — is the perfect prop for her character: an instrument of inspection wielded with theatrical flair.

The narrator reveals that Mrs. Amsden's enjoyment of verbal sparring with Colville represents "the last consolation which remained to her from a life of harmless coquetries" (029-092). This glancing revelation transforms her from a merely comic figure into something more affecting. There is a whole lost youth — decades of parties, flirtations, and social manoeuvres — compressed into that phrase "a life of harmless coquetries." Her present contentments are real but diminished: she chaperones other people's daughters, observes other people's romantic imbroglios, and savours conversation as the final pleasure left to a woman who can no longer be an actress in the social drama, only its most discerning spectator. When Effie Bowen innocently reports that Colville and Imogene are "just holding hands" on the far side of the Boboli meadow, Mrs. Amsden's response is a quintessentially Howellsian note: "Does he, indeed?" exclaimed Mrs. Amsden, with a cackle (029-072). The "cackle" is the sound of an old woman who has seen everything and is delighted to see it again.

Mrs. Amsden shares with the novel's other elderly Americans — Mr. Waters in particular — the condition of long expatriation. She "had not been in America for forty years" (022-147), yet she remains unmistakably American in her manner. The narrator notes that "she liked the American way of talking better than any other" (022-147), and her own speech — with its teasing directness, its mock-indignation at "the silence of the Bowen family in regard to the affairs of others" (028-110), its playful meta-commentary on the novelistic quality of the group's situation — marks her as a specifically American type: the expatriate who preserves a national style of social performance long after she has ceased to inhabit the nation itself. When Colville suggests their group is "a passage from a modern novel ... One of Mr. James's," she demurs: "Don't you think we ought to be rather more of the great world for that? I hardly feel up to Mr. James. I should have said Howells. Only nothing happens in that case!" (029-123). The joke is one of the novel's most overt metafictional gestures, and it is characteristic that Howells puts it in the mouth of Mrs. Amsden — the character whose function is precisely to observe, with shrewd and unsentimental clarity, that in the small dramas of the expatriate drawing-room, very little does happen, and everything is at stake.

## Relationships

- **Theodore Colville** — Mrs. Amsden's relationship with Colville is the most fully rendered of her connections in the novel. They engage in a running banter that both clearly enjoy: he gives her flowers and flatters her with extravagant gallantries, she parries with mock-suspicion and theatrical indignation. The narrator notes that "Even more than a man whom she could entangle in his speech she liked a man who could slip through the toils with unfailing ease" (029-091). Their repartee provides Colville with "a respite from the formless future pressing close upon him" (028-097 to 029-099 area), and it supplies Mrs. Amsden with the conversational stimulus that is the chief remaining pleasure of her social life.

- **Lina Bowen** — Mrs. Bowen deputises Mrs. Amsden to chaperone Imogene and Effie (028-132), trusting her as a reliable member of the expatriate community. Mrs. Amsden, for her part, treats Mrs. Bowen with a respect that borders on admiration, calling her "a very wise woman" (029-095) and promising to "make my brags to Mrs. Bowen" about Colville's gift of the anemone (028-145). At the same time, Colville detects in Mrs. Amsden an "unrequited curiosity in regard to his relations to the Bowen household" (035-109), suggesting that even old friendship does not extinguish the appetite for gossip.

- **Imogene Graham** — As Imogene's designated chaperone during the round of evening parties when Mrs. Bowen withdraws (035-109, 035-177), Mrs. Amsden occupies a supervisory relation to the young girl. She accompanies Imogene and Effie to the Boboli Gardens at Mrs. Bowen's request (028-132), and she later observes with sharp-eyed interest the romantic tensions playing out between Imogene and Colville. When Imogene and Colville both withdraw from society, Mrs. Amsden's "mind had been gradually clearing" under their simultaneous absence — only to cloud again when reports of their renewed intimacy reach her (038-103). She is, in this sense, a barometer of the novel's romantic weather.

- **Effie Bowen** — Mrs. Amsden's interactions with Effie reveal her avuncular warmth. She holds the child by the hand in the Boboli Gardens (028-081) and engages her in conversation while Colville and Imogene conduct their charged exchange across the meadow. When Effie innocently reports that Colville "always holds my hand when he is with me" (029-070), Mrs. Amsden's response — part amusement, part sharp-eared attention to the implications — captures the double register of her relationship to the child: affectionate but always alert to intelligence.

## Key Scenes

### First Indirect Mention at Mrs. Bowen's Reception (005-141 to 005-144)

Before she ever appears on stage, Mrs. Amsden is conjured into existence by Imogene Graham, who holds her up to Colville as an exemplar of a certain kind of American wit. Imogene's thumbnail sketch — the old lady who has lived in Florence for thirty years, perpetually on the verge of return, homesick but never departing, the source of the dictum that "Americans may not be *better* than other people, but they are *different*" — establishes Mrs. Amsden before she speaks a word as a figure of arch, self-aware expatriate wisdom.

> "There's the loveliest old lady here in Florence, who's lived here thirty years, and she's always going back and never getting back, and she's so homesick she doesn't know what to do, and she always says that Americans may not be *better* than other people, but they are *different*." (005-141 to 005-144)

### The Boboli Gardens Encounter (028-079 to 029-129)

Mrs. Amsden's first appearance in person — and her most sustained scene in the novel — occupies the latter third of chapter 28 and the early part of chapter 29. She enters calling out to Colville by name in the Boboli Gardens, chaperoning Effie and Imogene in Mrs. Bowen's stead. The scene unfolds as a series of comic exchanges between Mrs. Amsden and Colville, punctuated by the more fraught private conversation between Colville and Imogene on the far side of the meadow. Mrs. Amsden's remark on the propriety of the American colony abroad — "we're respectable, we Americans abroad; we're decorous, above all things" — doubles as light comedy and genuine social observation.

> "But we're respectable, we Americans abroad; we're decorous, above all things; and I don't know about meeting *you* here, Mr. Colville. It has a very bad appearance. Are you sure that you didn't know I was to go by here at exactly half-past four?" (028-136 to 028-139)

### The Carriage Ride and the Metafictional Joke (029-095 to 029-129)

As the party leaves the Boboli, Mrs. Amsden orchestrates the departure with her customary blend of theatricality and shrewdness. She draws her shawl about her shoulders with a gesture the narrator finds "charming" despite — or because of — its intimation of mortality. Her exchange with Colville about whether their group is a passage from Henry James or William Dean Howells is the novel's most explicit moment of self-reference, and it is fitting that Mrs. Amsden delivers the punchline: "I hardly feel up to Mr. James. I should have said Howells. Only nothing happens in that case!"

> "This is deliciously mysterious," said Mrs. Amsden, drawing her shawl up about her shoulders, which, if no longer rounded, had still a charming droop. One realises in looking at such old ladies that there are women who could manage their own skeletons winningly. (029-111 to 029-114)

### Chaperone and Social Barometer (035-109 to 038-103)

During the round of evening parties that Colville and Imogene attend, Mrs. Amsden serves as Imogene's chaperone, "apparently suffering an increase of unrequited curiosity" about Colville's connection to the Bowen household (035-109). Colville notes that she "has marked me for her prey: I can see it in her glittering eyeglass" (035-177). Later, when Colville and Imogene simultaneously withdraw from society, Mrs. Amsden's "mind had been gradually clearing under the simultaneous withdrawal of Imogene and Colville from society" — only to become "again as thickly clouded as a weather-glass before a storm" when reports of their renewed intimacy reach her (038-103). The weather-glass metaphor is apt: Mrs. Amsden registers every shift in the romantic atmosphere, and her curiosity — however unrequited — makes her the novel's most reliable social seismograph.
