## Description

Piazza San Marco is a broad, open square in the northern quarter of Florence, dominated by the church and convent of San Marco — the former Dominican priory long associated with Fra Girolamo Savonarola, the fiery fifteenth-century reformer. In Howells's novel, the piazza is the location of the Rev. Mr. Waters's modest lodging: "Colville was a frequent visitor of Mr. Waters, who had a lodging in Piazza San Marco, of the poverty which can always be decent in Italy. It was bare, but for the books that furnished it; with a table for his writing, on a corner of which he breakfasted, a wide sofa with cushions in coarse white linen that frankly confessed itself a bed by night, and two chairs of plain Italian walnut; but the windows, which had no sun, looked out upon the church and the convent sacred to the old Socinian for the sake of the meek, heroic mystic whom they keep alive in all the glory of his martyrdom" (015-040). The fountain in the piazza, with its gardened margin, provides a focal point for the square (025-149).

The piazza is situated on the logical route between Colville's hotel and the Palazzo Pinti on the Lung' Arno della Zecca, making it a natural waypoint that Colville passes through repeatedly. It is also close to Via Cavour, along which Colville walks when he goes "half-way to San Marco" before meeting Mr. Waters near the Riccardi Palace (026-167).

## Significance in the Novel

Piazza San Marco functions as a setting of quiet reflection and philosophical conversation — a counterpoint to the more socially charged atmosphere of Palazzo Pinti. The piazza is the home of Mr. Waters, the elderly New England minister whose serene agnosticism and cheerful detachment provide a moral and philosophical foil to Colville's romantic entanglements. Howells uses the proximity between the piazza and its adjoining church to draw an explicit contrast between Mr. Waters's liberal American Protestantism and the fiery idealism of Savonarola: "No two minds could well have been further apart than the New England minister and the Florentine monk, and no two souls nearer together, as Colville recognised with a not irreverent smile" (015-040).

The piazza also serves as a threshold space in Colville's psychological journey. When he is at his lowest point — "wondering with a sort of alien compassion what was to become of his aimless, empty existence" — it is through the Piazza San Marco that he passes, "half a mind to pick a pebble from the gardened margin of the fountain there and toss it against the Rev. Mr. Waters's window" to seek counsel (025-149). The impulse, though not acted upon, reveals the piazza as a place where Colville instinctively turns for wisdom and solace.

## Key Scenes

### Mr. Waters's Lodging and the Carnivale Maskers (Chapter 15)

Colville visits Mr. Waters on a morning during the Carnival season, and as they stand at the window, the piazza becomes a stage for a small but telling drama:

> Colville took the Piazza San Marco in on his way to Palazzo Pinti on the morning when he had made up his mind to go there, and he stood at the window looking out with the old man, when some more maskers passed through the place — two young fellows in old Florentine dress, with a third habited as a nun. (015-095)

The sight prompts Mr. Waters to remark gently, "I wish they hadn't introduced the nun!" — a reflection on the Italian reaction against the domination of the Church, which gives way to a discussion on Savonarola and the arc of history (015-095).

### Colville's Midnight Impulse (Chapter 25)

After a disorienting evening at the opera, Colville's passage through the piazza crystallises his existential unease:

> As he passed through the Piazza San Marco he had half a mind to pick a pebble from the gardened margin of the fountain there and toss it against the Rev. Mr. Waters's window, and when he put his skull-cap out, to ask that optimistic agnostic what a man had best do with a life that had ceased to interest him. (025-149)

The impulse to seek out Mr. Waters at a late hour — like a parishioner calling on a pastor in a moment of crisis — underscores the role the piazza (and its resident sage) plays as a repository of moral clarity in Colville's otherwise drifting life.

## Real-World Counterpart

The Piazza San Marco is a real square in the north-eastern part of Florence's historic center, located at the intersection of Via Cavour, Via Battisti, and Via degli Arazzieri. It is dominated by the Basilica and Convent of San Marco, whose museum houses an extraordinary collection of frescoes by Fra Angelico and preserves the cells where Girolamo Savonarola lived as prior before his excommunication and execution in 1498. In Howells's day, as now, the piazza was a quieter, more residential quarter of the city — removed from the fashionable promenades of the Cascine and the Lungarno that the expatriate community frequented. The "fountain" Howells mentions remains a feature of the square, and the proximity to Via Cavour (the route Colville takes toward San Marco from the Riccardi Palace) is topographically accurate. Howells's choice to lodge Mr. Waters here, within sight of Savonarola's convent, is a deliberate and characteristic piece of cultural geography: the old New England liberal lives literally in the shadow of the fiery reformer whose spirit his own tolerant creed has superseded.
