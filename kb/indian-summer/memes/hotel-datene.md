## Description

The Hotel d'Atene is a Florentine hotel of modest pretension that serves as Theodore Colville's residence during his return to Italy after seventeen years in America. Colville has taken a room specifically for the sun, which the landlord assured him "was in it from ten in the morning till four in the afternoon" — though when it did shine, it had "a trick of looking in at the sides of the window, and painting the chamber wall with a delusive glow" (003-150). Like all Florentine hotels of the period, the rooms were unreliable in their comfort: "your room in a Florentine hotel is always some degrees colder than outdoors, unless you have fire in it" (003-146). Colville resorted to raking away the ashes of his fireplace and throwing on "two or three fagots of broom and pine sprays" to produce a blaze that "gave out no warmth for the present" (003-155).

The hotel accommodated an international clientele typical of the Anglo-American Grand Tour circuit. Its public rooms included a reading-room, labelled as such "in homage to a predominance of English-speaking people among the guests" (003-159), furnished with a cold piano, locked bookcases, and a table displaying the London *Times*, the Vienna *Neue Freie Presse*, and the Rome *Italie*. The dining room hosted a daily table d'hôte where guests of various nationalities — English, French, Russian, Italian, and American — sat together at a common table. Colville himself referred to his quarters with characteristic irony as "my cell at Hotel d'Atene" (016-037).

## Significance in the Novel

The Hotel d'Atene functions as Colville's private retreat and the stage for the novel's early establishment of its cosmopolitan milieu. Unlike Palazzo Pinti, which is the novel's primary social setting and the domestic heart of Mrs. Bowen's expatriate circle, the Hotel d'Atene is a more impersonal, transient space — a waystation for travellers rather than a home. It is here that Colville, fresh from the provincial obscurity of Des Vaches, Indiana, re-immerses himself in the polyglot society of Europe. The table d'hôte scenes in chapter 4 introduce the novel's sustained meditation on national character, the comedy of manners, and the pleasures and perils of a life lived among strangers.

The hotel's modesty also serves to underscore Colville's ambiguous social standing. He is neither a wealthy grand tourist nor a permanent resident of Florence like Mrs. Bowen; he is a man between worlds, lodged in temporary quarters that reflect his unsettled state of mind. When he describes his room as a "cell," the word carries both self-deprecating humour and a hint of the confinement he feels — shut away writing a children's history rather than participating in the life of the city, and perhaps hiding from the emotional entanglements that await him at Palazzo Pinti. The hotel is, in this sense, Colville's refuge from responsibility as much as his base of operations.

The Hotel d'Atene also plays a crucial role in the novel's epistolary climax in chapter 26. After the emotional crisis of the veglione, Mrs. Bowen dispatches a letter "at once to the Hotel d'Atene" (026-116), and it is in his hotel room that Colville reads it "by the struggling light of his newly acquired candle" and composes his reply. The hotel thus becomes the site of the novel's most consequential written exchange — the letter in which Colville accepts the dissolution of his engagement to Imogene and begins the difficult path toward an honest declaration of his feelings for Mrs. Bowen.

## Key Scenes

### The Table d'Hôte Dinner (Chapter 4)

The novel's first extended social scene unfolds at the Hotel d'Atene's communal dinner table, where Colville finds himself among an international assembly of travellers:

> The dinner at the Hotel d'Atene made an imposing show on the *carte du jour*; it looked like ten or twelve courses, but in fact it was five, and even when eked out with roast chestnuts and butter into six, it seemed somehow to stop very abruptly, though one seemed to have had enough. You could have coffee afterward if you ordered it. Colville ordered it, and was sorry when the last of his commensals, slightly bowing him a good-night, left him alone to it. (004-064)

The dinner conversation ranges from the quality of the Chianti — served from "wicker-covered flasks, tied with tufts of red and green silk" (004-013) — to the metaphysics of national character, as a French lady observes that "the Amerhicans like to strhike the key of personality; they can't endure not being interhested" (004-030). Colville, finding himself "in an unexpectedly social mood" (004-060), joins in the talk and reflects that the English had "grown in the grace of facile speech with strangers" since his youth (004-053), while the American family kept "its talk within itself, and hushed to a tone so low that no one else could hear it" (004-054). The scene establishes the novel's comic-ironic perspective on expatriate society and Colville's place within it.

### Colville's Cell (Chapter 16)

When Mrs. Bowen asks Colville where he has been hiding himself for a week, he replies with characteristic self-deprecation:

> "Oh, shut up in my cell at Hotel d'Atene, writing a short history of the Florentine people for Miss Effie." (016-037)

The image of the middle-aged journalist immured in his chilly hotel room, occupied with a child's history book, captures both Colville's genuine affection for Effie Bowen and his habitual retreat from emotional complexity. Mrs. Bowen promptly insists he stay to lunch, drawing him back into the gravitational field of Palazzo Pinti.

### The Letter (Chapter 26)

The hotel is the destination of the letter that marks the novel's decisive emotional turn:

> "This letter must go at once to the Hotel d'Atene," said Mrs. Bowen peremptorily. (026-116)

Colville receives the letter in his room and reads it by candlelight before composing his reply — a moment of solitary reckoning that takes place entirely within the walls of the hotel, away from the social pressures of Palazzo Pinti.

## Real-World Counterpart

The Hotel d'Atene does not appear to correspond to a specific historical Florentine hotel and is best regarded as a fictional composite. Its name evokes classical antiquity (Athens) in the manner of many nineteenth-century European hotels that adopted Greco-Roman references to suggest cultivation and cosmopolitanism. The hotel's description — a pension-style establishment with a table d'hôte, a reading-room stocked with international newspapers, and the characteristic chill of unheated Florentine interiors — accurately reflects the experience of middle-class Anglo-American travellers in Florence during the 1880s. Howells himself knew this world intimately from his own residence in Florence as American consul (1861–1865), and the Hotel d'Atene represents the kind of modest, respectable lodging that a man of Colville's means and pretensions would have occupied.
