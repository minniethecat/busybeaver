[028-001] XIV

[028-002] When he entered the beautiful old garden, its benison of peace fell upon

[028-003] his tumult, and he began to breathe a freer air, reverting to his

[028-004] purpose to be gone in the morning and resting in it, as he strolled up

[028-005] the broad curve of its alley from the gate. He had not been there since

[028-006] he walked there with one now more like a ghost to him than any of the

[028-007] dead who had since died. It was there that she had refused him; he

[028-008] recalled with a grim smile the awkwardness of getting back with her to

[028-009] the gate from the point, far within the garden, where he had spoken.

[028-010] Except that this had happened in the fall, and now it was early spring,

[028-011] there seemed no change since then; the long years that had elapsed were

[028-012] like a winter between.

[028-013] He met people in groups and singly loitering through the paths, and

[028-014] chiefly speaking English; but no one spoke to him, and no one invaded

[028-015] the solitude in which he walked. But the garden itself seemed to know

[028-016] him, and to give him a tacit recognition; the great, foolish grotto

[028-017] before the gate, with its statues by Bandinelli, and the fantastic

[028-018] effects of drapery and flesh in party-coloured statues lifted high on

[028-019] either side of the avenue; the vast shoulder of wall, covered thick with

[028-020] ivy and myrtle, which he passed on his way to the amphitheatre behind

[028-021] the palace; the alternate figures and urns on their pedestals in the

[028-022] hemicycle, as if the urns were placed there to receive the ashes of the

[028-023] figures when they became extinct; the white statues or the colossal

[028-024] busts set at the ends of the long alleys against black curtains of

[028-025] foliage; the big fountain, with its group in the centre of the little

[028-026] lake, and the meadow, quiet and sad, that stretched away on one side

[028-027] from this; the keen light under the levels of the dense pines and

[028-028] ilexes; the paths striking straight on either hand from the avenue

[028-029] through which he sauntered, and the walk that coiled itself through the

[028-030] depths of the plantations; all knew him, and from them and from the

[028-031] winter neglect which was upon the place distilled a subtle influence, a

[028-032] charm, an appeal belonging to that combination of artifice and nature

[028-033] which is perfect only in an Italian garden under an Italian sky. He was

[028-034] right in the name which he mockingly gave the effect before he felt it;

[028-035] it was a debauch, delicate, refined, of unserious pensiveness, a smiling

[028-036] melancholy, in which he walked emancipated from his harassing hopes, and

[028-037] keeping only his shadowy regrets.

[028-038] [[theodore-colville|Colville]] did not care to scale the easy height from which you have the

[028-039] magnificent view, conscious of many photographs, of [[florence|Florence]]. He

[028-040] wandered about the skirts of that silent meadow, and seeing himself

[028-041] unseen, he invaded its borders far enough to pluck one of those large

[028-042] scarlet anemones, such as he had given his gentle enemy. It was tilting

[028-043] there in the breeze above the unkempt grass, and the grass was beginning

[028-044] to feel the spring, and to stir and stretch itself after its winter

[028-045] sleep; it was sprinkled with violets, but these he did not molest. He

[028-046] came back to a stained and mossy stone bench on the avenue, fronting a

[028-047] pair of rustic youths carved in stone, who had not yet finished some

[028-048] game in which he remembered seeing them engaged when he was there

[028-049] before. He had not walked fast, but he had walked far, and was warm

[028-050] enough to like the whiffs of soft wind on his uncovered head. The spring

[028-051] was coming; that was its breath, which you know unmistakably in Italy

[028-052] after all the kisses that winter gives. Some birds were singing in the

[028-053] trees; down an alley into which he could look, between the high walls of

[028-054] green, he could see two people in flirtation: he waited patiently till

[028-055] the young man should put his arm round the girl's waist, for the

[028-056] fleeting embrace from which she pushed it and fled further down the

[028-057] path.

[028-058] "Yes, it's spring," thought Colville; and then, with the selfishness of

[028-059] the troubled soul, he wished that it might be winter still and

[028-060] indefinitely. It occurred to him now that he should not go back to Des

[028-061] Vaches, for he did not know what he should do there. He would go to New

[028-062] York: though he did not know what he should do in New York, either.

[028-063] He became tired of looking at the people who passed, and of speculating

[028-064] about them through the second consciousness which enveloped the sad

[028-065] substance of his misgivings like an atmosphere; and he let his eyelids

[028-066] fall, as he leaned his head back against the tree behind his bench. Then

[028-067] their voices pursued him through the twilight that he had made himself,

[028-068] and forced him to the same weary conjecture as if he had seen their

[028-069] faces. He heard gay laughter, and laughter that affected gaiety; the

[028-070] tones of young men in earnest disquisition reached him through the veil,

[028-071] and the talk, falling to whisper, of girls, with the names of men in it;

[028-072] sums of money, a hundred francs, forty thousand francs, came in high

[028-073] tones; a husband and wife went by quarrelling in the false security of

[028-074] English, and snapping at each other as confidingly as if in the

[028-075] sanctuary of home. The man bade the woman not be a fool, and she asked

[028-076] him how she was to endure his company if she was not a fool.

[028-077] Colville opened his eyes to look after them, when a voice that he knew

[028-078] called out, "Why, it is Mr. Colville!"

[028-079] It was [[mrs-amsden|Mrs. Amsden]], and pausing with her, as if they had passed him in

[028-080] doubt, and arrested themselves when they had got a little way by, were

[028-081] [[effie-bowen|Effie Bowen]] and [[imogene-graham|Imogene Graham]]. The old lady had the child by the hand,

[028-082] and the girl stood a few paces apart from them. She was one of those

[028-083] beauties who have the property of looking very plain at times, and

[028-084] Colville, who had seen her in more than one transformation, now beheld

[028-085] her somehow clumsy of feature, and with the youth gone from her aspect.

[028-086] She seemed a woman of thirty, and she wore an unbecoming walking dress

[028-087] of a fashion that contributed to this effect of age. Colville was aware

[028-088] afterward of having wished that she was really as old and plain as she

[028-089] looked.

[028-090] He had to come forward, and put on the conventional delight of a

[028-091] gentleman meeting lady friends.

[028-092] "It's remarkable how your having your eyes shut estranged you," said

[028-093] Mrs. Amsden. "Now, if you had let me see you oftener in church, where

[028-094] people close their eyes a good deal for one purpose or another, I should

[028-095] have known you at once."

[028-096] "I hope you haven't lost a great deal of time, as it is, Mrs. Amsden,"

[028-097] said Colville. "Of course I should have had my eyes open if I had known

[028-098] you were going by."

[028-099] "Oh, don't apologise!" cried the old thing, with ready enjoyment of his

[028-100] tone.

[028-101] "I don't apologise for not being recognisable; I apologise for being

[028-102] visible," said Colville, with some shapeless impression that he ought to

[028-103] excuse his continued presence in Florence to Imogene, but keeping his

[028-104] eyes upon Mrs. Amsden, to whom what he said could not be intelligible.

[028-105] "I ought to be in Turin to-day."

[028-106] "In Turin! Are you going away from Florence?"

[028-107] "I'm going home."

[028-108] "Why, did _you_ know that?" asked the old lady of Imogene, who slightly

[028-109] nodded, and then of Effie, who also assented. "Really, the silence of

[028-110] the Bowen family in regard to the affairs of others is extraordinary.

[028-111] There never was a family more eminently qualified to live in Florence. I

[028-112] dare say that if I saw a little more of them, I might hope to reach the

[028-113] years of discretion myself some day. _Why_ are you going away? (You see

[028-114] I haven't reached them yet!) Are you tired of Florence already?"

[028-115] "No," said Colville passively; "Florence is tired of me."

[028-116] "You're quite sure?"

[028-117] "Yes; there's no mistaking one of her sex on such a point."

[028-118] Mrs. Amsden laughed. "Ah, a great many people mistake us, both ways. And

[028-119] you're really going back to America. What in the world for?"

[028-120] "I haven't the least idea."

[028-121] "Is America fonder of you than Florence?"

[028-122] "She's never told her love. I suspect it's merely that she's more used

[028-123] to me."

[028-124] They were walking, without any volition of his, down the slope of the

[028-125] broad avenue to the fountain, where he had already been.

[028-126] "Is your mother well?" he asked of the little girl. It seemed to him

[028-127] that he had better not speak to Imogene, who still kept that little

[028-128] distance from the rest, and get away as soon as he decently could.

[028-129] "She has a headache," said Effie.

[028-130] "Oh, I'm sorry," returned Colville.

[028-131] "Yes, she deputed me to take her young people out for an airing," said

[028-132] Mrs. Amsden; "and Miss Graham decided us for [[boboli-gardens|the Boboli]], where she

[028-133] hadn't been yet. I've done what I could to make the place attractive.

[028-134] But what is an old woman to do for a girl in a garden? We ought to have

[028-135] brought some other young people--some of the Inglehart boys. But we're

[028-136] respectable, we Americans abroad; we're decorous, above all things; and

[028-137] I don't know about meeting _you_ here, Mr. Colville. It has a very bad

[028-138] appearance. Are you sure that you didn't know I was to go by here at

[028-139] exactly half-past four?"

[028-140] "I was living from breath to breath in the expectation of seeing you.

[028-141] You must have noticed how eagerly I was looking out for you."

[028-142] "Yes, and with a single red anemone in your hand, so that I should know

[028-143] you without being obliged to put on my spectacles."

[028-144] "You divine everything, Mrs. Amsden," he said, giving her the flower.

[028-145] "I shall make my brags to [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]] when I see her," said the old lady.

[028-146] "How far into the country did you walk for this?"

[028-147] "As far as the meadow yonder."

[028-148] They had got down to the sheet of water from which the sea-horses of the

[028-149] fountain sprang, and the old lady sank upon a bench near it. Colville

[028-150] held out his hand toward Effie. "I saw a lot of violets over there in

[028-151] the grass."

[028-152] "Did you?" She put her hand eagerly into his, and they strolled off

[028-153] together. After a first motion to accompany them, Imogene sat down

[028-154] beside Mrs. Amsden, answering quietly the talk of the old lady, and

[028-155] seeming in nowise concerned about the expedition for violets. Except for

[028-156] a dull first glance, she did not look that way. Colville stood in the

[028-157] border of the grass, and the child ran quickly hither and thither in it,

[028-158] stooping from time to time upon the flowers. Then she came out to where

[028-159] he stood, and showed her bunch of violets, looking up into the face

[028-160] which he bent upon her, while he trifled with his cane. He had a very

[028-161] fatherly air with her.

[028-162] "I think I'll go and see what they've found," said Imogene irrelevantly,

[028-163] to a remark of Mrs. Amsden's about the expensiveness of Madame Bossi's

[028-164] bonnets.

[028-165] "Well," said the old lady. Imogene started, and the little girl ran to

[028-166] meet her. She detained Effie with her admiration of the violets till

[028-167] Colville lounged reluctantly up. "Go and show them to Mrs. Amsden," she

[028-168] said, giving back the violets, which she had been smelling. The child

[028-169] ran on. "Mr. Colville, I want to speak with you."

[028-170] "Yes," said Colville helplessly.

[028-171] "Why are you going away?"

[028-172] "Why? Oh, I've accomplished the objects--or no-objects--I came for," he

[028-173] said, with dreary triviality, "and I must hurry away to other fields of

[028-174] activity." He kept his eyes on her face, which he saw full of a

[028-175] passionate intensity, working to some sort of overflow.
