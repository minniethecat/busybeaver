[010-001] A little was quite enough for the eager officer. He had [[theodore-colville|Colville]] a

[010-002] partner in an instant, and the next he was on the floor.

[010-003] "Oh, what fun!" cried [[imogene-graham|Miss Graham]]; but the fun had not really begun yet.

[010-004] Colville had forgotten everything about the Lancers. He walked round

[010-005] like a bear in a pen: he capered to and fro with a futile absurdity;

[010-006] people poked him hither and thither; his progress was attended by

[010-007] rending noises from the trains over which he found his path. He smiled

[010-008] and cringed, and apologised to the hardening faces of the dancers: even

[010-009] Miss Graham's face had become very grave.

[010-010] "This won't do," said the Englishman at last, with cold insolence. He

[010-011] did not address himself to any one; he merely stopped; they all stopped,

[010-012] and Colville was effectively expelled the set; another partner was found

[010-013] for his lady, and he wandered giddily away. He did not know where to

[010-014] turn; the whole room must have seen what an incredible ass he had made

[010-015] of himself, but [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]] looked as if she had not seen.

[010-016] He went up to her, resolved to make fun of himself at the first sign she

[010-017] gave of being privy to his disgrace. But she only said, "Have you found

[010-018] your way to the supper-room yet?"

[010-019] "Oh yes; twice," he answered, and kept on talking with her and Madame

[010-020] Uccelli. After five minutes or so something occurred to Colville. "Have

[010-021] _you_ found the way to the supper-room yet, Mrs. Bowen?"

[010-022] "No!" she owned, with a small, pathetic laugh, which expressed a certain

[010-023] physical faintness, and reproached him with insupportable gentleness for

[010-024] his selfish obtuseness.

[010-025] "Let me show you the way," he cried.

[010-026] "Why, I _am_ rather hungry," said Mrs. Bowen, taking his arm, with a

[010-027] patient arrangement first of her fan, her bouquet, and her train, and

[010-028] then moving along by his side with a delicate footed pace, which

[010-029] insinuated and deprecated her dependence upon him.

[010-030] There were only a few people in the supper-room, and they had it

[010-031] practically to themselves. She took a cup of tea and a slice of buttered

[010-032] bread, with a little salad, which she excused herself from eating

[010-033] because it was the day after her headache. "I shouldn't have thought you

[010-034] _were_ hungry, Mrs. Bowen," he said, "if you hadn't told me so," and he

[010-035] recalled that, as a young girl, her friend used to laugh at her for

[010-036] having such a butterfly appetite; she was in fact one of those women who

[010-037] go through life the marvels of such of our brutal sex as observe the

[010-038] ethereal nature of their diet. But in an illogical revulsion of feeling.

[010-039] Colville, who was again cramming himself with all the solids and fluids

[010-040] in reach, and storing up a vain regret against the morrow, preferred her

[010-041] delicacy to the magnificent rapacity of Miss Graham: Imogene had passed

[010-042] from salad to ice, and at his suggestion had frankly reverted to salad

[010-043] again and then taken a second ice, with the robust appetite of perfect

[010-044] health and perfect youth. He felt a desire to speak against her to Mrs.

[010-045] Bowen, he did not know why and he did not know how; he veiled his

[010-046] feeling in an open attack. "Miss Graham has just been the cause of my

[010-047] playing the fool, with her dancing. She dances so superbly that she

[010-048] makes you want to dance too--she made me feel as if I _could_ dance."

[010-049] "Yes," said Mrs. Bowen; "it was very kind of you to complete the set. I

[010-050] saw you dancing," she added, without a glimmer of guilty consciousness

[010-051] in her eyes.

[010-052] It was very sweet, but Colville had to protest. "Oh no; you didn't see

[010-053] me _dancing_; you saw me _not dancing_. I am a ruined man, and I leave

[010-054] [[florence|Florence]] to-morrow; but I have the sad satisfaction of reflecting that I

[010-055] don't leave an unbroken train among the ladies of that set. And I have

[010-056] made one young Englishman so mad that there is a reasonable hope of his

[010-057] not recovering."

[010-058] "Oh no; you _don't_ think of going away for that!" said Mrs. Bowen, not

[010-059] heeding the rest of his joking.

[010-060] "Well, the time has been when I have left Florence for loss," said

[010-061] Colville, with the air of preparing himself to listen to reason.

[010-062] "You mustn't," said Mrs. Bowen briefly.

[010-063] "Oh, very well, then, I won't," said Colville whimsically, as if that

[010-064] settled it.

[010-065] Mrs. Bowen would not talk of the matter any more; he could see that with

[010-066] her kindness, which was always more than her tact, she was striving to

[010-067] get away from the subject. As he really cared for it no longer, this

[010-068] made him persist in clinging to it; he liked this pretty woman's being

[010-069] kind to him. "Well," he said finally, "I consent to stay in Florence on

[010-070] condition that you suggest some means of atonement for me which I can

[010-071] also make a punishment to Miss Graham."

[010-072] Mrs. Bowen did not respond to the question of placating and punishing

[010-073] her _protégée_ with sustained interest. They went back to Madame

[010-074] Uccelli, and to the other elderly ladies in the room that opened by

[010-075] archways upon the dancing-room.

[010-076] Imogene was on the floor, dancing not merely with unabated joy, but with

[010-077] a zest that seemed only to freshen from dance to dance. If she left the

[010-078] dance, it was to go out on her partner's arm to the supper-room.

[010-079] Colville could not decently keep on talking to Mrs. Bowen the whole

[010-080] evening; it would be too conspicuous; he devolved from frump to frump;

[010-081] he bored himself; he yawned in his passage from one of these mothers or

[010-082] fathers to another. The hours passed; it was two o'clock; Imogene was

[010-083] going out to the supper-room again. He was taking out his watch. She saw

[010-084] him, and "Oh, don't!" she cried, laughing, as she passed.

[010-085] The dancing went on; she was waltzing now in the interminable german.

[010-086] Some one had let down, a window in the dancing-room, and he was feeling

[010-087] it in his shoulder. Mrs. Bowen, across the room, looked heroically

[010-088] patient, but weary. He glanced, down at the frump on the sofa near, and

[010-089] realised that she had been making a long speech to him, which, he could

[010-090] see from her look, had ended in some sort of question.

[010-091] Three o'clock came, and they had to wait till the german was over. He

[010-092] felt that Miss Graham was behaving badly, ungratefully, selfishly; on

[010-093] the way home in the carriage he was silent from utter boredom and

[010-094] fatigue, but Mrs. Bowen was sweetly sympathetic with the girl's rapture.

[010-095] Imogene did not seem to feel his moodiness; she laughed, she joked, she

[010-096] told a number of things that happened, she hummed the air of the last

[010-097] waltz. "Isn't it divine?" she asked. "Oh! I feel as if I could dance for

[010-098] a week." She was still dancing; she gave Colville's foot an accidental

[010-099] tap in keeping time on the floor of the carriage to the tune she was

[010-100] humming. No one said anything about a next meeting when they parted at

[010-101] the gate of [[palazzo-pinti|Palazzo Pinti]], and Mrs. Bowen bade her coachman drive

[010-102] Colville to his hotel. But both the ladies' voices called good-night to

[010-103] him as he drove away. He fancied a shade of mocking in Miss Graham's

[010-104] voice.

[010-105] The great outer door of the hotel was locked, of course, and the poor

[010-106] little porter kept Colville thumping at it some time before he unlocked

[010-107] it, full of sleepy smiles and apologies. "I'm sorry to wake you up,"

[010-108] said Colville kindly.

[010-109] "It is my duty," said the porter, with amiable heroism. He discharged

[010-110] another duty by lighting a whole new candle, which would be set down to

[010-111] Colville's account, and went before him to his room, up the wide stairs,

[010-112] cold in their white linen path, and on through the crooked corridors

[010-113] haunted by the ghosts of extinct _tables d'hôte_, and full of goblin

[010-114] shadows. He had recovered a noonday suavity by the time he reached

[010-115] Colville's door, and bowed himself out, after lighting the candles

[010-116] within, with a sweet plenitude of politeness, which Colville, even in

[010-117] his gloomy mood, could not help admiring in a man in his shirt sleeves,

[010-118] with only one suspender up.

[010-119] If there had been a fire, Colville would have liked to sit down before

[010-120] it, and take an account of his feelings, but the atmosphere of a

[010-121] bed-chamber in a Florentine hotel at half-past three o'clock on a winter

[010-122] morning is not one that invites to meditation; and he made haste to get

[010-123] into bed, with nothing clearer in his mind than a shapeless sense of

[010-124] having been trifled with. He ought not to have gone to a dancing party,

[010-125] to begin with, and then he certainly ought not to have attempted to

[010-126] dance; so far he might have been master of the situation, and was

[010-127] responsible for it; but he was, over and above this, aware of not having

[010-128] wished to do either, of having been wrought upon against his convictions

[010-129] to do both. He regarded now with supreme loathing a fantastic purpose

[010-130] which he had formed while tramping round on those women's dresses, of

[010-131] privately taking lessons in dancing, and astonishing Miss Graham at the

[010-132] next ball where they met. Miss Graham? What did he care for that child?

[010-133] Or Mrs. Bowen either, for the matter of that? Had he come four thousand

[010-134] miles to be used, to be played with, by them? At this point Colville was

[010-135] aware of the brutal injustice of his mood. They were ladies, both of

[010-136] them, charming and good, and he had been a fool; that was all. It was

[010-137] not the first time he had been a fool for women. An inexpressible

[010-138] bitterness for that old wrong, which, however he had been used to laugh

[010-139] at it and despise it, had made his life solitary and barren, poured upon

[010-140] his soul; it was as if it had happened to him yesterday.

[010-141] A band of young men burst from one of the narrow streets leading into

[010-142] the piazza and straggled across it, letting their voices flare out upon

[010-143] the silence, and then drop extinct one by one. A whole world of faded

[010-144] associations flushed again in Colville's heart. This was Italy; this was

[010-145] Florence; and he execrated the hour in which he had dreamed of

[010-146] returning.

[010-147] VII

[010-148] The next morning's sunshine dispersed the black mood of the night

[010-149] before; but enough of Colville's self-disgust remained to determine him

[010-150] not to let his return to Florence be altogether vain, or his sojourn so

[010-151] idle as it had begun being. The vague purpose which he had cherished of

[010-152] studying the past life and character of the Florentines in their

[010-153] architecture shaped itself anew in the half-hour which he gave himself

[010-154] over his coffee; and he turned it over in his mind with that mounting

[010-155] joy in its capabilities which attends the contemplation of any sort of

[010-156] artistic endeavour. No people had ever more distinctly left the impress

[010-157] of their whole temper in their architecture, or more sharply

[010-158] distinguished their varying moods from period to period in their palaces

[010-159] and temples. He believed that he could not only supply that brief

[010-160] historical sketch of Florence which Mrs. Bowen had lamented the want of,

[010-161] but he could make her history speak an unintelligible, an unmistakeable

[010-162] tongue in every monument of the past, from the Etruscan wall at [[fiesole|Fiesole]]

[010-163] to the cheap, plain, and tasteless shaft raised to commemorate Italian

[010-164] Unity in the next piazza. With sketches from his own pencil,

[010-165] illustrative of points which he could not otherwise enforce, he could

[010-166] make such a book on Florence as did not exist, such a book as no one had

[010-167] yet thought of making. With this object in his mind, making and keeping

[010-168] him young, he could laugh with any one who liked at the vanity of the

[010-169] middle-aged Hoosier who had spoiled a set in the Lancers at Madame

[010-170] Uccelli's party; he laughed at him now alone, with a wholly impersonal

[010-171] sense of his absurdity.
