## Description

The Post-Democratic-Republican was a daily newspaper published in [[des-vaches|Des Vaches]], Indiana, that succeeded the [[democratic-republican|Democrat-Republican]] after [[theodore-colville|Theodore Colville]] sold his interest in the latter paper (001-073). Its name "had agglutinated the titles of two of its predecessors, after the fashion of American journals" (001-073 to 001-074), reflecting the consolidations that had shaped the local press — the *Republican*, the *Wabash Valley Democrat*, and the Democrat-Republican itself. The paper was launched by a Republican committee as a rival organ after Colville permitted his name to be used by the opposing party in a congressional race and was "defeated by a tremendous majority" (001-054 to 001-055). When Colville contemptuously offered to sell, the committee accepted (001-065 to 001-067).

## Role in the Novel

The Post-Democratic-Republican serves as [[theodore-colville|Colville]]'s severed connection to his former life and a measure of his post-disgrace condition. During the months he remained in [[des-vaches|Des Vaches]] closing his affairs, the paper "was fulsome in its complimentary allusions to him" (001-075), politely inventing "the fiction that he was going to Europe for his health, impaired by his journalistic labours" (001-076). Colville detected in these allusions "the point of a fine irony, of which he had himself introduced the practice in the Democrat-Republican" (001-080), and experienced "a sense of personal impoverishment" (001-081) at the discovery that the paper was as good without him.

The paper remains a presence in Colville's consciousness throughout his Florentine sojourn. In Chapter 1, seized by "a tide of ridiculous home-sickness," he finds that "the first passages of a letter to the Post-Democrat-Republican began to shape themselves in his mind" (001-174 to 001-175), reviving the habit of writing travel correspondence for his readers. In Chapter 2, standing on the [[ponte-vecchio]], Colville composes "that sarcastic study of his own condition of mind for the advantage of his late readers in the Post-Democrat-Republican" (002-051 to 002-052). In Chapter 5, he frames "a bitterly ironical comparison of the achievements of Italy and America in the last ten years" (005-044 to 005-045) as a continuation of that same letter. The newspaper thus functions as Colville's phantom audience — the imagined readership to whom he still performs his role as public wit, even as he has left journalism behind.

Politically, Colville's honesty compels him to admit that under the new management the Post-Democratic-Republican was not markedly worse: "Except that it was rather more Stalwart in its Republicanism, and had turned quite round on the question of the tariff, it was very much what it had always been" (001-090 to 001-091). It kept "the old decency of tone which he had given it, and it maintained the literary character which he was proud of" (001-092 to 001-093). This admission -- that the institution flourished without him -- deepens the novel's exploration of [[expatriate-identity]] and the expendability of the individual to the community he once served.

## Historical Basis

The Post-Democratic-Republican represents the archetypal nineteenth-century American small-city newspaper — the product of multiple mergers and name changes that were common in the era of intensely partisan journalism. Its name, agglutinating the titles of predecessor papers, reflects a genuine historical pattern: newspapers in towns like Howells's own Ohio and Indiana frequently consolidated under hyphenated titles (the *Democratic-Republican*, the *Whig-and-Journal*, the *Gazette-and-Free-Press*) as ownership changed hands and party alignments shifted. The paper's Stalwart Republican turn after Colville's departure mirrors the post-Reconstruction realignment of the Republican Party toward protectionism and machine politics. Howells, who had worked as a journalist and editor himself, renders with documentary fidelity the rhythms of newspaper life — the training of assistants to write in the editor's style, the continued excellence of the literary department, and the curious fact that "a journalist of strong characteristics leaves the tradition of himself in such degree with the journal he has created that he seems to bring very little away" (001-081 to 001-084).

## Key Scenes

### Chapter 1: The Paper's Origins (001-073 to 001-099)

The novel's longest passage about the Post-Democratic-Republican recounts its formation after [[theodore-colville|Colville]]'s [[colville-political-disgrace|political disgrace]] and his observations of its quality under new ownership:

> "During the months that he remained to close up his affairs after the sale of his paper, the Post-Democrat-Republican (the newspaper had agglutinated the titles of two of its predecessors, after the fashion of American journals) was fulsome in its complimentary allusions to him." (001-073 to 001-076)

### Chapter 2: The Ponte Vecchio Letter (002-051 to 002-052)

On the [[ponte-vecchio]] at dusk, Colville composes a letter for his old readers, the act of writing serving as a bridge between his American past and his Florentine present:

> "Colville had reached this point in that sarcastic study of his own condition of mind for the advantage of his late readers in the Post-Democrat-Republican, when he was aware of a polite rustling of draperies…" (002-051 to 002-052)

### Chapter 5: The Bitterly Ironical Letter (005-044 to 005-045)

Framing his thoughts for the paper, Colville indulges in self-mocking political commentary that reveals his ongoing absorption in the [[youth-and-age|comparative fortunes]] of the Old World and the New:

> "He framed a continuation of the letter he began on the Ponte Vecchio to the Post-Democrat-Republican, in which he made a bitterly ironical comparison of the achievements of Italy and America in the last ten years." (005-044 to 005-045)
