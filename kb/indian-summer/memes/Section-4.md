[004-001] The door opened, and some English ladies entered, faintly acknowledging,

[004-002] provisionally ignoring, his presence, and talking of what they had been

[004-003] doing since lunch. They agreed that it was really too cold in the

[004-004] churches for any pleasure in the pictures, and that the Pitti Gallery,

[004-005] where they had those braziers, was the only place you could go with

[004-006] comfort. A French lady and her husband came in; a Russian lady followed;

[004-007] an Italian gentleman, an American family, and three or four detached men

[004-008] of the English-speaking race, whose language at once became the law of

[004-009] the table.

[004-010] As the dinner progressed from soup to fish, and from the _entrée_ to the

[004-011] roast and salad, the combined effect of the pleasant cheer and the

[004-012] increasing earnestness of the stove made the room warmer and warmer.

[004-013] They drank Chianti wine from the wicker-covered flasks, tied with tufts

[004-014] of red and green silk, in which they serve table wine at [[florence|Florence]], and

[004-015] said how pretty the bottles were, but how the wine did not seem very

[004-016] good.

[004-017] "It certainly isn't so good as it used to be," said [[theodore-colville|Colville]].

[004-018] "Ah, then you have been in Florhence before." said the French lady,

[004-019] whose English proved to be much better than the French that he began to

[004-020] talk to her in.

[004-021] "Yes, a great while ago; in a state of pre-existence, in fact," he said.

[004-022] The lady looked a little puzzled, but interested. "In a state of

[004-023] prhe-existence?" she repeated.

[004-024] "Yes; when I was young," he added, catching the gleam in her eye. "When

[004-025] I was twenty-four. A great while ago."

[004-026] "You must be an Amerhican," said the lady, with a laugh.

[004-027] "Why do you think so? From my accent?"

[004-028] "Frhom your metaphysics too. The Amerhicans like to talk in that way."

[004-029] "I didn't know it," said Colville.

[004-030] "They like to strhike the key of personality; they can't endure not

[004-031] being interhested. They must rhelate everything to themselves or to

[004-032] those with whom they are talking."

[004-033] "And the French, no?" asked Colville.

[004-034] The lady laughed again. "There is a large Amerhican colony in Parhis.

[004-035] Perhaps we have learned to be like you."

[004-036] The lady's husband did not speak English, and it was probably what they

[004-037] had been saying that she interpreted to him, for he smiled, looking

[004-038] forward to catch Colville's eye in a friendly way, and as if he would

[004-039] not have him take his wife's talk too seriously.

[004-040] The Italian gentleman on Colville's right was politely offering him the

[004-041] salad, which had been left for the guests to pass to one another.

[004-042] Colville thanked him in Italian, and they began to talk of Italian

[004-043] affairs. One thing led to another, and he found that his new friend, who

[004-044] was not yet his acquaintance, was a member of Parliament, and a

[004-045] republican.

[004-046] "That interests me as an American," said Colville. "But why do you want

[004-047] a republic in Italy?"

[004-048] "When we have a constitutional king, why should we have a king?" asked

[004-049] the Italian.

[004-050] An Englishman across the table relieved Colville from the difficulty of

[004-051] answering this question by asking him another that formed talk about it

[004-052] between them. He made his tacit observation that the English, since he

[004-053] met them last, seemed to have grown in the grace of facile speech with

[004-054] strangers; it was the American family which kept its talk within itself,

[004-055] and hushed to a tone so low that no one else could hear it. Colville did

[004-056] not like their mumbling; for the honour of the country, which we all

[004-057] have at heart, however little we think it, he would have preferred that

[004-058] they should speak up, and not seem afraid or ashamed; he thought the

[004-059] English manner was better. In fact, he found himself in an unexpectedly

[004-060] social mood; he joined in helping to break the ice; he laughed and

[004-061] hazarded comment with those who were new-comers like himself, and was

[004-062] very respectful of the opinions of people who had been longer in the

[004-063] hotel, when they spoke of the cook's habit of underdoing the vegetables.

[004-064] The dinner at the [[hotel-datene|Hotel d'Atene]] made an imposing show on the _carte du

[004-065] jour_; it looked like ten or twelve courses, but in fact it was five,

[004-066] and even when eked out with roast chestnuts and butter into six, it

[004-067] seemed somehow to stop very abruptly, though one seemed to have had

[004-068] enough. You could have coffee afterward if you ordered it. Colville

[004-069] ordered it, and was sorry when the last of his commensals, slightly

[004-070] bowing him a good-night, left him alone to it.

[004-071] He had decided that he need not fear the damp in a cab rapidly driven to

[004-072] [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]]'s. When he went to his room he had his doubts about his

[004-073] dress-coat; but he put it on, and he took the crush hat with which he

[004-074] had provided himself in coming through London. That was a part of the

[004-075] social panoply unknown in [[des-vaches|Des Vaches]]; he had hardly been a dozen times

[004-076] in evening dress there in fifteen years, and his suit was as new as his

[004-077] hat. As he turned to the glass he thought himself personable enough, and

[004-078] in fact he was one of those men who look better in evening dress than in

[004-079] any other: the broad expanse of shirt bosom, with its three small studs

[004-080] of gold dropping, points of light, one below the other, softened his

[004-081] strong, almost harsh face, and balanced his rather large head. In his

[004-082] morning coat, people had to look twice at him to make sure that he did

[004-083] not look common; but now he was not wrong in thinking that he had an air

[004-084] of distinction, as he took his hat under his arm and stood before the

[004-085] pier-glass in his room. He was almost tempted to shave, and wear his

[004-086] moustache alone, as he used to do: he had let his beard grow because he

[004-087] found that under the lax social regimen at Des Vaches he neglected

[004-088] shaving, and went about days at a time with his face in an offensive

[004-089] stubble. Taking his chin between his fingers, and peering closer into

[004-090] the mirror, he wondered how Mrs. Bowen should have known him; she must

[004-091] have remembered him very vividly. He would like to take off his beard

[004-092] and put on the youthfulness that comes of shaving, and see what she

[004-093] would say. Perhaps, he thought, with a last glance at his toilet, he was

[004-094] overdoing it, if she were only to have a few people, as she promised. He

[004-095] put a thick neckerchief over his chest so as not to provoke that

[004-096] abominable rheumatism by any sort of exposure, and he put on his ulster

[004-097] instead of the light spring overcoat that he had gone about with all

[004-098] day.

[004-099] He found that [[palazzo-pinti|Palazzo Pinti]], when you came to it, was rather a grand

[004-100] affair, with a gold-banded porter eating salad in the lodge at the great

[004-101] doorway, and a handsome gate of iron cutting you off from the regions

[004-102] above till you had rung the bell of Mrs. Bowen's apartment, when it

[004-103] swung open of itself, and you mounted. At her door a man in modified

[004-104] livery received Colville, and helped him off with his overcoat so

[004-105] skilfully that he did not hurt his rheumatic shoulder at all; there were

[004-106] half a dozen other hats and coats on the carved chests that stood at

[004-107] intervals along the wall, and some gayer wraps that exhaled a faint,

[004-108] fascinating fragrance on the chilly air. Colville experienced the slight

[004-109] exhilaration, the mingled reluctance and eagerness, of a man who

[004-110] formally re-enters an assemblage of society after long absence from it,

[004-111] and rubbing his hands a little nervously together, he put aside the

[004-112] yellow Abruzzi blanket _portière_, and let himself into the brilliant

[004-113] interior.

[004-114] Mrs. Bowen stood in front of the fire in a brown silk of subdued

[004-115] splendour, and with her hands and fan and handkerchief tastefully

[004-116] composed before her. At sight of Colville she gave a slight start, which

[004-117] would have betrayed to him, if he had been another woman, that she had

[004-118] not really believed he would come, and came forward with a rustle and

[004-119] murmur of pleasure to meet him; he had politely made a rush upon her, so

[004-120] as to spare her this exertion, and he was tempted to a long-forgotten

[004-121] foppishness of attitude as he stood talking with her during the brief

[004-122] interval before she introduced him to any of the company. She had been

[004-123] honest with him; there were not more than twenty-five or thirty people

[004-124] there; but if he had overdone it in dressing for so small an affair, he

[004-125] was not alone, and he was not sorry. He was sensible of a better

[004-126] personal effect than the men in frock-coats and cut-aways were making,

[004-127] and he perceived with self-satisfaction that his evening dress was of

[004-128] better style than that of the others who wore it; at least no one else

[004-129] carried a crush hat.--

[004-130] At forty-one a man is still very much of a boy, and Colville was

[004-131] obscurely willing that Mrs. Bowen, whose life since they last met at an

[004-132] evening party had been passed chiefly at New York and Washington, should

[004-133] see that he was a man of the world in spite of Des Vaches. Before she

[004-134] had decided which of the company she should first present him to, her

[004-135] daughter came up to his elbow with a cup of tea and some bread and

[004-136] butter on a tray, and gave him good-evening with charming correctness of

[004-137] manner. "Really," he said, turning about to take the cup, "I thought it

[004-138] was you, Mrs. Bowen, who had got round to my side with a sash on. How do

[004-139] you and Miss Effie justify yourselves in looking so bewitchingly alike?"

[004-140] "You notice it, then?" Mrs. Bowen seemed delighted.

[004-141] "I did every moment you were together to-day. You don't mind my having

[004-142] been so personal in my observations?"

[004-143] "Oh, not at all," said Mrs. Bowen, and Colville laughed.

[004-144] "It must be true," he said, "what a French lady said to me at the

[004-145] _table-d'hôte_ dinner to-night: 'the Amerhicans always strhike the note

[004-146] of perhsonality.'" He neatly imitated the French lady's guttural accent.

[004-147] "I suppose we do," mused Mis. Bowen, "and that we don't mind it in each

[004-148] other. I wish _you_ would say which I shall introduce you to," she said,

[004-149] letting her glance stray invisibly over her company, where all the

[004-150] people seemed comfortably talking.

[004-151] "Oh, there's no hurry; put it off till to-morrow," said Colville.

[004-152] "Oh no; that won't do," said Mrs. Bowen, like a woman who has public

[004-153] duties to perform, and is resolute to sacrifice her private pleasure to

[004-154] them. But she postponed them a moment longer. "I hope you got home

[004-155] before the rain," she said.

[004-156] "Yes," returned Colville. "That is, I don't mind a little sprinkling.

[004-157] Who is the Junonian young person at the end of the room?"

[004-158] "Ah," said Mrs. Bowen, "you can't be introduced to _her_ first. But

[004-159] _isn't_ she lovely?"

[004-160] "Yes. It's a wonderful effect of white and gold."

[004-161] "You mustn't say that to her. She was doubtful about her dress, because

[004-162] she says that the ivory white with her hair makes her look just like

[004-163] white and gold furniture."

[004-164] "Present me at once, then, before I forget not to say it to her."

[004-165] "No; I must keep you for some other person: anybody can talk to a pretty

[004-166] girl."

[004-167] Colville said he did not know whether to smile or shed tears at this

[004-168] embittered compliment, and pretended an eagerness for the acquaintance

[004-169] denied him.

[004-170] Mrs. Bowen seemed disposed to intensify his misery. "Did you ever see a

[004-171] more statuesque creature--with those superb broad shoulders and that

[004-172] little head, and that thick braid brought round over the top? Doesn't

[004-173] her face, with that calm look in those starry eyes, and that peculiar

[004-174] fall of the corners of the mouth, remind you of some of those exquisite

[004-175] great Du Maurier women? That style of face is very fashionable now: you

[004-176] might think he had made it so."
