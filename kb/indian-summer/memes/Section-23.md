[023-001] The latter turned, with his cup still in his hand, and wandered about

[023-002] through the company, hoping he might see [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]] among the groups

[023-003] peering at the pictures or solidly blocking the view in front of them.

[023-004] He did not find her, but he found [[imogene-graham|Imogene Graham]] standing somewhat apart

[023-005] near a window. He saw her face light up at sight of him, and then darken

[023-006] again as he approached.

[023-007] "Isn't this rather an unnatural state of things?" he asked when he had

[023-008] come up. "I ought to be obliged to fight my way to you through

[023-009] successive phalanxes of young men crowding round with cups of tea

[023-010] outstretched in their imploring hands. Have you had some tea?"

[023-011] "Thank you, no; I don't wish any," said the young girl, so coldly that

[023-012] he could not help noticing, though commonly he was man enough to notice

[023-013] very few things.

[023-014] "How is Effie to-day?" he asked quickly.

[023-015] "Oh, quite well," said Imogene.

[023-016] "I don't see Mrs. Bowen," he ventured further.

[023-017] "No," answered the girl, still very lifelessly; "I came with Mrs.

[023-018] Fleming." She looked about the room as if not to look at him.

[023-019] He now perceived a distinct intention to snub him. He smiled. "Have you

[023-020] seen the pictures? There are two or three really lovely ones."

[023-021] "[[mrs-fleming|Mrs. Fleming]] will be here in a moment, I suppose," said Imogene

[023-022] evasively, but not with all her first coldness.

[023-023] "Let us steal a march on her," said [[theodore-colville|Colville]] briskly. "When she comes

[023-024] you can tell her that I showed you the pictures."

[023-025] "I don't know," faltered the girl.

[023-026] "Perhaps it isn't necessary you should," he suggested.

[023-027] She glanced at him with questioning trepidation.

[023-028] "The respective duties of chaperone and _protégée_ are rather undefined.

[023-029] Where the chaperone isn't there to command, the _protégée_ isn't there

[023-030] to obey. I suppose you'd know if you were at home?"

[023-031] "Oh yes!"

[023-032] "Let me imagine myself at a loan exhibition in Buffalo. Ah! that appeal

[023-033] is irresistible. You'll come, I see."

[023-034] She hesitated; she looked at the nearest picture, then followed him to

[023-035] another. He now did what he had refused to do for the old lady who

[023-036] tempted him to it; he made fun of the pictures a little, but so amiably

[023-037] and with so much justice to their good points that the painter himself

[023-038] would not have minded his jesting. From time to time he made Imogene

[023-039] smile, but in her eyes lurked a look of uneasiness, and her manner

[023-040] expressed a struggle against his will which might have had its pathos

[023-041] for him in different circumstances, but now it only incited him to make

[023-042] her forget herself more and more; he treated her as one does a child

[023-043] that is out of sorts--coaxingly, ironically.

[023-044] When they had made the round of the rooms Mrs. Fleming was not at the

[023-045] window where she had left Imogene; the girl detected the top of her

[023-046] bonnet still in the next room.

[023-047] "The chaperone is never there when you come back with the _protégée_,"

[023-048] said Colville. "It seems to be the nature of the chaperone."

[023-049] Imogene turned very grave. "I think I ought to go to her," she murmured.

[023-050] "Oh no; she ought to come to you; I stand out for _protégée_'s rights."

[023-051] "I suppose she will come directly."

[023-052] "She sees me with you; she knows you are safe."

[023-053] "Oh, of course," said the girl. After a constraint which she marked by

[023-054] rather a long silence, she added, "How strange a roomful of talking

[023-055] sounds, doesn't it? Just like a great caldron boiling up and bubbling

[023-056] over. Wouldn't you like to know what they're all saying?"

[023-057] "Oh, it's quite bad enough to see them," replied Colville frivolously.

[023-058] "I think a company of gentlemen with their hats off look very queer,

[023-059] don't you?" she asked, after another interval.

[023-060] "Well, really," said Colville, laughing, "I don't know that the

[023-061] spectacle ever suggested any metaphysical speculations to me. I rather

[023-062] think they look queerer with their hats on."

[023-063] "Oh yes."

[023-064] "Though there is not very much to choose. We're a queer-looking set,

[023-065] anyway."

[023-066] He got himself another cup of tea, and coming back to her, allowed her

[023-067] to make the efforts to keep up the conversation, and was not without a

[023-068] malicious pleasure in her struggles. They interested him as social

[023-069] exercises which, however abrupt and undexterous now, were destined, with

[023-070] time and practice, to become the finesse of a woman of society, and to

[023-071] be accepted, even while they were still abrupt and undexterous, as

[023-072] touches of character. He had broken up that coldness with which she had

[023-073] met him at first, and now he let her adjust the fragments as she could

[023-074] to the new situation. He wore that air of a gentleman who has been

[023-075] talking a long time to a lady, and who will not dispute her possession

[023-076] with a new-comer.

[023-077] But no one came, though, as he cast his eyes carelessly over the

[023-078] company, he found that it had been increased by the accession of eight

[023-079] or ten young fellows, with a refreshing light of originality in their

[023-080] faces, and little touches of difference from the other men in their

[023-081] dress.

[023-082] "Oh, there are the Inglehart boys!" cried the girl, with a flash of

[023-083] excitement.

[023-084] There was a sensation of interest and friendliness in the company as

[023-085] these young fellows, after their moment of social intimidation, began to

[023-086] gather round the pictures, and to fling their praise and blame about,

[023-087] and talk the delightful shop of the studio.

[023-088] The sight of their fresh young faces, the sound of their voices, struck

[023-089] a pang of regret that was almost envy to Colville's heart.

[023-090] Imogene followed them with eager eyes. "Oh," she sighed, "shouldn't you

[023-091] like to be an artist?"

[023-092] "I should, very much."

[023-093] "Oh, I beg your pardon; I forgot. I knew you were an architect."

[023-094] "I should say I used to be, if you hadn't objected to my perfects and

[023-095] preterits."

[023-096] What came next seemed almost an accident.

[023-097] "I didn't suppose you cared for my objections, so long as I amused you."

[023-098] She suddenly glanced at him, as if terrified at her own words.

[023-099] "Have you been trying to amuse me?" he asked.

[023-100] "Oh no. I thought----"

[023-101] "Oh, then," said Colville sharply, "you meant that I was amusing myself

[023-102] with you?" She glanced at him in terror of his divination, but could not

[023-103] protest. "Has any one told you that?" he pursued, with sudden angry

[023-104] suspicion.

[023-105] "No, _no_ one," began Imogene. She glanced about her, frightened. They

[023-106] stood quite alone where they were; the people had mostly wandered off

[023-107] into the other rooms. "Oh, don't--I didn't mean--I didn't intend to say

[023-108] anything----"

[023-109] "But you have said something--something that surprises me from _you_,

[023-110] and hurts me. I wish to know whether you say it from yourself."

[023-111] "I don't know--yes. That is, not----Oh, I wish Mrs. Fleming----"

[023-112] She looked as if another word of pursuit would put it beyond her power

[023-113] to control herself.

[023-114] "Let me take you to Mrs. Fleming," said Colville, with freezing

[023-115] _hauteur_; and led the way where the top of Mrs. Fleming's bonnet still

[023-116] showed itself. He took leave at once, and hastily parting with his host,

[023-117] found himself in the street, whirled in many emotions. The girl had not

[023-118] said that from herself, but it was from some woman; he knew that by the

[023-119] directness of the phrase and its excess, for he had noticed that women

[023-120] who liked to beat about the bush in small matters have a prodigious

[023-121] straightforwardness in more vital affairs, and will even call grey black

[023-122] in order clearly to establish the presence of the black in that colour.

[023-123] He could hardly keep himself from going to [[palazzo-pinti|Palazzo Pinti]].

[023-124] But he contrived to go to his hotel instead, where he ate a moody

[023-125] dinner, and then, after an hour's solitary bitterness in his room, went

[023-126] out and passed the evening at the theatre. The play was one of those

[023-127] fleering comedies which render contemptible for the time all honest and

[023-128] earnest intention, and which surely are a whiff from the bottomless pit

[023-129] itself. It made him laugh at the serious strain of self-question that

[023-130] had mingled with his resentment; it made him laugh even at his

[023-131] resentment, and with its humour in his thoughts, sent him off to sleep

[023-132] in a sottish acceptance of whatever was trivial in himself as the only

[023-133] thing that was real and lasting.

[023-134] He slept late, and when Paolo brought up his breakfast, he brought with

[023-135] it a letter which he said had been left with the porter an hour before.

[023-136] A faint appealing perfume of violet exhaled from the note, and mingled

[023-137] with the steaming odours of the coffee and boiled milk, when Colville,

[023-138] after a glance at the unfamiliar handwriting of the superscription,

[023-139] broke the seal.

[023-140] "DEAR MR. COLVILLE,--I don't know what you will think of my writing to

[023-141] you, but perhaps you can't think worse of me than you do already, and

[023-142] anything will be better than the misery that I am in. I have not been

[023-143] asleep all night. I hate myself for telling you, but I do want you to

[023-144] understand how I have felt. I would give worlds if I could take back the

[023-145] words that you say wounded you. I didn't mean to wound you. Nobody is to

[023-146] blame for them but me; nobody ever breathed a word about you that was

[023-147] meant in unkindness.

[023-148] "I am not ashamed of writing this, _whatever_ you think, and I will sign

[023-149] my name in full. IMOGENE GRAHAM."

[023-150] Colville had commonly a good appetite for his breakfast, but now he let

[023-151] his coffee stand long un-tasted. There were several things about this

[023-152] note that touched him--the childlike simplicity and directness, the

[023-153] generous courage, even the imperfection and crudity of the literature.

[023-154] However he saw it afterward, he saw it then in its true intention. He

[023-155] respected that intention; through all the sophistications in which life

[023-156] had wrapped him, it awed him a little. He realised that if he had been

[023-157] younger he would have gone to Imogene herself with her letter. He felt

[023-158] for the moment a rush of the emotion which he would once not have

[023-159] stopped to examine, which he would not have been capable of examining.

[023-160] But now his [[social-duty|duty]] was clear; he must go to Mrs. Bowen. In the noblest

[023-161] human purpose there is always some admixture, however slight, of less

[023-162] noble motive, and Colville was not without the willingness to see

[023-163] whatever embarrassment she might feel when he showed her the letter, and

[023-164] to invoke her finest tact to aid him in re-assuring the child.

[023-165] She was alone in her drawing-room, and she told him in response to his

[023-166] inquiry for their health that Imogene and Effie had gone out to drive.

[023-167] She looked so pretty in the quiet house dress in which she rose from the

[023-168] sofa and stood, letting him come the whole way to greet her, that he did

[023-169] not think of any other look in her, but afterward he remembered an

[023-170] evidence of inner tumult in her brightened eyes.

[023-171] He said, smiling, "I'm so glad to see you alone," and this brought still

[023-172] another look into her face, which also he afterward remembered. She did

[023-173] not reply, but made a sound in her throat like a bird when it stirs

[023-174] itself for flight or song. It was a strange, indefinite little note, in

[023-175] which Colville thought he detected trepidation at the time, and recalled

[023-176] for the sort of expectation suggested in it. She stood waiting for him

[023-177] to go on.

[023-178] "I have come to get you to help me out of trouble."

[023-179] "Yes?" said Mrs. Bowen, with a vague smile. "I always supposed you would

[023-180] be able to help yourself out of trouble. Or perhaps wouldn't mind it if

[023-181] you were in it."

[023-182] "Oh yes, I mind it very much," returned Colville, refusing her banter,

[023-183] if it were banter. "Especially this sort of trouble, which involves some

[023-184] one else in the discomfort." He went on abruptly: "I have been held up

[023-185] to a young lady as a person who was amusing himself with her, and I was

[023-186] so absurd as to be angry when she told me, and demanded the name of my

[023-187] friend, whoever it was. My behaviour seems to have given the young lady

[023-188] a bad night, and this morning she writes to tell me so, and to take all

[023-189] the blame on herself, and to assure me that no harm was meant me by any

[023-190] one. Of course I don't want her to be distressed about it. Perhaps you

[023-191] can guess who has been writing to me."
