# Fiesole

## Description

Fiesole is an ancient hill town overlooking Florence from the northeast, reached by a "long, devious climb of the hills crowned by the ancient Etruscan city" (041). The town sits atop a ridge that commands sweeping views of the Arno valley and the city of Florence below. Its compact centre is built around a "quaint little piazza" (041-151) where vendors sell straw fans, baskets, and parasols to visitors. The chief antiquities of Fiesole are the ruins of a Roman theatre and a stretch of Etruscan wall beyond it — the latter consisting of huge stone blocks laid with such precision that they seem modern, while the partially excavated theatre bears the marks of ongoing decay. The town also contains a modest cathedral and a small archaeological museum. At the time of the novel's visit in early spring, the hillsides are dotted with pink peach blossoms and the sky has "an American blueness and vastness" (041).

## Significance in the Novel

The Fiesole excursion is a pivotal social event in the novel's latter half, bringing together all the major characters — Colville, Mrs. Bowen, Imogene Graham, Effie, and the young clergyman Mr. Morton — for a day away from Florence. The outing is planned in chapter 38 at Mrs. Bowen's suggestion: she reveals that they "had been talking all winter of going to Fiesole, where Imogene had never been yet; and upon comparison it appeared that none of them had yet been to Fiesole except herself" (038-113–038-114). The trip nearly founders the night before, when Imogene — in a moment of tension with Mrs. Bowen — inquires "We were to have gone to Fiesole to-morrow" with an uncertain note (040-042), but Imogene insists on going.

Throughout the drive and the visit, Fiesole serves as a stage for the fraught dynamics among the characters. Colville labours to sustain conversation while Mrs. Bowen and Imogene retreat into silence, burdened by the unresolved emotional entanglements among them. The Etruscan antiquity of the town becomes a vehicle for Colville's self-deprecating wit: he jokes to Effie that the straw vendors are "little Etruscan girls, all at least twenty-five hundred years old" (041-123), and when challenged by Imogene for his interest in the past, he replies, "I should really like to meet a few Etruscans in Fiesole this morning. I should feel as if I'd got amongst my contemporaries at last; they would understand me" (041-143). This banter masks a genuine alienation Colville feels — out of step with the young, out of step with love, and increasingly aware of his own incongruity.

The arrival of the Inglehart boys — young American painters who had "walked up to Fiesole the afternoon before, and they had been sketching there all the morning" (041-159–041-160) — adds a note of artistic bohemianism and provides Colville with temporary relief from his emotional burdens as they all tour the Cathedral, the Museum, and the Belvedere together (041-164–041-165).

## Key Scenes

### The Roman Theatre and Etruscan Wall

After lunch, the party visits the ruins. The Etruscan wall proves too remote in its antiquity to stir the visitors:

> The former seems older than the latter, whose huge blocks of stone lie as firmly and evenly in their courses as if placed there a year ago; the turf creeps to the edge at top, and some small trees nod along the crest of the wall, whose ancient face, clean and bare, looks sternly out over a vast prospect, now young and smiling in the first delight of spring. (042-014–042-017)

The Roman theatre, by contrast, restores "the human interest" (042). Effie runs up and down the stairways, Mrs. Bowen sits on the lower steps with Mr. Morton, and Imogene asks Colville to climb with her to the top, where their private conversation resumes on a strained note. Colville reflects wryly on the Etruscan wall's failure to engage the party:

> "I suspect that there is such a thing as a ruin's being too old," said Colville. "Our Etruscan friends made the mistake of building their wall several thousand years too soon for our purpose." (042-030–042-031)

### The Carriage Accident on the Return

The return journey is marked by a dramatic incident. As the carriage descends the hills, the party encounters "a peasant driving a herd of the black pigs of the country" (042-163). The horses rear at the sight of the "sable crew" and "backing violently uphill, set the carriage across the road, with its hind wheels a few feet from the brink of the wall" (042-166–042-168). Colville rushes to rescue Mrs. Bowen and Effie, but Imogene — frozen in defiance — is pulled to safety by Mr. Morton, a moment that crystallises the shifting allegiances among the characters and leaves Colville shaken. The artists, who had accompanied the carriage partway, return to spend the night in Fiesole (042-154).

## Real-World Counterpart

Fiesole (ancient Faesulae) is a real town in Tuscany, approximately 8 kilometres northeast of Florence. Founded by the Etruscans and later a Roman colony, it preserves substantial archaeological remains including a first-century BCE Roman theatre, Roman baths, and a stretch of Etruscan walls dating to the fourth century BCE — the very wall that Howells' characters find so inaccessibly ancient. The Cathedral of San Romolo (the "Cathedral" of the novel), the Bandini Museum, and the panoramic Belvedere remain tourist destinations today. In the nineteenth century, Fiesole was a standard day-trip for the Anglo-American expatriate community in Florence, exactly as Howells depicts it. The Renaissance sculptor Mino da Fiesole (c. 1429–1484), mentioned elsewhere in the novel (013-071), took his name from the town. Howells himself knew the Florence–Fiesole area intimately from his years as American consul in Venice and his travels in Italy, and his description of the sites, the drive, and the atmosphere is faithful to the experience of a late-nineteenth-century visitor.
