# Des Vaches

## Description

Des Vaches is a fictional town on the banks of the Wabash River in Indiana, where Theodore Colville spent fifteen years as the editor and proprietor of the *Democrat-Republican* newspaper. The town takes its name from the old French *voyageurs* who, camping on the site, saw a herd of buffalo cows grazing there -- "vaches" being the French word for cows (001-141--001-143). Its full or original name appears in the novel as "Prairie des Vaches" (031-057). The town is described as "ambitious" (001-033), with the Main Street Bridge as its most frequently mentioned landmark, spanning the "tawny sweep of the Wabash" (001-031--001-032). By the time Colville arrives, it is substantial enough to support two competing daily newspapers, and to have a jail and court-house already built -- the only architecture the town seems to require (001-145--001-146).

Howells sketches the town's social texture through Colville's dual experience of alienation and assimilation. When he first arrived, Colville "hated it" (001-144). There were initial doubts about him "growing out of his Eastern accent and the work of his New York tailor" (001-149). Yet over time he "learned in time to relish the humorous intimacy of the life about him" (001-147), and eventually "he found himself the object of a pleasing popularity" (001-150). He bought his brother out and became a fixture of the community (001-151). The town's social climate is informal and unpolished: evening dress is a "social panoply unknown in Des Vaches" (004-075--004-076), and Colville lets his beard grow because "under the lax social regimen at Des Vaches he neglected shaving" (004-087). The community is characterised by industry; Colville "had lived long in a community where every one was intensely occupied, and he unconsciously paid to Des Vaches the tribute of feeling that an objectless life was disgraceful to a man" (001-161--001-163).

## Significance in the Novel

Des Vaches is the American antipode to Florence, the counter-world against which the novel's expatriate experience is measured. Throughout *Indian Summer*, Colville's consciousness oscillates between the two places; he mentally replaces the Arno with the Wabash, the Ponte Vecchio with the Main Street Bridge. The opening scene establishes this structural contrast: as Colville stands on the Ponte Vecchio, "for any sensible difference, he might have been standing on the Main Street Bridge at Des Vaches, Indiana, looking down at the tawny sweep of the Wabash" (001-030--001-032). This imagined substitution -- the most famous sentence in the novel -- defines the book's central tension between American provincialism and European culture.

The town represents American practicality, industry, and the "real world" of work from which Colville has fled. Where Florence offers beauty, art, and the ruins of history, Des Vaches offers newspapers, politics, and the relentless present -- the "constant excitements and its endless variety" of newspaper life that Colville finds himself missing in Italy (001-152). The novel does not simply valorise one world over the other: Des Vaches is both a prison of provinciality and a source of authentic purpose. Colville's departure from it "sprang from disgust" (001-039), but the disgust is with his political humiliation, not with the town itself. In leaving, he suffered "keenly and at every moment the loss of the occupation of which he had bereaved himself" (001-164--001-165) -- a formulation that captures the town's ambiguous significance as the setting for a life he both resented and missed. He was self-conscious about its provincial reputation: "he had himself been at the pains always to let people know, at the American watering-places where he spent his vacations, that though presently from Des Vaches, Indiana, he was really born in Rhode Island" (009-007--009-008).

## Key Scenes

### The Main Street Bridge (Chapter I)

The novel opens with Colville on the Ponte Vecchio in Florence, but his mind immediately replaces the scene with its American counterpart. This one-sentence substitution establishes the novel's entire geographic and emotional axis.

> He isolated himself so perfectly from it, as he brooded upon the river, that, for any sensible difference, he might have been standing on the Main Street Bridge at Des Vaches, Indiana, looking down at the tawny sweep of the Wabash. He had no love for that stream, nor for the ambitious town on its banks, but ever since he woke that morning he had felt a growing conviction that he had been a great ass to leave them. (001-029--001-034)

### The Brother's Summons (Chapter I)

Colville's backstory -- how he came to Des Vaches -- is narrated in a flashback that reveals the town's accidental role in redirecting the course of his life.

> He received a letter from his elder brother suggesting that he should come and see how he liked the architecture of Des Vaches. His brother had been seven years at Des Vaches, where he had lands, and a lead-mine, and a scheme for a railroad, and had lately added a daily newspaper to his other enterprises. He had, in fact, added two newspapers; for having unexpectedly and almost involuntarily become the owner of the Des Vaches _Republican_, the fancy of building up a great local journal seized him, and he bought the _Wabash Valley Democrat_, uniting them under the name of the _Democrat-Republican_. (001-124--001-132)

### Becoming a Newspaper Man (Chapter I)

Colville's transformation from architect to journalist is a consequence of the town's lack of demand for his original profession -- a detail that captures the pragmatic, unromantic character of provincial American life.

> He was still romantic enough to like the notion of the place a little, because it bore the name given to it by the old French _voyageurs_ from a herd of buffalo cows which they had seen grazing on the site of their camp there; but when he came to the place itself he did not like it. He hated it; but he stayed, and as an architect was the last thing any one wanted in Des Vaches since the jail and court-house had been built, he became, half without his willing it, a newspaper man. (001-140--001-146)

### The Provincial's Defense (Chapter IX)

At an evening party in Florence, Colville reflects on the fading geographical prejudices that had once made him self-conscious about his Indiana background.

> He had himself been at the pains always to let people know, at the American watering-places where he spent his vacations, that though presently from Des Vaches, Indiana, he was really born in Rhode Island; but in Florence it was not at all necessary. (009-007--009-008)

## Real-World Counterpart

Des Vaches is a fictional creation, but it is modelled closely on the midwestern American newspaper towns of the late nineteenth century -- the kind of ambitious county seats that grew up along railroads and rivers, sustained by local journalism, lead-mining, land speculation, and the relentless boosterism of the Gilded Age frontier. Howells himself had deep familiarity with this world: as a young man he worked as a newspaper editor in small-town Ohio, and his novel *A Modern Instance* (1882) had already explored the moral texture of small-town journalism in America. The town's location on the Wabash -- a real Indiana river -- anchors it in a recognisable geography, while its French-derived name gestures toward the colonial fur-trading past of the Old Northwest. In its role as the American foil to Florence, Des Vaches represents not a specific real-world place but a composite of the earnest, provincial, relentlessly practical communities that Howells saw as the seedbed of American character -- and the very thing the expatriate fled.
