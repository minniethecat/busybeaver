[044-001] "Oh, beseech her from me not to imagine that any reparation is due!

[044-002] Where there has been an error there must be blame; but wherever it lies

[044-003] in ours, I am sure it isn't at her door. Tell her I say this; tell her

[044-004] that I acquit her with all my heart of every shadow of wrong; that I am

[044-005] not unhappy, but glad for her sake and my own that this has ended as it

[044-006] has." He stretched his left hand across the coverlet to her, and said,

[044-007] with the feebleness of exhaustion, "Good-bye. Bid her good-bye for me."

[044-008] Mrs. Graham pressed his hand and went out. A moment after the door was

[044-009] flung open, and [[imogene-graham|Imogene]] burst into the room. She threw herself on her

[044-010] knees beside his bed. "I will _pray_ to you!" she said, her face intense

[044-011] with the passions working in her soul. She seemed choking with words

[044-012] which would not come; then, with an inarticulate cry that must stand for

[044-013] all, she caught up the hand that lay limp on the coverlet; she crushed

[044-014] it against her lips, and ran out of the room.

[044-015] He sank into a deathly torpor, the physical refusal of his brain to take

[044-016] account of what had passed. When he woke from it, little [[effie-bowen|Effie Bowen]] was

[044-017] airily tiptoeing about the room, fondly retouching its perfect order. He

[044-018] closed his eyes, and felt her come to him and smooth the sheet softly

[044-019] under his chin. Then he knew she must be standing with clasped hands

[044-020] admiring the effect. Some one called her in whisper from the door. It

[044-021] closed, and all was still again.

[044-022] XXII

[044-023] [[theodore-colville|Colville]] got himself out of the comfort and quiet of [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]]'s house

[044-024] as soon as he could. He made the more haste because he felt that if he

[044-025] could have remained with the smallest trace of self-respect, he would

[044-026] have been glad to stay there for ever.

[044-027] Even as it was, the spring had advanced to early summer, and the sun was

[044-028] lying hot and bright in the piazzas, and the shade dense and cool in the

[044-029] narrow streets, before he left [[palazzo-pinti|Palazzo Pinti]]; the [[lungarno|Lung' Arno]] was a glare

[044-030] of light that struck back from the curving line of the buff houses; the

[044-031] river had shrivelled to a rill in its bed; the black cypresses were dim

[044-032] in the tremor of the distant air on the hill-slopes beyond; the olives

[044-033] seemed to swelter in the sun, and the villa walls to burn whiter and

[044-034] whiter. At evening the mosquito began to wind his tiny horn. It was the

[044-035] end of May, and nearly everybody but the Florentines had gone out of

[044-036] [[florence|Florence]], dispersing to Via Reggio by the sea, to the hills of

[044-037] Pistoja, and to the high, cool air of Siena. More than once Colville had

[044-038] said that he was keeping Mrs. Bowen after she ought to have got away,

[044-039] and she had answered that she liked hot weather, and that this was not

[044-040] comparable to the heat of Washington in June. She was looking very well,

[044-041] and younger and prettier than she had since the first days of their

[044-042] renewed acquaintance in the winter. Her southern complexion enriched

[044-043] itself in the sun; sometimes when she came into his room from outdoors

[044-044] the straying brown hair curled into loose rings on her temples, and her

[044-045] cheeks glowed a deep red.

[044-046] She said those polite things to appease him as long as he was not well

[044-047] enough to go away, but she did not try to detain him after his strength

[044-048] sufficiently returned. It was the blow on the head that kept him

[044-049] longest. After his broken arm and his other bruises were quite healed,

[044-050] he was aware of physical limits to thinking of the future or regretting

[044-051] the past, and this sense of his powerlessness went far to reconcile him

[044-052] to a life of present inaction and oblivion. Theoretically he ought to

[044-053] have been devoured by remorse and chagrin, but as a matter of fact he

[044-054] suffered very little from either. Even in people who are in full

[044-055] possession of their capacity for mental anguish one observes that after

[044-056] they have undergone a certain amount of pain they cease to feel.

[044-057] Colville amused himself a good deal with Effie's endeavours to entertain

[044-058] him and take care of him. The child was with him every moment that she

[044-059] could steal from her tasks, and her mother no longer attempted to stem

[044-060] the tide of her devotion. It was understood that Effie should joke and

[044-061] laugh with Mr. Colville as much as she chose; that she should fan him as

[044-062] long as he could stand it; that she should read to him when he woke, and

[044-063] watch him when he slept. She brought him his breakfast, she petted him

[044-064] and caressed him, and wished to make him a monster of dependence and

[044-065] self-indulgence. It seemed to grieve her that he got well so fast.

[044-066] The last night before he left the house she sat on his knee by the

[044-067] window looking out beyond the firefly twinkle of Oltrarno, to the

[044-068] silence and solid dark of the solemn company of hills beyond. They had

[044-069] not lighted the lamps because of the mosquitoes, and they had talked

[044-070] till her head dropped against his shoulder.

[044-071] Mrs. Bowen came in to get her. "Why, is she asleep?"

[044-072] "Yes. Don't take her yet," said Colville.

[044-073] Mrs. Bowen rustled softly into the chair which Effie had left to get

[044-074] into Colville's lap. Neither of them spoke, and he was so richly content

[044-075] with the peace, the tacit sweetness of the little moment, that he would

[044-076] have been glad to have it silently endure forever. If any troublesome

[044-077] question of his right to such a moment of bliss obtruded itself upon

[044-078] him, he did not concern himself with it.

[044-079] "We shall have another hot day to-morrow," said Mrs. Bowen at length. "I

[044-080] hope you will find your room comfortable."

[044-081] "Yes: it's at the back of the hotel, mighty high, and wide, and no sun

[044-082] ever comes into it except when they show it to foreigners in winter.

[044-083] Then they get a few rays to enter as a matter of business, on condition

[044-084] that they won't detain them. I dare say I shall stay there some time. I

[044-085] suppose you will be getting away from Florence very soon.

[044-086] "Yes. But I haven't decided where to go yet."

[044-087] "Should you like some general expression of my gratitude for all you've

[044-088] done for me, Mrs. Bowen?"

[044-089] "No; I would rather not. It has been a great pleasure--to Effie."

[044-090] "Oh, a luxury beyond the dreams of avarice." They spoke in low tones,

[044-091] and there was something in the hush that suggested to Colville the

[044-092] feasibility of taking into his unoccupied hand one of the pretty hands

[044-093] which the pale night-light showed him lying in Mrs. Bowen's lap. But he

[044-094] forbore, and only sighed. "Well, then, I will say nothing. But I shall

[044-095] keep on thinking all my life."

[044-096] She made no answer.

[044-097] "When you are gone, I shall have to make the most of [[mr-waters|Mr. Waters]]," he

[044-098] said.

[044-099] "He is going to stop all summer, I believe."

[044-100] "Oh yes. When I suggested to him the other day that he might find it too

[044-101] hot, he said that he had seventy New England winters to thaw out of his

[044-102] blood, and that all the summers he had left would not be more than he

[044-103] needed. One of his friends told him that he could cook eggs in his

[044-104] piazza in August, and he said that he should like nothing better than to

[044-105] cook eggs there. He's the most delightfully expatriated compatriot I've

[044-106] ever seen."

[044-107] "Do you like it?"

[044-108] "It's well enough for him. Life has no claims on him any more. I think

[044-109] it's very pleasant over here, now that everybody's gone," added

[044-110] Colville, from a confused resentfulness of collectively remembered Days

[044-111] and Afternoons and Evenings. "How still the night is!"

[044-112] A few feet clapping by on the pavement below alone broke the hush.

[044-113] "Sometimes I feel very tired of it all, and want to get home," sighed

[044-114] Mrs. Bowen.

[044-115] "Well, so do I."

[044-116] "I can't believe it's right staying away from the country so long."

[044-117] People often say such things in Europe.

[044-118] "No, I don't either, if you've got anything to do there."

[044-119] "You can always make something to do there."

[044-120] "Oh yes." Some young young men, breaking from a street near by, began to

[044-121] sing. "We shouldn't have that sort of thing at home."

[044-122] "No," said Mrs. Bowen pensively.

[044-123] "I heard just such singing before I fell asleep the night after that

[044-124] party at [[madame-uccelli|Madame Uccelli]]'s, and it filled me with fury."

[044-125] "Why should it do that?"

[044-126] "I don't know. It seemed like voices from our youth--Lina."

[044-127] She had no resentment of his use of her name in the tone with which she

[044-128] asked: "Did you hate that so much?"

[044-129] "No; the loss of it."

[044-130] They both fetched a deep breath.

[044-131] "The Uccellis have a villa near the baths of Lucca," said Mrs. Bowen.

[044-132] "They have asked me to go."

[044-133] "Do you think of going?" inquired Colville. "I've always fancied it must

[044-134] be pleasant there."

[044-135] "No; I declined. Sometimes I think I will just stay on in Florence."

[044-136] "I dare say you'd find it perfectly comfortable. There's nothing like

[044-137] having the range of one's own house in summer." He looked out of the

[044-138] window on the blue-black sky.

[044-139] "'And deepening through their silent spheres,

[044-140] Heaven over heaven rose the night,'"

[044-141] he quoted. "It's wonderful! Do you remember how I used to read _Mariana

[044-142] in the South_ to you and poor Jenny? How it must have bored her! What an

[044-143] ass I was!"

[044-144] "Yes," said Mrs. Bowen breathlessly, in sympathy with his reminiscence

[044-145] rather than in agreement with his self-denunciation.

[044-146] Colville broke into a laugh, and then she began to laugh to; but not

[044-147] quite willingly as it seemed.

[044-148] Effie started from her sleep. "What--what is it?" she asked, stretching

[044-149] and shivering as half-wakened children do.

[044-150] "Bed-time," said her mother promptly, taking her hand to lead her away.

[044-151] "Say good-night to Mr. Colville."

[044-152] The child turned and kissed him. "Good night," she murmured.

[044-153] "Good night, you sleepy little soul!" It seemed to Colville that he must

[044-154] be a pretty good man, after all, if this little thing loved him so.

[044-155] "Do you always kiss Mr. Colville good-night?" asked her mother when she

[044-156] began to undo her hair for her in her room.

[044-157] "Sometimes. Don't you think it's nice?"

[044-158] "Oh yes; nice enough."

[044-159] Colville sat by the window a long time thinking Mrs. Bowen might come

[044-160] back; but she did not return.

[044-161] Mr. Waters came to see him the next afternoon at his hotel.

[044-162] "Are you pretty comfortable here?" he asked.

[044-163] "Well, it's a change," said Colville. "I miss the little one awfully."

[044-164] "She's a winning child," admitted the old man. "That combination of

[044-165] conventionality and _naïveté_ is very captivating. I notice it in the

[044-166] mother."

[044-167] "Yes, the mother has it too. Have you seen them to-day?"

[044-168] "Yes; Mrs. Bowen was sorry to be out when you came."

[044-169] "I had the misfortune to miss them. I had a great mind to go again

[044-170] to-night."

[044-171] The old man said nothing to this. "The fact is," Colville went on, "I'm

[044-172] so habituated to being there that I'm rather spoiled."

[044-173] "Ah, it's a nice place," Mr. Waters admitted.

[044-174] "Of course I made all the haste I could to get away, and I have the

[044-175] reward of a good conscience. But I don't find that the reward is very

[044-176] great."

[044-177] The old gentleman smiled. "The difficulty is to know conscience from

[044-178] self-interest."

[044-179] "Oh, there's no doubt of it in my case," said Colville. "If I'd

[044-180] consulted my own comfort and advantage, I should still be at Palazzo

[044-181] Pinti."

[044-182] "I dare say they would have been glad to keep you."

[044-183] "Do you really think so?" asked Colville, with sudden seriousness. "I

[044-184] wish you would tell me why. Have you any reason--grounds? Pshaw! I'm

[044-185] absurd!" He sank back into the easy-chair from whose depths he had

[044-186] pulled himself in the eagerness of his demand, and wiped his forehead

[044-187] with his handkerchief. "Mr. Waters, you remember my telling you of my

[044-188] engagement to Miss Graham?"
