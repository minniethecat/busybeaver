[027-001] [[theodore-colville|Colville]] laughed. "I like your way of bringing a fresh mind to all these

[027-002] questions in history and morals, whether they are conventionally settled

[027-003] or not. Don't you think the modern scientific spirit could evolve

[027-004] something useful out of the old classic idea of suicide?"

[027-005] "Perhaps," said [[mr-waters|Mr. Waters]]; "I haven't yet thought it over. The worst

[027-006] thing about suicide--and this must always rank it below political

[027-007] assassination--is that its interest is purely personal. No man ever

[027-008] kills himself for the good of others."

[027-009] "That's certainly against it. We oughtn't to countenance such an

[027-010] abominably selfish practice. But you can't bring that charge against

[027-011] euthanasy. What have you to say of that?"

[027-012] "I have heard one of the most benevolent and tender-hearted men I ever

[027-013] knew defend it in cases of hopeless suffering. But I don't know that I

[027-014] should be prepared to take his ground. There appears to be something so

[027-015] sacred about human life that we must respect it even in spite of the

[027-016] prayers of the sufferer who asks us to end his irremediable misery."

[027-017] "Well," said Colville, "I suspect we must at least class murder with the

[027-018] ballet as a means of good. One might say there was still some virtue in

[027-019] the primal, eldest curse against bloodshed."

[027-020] "Oh, I don't by any means deny those things," said the old man, with the

[027-021] air of wishing to be scrupulously just. "Which way are you walking?"

[027-022] "Your way, if you will let me," replied Colville. "I was going to your

[027-023] house to ask you to take a walk with me."

[027-024] "Ah, that's good. I was reading of the great siege last night, and I

[027-025] thought of taking a look at Michelangelo's bastions. Let us go together,

[027-026] if you don't think you'll find it too fatiguing."

[027-027] "I shall be ashamed to complain if I do."

[027-028] "And you didn't go to Rome after all?" said Mr. Waters.

[027-029] "No; I couldn't face the landlord with a petition so preposterous as

[027-030] mine. I told him that I found I had no money to pay his bill till I had

[027-031] seen my banker, and as he didn't propose that I should send him the

[027-032] amount back from Rome, I stayed. Landlords have their limitations; they

[027-033] are not imaginative, as a class."

[027-034] "Well, a day more will make no great difference to you, I suppose," said

[027-035] the old man, "and a day less would have been a loss to me. I shall miss

[027-036] you."

[027-037] "Shall you, indeed?" asked Colville, with a grateful stir of the heart.

[027-038] "It's very nice of you to say that."

[027-039] "Oh no. I meet few people who are willing to look at life objectively

[027-040] with me, and I have fancied some such willingness in you. What I chiefly

[027-041] miss over here is a philosophic lift in the human mind, but probably

[027-042] that is because my opportunities of meeting the best minds are few, and

[027-043] my means of conversing with them are small. If I had not the whole past

[027-044] with me, I should feel lonely at times."

[027-045] "And is the past such good company always?".

[027-046] "Yes, in a sense it is. The past is humanity set free from circumstance,

[027-047] and history studied where it was once life is the past rehumanised."

[027-048] As if he found this rarefied air too thin for his lungs, Colville made

[027-049] some ineffectual gasps at response, and the old man continued: "What I

[027-050] mean is that I meet here the characters I read of, and commune with them

[027-051] before their errors were committed, before they had condemned themselves

[027-052] to failure, while they were still wise and sane, and still active and

[027-053] vital forces."

[027-054] "Did they all fail? I thought some of the bad fellows had a pretty fair

[027-055] worldly success?"

[027-056] "The blossom of decay."

[027-057] "Oh! what black pessimism!"

[027-058] "Not at all! Men fail, but man succeeds. I don't know what it all means,

[027-059] or any part of it; but I have had moods in which it seemed as if the

[027-060] whole, secret of the mystery were about to flash upon me. Walking along

[027-061] in the full sun, in the midst of men, or sometimes in the solitude of

[027-062] midnight, poring over a book, and thinking of quite other things, I have

[027-063] felt that I had almost surprised it."

[027-064] "But never quite?"

[027-065] "Oh, it isn't too late yet."

[027-066] "I hope you won't have your revelation before I get away from [[florence|Florence]],

[027-067] or I shall see them burning you here like the great _frate_."

[027-068] They had been walking down the Via Calzioli from the Duomo, and now they

[027-069] came out into the Piazza della Signoria, suddenly, as one always seems

[027-070] to do, upon the rise of the old palace and the leap of its tower into

[027-071] the blue air. The history of all Florence is there, with memories of

[027-072] every great time in bronze or marble, but the supreme presence is the

[027-073] martyr who hangs for ever from the gibbet over the quenchless fire in

[027-074] the midst.

[027-075] "Ah, they _had_ to kill him!" sighed the old man. "It has always been so

[027-076] with the benefactors. They have always meant mankind more good than any

[027-077] one generation can bear, and it must turn upon them and destroy them."

[027-078] "How will it be with you, then, when you have read us 'the riddle of the

[027-079] painful earth'?"

[027-080] "That will be so simple that every one will accept it willingly and

[027-081] gladly, and wonder that no one happened to think of it before. And,

[027-082] perhaps, the world is now grown old enough and docile enough to receive

[027-083] the truth without resentment."

[027-084] "I take back my charge of pessimism," said Colville. "You are an

[027-085] optimist of the deepest dye."

[027-086] They walked out of the Piazza and down to the [[lungarno|Lung' Arno]], through the

[027-087] corridor of the Uffizzi, where the illustrious Florentines stand in

[027-088] marble under the arches, all reconciled and peaceful and equal at last.

[027-089] Colville shivered a little as he passed between the silent ranks of the

[027-090] statues.

[027-091] "I can't stand those fellows, to-day. They seem to feel such a smirk

[027-092] satisfaction at having got out of it all."

[027-093] They issued upon the river, and he went to the parapet and looked down

[027-094] on the water. "I wonder," he mused aloud, "if it has the same Sunday

[027-095] look to these Sabbathless Italians as it has to us."

[027-096] "No; Nature isn't puritan," replied the old minister.

[027-097] "Not at Haddam East Village?"

[027-098] "No; there less than here; for she's had to make a harder fight for her

[027-099] life there."

[027-100] "Ah, then you believe in Nature--you're a friend of Nature?" asked

[027-101] Colville, following the lines of an oily swirl in the current with

[027-102] indolent eye.

[027-103] "Only up to a certain point." Mr. Waters seemed to be patient of any

[027-104] direction which the other might be giving the talk. "Nature is a savage.

[027-105] She has good impulses, but you can't trust her altogether."

[027-106] "Do you know," said Colville, "I don't think there's very much of her

[027-107] left in us after we reach a certain point in life? She drives us on at a

[027-108] great pace for a while, and then some fine morning we wake up and find

[027-109] that Nature has got tired of us and has left us to taste and conscience.

[027-110] And taste and conscience are by no means so certain of what they want

[027-111] you to do as Nature was."

[027-112] "Yes," said the minister, "I see what you mean." He joined Colville in

[027-113] leaning on the parapet, and he looked out on the river as if he saw his

[027-114] meaning there. "But by the time we reach that point in life most of us

[027-115] have got the direction which Nature meant us to take, and there's no

[027-116] longer any need of her driving us on."

[027-117] "And what about the unlucky fellows who haven't got the direction, or

[027-118] haven't kept it?"

[027-119] "They had better go back to it."

[027-120] "But if Nature herself seemed to change her mind about you?"

[027-121] "Ah, you mean persons of weak will. They are a great curse to themselves

[027-122] and to everybody else."

[027-123] "I'm not so sure of that," said Colville. "I've seen cases in which a

[027-124] strong will looked very much more like the devil."

[027-125] "Yes, a perverted will. But there can be no good without a strong will.

[027-126] A weak will means inconstancy. It means, even in good, good attempted

[027-127] and relinquished, which is always a terrible thing, because it is sure

[027-128] to betray some one who relied upon its accomplishment."

[027-129] "And in evil? Perhaps the evil, attempted and relinquished, turns into

[027-130] good."

[027-131] "Oh, never!" replied the minister fervently. "There is something very

[027-132] mysterious in what we call evil. Apparently it has infinitely greater

[027-133] force and persistence than good. I don't know why it should be so. But

[027-134] so it appears."

[027-135] "You'll have the reason of that along with the rest of the secret when

[027-136] your revelation comes," said Colville, with a smile. He lifted his eyes

[027-137] from the river, and looked up over the clustering roofs beyond it to the

[027-138] hills beyond them, flecked to the crest of their purple slopes with the

[027-139] white of villas and villages. As if something in the beauty of the

[027-140] wonderful prospect had suggested the vision of its opposite, he said,

[027-141] dreamily, "I don't think I shall go to Rome to-morrow, after all. I will

[027-142] go to [[des-vaches|Des Vaches]]! Where did you say you were walking, Mr. Waters? Oh

[027-143] yes! You told me. I will cross the bridge with you. But I couldn't stand

[027-144] anything quite so vigorous as the associations of the siege this

[027-145] afternoon. I'm going to [[boboli-gardens|the Boboli]] Gardens, to debauch myself with a

[027-146] final sense of nerveless despotism, as it expressed itself in marble

[027-147] allegory and formal alleys. The fact is that if I stay with you any

[027-148] longer I shall tell you something that I'm too old to tell and you're

[027-149] too old to hear." The old man smiled, but offered no urgence or comment,

[027-150] and at the thither end of the bridge Colville said hastily, "Good-bye.

[027-151] If you ever come to Des Vaches, look me up."

[027-152] "Good-bye," said the minister. "Perhaps we shall meet in Florence

[027-153] again."

[027-154] "No, no. Whatever happens, that won't."

[027-155] They shook hands and parted. Colville stood a moment, watching the

[027-156] slight bent figure of the old man as he moved briskly up the Via de'

[027-157] Bardi, turning his head from, side to side, to look at the palaces as he

[027-158] passed, and so losing himself in the dim, cavernous curve of the street.

[027-159] As soon as he was out of sight, Colville had an impulse to hurry after

[027-160] him and rejoin him; then he felt like turning about and going back to

[027-161] his hotel.

[027-162] But he shook himself together into the shape of resolution, however

[027-163] slight and transient. "I must do _something_ I intended to do," he said,

[027-164] between his set teeth, and pushed on up through the Via Guicciardini. "I

[027-165] will go to the Boboli because I _said_ I would."

[027-166] As he walked along, he seemed to himself to be merely crumbling away in

[027-167] this impulse and that, in one abortive intent and another. What did it

[027-168] all mean? Had he been his whole life one of these weak wills which are a

[027-169] curse to themselves and others, and most a curse when they mean the

[027-170] best? Was that the secret of his failure in life? But for many years he

[027-171] had seemed to succeed, to be as other men were, hard, practical men; he

[027-172] had once made a good newspaper, which was certainly not a dream of

[027-173] romance. Had he given that up at last because he was a weak will? And

[027-174] now was he running away from Florence because his will was weak? He

[027-175] could look back to that squalid tragedy of his youth, and see that a

[027-176] more violent, a more determined man could have possessed himself of the

[027-177] girl whom he had lost. And now would it not be more manly, if more

[027-178] brutal, to stay here, where a hope, however fleeting, however fitful, of

[027-179] what might have been, had revisited him in the love of this young girl?

[027-180] He felt sure, if anything were sure, that something in him, in spite of

[027-181] their wide disparity of years, had captured her fancy, and now, in his

[027-182] abasement, he felt again the charm of his own power over her. They were

[027-183] no farther apart in years than many a husband and wife; they would grow

[027-184] more and more together; there was youth enough in his heart yet; and who

[027-185] was pushing him away from her, forbidding him this treasure that he had

[027-186] but to put out his hand and make his own? Some one whom through all his

[027-187] thoughts of another he was trying to please, but whom he had made

[027-188] finally and inexorably his enemy. Better stay, then, something said to

[027-189] him; and when he answered, "I will," something else reminded him that

[027-190] this also was not willing but unwilling.
