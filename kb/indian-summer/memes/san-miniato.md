# San Miniato

## Description

San Miniato (formally the Basilica of San Miniato al Monte) is a Romanesque church perched on a hill overlooking Florence. In *Indian Summer*, it is glimpsed from the window of Mrs. Bowen's apartment, where Colville sketches its outline while telling Effie the legend of the saint for whom it is named (020-052). The church sits above the Piazzale Michelangelo, and from the city below its pale marble facade catches the light, most memorably at sunset (046-031). Howells does not describe the church's architecture in detail; rather, San Miniato appears as a fixed point on the Florentine skyline — a landmark that anchors both the city and the novel's emotional landscape.

## Significance in the Novel

San Miniato serves two distinct narrative functions in *Indian Summer*. In Chapter 20, it becomes the springboard for Colville's storytelling — he uses the church as a visual prompt to tell Effie the legendary tale of Saint Miniato's martyrdom, mixing Florentine history with fairy-tale whimsy (020-055). The legend itself — of a beheaded saint who picked up his head, waded the Arno, walked up the hill, and only then expired — mirrors the novel's broader meditation on how "the truth seems to die out of stories after a while" (020-070).

Later, in Chapter 46, San Miniato reappears as the silent backdrop to one of the novel's most quietly charged domestic scenes. Colville and Mrs. Bowen sit together in the salotto, watching "the sunset die on San Miniato" while the bronze copy of Michelangelo's David blackens against the pink sky (046-031). The church, high and distant, becomes a measure of the emotional distance Colville feels from the domestic happiness that seems at once within reach and impossibly remote.

## Key Scenes

### Colville Tells the Legend of San Miniato (020)

While sketching with Effie on his knee, Colville looks out the window and begins the story of the church on the hill:

> "Ah, there's San Miniato," he said, with a glance from the window. "Must get its outline in. You've heard how there came to be a church up there? No? Well, it shows the sort of man San Miniato really was. He was one of the early Christians, and he gave the poor pagans a great deal of trouble. They first threw him to the wild beasts in the amphitheatre, but the moment those animals set eyes on him they saw it would be of no use; they just lay down and died. Very well, then; the pagans determined to see what effect the axe would have upon San Miniato: but as soon as they struck off his head he picked it up, set it back on his shoulders again, waded across the Arno, walked up the hill, and when he came to a convenient little oratory up there he knelt down and expired." (020-052–020-060)

### Sunset Over San Miniato (046)

In the novel's penultimate chapter, San Miniato provides the visual anchor for a scene of quiet longing:

> When Colville had finished his ice, Mrs. Bowen led the way to the salotto; and they all sat down by the window there and watched the sunset die on San Miniato. The bronze copy of Michelangelo's David, in the Piazzale below the church, blackened in perfect relief against the pink sky and then faded against the grey while they talked. (046-031)

### Giovanni Gualberto and the Church (020)

Colville ties San Miniato into a second Florentine legend, placing the church as the setting for Giovanni Gualberto's moment of mercy in the year 1003 — when he spared the man who had killed his brother, an act that led to the founding of the Vallombrosan order (020-104).

## Real-World Counterpart

The Basilica of San Miniato al Monte is one of the finest examples of Romanesque architecture in Tuscany. Built between the 11th and 13th centuries on the site of a 4th-century oratory, it stands on the Monte alle Croci overlooking Florence. The church features a striking facade of white Carrara and green Prato marble, a mosaic of Christ between the Virgin and Saint Miniatus, and an interior with a raised choir and a 13th-century mosaic pavement. The adjoining cemetery, the Porte Sante, contains the tombs of notable Florentine figures including Carlo Collodi, the author of *Pinocchio*. The church remains an active Olivetan Benedictine monastery, and its hillside location continues to offer the same panoramic views of Florence that Howells' characters enjoyed in the 1880s.
