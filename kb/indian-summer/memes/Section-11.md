[011-001] After breakfast he went without delay to Viesseux's reading-room, to

[011-002] examine his catalogue, and see what there was in it to his purpose.

[011-003] While he was waiting his turn to pay his subscription, with the people

[011-004] who surrounded the proprietor, half a dozen of the acquaintances he had

[011-005] made at [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]]'s passed in and out. Viesseux's is a place where

[011-006] sooner or later you meet every one you know among the foreign residents

[011-007] at [[florence|Florence]]; the natives in smaller proportion resort there too; and

[011-008] [[theodore-colville|Colville]] heard a lady asking for a book in that perfect Italian which

[011-009] strikes envy to the heart of the stranger sufficiently versed in the

[011-010] language to know that he never shall master it. He rather rejoiced in

[011-011] his despair, however, as an earnest of his renewed intellectual life.

[011-012] Henceforth his life would be wholly intellectual. He did not regret his

[011-013] little excursion into society; it had shown him with dramatic sharpness

[011-014] how unfit for it he was.

[011-015] "Good _morning_!" said some one in a bland undertone full of a pleasant

[011-016] recognition of the claims to quiet of a place where some others were

[011-017] speaking in their ordinary tones.

[011-018] Colville looked round on the [[mr-waters|Rev. Mr. Waters]], and took his friendly

[011-019] hand. "Good morning--glad to see you," he answered.

[011-020] "Are you looking for that short Florentine history for Mrs. Bowen's

[011-021] little girl?" asked Mr. Waters, inclining his head slightly for the

[011-022] reply. "She mentioned it to me."

[011-023] By day Colville remarked more distinctly that the old gentleman was

[011-024] short and slight, with a youthful eagerness in his face surviving on

[011-025] good terms with the grey locks that fell down his temples from under the

[011-026] brim of his soft felt hat. With the boyish sweetness of his looks

[011-027] blended a sort of appreciative shrewdness, which pointed his smiling

[011-028] lips slightly aslant in what seemed the expectation rather than the

[011-029] intention of humour.

[011-030] "Not exactly," said Colville, experiencing a difficulty in withholding

[011-031] the fact that in some sort he was just going to write a short Florentine

[011-032] history, and finding a certain pleasure in Mrs. Bowen's having

[011-033] remembered that he had taken an interest in Effie's reading. He had a

[011-034] sudden wish to tell Mr. Waters of his plan, but this was hardly the time

[011-035] or place.

[011-036] They now found themselves face to face with the librarian, and Mr.

[011-037] Waters made a gesture of waiving himself in Colville's favour.

[011-038] "No, no!" said the latter; "you had better ask. I am going to put this

[011-039] gentleman through rather an extended course of sprouts."

[011-040] The librarian smiled with the helplessness of a foreigner, who knows his

[011-041] interlocutor's English, but not the meaning of it.

[011-042] "Oh, I merely wanted to ask," said Mr. Waters, addressing the librarian,

[011-043] and explaining to Colville, "whether you had received that book on

[011-044] [[girolamo-savonarola|Savonarola]] yet. The German one."

[011-045] "I shall see," said the librarian, and he went upon a quest that kept

[011-046] him some minutes.

[011-047] "You're not thinking of taking Savonarola's life, I suppose?" suggested

[011-048] Colville.

[011-049] "Oh no. Villari's book has covered the whole ground for ever, it seems

[011-050] to me. It's a wonderful book. You've read it?"

[011-051] "Yes. It's a thing that makes you feel that, after all, the Italians

[011-052] have only to make a real effort in any direction, and they go ahead of

[011-053] everybody else. What biography of the last twenty years can compare with

[011-054] it?"

[011-055] "You're right, sir--you're right," cried the old man enthusiastically.

[011-056] "They're a gifted race, a people of genius."

[011-057] "I wish for their own sakes they'd give their minds a little to

[011-058] generalship," said Colville, pressed by the facts to hedge somewhat.

[011-059] "They did get so badly smashed in their last war, poor fellows."

[011-060] "Oh, I don't think I should like them any better if they were better

[011-061] soldiers. Perhaps the lesson of noble endurance that they've given our

[011-062] times is all that we have the right to demand of them in the way of

[011-063] heroism; no one can say they lack courage. And sometimes it seems to me

[011-064] that in simply outgrowing the different sorts of despotism that had

[011-065] fastened upon them, till their broken bonds fell away without positive

[011-066] effort on their part, they showed a greater sublimity than if they had

[011-067] violently conquered their freedom. Most nations sink lower and lower

[011-068] under tyranny; the Italians grew steadily more and more civilised, more

[011-069] noble, more gentle, more grand. It was a wonderful spectacle--like a

[011-070] human soul perfected through suffering and privation. Every period of

[011-071] their history is full of instruction. I find my ancestral puritanism

[011-072] particularly appealed to by the puritanism of Savonarola."

[011-073] "Then Villari hasn't satisfied you that Savonarola wasn't a Protestant?"

[011-074] "Oh yes, he has. I said his puritanism. Just now I'm interested in

[011-075] justifying his failure to myself, for it's one of the things in history

[011-076] that I've found it hardest to accept. But no doubt his puritanic state

[011-077] fell because it was dreary and ugly, as the puritanic state always has

[011-078] been. It makes its own virtues intolerable; puritanism won't let you see

[011-079] how good and beautiful the Puritans often are. It was inevitable that

[011-080] Savonarola's enemies should misunderstand and hate him."

[011-081] "You are one of the last men I should have expected to find among the

[011-082] _Arrabiati_," said Colville.

[011-083] "Oh, there's a great deal to be said for the Florentine Arrabiati, as

[011-084] well as for the English Malignants, though the Puritans in neither case

[011-085] would have known how to say it. Savonarola perished because he was

[011-086] excessive. I am studying him in this aspect; it is fresh ground. It is

[011-087] very interesting to inquire just at what point a man's virtues become

[011-088] mischievous and intolerable."

[011-089] These ideas interested Colville; he turned to them with relief from the

[011-090] sense of his recent trivialities; in this old man's earnestness he found

[011-091] support and encouragement in the new course he had marked out for

[011-092] himself. Sometimes it had occurred to him not only that he was too old

[011-093] for the interests of his youth at forty, but that there was no longer

[011-094] time for him to take up new ones. He considered Mr. Waters's grey hairs,

[011-095] and determined to be wiser. "I should like to talk these things over

[011-096] with you--and some other things," he said.

[011-097] The librarian came toward them with the book for Mr. Waters, who was

[011-098] fumbling near-sightedly in his pocket-book for his card. "I shall be

[011-099] very happy to see you at my room," he said. "Ah, thank you," he added,

[011-100] taking his book, with a simple relish as if it were something whose

[011-101] pleasantness was sensible to the touch. He gave Colville the scholar's

[011-102] far-off look as he turned to go: he was already as remote as the

[011-103] fifteenth century through the magic of the book, which he opened and

[011-104] began to read at once. Colville stared after him; he did not wish to

[011-105] come to just that yet, either. Life, active life, life of his own day,

[011-106] called to him; he had been one of its busiest children: could he turn

[011-107] his back upon it for any charm or use that was in the past? Again that

[011-108] unnerving doubt, that paralysing distrust, beset him, and tempted him to

[011-109] curse the day in which he had returned to this outworn Old World. Idler

[011-110] on its modern surface, or delver in its deep-hearted past, could he

[011-111] reconcile himself to it? What did he care for the Italians of to-day, or

[011-112] the history of the Florentines as expressed in their architectural

[011-113] monuments? It was the problems of the vast, tumultuous American life,

[011-114] which he had turned his back on, that really concerned him. Later he

[011-115] might take up the study that fascinated yonder old man, but for the

[011-116] present it was intolerable.

[011-117] He was no longer young, that was true; but with an ache of old regret he

[011-118] felt that he had not yet lived his life, that his was a baffled destiny,

[011-119] an arrested fate. A lady came up and took his turn with the librarian,

[011-120] and Colville did not stay for another. He went out and walked down the

[011-121] [[lungarno|Lung' Arno]] toward the [[cascine|Cascine]]. The sun danced on the river, and bathed

[011-122] the long line of pale buff and grey houses that followed its curve, and

[011-123] ceased in the mist of leafless tree-tops where the Cascine began. It was

[011-124] not the hour of the promenade, and there was little driving; but the

[011-125] sidewalks were peopled thickly enough with persons, in groups, or

[011-126] singly, who had the air of straying aimlessly up or down, with no

[011-127] purpose but to be in the sun, after the rainy weather of the past week.

[011-128] There were faces of invalids, wistful and thin, and here and there a

[011-129] man, muffled to the chin, lounged feebly on the parapet and stared at

[011-130] the river. Colville hastened by them; they seemed to claim him as one of

[011-131] their ailing and [[youth-and-age|aging]] company, and just then he was in the humour of

[011-132] being very young and strong.

[011-133] A carriage passed before him through the Cascine gates, and drove down

[011-134] the road next the river. He followed, and when it had got a little way

[011-135] it stopped at the roadside, and a lady and little girl alighted, who

[011-136] looked about and caught sight of him, and then obviously waited for him

[011-137] to come up with them. It was [[imogene-graham|Imogene]] and [[effie-bowen|Effie Bowen]], and the young girl

[011-138] called to him: "We _thought_ it was you. Aren't you astonished to find

[011-139] us here at this hour?" she demanded, as soon as he came up, and gave him

[011-140] her hand. "Mrs. Bowen sent us for our health--or Effie's health--and I

[011-141] was just making the man stop and let us out for a little walk."

[011-142] "My health is very much broken too, Miss Effie," said Colville. "Will

[011-143] you let me walk with you?" The child smiled, as she did at Colville's

[011-144] speeches, which she apparently considered all jokes, but diplomatically

[011-145] referred the decision to Imogene with an upward glance.

[011-146] "We shall be very glad indeed," said the girl.

[011-147] "That's very polite of you. But Miss Effie makes no effort to conceal

[011-148] her dismay," said Colville.

[011-149] The little girl smiled again, and her smile was so like the smile of

[011-150] [[miss-ridgely|Lina Ridgely]], twenty years ago, that his next words were inevitably

[011-151] tinged with reminiscence.

[011-152] "Does one still come for one's health to the Cascine? When I was in

[011-153] Florence before, there was no other place if one went to look for it

[011-154] with young ladies--the Cascine or [[boboli-gardens|the Boboli]] Gardens. Do they keep the

[011-155] fountain of youth turned on here during the winter still?"

[011-156] "I've never seen it," said Imogene gaily.

[011-157] "Of course not. You never looked for it. Neither did I when I was here

[011-158] before. But it wouldn't escape me now."

[011-159] Since he had met them he had aged again, in spite of his resolutions to

[011-160] the contrary; somehow, beside their buoyancy and bloom, the youth in his

[011-161] heart faded.

[011-162] Imogene had started forward as soon as he joined them, and Colville,

[011-163] with Effie's gloved hand stolen shyly in his, was finding it quite

[011-164] enough to keep up with her in her elastic advance.

[011-165] She wore a long habit of silk, whose fur-trimmed edge wandered

[011-166] diagonally across her breast and down to the edge of her walking dress.

[011-167] To Colville, whom her girlish slimness in her ball costume had puzzled

[011-168] after his original impressions of Junonian abundance, she did not so

[011-169] much dwindle as seem to vanish from the proportions his visions had

[011-170] assigned her that first night when he saw her standing before the

[011-171] mirror. In this outdoor avatar, this companionship with the sun and

[011-172] breeze, she was new to him again, and he found himself searching his

[011-173] consciousness for his lost acquaintance with her, and feeling as if he

[011-174] knew her less and less. Perhaps, indeed, she had no very distinctive

[011-175] individuality; perhaps at her age no woman has, but waits for it to come

[011-176] to her through life, through experience. She was an expression of youth,

[011-177] of health, of beauty, and of the moral loveliness that comes from a

[011-178] fortunate combination of these; but beyond this she was elusive in a way

[011-179] that seemed to characterise her even materially. He could not make

[011-180] anything more of the mystery as he walked at her side, and he went

[011-181] thinking--formlessly, as people always think--that with the child or

[011-182] with her mother he would have had a community of interest and feeling

[011-183] which he lacked with this splendid girlhood! he was both too young and

[011-184] too old for it; and then, while he answered this or that to Imogene's

[011-185] talk aptly enough, his mind went back to the time when this mystery was

[011-186] no mystery, or when he was contemporary with it, and if he did not

[011-187] understand it, at least accepted it as if it wore the most natural thing

[011-188] in the world. It seemed a longer time now since it had been in his world

[011-189] than it was since he was a child.
