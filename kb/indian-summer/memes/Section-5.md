[005-001] "Is there a fashion in faces?" asked [[theodore-colville|Colville]].

[005-002] "Why, certainly. You must know that."

[005-003] "Then why aren't all the ladies in the fashion?"

[005-004] "It isn't one that can be put on. Besides, every one hasn't got [[imogene-graham|Imogene]]

[005-005] Graham's figure to carry it off."

[005-006] "That's her name, then--Imogene Graham. It's a very pretty name."

[005-007] "Yes. She's staying with me for the winter. Now that's all I can allow

[005-008] you to know for the present. Come! You must!"

[005-009] "But this is worse than nothing." He made a feint of protesting as she

[005-010] led him away, and named him to the lady she wished him to know. But he

[005-011] was not really sorry; he had his modest misgivings whether he were equal

[005-012] to quite so much young lady as Miss Graham seemed. When he no longer

[005-013] looked at her he had a whimsical impression of her being a heroic statue

[005-014] of herself.

[005-015] The lady whom [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]] left him with had not much to say, and she made

[005-016] haste to introduce her husband, who had a great deal to say. He was an

[005-017] Italian, but master of that very efficient English which the Italians

[005-018] get together with unimaginable sufferings from our orthography, and

[005-019] Colville repeated the republican deputy's saying about a constitutional

[005-020] king, which he had begun to think was neat.

[005-021] "I might prefer a republic myself," said the Italian, "but I think that

[005-022] gentleman is wrong to be a republican where he is, and for the present.

[005-023] The monarchy is the condition of our unity; nothing else could hold us

[005-024] together, and we must remain united if we are to exist as a nation. It's

[005-025] a necessity, like our army of half a million men. We may not like it in

[005-026] itself, but we know that it is our salvation." He began to speak of the

[005-027] economic state of Italy, of the immense cost of freedom and independence

[005-028] to a people whose political genius enables them to bear quietly burdens

[005-029] of taxation that no other government would venture to impose. He spoke

[005-030] with that fond, that appealing patriotism, which expresses so much to

[005-031] the sympathetic foreigner in Italy: the sense of great and painful

[005-032] uncertainty of Italy's future through the complications of diplomacy,

[005-033] the [[the-past|memory]] of her sufferings in the past, the spirit of quiet and

[005-034] inexhaustible patience for trials to come. This resolution, which is

[005-035] almost resignation, poetises the attitude of the whole people; it made

[005-036] Colville feel as if he had done nothing and borne nothing yet.

[005-037] "I am ashamed," he said, not without a remote resentment of the

[005-038] unworthiness of the republican voters of [[des-vaches|Des Vaches]], "when I hear of

[005-039] such things, to think of what we are at home, with all our resources and

[005-040] opportunities."

[005-041] The Italian would have politely excused us to him, but Colville would

[005-042] have no palliation of our political and moral nakedness; and he framed a

[005-043] continuation of the letter he began on the [[ponte-vecchio|Ponte Vecchio]] to the

[005-044] _[[post-democratic-republican|Post-Democrat-Republican]]_, in which he made a bitterly ironical

[005-045] comparison of the achievements of Italy and America in the last ten

[005-046] years.

[005-047] He forgot about Miss Graham, and had only a vague sense of her splendour

[005-048] as he caught sight of her in the long mirror which she stood before. She

[005-049] was talking to a very handsome young clergyman, and smiling upon him.

[005-050] The company seemed to be mostly Americans, but there were a good many

[005-051] evident English also, and Colville was dimly aware of a question in his

[005-052] mind whether this clergyman was English or American. There were three or

[005-053] four Italians and there were some Germans, who spoke English.

[005-054] Colville moved about from group to group as his enlarging acquaintance

[005-055] led, and found himself more interested in society than he could ever

[005-056] have dreamed of being again. It was certainly a defect of the life at

[005-057] Des Vaches that people, after the dancing and love-making period, went

[005-058] out rarely or never. He began to see that the time he had spent so

[005-059] busily in that enterprising city had certainly been in some sense

[005-060] wasted.

[005-061] At a certain moment in the evening, which perhaps marked its

[005-062] advancement, the tea-urn was replaced by a jug of the rum punch, mild or

[005-063] strong according to the custom of the house, which is served at most

[005-064] Florentine receptions. Some of the people went immediately after, but

[005-065] [[mr-morton|the young clergyman]] remained talking with Miss Graham.

[005-066] Colville, with his smoking glass in his hand, found himself at the side

[005-067] of a friendly old gentleman who had refused the punch. They joined in

[005-068] talk by a common impulse, and the old gentleman said, directly, "You are

[005-069] an American, I presume?"

[005-070] His accent had already established the fact of his own nationality, but

[005-071] he seemed to think it the part of candour to say, when Colville had

[005-072] acknowledged his origin, "I'm an American myself."

[005-073] "I've met several of our countrymen since I arrived," suggested

[005-074] Colville.

[005-075] The old gentleman seemed to like this way of putting it. "Well, yes,

[005-076] we're not unfairly represented here in numbers, I must confess. But I'm

[005-077] bound to say that I don't find our countrymen so aggressive, so loud, as

[005-078] our international novelists would make out. I haven't met any of their

[005-079] peculiar heroines as yet, sir."

[005-080] Colville could not help laughing. "I wish _I_ had. But perhaps they

[005-081] avoid people of our years and discretion, or else take such a filial

[005-082] attitude toward us that we can't recognise them."

[005-083] "Perhaps, perhaps," cried the old gentleman, with cheerful assent.

[005-084] "I was talking with one of our German friends here just now, and he

[005-085] complained that the [[american-girlhood|American girls]]--especially the rich ones--seem very

[005-086] calculating and worldly and conventional. I told him I didn't know how

[005-087] to account for that. I tried to give him some notion of the ennobling

[005-088] influences of society in Newport, as I've had glimpses of it."

[005-089] The old gentleman caressed his elbows, which he was holding in the palms

[005-090] of his hands, in high enjoyment of Colville's sarcasm. "Ah! very good!

[005-091] very good!" he said. "I quite agree with you, and I think the other sort

[005-092] are altogether preferable."

[005-093] "I think," continued Colville, dropping his ironical tone, "that we've

[005-094] much less to regret in their unsuspecting, [[american-innocence|unsophisticated freedom]] than

[005-095] in the type of hard materialism which we produce in young girls,

[005-096] perfectly wide awake, disenchanted, unromantic, who prefer the worldly

[005-097] vanities and advantages deliberately and on principle, recognising

[005-098] something better merely to despise it. I've sometimes seen them----"

[005-099] Mrs. Bowen came up in her gentle, inquiring way. "I'm glad that you and

[005-100] Mr. Colville have made acquaintance," she said to the old gentleman.

[005-101] "Oh, but we haven't," said Colville. "We're entire strangers."

[005-102] "Then I'll introduce you to [[mr-waters|Rev. Mr. Waters]]. And take you away," she

[005-103] added, putting her hand through Colville's arm with a delicate touch

[005-104] that flattered his whole being, "for your time's come at last, and I'm

[005-105] going to present you to Miss Graham."

[005-106] "I don't know," he said. "Of course, as there is a Miss Graham, I can't

[005-107] help being presented to her, but I had almost worked myself up to the

[005-108] point of wishing there were none. I believe I'm afraid."

[005-109] "Oh, I don't believe that at all. A simple schoolgirl like that!" Mrs.

[005-110] Bowen's sense of humour had not the national acuteness. She liked joking

[005-111] in men, but she did not know how to say funny things back "You'll see,

[005-112] as you come up to her."

[005-113] IV

[005-114] Miss Graham did, indeed, somehow diminish in the nearer perspective. She

[005-115] ceased to be overwhelming. When Colville lifted his eyes from bowing

[005-116] before her he perceived that she--was neither so very tall nor so very

[005-117] large, but possessed merely a generous amplitude of womanhood. But she

[005-118] was even more beautiful, with a sweet and youthful radiance of look that

[005-119] was very winning. If she had ceased to be the goddess she looked across

[005-120] the length of the _salon_, she had gained much by becoming an extremely

[005-121] lovely young girl; and her teeth, when she spoke, showed a fascinating

[005-122] little irregularity that gave her the last charm.

[005-123] Mrs. Bowen glided away with the young clergyman, but Effie remained at

[005-124] Miss Graham's side, and seemed to have hold of the left hand which the

[005-125] girl let hang carelessly behind her in the volume of her robe. The

[005-126] child's face expressed an adoration of Miss Graham far beyond her

[005-127] allegiance to her mother.

[005-128] "I began to doubt whether Mrs. Bowen was going to bring you at all," she

[005-129] said frankly, with an innocent, nervous laugh, which made favour for her

[005-130] with Colville. "She promised it early in the evening."

[005-131] "She has used me much worse, Miss Graham," said Colville. "She has kept

[005-132] me waiting from the beginning of time. So that I have grown grey on my

[005-133] way up to you," he added, by an inspiration. "I was a comparatively

[005-134] young man when Mrs. Bowen first told me she was going to introduce me."

[005-135] "Oh, how _good_!" said Miss Graham joyously. And her companion, after a

[005-136] moment's hesitation, permitted herself a polite little titter. She had

[005-137] made a discovery; she had discovered that Mr. Colville was droll.

[005-138] "I'm very glad you like it," he said, with a gravity that did not

[005-139] deceive them.

[005-140] "Oh yes," sighed Miss Graham, with generous ardour. "Who but an American

[005-141] could say just such things? There's the loveliest old lady here in

[005-142] [[florence|Florence]], who's lived here thirty years, and she's always going back and

[005-143] never getting back, and she's so homesick she doesn't know what to do,

[005-144] and she always says that Americans may not be _better_ than other

[005-145] people, but they are _different_."

[005-146] "That's very pretty. They're different in everything but thinking

[005-147] themselves better. Their native modesty prevents that."

[005-148] "I don't exactly know what you mean," said Miss Graham, after a little

[005-149] hesitation.

[005-150] "Well," returned Colville, "I haven't thought it out very clearly myself

[005-151] yet. I may mean that the Americans differ from other people in not

[005-152] thinking well of themselves, or they may differ from them in not

[005-153] thinking well enough. But what I said had a very epigrammatic sound, and

[005-154] I prefer not to investigate it too closely."

[005-155] This made Miss Graham and Miss Effie both cry out "Oh!" in delighted

[005-156] doubt of his intention. They both insensibly drifted a little nearer to

[005-157] him.

[005-158] "There was a French lady said to me at the _table-d'hote_ this evening

[005-159] that she knew I was an American, because the Americans always strike the

[005-160] key of personality." He practised these economies of material in

[005-161] conversation quite recklessly, and often made the same incident or

[005-162] suggestion do duty round a whole company.

[005-163] "Ah, I don't believe that," said Miss Graham.

[005-164] "Believe what?"

[005-165] "That the Americans always talk about themselves."

[005-166] "I'm not sure she meant that. You never can tell what a person means by

[005-167] what he says--or _she_."

[005-168] "How shocking!".

[005-169] "Perhaps the French lady meant that we always talk about other people.

[005-170] That's in the key of personality too."

[005-171] "But I don't believe we do," said Miss Graham. "At any rate, _she_ was

[005-172] talking about _us_, then."

[005-173] "Oh, she accounted for that by saying there was a large American colony

[005-174] in Paris, who had corrupted the French, and taught them our pernicious

[005-175] habit of introspection."

[005-176] "Do you think we're very introspective?"

[005-177] "Do you?"

[005-178] "I know I'm not. I hardly ever think about myself at all. At any rate,

[005-179] not till it's too late. That's the great trouble. I wish I could. But

[005-180] I'm always studying other people. They're so much more interesting."

[005-181] "Perhaps if you knew yourself better you wouldn't think so," suggested

[005-182] Colville.

[005-183] "Yes, I know they are. I don't think any young person can be

[005-184] interesting."

[005-185] "Then what becomes of all the novels? They're full of young persons."

[005-186] "They're ridiculous. If I were going to write a novel, I should take an

[005-187] old person for a hero--thirty-five or forty." She looked at Colville,

[005-188] and blushing a little, hastened to add, "I don't believe that they begin

[005-189] to be interesting much before that time. Such flat things as young men

[005-190] are always saying! Don't you remember that passage somewhere in Heine's

[005-191] _Pictures of Travel_, where he sees the hand of a lady coming out from

[005-192] under her mantle, when she's confessing in a church, and he knows that

[005-193] it's the hand of a young person who has enjoyed nothing and suffered

[005-194] nothing, it's so smooth and flower-like? After I read that I hated the

[005-195] look of my hands--I was only sixteen, and it seemed as if I had had no

[005-196] more experience than a child. Oh, I like people to go _through_

[005-197] something. Don't you?"
