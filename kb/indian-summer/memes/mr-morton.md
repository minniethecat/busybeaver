## Role

Mr. Morton is a supporting character in *Indian Summer* who functions as the "other suitor" to Imogene Graham — a young American Episcopal clergyman whose quiet, deferential attentions to her form the secondary thread of the novel's romantic complications. He serves as a dramatic foil to Theodore Colville: where Colville is middle-aged, ironic, and self-doubting, Morton is young, earnest, and conventional. His presence in Florence establishes the love-triangle tension that drives Mrs. Bowen's moral insistence that Colville disclose his engagement, and his dogged faithfulness to Imogene, even after her dismissal of him, earns him Colville's grudging admiration and eventually the reader's sympathy. At the novel's close, the suggestion that Morton may yet win Imogene's hand (047-071–047-082) provides one of the few notes of narrative consolation.

## Character Analysis

Mr. Morton is introduced as a handsome figure in Mrs. Bowen's salon — Colville's first glimpse of him is as "a very handsome young clergyman" to whom Imogene is smiling (005-049). He moves with a certain ecclesiastical formality; Colville notes "the ecclesiastical scrupulosity of his dress" (039-039–039-040), and the narrator later describes "the suave house-priest manners of the young clergyman" (036-170). He is said to be about twenty-five years old, approximately ten years younger than Mrs. Bowen (036-181). His origins are American — he asks about Buffalo weather during the Fiesole drive (040-120–040-121) and at the novel's end has taken a parish at Erie, Pennsylvania, with talk of coming to Buffalo (047-072–047-073).

In personality, Morton is deferential, well-mannered, and intellectually earnest though conventional. Colville, initially hostile toward him as a rival, is forced to admit that "the young man showed sense and judgment, and if he was a little academic in his mind and manners, Colville tolerantly reflected that some people seemed to be born so, and that he was probably not artificial" (039-036–039-039). Morton's taste in art and literature is described as "rather conventional" (036-172), yet his preaching is praised as "rather well judged, and simply and fitly expressed" (036-183–036-184). He possesses what Colville ultimately recognises as "an excellent head and a noble heart" (045-057–045-058), and in his quieter moments reveals "a fine head, and an intelligent, handsome, gentle face" (037-180).

Morton's courtship of Imogene is patient and undemonstrative. He brings her books of Italian poetry and Kingsley (038-172, 038-178), reads Browning aloud to the ladies (012-184), brings roses (013-124), and helps Effie gather wildflowers (037-144). Imogene, however, privately dismisses him with the tepid observation "Oh, he's a clergyman" (006-116) and tells Mrs. Bowen flatly "I don't care anything for Mr. Morton" (020-176). Despite this rejection, Morton remains steadfast — he saves Imogene from the overturned carriage in Fiesole when Colville himself fails to reach her (042-178), and continues his attentions through to the novel's end. Colville, by the close of the story, finds himself wishing that Morton and Imogene would marry, a hope the novel's final paragraphs suggest may be realised.

## Relationships

- **Imogene Graham** — Morton's romantic interest. He courts her with books, poetry readings, flowers, and quiet devotion. Imogene is aware of his feelings but, captivated by Colville's wit and supposed tragic depth, repeatedly dismisses Morton as uninteresting (006-116, 020-176). Mrs. Bowen directly tells Imogene that Morton "is in love with you" (039-138), and the novel ends with the suggestion that Morton, now holding a parish at Erie, may be on the verge of winning her after all (047-071–047-076).

- **Mrs. Evalina Bowen** — Morton's patron in Florence. She was introduced to him "by an old friend who was greatly interested in him" and "had been able to be of use to him" (038-038–038-041). Colville and Mr. Waters suspect that Mrs. Bowen has helped Morton financially upon occasion (037-007, 036-179–036-180). Mrs. Bowen feels a protective duty toward him — it is she who insists that Morton must be told of Colville and Imogene's engagement, acting as "Mr. Morton's friend" (039-081). Her concern for his feelings drives one of the key moral pressures of the story.

- **Theodore Colville** — Morton's unwitting rival for Imogene's affections. Colville's attitude toward Morton shifts over the novel from irritated jealousy (012-186) to grudging respect (039-035–039-036) and finally to generous warmth. After the engagement is broken, Colville declares Morton "an admirable young fellow, with an excellent head and a noble heart" (045-058) and wishes for his happiness. Colville's guilt over not telling Morton of the engagement — and his admission that "it was Mrs. Bowen who thought of it" rather than himself (042-113–042-114) — exposes his own moral passivity.

- **Effie Bowen** — The child of the household, who treats Morton with friendly ease. Morton helps Effie gather daisies and violets in the Cascine (037-144) and participates in the household's social life as a familiar presence.

## Key Scenes

### First Appearance at Mrs. Bowen's Party (Chapter V)

Morton first appears at Mrs. Bowen's evening party, where Colville catches sight of Imogene standing before a mirror, "talking to a very handsome young clergyman, and smiling upon him" (005-049). Colville is "dimly aware of a question in his mind whether this clergyman was English or American" (005-052), and notes that while other guests leave after the punch is served, "the young clergyman remained talking with Miss Graham" (005-065). This opening tableau establishes Morton as Colville's rival before the reader even learns his name.

> "She was talking to a very handsome young clergyman, and smiling upon him. The company seemed to be mostly Americans, but there were a good many evident English also, and Colville was dimly aware of a question in his mind whether this clergyman was English or American." (005-049–005-052)

### The Fiesole Excursion (Chapters XL–XLII)

Morton arrives first for the excursion to Fiesole, bringing bouquets for both Imogene and Mrs. Bowen, and his arrival is met with "a reckless welcome" from Imogene that "seemed to fill him with joy, and Mrs. Bowen with silent perplexity" (040-073–040-075). In the carriage, he takes the seat beside Imogene while Colville sits behind with Effie and her mother (040-099–040-100). Throughout the day's tension — as Colville wrestles with whether he must tell Morton of the engagement — Morton remains politely oblivious, engaging Colville in respectful conversation about Italian springs and American weather (040-132–040-141). At the day's climax, when the carriage overturns, it is Morton who acts decisively: Colville hears "the young clergyman calling to him: 'she's safe!'" and catches "a glimpse of Imogene, whom Mr. Morton had pulled from the other side of the carriage" (042-177–042-178).

> "Mr. Morton was the first to arrive, and if his discouragement began at once, the first steps masked themselves in a reckless welcome, which seemed to fill him with joy, and Mrs. Bowen with silent perplexity." (040-073–040-075)

### Reading Poetry at Palazzo Pinti (Chapter XII)

Morton reads Browning's "The Statue and the Bust" aloud to Mrs. Bowen and Imogene, an intimacy that provokes Colville's unacknowledged jealousy. Imogene tells Colville that they "had a long discussion" afterward, and that Morton also read them the "Legend of Pornic" (012-193). Colville's resentment of "this freedom of Mr. Morton's" is irrational — "he did not know just why" (012-186) — but reveals the depth of his own unconscious investment in Imogene.

### Conversation with Colville on the Roman Theatre Steps (Chapter XLII)

In a quieter moment among the ruins at Fiesole, Morton sits "respectfully near" Mrs. Bowen on the lower steps of the Roman theatre while Imogene ascends with Colville (042-039–042-040). This small tableau of loyalty — Morton attending to Mrs. Bowen while the romantic complications swirl around him — encapsulates his steadiness of character. He shows "a respectful toleration of the drolleries of a gifted but eccentric man" (041-011–041-012) in his dealings with Colville, and never loses his composure despite being caught in a romantic situation he does not understand.

## Critical Reception

Morton has received relatively little focused critical attention, as he is principally a functional figure within the novel's architecture of romantic competition. Where critics have discussed him, they tend to treat him as an index of Colville's moral sensibility: Colville's evolving appreciation of Morton — from competitive irritation to warm endorsement — charts the protagonist's growth in generosity and self-knowledge. Morton's quiet, conventional decency serves as a structural counterweight to Colville's ironic complexity, and his patient persistence with Imogene offers a muted but hopeful commentary on the value of steady affection over romantic intensity.
