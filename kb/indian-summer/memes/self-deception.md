# Self-Deception

## Definition

Self-deception in *Indian Summer* is the process by which characters rationalize their own motives, mistake self-interest for conscience, and persuade themselves of feelings and intentions they do not genuinely possess. This is not simple hypocrisy but a more insidious phenomenon: characters believe their own fabrications, using what the narrator calls the "plausible pretences" of the heart (019-297 to 019-299) to avoid confronting uncomfortable truths about their desires, their age, and their responsibility to others. The novel traces self-deception as a structural feature of romantic love in middle age — the way a "bachelor of forty" (022-092) is "flattered" into believing himself young, the way a romantic young woman convinces herself that pity is love, and the way both parties construct elaborate justifications for a relationship that neither, at some level, truly wants. Howells treats self-deception not as a moral failing of particular characters but as a near-universal condition: as Mr. Waters observes, "The difficulty is to know conscience from self-interest" (044-177 to 044-178).

## Context in the Novel

The motif of self-deception is established in the novel's opening pages. Colville's decision to abandon his newspaper career in Des Vaches springs from a wound to his pride — he has been "defeated by a tremendous majority" after permitting his name to be used by the opposing party (001-107 to 001-111). Yet he cannot simply acknowledge that pride is his motive: "he could not wholly acquit himself of a measure of bad faith; his conscience troubled him even more than his pride" (001-125 to 001-128). From the very beginning, Colville is unable to distinguish moral compunction from wounded vanity — a pattern that will repeat itself in the novel's romantic plot. He leaves America under the justification of returning to architectural study, but the narrator notes drily that he "became, half without his willing it, a newspaper man" (001-292 to 001-293) in the first place, and that six weeks before selling his paper "he would have scoffed at a prophecy of his return to Europe for the resumption of any artistic purpose whatever" (001-306 to 001-309). Colville's self-narrative is always a retrospective construction, a story told after the fact to give coherence to impulses he did not fully understand.

The novel's central romantic confusion is itself a product of layered self-deceptions. Colville, at forty-one, has been living a "solitary and barren" life (010-139) since an old romantic disappointment — what he calls "my great mistake" (022-089). When he meets Imogene Graham, a girl of twenty, he is "flattered" by her attention (045-007). He persuades himself that he is in love, or at least that he ought to be. But his proposal is prompted less by genuine feeling than by social momentum and a middle-aged man's susceptibility to the admiration of a beautiful young woman: "A bachelor of forty is always flattered by any suggestion of marriage; the suggestion that a beautiful and charming woman would marry him is too much for whatever reserves of modesty and wisdom he may have stored up" (022-092 to 022-096). The narrator's ironic generalization makes clear that Colville's case is typical rather than exceptional — self-deception about age and desirability is, for Howells, almost a law of male psychology.

Imogene's self-deception is equally profound. She believes she loves Colville, but Colville himself understands that "she loves me through her mistaken pity, and because she thinks she can sacrifice herself to me" (037-171 to 037-173). Her engagement produces a loss of stable selfhood: writing to her diary, she confesses, "I can hardly know myself for the same person" (031-309 to 031-310). Later, when the engagement begins to unravel, she asks Mrs. Bowen the devastating question that reveals her own suppressed awareness: "Sometimes it seems as if he didn't — as if I had forced myself on him through a mistake, and he had taken me to save me from the shame of knowing that I had made a mistake" (040-006 to 040-009). Imogene has been deceiving herself about Colville's feelings, and at some level she has always known it.

Colville's self-deception reaches its most elaborate form in his rationalization for continuing the engagement even after he recognizes it as a mistake. The narrator describes his mental state with clinical precision: "The task employed his thoughts all that day, and kept him vibrating between an instinctive conviction of monstrous wickedness and a logical and well-reasoned perception that he had all the facts and materials for a perfectly good conscience" (034-425 to 034-431). The mind, in this account, is not a reliable instrument for moral discernment but a machine for generating self-justification. Colville can construct a "logical and well-reasoned" case for any course of action he wishes to take, and the more elaborate the reasoning, the more thoroughly it conceals the emotional truth he wishes to avoid.

The novel's most concentrated meditation on self-deception occurs in Colville's conversation with the elderly Unitarian minister, Mr. Waters. Claiming the "reward of a good conscience" (044-174 to 044-176) for having removed himself from Mrs. Bowen's household, Colville is met with Waters's devastatingly simple reply: "The difficulty is to know conscience from self-interest" (044-177 to 044-178). Colville protests — "Oh, there's no doubt of it in my case" (044-179) — but his very eagerness to defend himself betrays the doubt he denies. Moments later, Waters's casual suggestion that Mrs. Bowen would have been "glad to keep you" (044-182) provokes Colville to a comically transparent outburst: "Do you really think so? I wish you would tell me why. Have you any reason — grounds? Pshaw! I'm absurd!" (044-183 to 044-186). The man who claimed certainty about his motives is instantly undone by the suggestion that his departure might not have been necessary. His self-deception is not merely intellectual; it is an emotional structure that collapses at the slightest pressure from reality.

By the novel's end, Colville achieves a measure of self-knowledge. He admits to Mr. Waters that the engagement "was a great mistake for both of us — a tragical one for her, poor child, a ridiculous one for me" (045-006 to 045-009). He acknowledges his own role in allowing it to develop: "I'm afraid I was flattered by an illusion of hers that ought to have pained and alarmed me, and the rest followed inevitably, though I was always just on the point of escaping the consequences of my weakness — my wickedness" (045-014 to 045-021). The word "wickedness" is perhaps too strong; what Colville describes is not wickedness but the ordinary, cumulative self-deception of a man who allows himself to be carried by circumstances because facing the truth would require a courage he does not possess. Waters's reply, invoking "the old idea of a compact with the devil" (045-013), frames Colville's experience in mythic terms: self-deception is a Faustian bargain in which the debtor is "always on the point of escaping" but "did not pay at last" (045-014 to 045-015). The inexorable logic of self-deception, for Howells, is that one cannot escape consequences simply by recognizing them.

## Key Passages

> When a woman has done nothing she fills the soul of the man whose conscience troubles him with an instinctive apprehension. There is then no safety, his nerves tell him, except in bringing the affair, whatever it is, to an early issue — in having it out with her. Colville subdued the cowardly impulse of his own heart, which would have deceived him with the suggestion that Mrs. Bowen might be occupied with Effie, and it would be better to ask for Miss Graham. He asked for Mrs. Bowen, and she came in directly. (019-288 to 019-303)

This passage anatomizes self-deception at the moment of its operation. Colville's conscience is troubled because he has behaved badly toward Mrs. Bowen, and the "cowardly impulse of his own heart" immediately supplies a "plausible pretence" for avoiding the confrontation that conscience demands: he could simply ask for Miss Graham instead. The heart, in Howells's rendering, is not a guide to truth but a manufacturer of excuses — it "would have deceived him" into believing that avoidance was simply convenience. That Colville "subdues" this impulse and asks for Mrs. Bowen marks one of the novel's rare moments of successful resistance to self-deception, and it is significant that this resistance requires active effort: self-deception is the default, and honesty must be achieved against the grain of impulse.

> The task employed his thoughts all that day, and kept him vibrating between an instinctive conviction of monstrous wickedness and a logical and well-reasoned perception that he had all the facts and materials for a perfectly good conscience. He was the betrothed lover of this poor child, whose affection he could not check without a degree of brutality for which only a better man would have the courage. (034-425 to 034-434)

This is the novel's most penetrating analysis of the mechanics of self-justification. Colville's mental state is described as a "vibration" between two poles: an "instinctive conviction" of wrongdoing and a "logical and well-reasoned perception" of his own virtue. The contrast between "instinctive" and "logical" is crucial — it is reason itself, the faculty that ought to lead toward truth, that enables self-deception. The "logical and well-reasoned" case for his actions is also the false one. The second sentence reveals the emotional foundation beneath the rational superstructure: Colville cannot bring himself to hurt Imogene's feelings, and he constructs his moral justification to spare himself the pain of having to be cruel. His self-deception is ultimately a form of cowardice disguised as kindness — a pattern the novel treats with compassion but without excuse.

> The old gentleman smiled. "The difficulty is to know conscience from self-interest." (044-177 to 044-178)

This single sentence, delivered by Mr. Waters with a smile, is the novel's most concentrated philosophical statement about self-deception. It is brief to the point of aphorism, yet it cuts through all of Colville's elaborate self-justifications. The word "difficulty" is carefully chosen: Waters does not say it is impossible to distinguish conscience from self-interest, only that it is difficult. The statement implies that self-deception is not a special pathology but a permanent condition of moral life — something everyone must struggle with, not something from which anyone is exempt. Colville's immediate protestation ("Oh, there's no doubt of it in my case," 044-179) only confirms Waters's point: the person most confident of his own moral clarity is the person most thoroughly self-deceived.

> "That is broken off — if it were ever really on. It was a great mistake for both of us — a tragical one for her, poor child, a ridiculous one for me. My only consolation is that it was a mistake and no more; but I don't conceal from myself that I might have prevented it altogether if I had behaved with greater wisdom and dignity at the outset. But I'm afraid I was flattered by an illusion of hers that ought to have pained and alarmed me, and the rest followed inevitably, though I was always just on the point of escaping the consequences of my weakness — my wickedness." (045-005 to 045-021)

Colville's final confession to Mr. Waters represents his closest approach to honest self-knowledge. He distinguishes between Imogene's "tragical" mistake and his own "ridiculous" one — the adjectives measuring the moral distance between her youthful sincerity and his middle-aged weakness. The phrase "flattered by an illusion of hers that ought to have pained and alarmed me" is a precise description of self-deception in retrospect: an emotion that should have served as a warning (pain, alarm) was instead experienced as a gratification (flattery). The self-deceived person misinterprets the meaning of his own feelings. The concluding admission — that he was "always just on the point of escaping the consequences" but never did — captures the temporal structure of self-deception: it is sustained not by a single lie but by an endless series of near-escapes, each of which feels like a decision deferred rather than a choice made. His final word, "wickedness," may overstate the case, but it reflects Howells's moral seriousness: self-deception, even when it produces no deliberate harm, is a form of moral failure that must be named and confronted.

## Related Concepts

- **Conscience and Self-Interest** — The difficulty, identified by Mr. Waters, of distinguishing genuine moral scruple from rationalized self-interest (044-177 to 044-178). Self-deception in the novel almost always operates at this boundary, where the vocabulary of conscience provides cover for the operations of vanity, fear, and desire. The two concepts are paired throughout the novel: Colville's conscience about his newspaper (001-125 to 001-128), his conscience about his engagement (034-425 to 034-431), and his conscience about leaving Palazzo Pinti (044-174 to 044-176) are all shown to be entangled with self-interest.
- **Illusion and Disillusion** — The life cycle of a self-deception, from its construction to its collapse. Mr. Waters observes that "with women, nothing is so indestructible as an illusion" (037-100 to 037-101), while Colville is ultimately forced to recognize the "illusion of hers" that flattered him (045-007 to 045-008). The novel traces the arc from illusion through crisis to disillusion, suggesting that self-knowledge, when it arrives, is always retrospective and always painful.
- **Middle Age and Self-Knowledge** — The particular vulnerability of middle-aged characters to self-deception about their own capacities and desires. Colville's claim that "Nature has got tired of us and has left us to taste and conscience" (027-219 to 027-221) and that "taste and conscience are by no means so certain of what they want you to do as Nature was" (027-221 to 027-223) describes middle age as a condition of epistemological uncertainty: the reliable biological drives of youth have faded, and what remains is the unreliable apparatus of self-interpretation.
