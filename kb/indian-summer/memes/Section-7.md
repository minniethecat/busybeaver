[007-001] "Mrs. Milbury, you mean?" gasped [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Bowen]], in affright at his

[007-002] boldness.

[007-003] "Milbury, Bilbury, Pilsbury--it's all one, so long as it isn't----"

[007-004] "They're living in Chicago!" she hastened to reply, as if she were

[007-005] afraid he was going to say, "so long as it isn't [[theodore-colville|Colville]]," and she

[007-006] could not have borne that.

[007-007] Colville clasped his hands at the back of his head and looked at Mrs.

[007-008] Bowen with eyes that let her know that he was perfectly aware she had

[007-009] been telling [[imogene-graham|Miss Graham]] of his youthful romance, and that he had now

[007-010] touched it purposely. "And you wouldn't," he said, as if that were quite

[007-011] relevant to what they had been talking about--"you wouldn't let Miss

[007-012] Graham go out walking alone with a dotard like me?"

[007-013] "Certainly not," said Mrs. Bowen.

[007-014] Colville got to his feet by a surprising activity. "Good-bye, Miss

[007-015] Graham." He offered his hand to her with burlesque despair, and then

[007-016] turned to Mrs. Bowen. "Thank you for _such_ a pleasant evening! What was

[007-017] your day, did you say?"

[007-018] "Oh, any day!" said Mrs. Bowen cordially, giving her hand.

[007-019] "Do you know whom you look like?" he asked, holding it.

[007-020] "No."

[007-021] "[[miss-ridgely|Lina Ridgely]]."

[007-022] The ladies stirred softly in their draperies after he was gone. They

[007-023] turned and faced the hearth, where a log burned in a bed of hot ashes,

[007-024] softly purring and ticking to itself, and whilst they stood pressing

[007-025] their hands against the warm fronts of their dresses, as the fashion of

[007-026] women is before a fire, the clock on the mantel began to strike twelve.

[007-027] "Was that her name?" asked Miss Graham, when the clock had had its say.

[007-028] "Lina Ridgely?"

[007-029] "No; that was _my_ name," answered Mrs. Bowen.

[007-030] "Oh yes!" murmured the young girl apologetically.

[007-031] "She led him on; she certainly encouraged him. It was shocking. He was

[007-032] quite wild about it."

[007-033] "She must have been a cruel girl. How _could_ he speak of it so

[007-034] lightly?"

[007-035] "It was best to speak of it, and have done with it," said Mrs. Bowen.

[007-036] "He knew that I must have been telling you something about it."

[007-037] "Yes. How bold it was! A _young_ man couldn't have done it! Yes, he's

[007-038] fascinating. But how old and sad he looked, as he lay back there in the

[007-039] chair!"

[007-040] "Old? I don't think he looked old. He looked sad. Yes, it's left its

[007-041] mark on him."

[007-042] The log burned quite through to its core, and fell asunder, a bristling

[007-043] mass of embers. They had been looking at it with downcast heads. Now

[007-044] they lifted their faces, and saw the pity in each other's eyes, and the

[007-045] beautiful girl impulsively kissed the pretty woman good-night.

[007-046] V

[007-047] Colville fell asleep with the flattered sense which abounds in the heart

[007-048] of a young man after his first successful evening in society, but which

[007-049] can visit maturer life only upon some such conditions of long exile and

[007-050] return as had been realised in his. The looks of these two charming

[007-051] women followed him into his dreams; he knew he must have pleased them,

[007-052] the dramatic homage of the child was evidence of that; and though it had

[007-053] been many years since he had found it sufficient cause of happiness to

[007-054] have pleased a woman, the desire to do so was by no means extinct in

[007-055] him. The eyes of the girl hovered above him like stars; he felt in their

[007-056] soft gaze that he was a romance to her young heart, and this made him

[007-057] laugh; it also made him sigh.

[007-058] He woke at dawn with a sharp twinge in his shoulder, and he rose to give

[007-059] himself the pleasure of making his own fire with those fagots of broom

[007-060] and pine twigs which he had enjoyed the night before, promising himself

[007-061] to get back into bed when the fire was well going, and sleep late. While

[007-062] he stood before the open stove, the jangling of a small bell outside

[007-063] called him to the window, and he saw a procession which had just issued

[007-064] from the church going to administer the extreme unction to some dying

[007-065] person across the piazza. The parish priest went first, bearing the

[007-066] consecrated wafer in its vessel, and at his side an acolyte holding a

[007-067] yellow silk umbrella over the Eucharist; after them came a number of

[007-068] _facchini_ in white robes and white hoods that hid their faces; their

[007-069] tapers burned sallow and lifeless in the new morning light; the bell

[007-070] jangled dismally.

[007-071] "They even die dramatically in this country," thought Colville, in whom

[007-072] the artist was taken with the effectiveness of the spectacle before his

[007-073] human pity was stirred for the poor soul who was passing. He reproached

[007-074] himself for that, and instead of getting back to bed, he dressed and

[007-075] waited for the mature hour which he had ordered his breakfast for. When

[007-076] it came at last, picturesquely borne on the open hand of Giovanni,

[007-077] steaming coffee, hot milk, sweet butter in delicate disks, and two white

[007-078] eggs coyly tucked in the fold of a napkin, and all grouped upon the wide

[007-079] salver, it brought him a measure of the consolation which good cheer

[007-080] imparts to the ridiculous human heart even in the house where death is.

[007-081] But the sad incident tempered his mind with a sort of pensiveness that

[007-082] lasted throughout the morning, and quite till lunch. He spent the time

[007-083] in going about the churches; but the sunshine which the day began with

[007-084] was overcast, as it was the day before, and the churches were rather too

[007-085] dark and cold in the afternoon. He went to Viesseux's reading-room and

[007-086] looked over the English papers, which he did not care for much; and he

[007-087] also made a diligent search of the catalogue for some book about

[007-088] [[florence|Florence]] for little [[effie-bowen|Effie Bowen]]: he thought he would like to surprise

[007-089] her mother with his interest in the matter. As the day waned toward

[007-090] dark, he felt more and more tempted to take her at her word, when she

[007-091] had said that any day was her day to him, and go to see her. If he had

[007-092] been a younger man he would have anxiously considered this indulgence

[007-093] and denied himself, but after forty a man denies himself no reasonable

[007-094] and harmless indulgence; he has learned by that time that it is a pity

[007-095] and a folly to do so.

[007-096] Colville found Mrs. Bowen's room half full of arriving and departing

[007-097] visitors, and then he remembered that it was this day she had named to

[007-098] him on the [[ponte-vecchio|Ponte Vecchio]], and when Miss Graham thanked him for coming

[007-099] his first Thursday, he made a merit of not having forgotten it, and said

[007-100] he was going to come every Thursday during the winter. Miss Graham drew

[007-101] him a cup of tea from the Russian samovar which replaces in some

[007-102] Florentine houses the tea-pot of Occidental civilisation, and Colville

[007-103] smiled upon it and upon her, bending over the brazen urn with a

[007-104] flower-like tilt of her beautiful head. She wore an aesthetic dress of

[007-105] creamy camel's-hair, whose colour pleased the eye as its softness would

[007-106] have flattered the touch.

[007-107] "What a very Tourguéneffish effect the samovar gives!" he said, taking a

[007-108] biscuit from the basket Effie Bowen brought him, shrinking with

[007-109] redoubled shyness from the eyebrows which he arched at her. "I wonder

[007-110] you can keep from calling me Fedor Colvillitch. Where is your mother,

[007-111] Effie Bowenovna?" he asked of the child, with a temptation to say

[007-112] Imogene Grahamovna.

[007-113] They both looked mystified, but Miss Graham said, "I'm sorry to say you

[007-114] won't see Mrs. Bowen today. She has a very bad headache, and has left

[007-115] Effie and me to receive. We feel very incompetent, but she says it will

[007-116] do us good."

[007-117] There were some people there of the night before, and Colville had to

[007-118] talk to them. One of the ladies asked him if he had met the Inglehart

[007-119] boys as he came in.

[007-120] "The Inglehart boys? No. What are the Inglehart boys?"

[007-121] "They were here all last winter, and they've just got back. It's rather

[007-122] exciting for Florence." She gave him a rapid sketch of that interesting

[007-123] exodus of a score of young painters from the art school at Munich, under

[007-124] the head of the singular and fascinating genius by whose name they

[007-125] became known. "They had their own school for a while in Munich, and then

[007-126] they all came down into Italy in a body. They had their studio things

[007-127] with them, and they travelled third class, and they made the greatest

[007-128] excitement everywhere, and had the greatest fun. They were a great

[007-129] sensation in Florence. They went everywhere, and were such favourites. I

[007-130] hope they are going to stay."

[007-131] "I hope so too," said Colville. "I should like to see them."

[007-132] "Dear me!" said the lady, with a glance at the clock. "It's five! I must

[007-133] be going."

[007-134] The other ladies went, and Colville approached to take leave, but Miss

[007-135] Graham detained him.

[007-136] "What is Tourguéneffish?" she demanded.

[007-137] "The quality of the great Russian novelist, Tourguéneff," said Colville,

[007-138] perceiving that she had not heard of him.

[007-139] "Oh!"

[007-140] "You ought to read him. The samovar sends up its agreeable odour all

[007-141] through his books. Read _Lisa_ if you want your heart really broken.

[007-142] "I'm glad you approve of heart-breaks in books. So many people won't

[007-143] read anything but cheerful books. It's the only quarrel I have with Mrs.

[007-144] Bowen. She says there are so many sad things in life that they ought to

[007-145] be kept out of books."

[007-146] "Ah, there I perceive a divided [[social-duty|duty]]," said Colville. "I should like to

[007-147] agree with both of you. But if Mrs. Bowen were here I should remind her

[007-148] that if there are so many sad things in life that is a very good reason

[007-149] for putting them in books too."

[007-150] "Of course I shall tell her what you said."

[007-151] "Why, I don't object to a certain degree of cheerfulness in books; only

[007-152] don't carry it too far--that's all."

[007-153] This made the young girl laugh, and Colville was encouraged to go on. He

[007-154] told her of the sight he had seen from his window at daybreak, and he

[007-155] depicted it all very graphically, and made her feel its pathos perhaps

[007-156] more keenly than he had felt it. "Now, that little incident kept with me

[007-157] all day, tempering my boisterous joy in the Giottos, and reducing me to

[007-158] a decent composure in the presence of the Cimabues; and it's pretty hard

[007-159] to keep from laughing at some of them, don't you think?"

[007-160] The young people perceived that he was making fun again; but he

[007-161] continued with an air of greater seriousness. "Don't you see what a very

[007-162] good thing that was to begin one's day with? Why, even in Santa Croce,

[007-163] with the thermometer ten degrees below zero in the shade of Alfieri's

[007-164] monument, I was no gayer than I should have been in a church at home. I

[007-165] suppose Mrs. Bowen would object to having that procession go by under

[007-166] one's window in a book; but I can't really see how it would hurt the

[007-167] reader, or damp his spirits permanently. A wholesome reaction would

[007-168] ensue, such as you see now in me, whom the thing happened to in real

[007-169] life."

[007-170] He stirred his tea, and shook with an inward laugh as he carried it to

[007-171] his lips.

[007-172] "Yes," said Miss Graham thoughtfully, and she looked at him searchingly

[007-173] in the interval of silence that ensued. But she only added, "I wish it

[007-174] would get warmer in the churches. I've seen hardly anything of them

[007-175] yet."

[007-176] "From the way I felt in them to-day," sighed Colville, "I should think

[007-177] the churches would begin to thaw out about the middle of May. But if one

[007-178] goes well wrapped up in furs, and has a friend along to rouse him and

[007-179] keep him walking when he is about to fall into that lethargy which

[007-180] precedes death by freezing, I think they may be visited even now with

[007-181] safety. Have you been in Santa Maria Novella yet?"
