# Art and Beauty

## Definition

Art and beauty in *Indian Summer* denote the encompassing aesthetic dimension of expatriate life in Florence — the appreciation of Italian painting, sculpture, and architecture, the absorption in landscape and cityscape, and the cultivation of taste as a mode of social identity. For Howells's Americans abroad, aesthetic experience is never merely decorative; it is constitutive of their very presence in Europe. The beauty of Florence, its churches and galleries, its "inexhaustible beauty of the city, the sky, the air" (015-029), provides both the setting and the substance of expatriate self-fashioning. Yet the novel treats this aesthetic immersion with characteristic ambivalence: beauty is alternately a source of authentic enrichment and a seductive escape from the responsibilities of American life, a genuine vocation and a sophisticated form of self-deception. The art-and-beauty theme runs beneath the novel's romantic comedy like the Arno beneath the Ponte Vecchio — continuous, mutable, shaping every surface it touches while resisting any single interpretive claim.

## Context in the Novel

The novel's opening scene on the Ponte Vecchio immediately establishes the tension between aesthetic saturation and aesthetic resistance. Colville stands amid one of the most beautiful urban prospects in Europe and wills himself to indifference: "Colville was finding a sort of vindictive satisfaction in the power to ignore the surrounding frippery of landscape and architecture" (001-028). The word "frippery" — dismissive, faintly contemptuous — registers his disillusionment, but the word "vindictive" betrays the effort such indifference requires. Colville cannot simply be unmoved by Florence; he must punish the city by refusing to see it. This opening posture establishes a pattern that will recur throughout the novel: aesthetic experience is never neutral for Howells's characters; it demands a response, and the refusal of response is itself a form of engagement.

Colville's own history with art is established in the biographical retrospect of the first chapter. As a young man he was "a very young architect, with an inclination toward the literary side of his profession, which made it seem profitable to linger, with his Ruskin in his hand, among the masterpieces of Italian Gothic" (001-109 to 001-111). The Ruskinian framework — Victorian, moralising, aesthetically earnest — is crucial: Colville's early encounter with Italian art was mediated by the most influential art critic of the age, and his architectural vocation was literary before it was practical. The young architect read buildings before he designed them. This Ruskinian formation means that Colville's return to Florence and his later attempt to study Florentine architecture carry the weight not only of personal ambition but of a whole nineteenth-century discourse about the moral significance of art. When the middle-aged Colville resolves to study "the past life and character of the Florentines in their architecture" (010-153 to 010-155), he is reaching back not only to his own youth but to the Ruskinian conviction that a civilisation's architecture is the most legible record of its soul: "No people had ever more distinctly left the impress of their whole temper in their architecture, or more sharply distinguished their varying moods from period to period in their palaces and temples" (010-157).

The aestheticism of the expatriate community provides a social dimension to the theme. The more recent arrivals in Florence "expressed something of the prevailing English and American aestheticism in the decoration of their apartments" (014-114), importing the taste for bric-a-brac, for carefully arranged interiors, that had transformed the expatriate relationship to art. This domestic aestheticism is treated with gentle irony — it is the art of curtains and carpets rather than statues and pictures, as the novel notes that "people who came abroad no longer gave orders for statues and pictures, but spent their money on curtains and carpets, old chests and chairs, and pots and pans" (015-009 to 015-010). The shift from patronage of living artists to consumption of antique furniture marks a transformation in the meaning of expatriate taste: art becomes decoration, the artist's studio yields to the antique dealer's shop, and the aesthetic life becomes a matter of acquisition rather than creation.

The novel extends its treatment of beauty from the built environment to the natural landscape and the human figure. Mrs. Bowen's first greeting to Colville — "How glad you must be to get back! Did you ever see Florence more beautiful than it was this morning?" (002-145) — establishes the city's beauty as a shared idiom of social exchange, something to be remarked upon like the weather. The Arno viewed from the Cascine prompts Imogene Graham to exclaim, "Oh, that beautiful river! How it twists and writhes along!" (012-002), linking natural beauty to poetic recollection — she immediately invokes Longfellow's sonnet on the Ponte Vecchio. The statues in the Cascine draw Colville into "the rapture that a blackened garden statue imparts to one who beholds it from the vantage-ground of sufficient years and experience" (012-133), a phrase that qualifies aesthetic rapture with the ironic self-awareness of middle age. Human beauty, too, is a recurring aesthetic object: Imogene Graham is described with "a flower-like tilt of her beautiful head" wearing "an aesthetic dress of creamy camel's-hair" (007-104), and she is "an expression of youth, of health, of beauty, and of the moral loveliness that comes from a fortunate combination of these" (011-177).

Perhaps the novel's most concentrated meditation on expatriate aesthetic life comes in its portrait of the American artists' colony in Florence. Colville "haunted the studios a good deal, and through a retrospective affinity with art, and a human sympathy with the sacrifice which it always involves, he was on friendly terms with sculptors and painters" (015-003). These artists, who had come to Florence as "ardent and hopeful beginners" twenty years before and were now "grey or bald," live "simple and innocent lives in a world of the ideal, and rich in the inexhaustible beauty of the city, the sky, the air" (015-029). The phrase "a world of the ideal" captures the expatriate aesthetic condition at its most utopian: the artist in Florence dwells within beauty, sustained by it, even as the commercial conditions that might support his work erode around him. And in a striking image of aesthetic integration, the novel describes how the Carnival costumes "sympathised in a striking way with the architecture of the city, and gave him an effect of Florence which he could not otherwise have had" (016-117) — human artifice and architectural permanence momentarily fusing into a single aesthetic whole.

## Key Passages

> Colville was finding a sort of vindictive satisfaction in the power to ignore the surrounding frippery of landscape and architecture. He isolated himself so perfectly from it, as he brooded upon the river, that, for any sensible difference, he might have been standing on the Main Street Bridge at Des Vaches, Indiana, looking down at the tawny sweep of the Wabash. (001-028 to 001-030)

This opening passage performs the novel's founding aesthetic gesture: the refusal of beauty. Colville's "vindictive satisfaction" is a negative tribute to the power of Florentine architecture and landscape — one can only ignore what insistently demands attention. The imagined substitution of the Wabash for the Arno, Des Vaches for Florence, is the novel's sharpest articulation of the contrast between American practicality and European beauty, and it establishes Colville's doubleness: he is a man who can inhabit Florence while mentally replacing it with Indiana, his aesthetic sensitivity and his rejection of it existing in uneasy simultaneity.

> At that period he was a very young architect, with an inclination toward the literary side of his profession, which made it seem profitable to linger, with his Ruskin in his hand, among the masterpieces of Italian Gothic, when perhaps he might have been better employed in designing red-roofed many-verandaed, consciously mullioned seaside cottages on the New England coast. (001-109 to 001-111)

This sentence compresses Colville's entire vocational history into a single ironic arc. The Ruskin in his hand is the emblem of serious aesthetic ambition, but the "red-roofed many-verandaed, consciously mullioned seaside cottages" are what he ought to have been designing — the practical, commercial architecture of the American leisure class. The adverb "consciously" is the sentence's most pointed detail: the American cottages are not merely mullioned but self-consciously so, their aesthetic features quotations rather than organic expressions. The passage exposes the tension at the heart of Colville's artistic vocation: architecture for him was always more literary than practical, more Ruskinian than commercial, and this very literariness made it vulnerable — susceptible to translation into the journalism that eventually displaced it.

> The vague purpose which he had cherished of studying the past life and character of the Florentines in their architecture shaped itself anew in the half-hour which he gave himself over his coffee; and he turned it over in his mind with that mounting joy in its capabilities which attends the contemplation of any sort of artistic endeavour. No people had ever more distinctly left the impress of their whole temper in their architecture, or more sharply distinguished their varying moods from period to period in their palaces and temples. (010-153 to 010-157)

This is the novel's most concentrated expression of the Ruskinian architectural faith: that a civilisation's built environment is the legible record of its "whole temper," its collective character deposited in stone. The "mounting joy in its capabilities" is a precisely observed account of the artistic temperament — pleasure located not in the work but in the anticipation of the work, in what the project "could" become. The transformation from "vague purpose" to specific intention over a half-hour's coffee is itself a small parable about the rhythms of middle-aged creativity: inspiration arrives not as the thunderbolt of youth but as the gradual crystallisation of a half-forgotten intention. Yet the novel will go on to trace the attenuation of this very purpose — the mounting joy yields to doubt, and the architectural study becomes one more project begun but not completed.

> "How glad you must be to get back! Did you ever see Florence more beautiful than it was this morning?" (002-145)

Mrs. Bowen's greeting to Colville establishes the beauty of Florence as the shared language of the expatriate community — a social currency as conventional as remarks about the weather yet carrying genuine emotional weight. The question assumes a shared history of looking at Florence, a communal memory of the city's appearances, and it positions Colville as one who belongs to this community of beholders. The beauty of Florence is here not an object of solitary contemplation but the medium of social connection, something to be remarked upon, compared, and remembered together.

> Colville thought them mostly very happy men, leading simple and innocent lives in a world of the ideal, and rich in the inexhaustible beauty of the city, the sky, the air. (015-029)

This sentence distils the expatriate aesthetic ideal at its most luminous. The artists' world is "the ideal" — a domain of pure artistic value, insulated from the commercial pressures that have eroded their livelihoods. Their wealth is not financial but perceptual: they are "rich in the inexhaustible beauty" of Florence, the city's aesthetic resources compensating for material hardship. The triad "the city, the sky, the air" expands beauty from the architectural to the atmospheric, from what is built to what is simply there — the Florentine sky and air are as beautiful as its palaces and churches. The word "inexhaustible" is the passage's key term: beauty in Florence is a resource that cannot be depleted, a permanent endowment that sustains the expatriate artist even as other forms of support fail.

## Related Concepts

- **Artistic Vocation** — Colville's architectural ambitions, both youthful and revived, are the most direct expression of the art-and-beauty theme: architecture is the art form through which he negotiates his relationship to beauty, vocation, and self-justification in middle age.
- **Expatriate Identity** — The aesthetic appreciation of Italy is inseparable from the expatriate condition; Florence is beautiful to Colville precisely because he is an American abroad, and his aesthetic immersion is both the reward of expatriation and a potential evasion of American responsibilities.
- **Youth and Age** — The novel ties aesthetic experience to the life course: the young architect with his Ruskin experiences Italian art differently from the middle-aged returnee, and the beauty of Florence changes its meaning for the beholder who has aged.
- **Indian Summer (Metaphor)** — The novel's title metaphor describes a season of renewed warmth after summer's end, and Colville's aesthetic awakening in Florence — his revived architectural studies, his susceptibility to beauty — is itself a form of Indian summer: a late flowering of artistic sensibility that carries the poignancy of its own belatedness.
