Theodore Colville is the protagonist of William Dean Howells's *Indian Summer*, a forty-one-year-old American expatriate and former newspaper editor who returns to [[florence|Florence]] after seventeen years, hoping to resume his youthful architectural studies. Instead, he finds himself caught between two women — the youthful [[imogene-graham|Imogene Graham]] and the self-possessed [[lina-bowen|Mrs. Lina Bowen]] — in a romantic entanglement that forces him to confront the gap between his [[self-deception|self-deceptions]] and the realities of [[youth-and-age|middle age]]. Colville is a distinctively Howellsian protagonist: ironic, self-aware, passive to the point of moral drift, yet fundamentally decent. His journey from [[colville-political-disgrace|political disgrace]] in America to romantic redemption in Italy embodies the novel's central exploration of the [[indian-summer-metaphor|Indian summer]] — that bittersweet season of belated ripeness that gives the book its title.

## Biographical Sketch

Theodore Colville was born in New England but made his adult life in Prairie [[des-vaches|Des Vaches]], Indiana (031-057). As a young man, he studied architecture in Europe, devoting himself to Italian Gothic in Florence with his Ruskin in hand (001-109–001-111). His studies were cut short by a failed love affair in Venice — a young woman who left him heartbroken and adrift. At this vulnerable moment, he received a letter from his elder brother inviting him to Des Vaches, ostensibly to look at architecture but in reality to take over the management of the family newspaper.

> He went, because he was at that moment in the humour to go anywhere, and because his money was running low.
> (001-138)

Once in Indiana, Colville discovered there was no demand for architects and "became, half without his willing it, a newspaper man" (001-146). He proved unexpectedly suited to the work. He bought his brother out, renamed the paper the *[[democratic-republican|Democrat-Republican]]*, and built it into a respected voice in Indiana politics over fifteen industrious years (001-150–001-151).

His fall came through a political miscalculation. Permitting his name to be put forward as a congressional candidate by the opposing party, Colville was "defeated by a tremendous majority" (001-053–001-055). The defeat was not merely electoral but existential: "a mortification for Colville which his pride could not brook" (001-059). He contemptuously sold the paper and fled back to Italy, hoping to recover the artistic purpose of his youth (001-154–001-157). But the [[the-past|past]] would not be so easily reclaimed.

## Character Analysis

Colville is defined by a distinctive blend of irony, self-awareness, and passivity. He possesses a keen sense of the ridiculous — including his own — and habitually deflects serious emotion with wit. When Mrs. Bowen recognizes him on the [[ponte-vecchio|Ponte Vecchio]] after seventeen years, he instantly undercuts the moment:

> "I didn't know that I had preserved my youthful beauty to that degree."
> (002-099)

His physiognomy itself reflects this temperament: "the non-committal, rather weary-looking eyes; the brown hair, slightly frosted," with "the ironical self-conscious smile that lurked under" his beard (002-083–002-085).

Yet beneath the banter, Colville is a man driven by disgust and disappointment. His departure from America "sprang from disgust" — not only with the political failure that humiliated him, but with a life that had become too small (001-039). He experienced "a bitter disappointment in the city to whose prosperity he had given the energies of his best years" (001-040–001-041). In Florence, he finds himself unable to recover the romantic enthusiasm of his youth — "certain emotions, certain sensations failed to repeat themselves to Colville at sight of the familiar monuments" (001-191) — a failure that mirrors his broader midlife crisis and situates him within the novel's treatment of [[expatriate-identity|expatriate identity]].

Colville's tragic flaw is a kind of reflexive selflessness that becomes indistinguishable from [[moral-cowardice|weakness]]. He drifts into situations rather than choosing them, then rationalizes his passivity as deference to others. Throughout the novel he "could not say much without seeming to seek for myself the release I was offering her" (043-151–043-153). His own self-assessment is harsh: "I'm afraid I'm not apt to think of the comforts and rights of other people" (042-112–042-113). Imogene's mother sees him more clearly than he sees himself — she notes that some people are "younger at your age than at hers" (043-092–043-093).

After [[the-carriage-accident|the carriage accident]] at [[fiesole|Fiesole]] — symbolically the breaking point of his self-deception — Colville undergoes a transformation. Released from the engagement with Imogene, he musters the courage to declare his true feelings to Mrs. Bowen, even at the risk of losing her:

> "I want to assure you, Lina — my love, my dearest, as I shall call you for the first and last time! — that I *do* understand everything."
> (046-175–046-176)

His willingness to submit to her judgment, rather than to manipulate the situation, is his redemption: "His instinct of forbearance had served him better than the subtlest art. His submission was the best defence" (047-001–047-002).

## Key Scenes

### The Return to Florence (Chapter I)

The novel opens with Colville lounging on the [[ponte-vecchio|Ponte Vecchio]], staring at the [[arno-river|Arno]] and wishing he were looking at the Wabash instead. The passage establishes his emotional state and the novel's central thematic tension: a man who has fled his life but cannot escape his regrets.

> He isolated himself so perfectly from it, as he brooded upon the river, that, for any sensible difference, he might have been standing on the Main Street Bridge at Des Vaches, Indiana, looking down at the tawny sweep of the Wabash. He had no love for that stream, nor for the ambitious town on its banks, but ever since he woke that morning he had felt a growing conviction that he had been a great ass to leave them.
> (001-029–001-034)

### The Reunion with Mrs. Bowen (Chapter II)

While composing a sarcastic mental letter to his former readers, Colville is interrupted by the arrival of a lady and child at the parapet. The recognition scene that follows is a masterpiece of Howellsian social observation, revealing both characters through their mutual appraisal — a moment that echoes the [[colville-return-to-florence|larger pattern of his return]].

> She bent on his face a demand for just what quality and degree of change he found in hers, and apparently she satisfied herself that his inspection was not to her disadvantage, for she smiled brightly, and devoted the rest of her glance to an electric summary of the facts of Colville's physiognomy: the sufficiently good outline of his visage, with its full, rather close-cut, drabbish-brown beard and moustache, both shaped a little by the ironical self-conscious smile that lurked under them.
> (002-078–002-085)

### The Declaration and Its Undoing (Chapter XXXII)

When Colville comes to speak with Mrs. Bowen about his engagement to Imogene, his repressed feelings burst through in an unguarded moment — the novel's central dramatic irony: Colville is engaged to one woman while involuntarily confessing his devotion to another.

> "I'm sorry, Mrs. Bowen, to do anything — say anything — that is painful to you," Colville began. "You know that I would give the world to please you ----" The words escaped him and left him staring at her.
> "What are you saying to me, Theodore Colville?" she exclaimed, flashing a full-eyed glance upon him, and then breaking into a laugh, as unnatural for her.
> (032-086–032-091)

### The Carriage Accident and Aftermath (Chapters XLII–XLIII)

[[the-carriage-accident|The carriage accident]] at [[fiesole|Fiesole]] is the novel's dramatic turning point. When the horses rear and the carriage threatens to go over a wall, Colville rushes to save the women. Imogene's refusal to take his hand — "she shrank away" (042-175) — is the symbolic end of their relationship. Mrs. Graham later tells him that Imogene realized, even as Colville risked his life, that she did not love him: "if your heroic devotion to her — if your present danger did not awaken her to some such feeling for you as she had once imagined she had; if they both only increased her despair and self-abhorrence, then the case was indeed hopeless" (043-131–043-135).

### The Proposal to Mrs. Bowen (Chapter XLVI)

In the novel's most emotionally charged scene — the culmination of the [[the-final-confrontation|final confrontation]] between Colville and Mrs. Bowen over the failed engagement — Colville finally tells her the truth. His words are simple and direct, stripped of his usual irony.

> "About you. And he encouraged me to believe — imagine — that I might speak to you — ask — tell you that — I loved you, Lina." He leaned forward and took one of the hands that lay in her lap. It trembled with a violence inconceivable in relation to the perfect quiet of her attitude. But she did not try to take it away. "Could you — do you love me?"
> "Yes," she whispered.
> (046-063–046-068)

Though she admits her love, she initially refuses to marry him — not from lack of feeling, but from a fiercely held moral code involving [[sacrifice-and-renunciation|renunciation and sacrifice]]. It is only when Colville demonstrates genuine self-sacrifice — preparing to leave Florence without manipulation — that she relents.

## Critical Reception

*Indian Summer* is generally regarded, alongside *The Rise of Silas Lapham*, as one of Howells's finest novels. Critics have praised Colville as a nuanced portrait of middle-aged ambivalence — a character who embodies Howells's realist commitment to moral complexity over melodrama. Colville's blend of sincerity and [[self-deception|self-deception]], his wit and his weakness, his decency and his ability to cause harm without intending it, make him a distinctly modern protagonist. The novel's quiet insistence that moral life is a matter of "the grain of sand" rather than grand gestures (047-066–047-069) is embodied in Colville's ultimate acceptance of his own fallibility — a resolution that situates the novel squarely within the [[indian-summer-metaphor|Indian summer]] metaphor of belated, bittersweet fulfillment.
