# The Past

## Definition

The past in *Indian Summer* operates as both a lure and a burden — a repository of loss that simultaneously beckons toward recovery and warns against it. Unlike the conventional novelistic use of backstory as mere exposition, Howells makes the past an active dramatic force. Colville's return to Florence after seventeen years is not simply a revisiting of old scenes but a confrontation with an unresolved self, one whose romantic and vocational disappointments structure his present choices. The novel poses a question it refuses to answer simply: whether the past can be recuperated through present action — through a new love, a renewed vocation, a second youth — or whether maturity demands its renunciation. This question organises the entire romantic plot, from Colville's initial fantasy of recovering his lost youth through Imogene Graham to his final marriage to Lina Bowen, a union grounded not in the recovery of the past but in the shared acknowledgment of its irrecoverability. The novel's title metaphor — the Indian summer, a season of warmth that returns after summer has ended — is itself a figure for the relationship to the past: something that resembles youth but is not youth, something that can be experienced only by those who have already passed beyond it.

## Context in the Novel

The novel opens with the past insistently present. Colville has taken "the prodigious risk of breaking his life sharp off from the course in which it had been set for many years, and of attempting to renew it in a direction from which it had long been diverted" (001-035 to 001-037). The spatial metaphor of "direction" is significant: the past is conceived as a course or path from which one can be diverted and to which one might return. This is the novel's foundational gesture — a man fleeing a disappointing recent past by returning to an earlier, supposedly more promising one — and the novel will subject it to sustained critique.

Florence itself functions as a topography of memory. The "well-remembered figures" (002-008) that Colville encounters — the officers, the dandies, the beggars — create a disorienting effect in which the city appears unchanged while Colville himself has aged. Mrs. Bowen voices the sensation directly: "I never can believe in the lapse of time when I get back to Italy; it always makes me feel as young as when I left it last" (002-155). This illusion of temporal stasis — the feeling that the past remains accessible because its physical setting persists — is one of the seductions the novel systematically dismantles. When Colville first meets Mrs. Bowen after seventeen years, "he knew that she was thinking of that old love affair of his, to which she was privy in some degree" (002-149 to 002-150). The past is not merely a private memory but a shared knowledge, a social fact that other characters carry and that shapes their perceptions of Colville from the moment of his return.

Colville's meditation at Viesseux's reading room crystallises the novel's central question about the past. Watching the scholar Mr. Waters retreat into fifteenth-century studies, Colville recoils: "Life, active life, life of his own day, called to him; he had been one of its busiest children: could he turn his back upon it for any charm or use that was in the past?" (011-106 to 011-107). The question is posed rhetorically but the doubt it registers is real and destabilising. Colville feels himself to be "idler on its modern surface, or delver in its deep-hearted past" (011-110) — unable to commit to either mode of existence. This paralysis is grounded in the conviction that "he had not yet lived his life, that his was a baffled destiny, an arrested fate" (011-118 to 011-119). The past is not merely what has been lost; it is what was never properly had in the first place — a double privation that makes the fantasy of recovery all the more seductive.

The romantic plot literalises the question of recovering the past. Imogene Graham offers herself explicitly as a vehicle for Colville's temporal restoration, declaring: "I want you to feel that I am your youth — the youth you were robbed of — given back to you" (033-165 to 033-166). She frames this as a mission of repair: "you might go back in my life and take up yours where it was broken off; that I might make your life what it would have been — complete your destiny" (033-171 to 033-172). The language of breakage and completion reveals Imogene's romantic idealism — the belief that the past is a damaged object that love can restore. Colville's horrified reply — "do you think I could be such a vampire as to let you?" (033-174) — registers the novel's judgment on this fantasy. The metaphor of vampirism is precise and damning: recovering the past by feeding on another's youth is not restoration but predation. Imogene's own letter to a friend reveals the same logic operating from her side: "She had killed his youth; she had spoiled his life: if I could revive them, restore them!" (031-174 to 031-175). The fantasy of temporal repair is reciprocal — she imagines herself as the agent of a restoration that Colville simultaneously recognises as parasitic.

Colville's later confession to Mr. Waters confirms this reading and universalises it beyond the specific romantic predicament. When Waters describes another man who attempted to recover lost youth through a scheme of agricultural self-improvement, Colville bursts out: "Yes, yes; they are alike. *I* dreamed, too, of recovering and restoring my own lost and broken past in the love of a young soul, and it was in essence the same cruelly egotistic dream; and it's nothing in my defence that it was all formless and undirected at first, and that as soon as I recognised it I abhorred it" (045-028 to 045-032). The triple emphasis — "recovering," "restoring," "broken" — insists on the fantasy's internal structure: the past is imagined as something intact that has been shattered and can be reassembled. The novel's moral insight, fully articulated here, is that this is a category error. The past is not a broken object but an altered subject, and no amount of love or effort can reconstitute a younger self. The qualifier "cruelly egotistic" is the novel's harshest self-indictment, linking the desire to recover the past with a fundamental failure to recognise the separateness of other persons.

The novel's resolution enacts the necessary renunciation. After Colville's engagement to Imogene collapses and he eventually marries Mrs. Bowen, the narrator observes that in Rome, "arriving as a married couple, there was no inquiry and no interest in their past" (047-065). This sentence, placed near the novel's close, is freighted with thematic meaning. The past is not recovered, redeemed, or healed — it is simply left behind. The Colvilles' marriage is not a second youth but a mature accommodation to the present, one that depends on the past's erasure from social view. The novel's title metaphor thus finds its deepest application not in Colville's infatuation with Imogene but in his marriage to Lina, which accepts that the summer of youth is definitively past. What remains is not the recovery of what was lost but the possibility of living after loss — an Indian summer of the heart.

## Key Passages

> He had, in fact, taken the prodigious risk of breaking his life sharp off from the course in which it had been set for many years, and of attempting to renew it in a direction from which it had long been diverted. Such an act could be precipitated only by a strong impulse of conscience or a profound disgust, and with Colville it sprang from disgust. (001-035 to 001-039)

This opening statement of the novel's premise presents the relationship to the past in terms of rupture and attempted renewal. The word "prodigious" invests Colville's risk with an almost heroic scale, while "disgust" — placed at the sentence's emphatic end — strips that heroism of any nobility. The past is not something Colville lovingly revisits but something from which he flees, only to find himself returning to an earlier iteration of it. The spatial metaphor of being "diverted" from a "direction" implies that a correct temporal trajectory once existed and might be rejoined; the novel's subsequent action will systematically question this spatial model of temporal experience.

> Life, active life, life of his own day, called to him; he had been one of its busiest children: could he turn his back upon it for any charm or use that was in the past? Again that unnerving doubt, that paralysing distrust, beset him, and tempted him to curse the day in which he had returned to this outworn Old World. Idler on its modern surface, or delver in its deep-hearted past, could he reconcile himself to it? ... He was no longer young, that was true; but with an ache of old regret he felt that he had not yet lived his life, that his was a baffled destiny, an arrested fate. (011-106 to 011-119)

This is the novel's most concentrated meditation on the problem of the past. The rhetorical question — "could he turn his back upon it for any charm or use that was in the past?" — expects the answer "no," but Colville is simultaneously unable to commit to the present. The binary of "idler" versus "delver" articulates his paralysis: he can neither skim the surface of modern life nor immerse himself in historical study as Waters does. The final sentence shifts register from cultural critique to existential lament. "Baffled destiny" and "arrested fate" are phrases that locate the wound not in what was lost but in what was never achieved — a past defined by its absences rather than its contents.

> "I want you to feel that I am your youth — the youth you were robbed of — given back to you. Will you do it? ... It's been this idea, this hope, with me always — ever since I knew what happened to you here long ago — that you might go back in my life and take up yours where it was broken off; that I might make your life what it would have been — complete your destiny —" Colville wrenched himself loose from the hold that had been growing more tenderly close and clinging. "And do you think I could be such a vampire as to let you?" (033-165 to 033-174)

Imogene's speech is the novel's most explicit articulation of the fantasy of recovering the past through a new love. The dashes around "the youth you were robbed of — given back to you" enact syntactically the transfer she imagines: the appositive structure makes the two phrases equivalent, as if theft and restitution were a closed cycle. Colville's single-word reply — "vampire" — is the novel's definitive judgment on this fantasy. The metaphor transforms Imogene from restorer to victim and Colville from beneficiary to predator, exposing the power asymmetry that the language of "completion" and "destiny" had obscured. His physical gesture — "wrenched himself loose" — makes the renunciation bodily before it is verbal.

> "Yes, yes; they are alike. *I* dreamed, too, of recovering and restoring my own lost and broken past in the love of a young soul, and it was in essence the same cruelly egotistic dream; and it's nothing in my defence that it was all formless and undirected at first, and that as soon as I recognised it I abhorred it." (045-028 to 045-032)

Colville's confession to Mr. Waters is the novel's moral climax on the theme of the past. The italicised "*I*" insists on personal implication — this is not a general observation but a self-accusation. The paired gerunds "recovering and restoring" name the two phases of the fantasy: first retrieval, then repair. "Cruelly egotistic" is Howells's most severe moral vocabulary, linking the desire to recover the past with a failure of empathy so fundamental that it amounts to cruelty. The final clause — "as soon as I recognised it I abhorred it" — offers the only available exoneration: not innocence but self-knowledge, not the absence of the dream but the recognition of its nature.

> ...arriving as a married couple, there was no inquiry and no interest in their past. (047-065)

The novel's final word on its central theme, this sentence achieves its force through understatement. The double negation — "no inquiry and no interest" — enacts the past's erasure from the social world that the Colvilles now inhabit. Rome, unlike Florence, is a city without memory for them, and their marriage depends on this forgetting. The sentence does not claim that the past has been healed or integrated; it claims only that it no longer matters, that life can proceed without its resolution. This is the novel's version of renunciation: not a dramatic act of sacrifice but a quiet acceptance that certain wounds do not close and do not need to.

## Related Concepts

- **Indian Summer (Metaphor)** — The novel's title figure is itself a temporal metaphor for the relationship to the past: a warmth that returns after summer has ended, resembling youth without being youth, available only to those who have passed beyond it.
- **Youth and Age** — The generational tension that structures the novel's romantic plot is inseparable from the problem of the past: Colville's desire to recover his youth through Imogene is a specific form of the more general desire to undo time.
- **Romantic Disillusionment** — The collapse of Colville's engagement to Imogene is the plot mechanism through which the fantasy of recovering the past is defeated; romantic disillusionment in this novel is fundamentally temporal disillusionment.
- **Expatriate Identity** — Colville's past is geographically localised in Florence, and his expatriate return is also a temporal return; the two themes intersect in the figure of the American who attempts to recover a lost self by recovering a lost place.
- **Sacrifice and Renunciation** — Colville's eventual renunciation of the fantasy of recovering his past is the moral act that enables his mature marriage to Lina Bowen; renunciation in this novel is not the loss of the past but the loss of the belief that the past can be restored.
