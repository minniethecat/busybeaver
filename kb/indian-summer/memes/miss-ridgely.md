## Role

Miss Ridgely (Lina Ridgely) is a minor but significant off-stage presence in *Indian Summer*. She is the remembered younger self of Lina Bowen, the novel's female protagonist, as she existed twenty years before the main action — a young American woman spending a year in Europe as part of the expatriate society circle in Florence. Although she never appears directly in the present-day narrative, she is a constant presence in Theodore Colville's memories, where her remembered character — graceful, serene, and quietly compassionate — provides essential backstory for understanding Colville's romantic history (003-117). Her transformation from Miss Ridgely into Mrs. Bowen is one of the novel's submerged arcs, traced only through Colville's retrospective recognition of her worth.

## Character Analysis

In Colville's recollections, Miss Ridgely emerges as a figure of distinctive composure and understated elegance. Where her friend — the woman who would eventually jilt Colville — was "dashing and effective," Miss Ridgely was "serene and elegant" (003-125). This contrast is central: she was never the attention-seeking center of any social scene but rather the composed, watchful presence observing the romantic dramas around her with quiet perceptiveness.

Colville, absorbed in his infatuation with her friend, was at the time afraid of Miss Ridgely in a way that reveals the authority she already possessed as a very young woman. He was "even a little more afraid of Miss Ridgely than of her friend" (003-124), daunted by the judgment he imagined behind her serene exterior. Yet this fear was misplaced. In retrospect, Colville recognized that "Lina Ridgely in many fine little ways had shown a kindness, almost a compassion, for him, as for one whose unconsciousness a hopeless doom impended over" (003-129). Her kindness was subtle — expressed in small gestures and quiet advocacy rather than open intervention — exactly the kind of generosity that can only be fully appreciated in hindsight.

The biographical dimension of her character is equally telling. As a young American from the West, Miss Ridgely experienced the casual condescension of the Eastern establishment. When she and her friend were in Florence, "people from quite small towns in the East had made pretty Lina Ridgely and her friend feel the disadvantage of having come from the Western side of an imaginary line" (009-004). This detail positions her as someone navigating complex social hierarchies with the same quiet dignity that marked all her interactions, and it foreshadows the cosmopolitan polish she would later command as Mrs. Bowen at the Palazzo Pinti.

Colville's memories of Miss Ridgely are tinged with a rueful tenderness. Looking back, he "abandoned himself to regrets, which were proper enough in regard to Miss Ridgely, but were certainly a little unlawful concerning Mrs. Bowen" (003-136). The distinction he draws — between the unmarried girl of memory and the married woman before him — speaks to the enduring impression she made, and to the way past and present become entangled in his growing feelings for the woman she has become.

## Relationships

- **Jenny Wheelwright (the "other one")** — Miss Ridgely's close friend and confidante, who jilted Colville twenty years before the novel's main events (003-117). More assertive and "dashing" than Miss Ridgely, she occupied the foreground of their social circle while Miss Ridgely remained the composed observer. Colville came to believe that Miss Ridgely had secretly defended and advocated for him to her friend (003-129).

- **Theodore Colville** — The young man who was infatuated with her friend. Though somewhat afraid of Miss Ridgely's serene composure at the time (003-124), he later recognized her quiet kindness toward him. In their shared youth, they danced the Lancers together (009-178) and he read poetry to her and her friend (012-189). Two decades later, her youthful smile still haunted his memory, triggered by the sight of her daughter Effie (011-150).

- **The Hon. Mr. Bowen** — The man she eventually married, whom Colville imagines "must have had Lina Ridgely in his eye" when he dreamed of his ideal wife (015-152). Their marriage transformed her from a young Western girl navigating expatriate society into the poised and respected Mrs. Bowen of the Palazzo Pinti.

- **Mrs. Milbury** — A figure from their shared Florentine youth, mentioned in passing alongside Lina Ridgely as part of the social circle of the earlier period (007-001). The name surfaces when Colville reminisces about dancing the Lancers "with Lina Ridgely and the other one, Mrs. Milbury" (009-178).

- **Effie Bowen** — Miss Ridgely's future daughter, whose smile is so like her mother's that it repeatedly triggers Colville's memories of the young Lina Ridgely (011-150). Effie thus serves as an uncanny bridge between past and present, the young Miss Ridgely reincarnated in her child.

## Key Scenes

### The Young Confidante Remembered (Chapter 3)

Colville's extended recollection of his youthful romance reveals Miss Ridgely's role as the perceptive bystander to his heartbreak:

> Twenty years earlier, when Mrs. Bowen was Miss Lina Ridgely, she used to be the friend and confidante of the girl who jilted Colville. They were then both so young that they could scarcely have been a year out of school before they left home for the year they were spending in Europe; but to the young man's inexperience they seemed the wisest and maturest of society women. His heart quaked in his breast when he saw them talking and laughing together, for fear they should be talking and laughing about him; he was even a little more afraid of Miss Ridgely than of her friend, who was dashing and effective, where Miss Ridgely was serene and elegant, according to his feeling at that time. (003-117—003-125)

### Dancing the Lancers (Chapter 9)

During an evening gathering in the present-day narrative, Colville steps back into his youth through the memory of a dance:

> He used to dance the Lancers, too, and very badly, seventeen years before. He had danced it with Lina Ridgely and the other one, Mrs. Milbury. (009-178)

### The Smile Across Twenty Years (Chapter 11)

When Effie Bowen smiles, Colville is transported back two decades to the young Lina Ridgely:

> The little girl smiled again, and her smile was so like the smile of Lina Ridgely, twenty years ago, that his next words were inevitably tinged with reminiscence. (011-150)

### Reading Poetry to Ladies (Chapter 12)

Colville's irritation at hearing that Mr. Morton has been reading Browning to Mrs. Bowen and Imogene dissolves into a wry recollection of his own youthful courtship rituals:

> his pique was lost in sarcastic recollection of the time when he too used to read poems to ladies. He had read that poem to Lina Ridgely and the other one. (012-189)
