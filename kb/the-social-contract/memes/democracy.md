# Democracy

A form of government in which sovereign authority is exercised by the whole people or a majority of citizens — praised by Rousseau as the most natural form yet judged impossible in its pure form, attainable only by "a people of gods."

## Definition

Rousseau defines democracy as the governmental form in which "the Sovereign may commit the charge of the government to the whole people or to the majority of the people, so that more citizens are magistrates than are mere private individuals. This form of government is called 'democracy'" (028-003). Crucially, democracy for Rousseau is a form of government — the executive — not of sovereignty. The sovereign power, the general will, remains always with the people regardless of governmental form.

This distinction is fundamental to Rousseau's system. As Cole observes, "even a completely democratic government is not at the same time the Sovereign" (P01-036) — a sharp departure from Hobbes, for whom sovereign and government were identical. By separating them, Rousseau prevents the collapse of the body politic that Hobbes feared would follow from governmental overthrow.

## Rousseau's Account

### The Impossibility of Pure Democracy

Rousseau's most striking claim about democracy is that it has never existed and never will: "If we take the term in the strict sense, there never has been a real democracy, and there never will be. It is against the natural order for the many to govern and the few to be governed. It is unimaginable that the people should remain continually assembled to devote their time to public affairs, and it is clear that they cannot set up commissions for that purpose without the form of administration being changed" (029-004). Pure democracy — the people governing themselves directly without delegation — is a contradiction in practice, since any standing administrative body transforms the government into aristocracy.

Rousseau reinforces this conclusion with an often-quoted provocation: "Election by lot would have few disadvantages in a real democracy, in which, as equality would everywhere exist in morals and talents as well as in principles and fortunes, it would become almost a matter of indifference who was chosen. But I have already said that a real democracy is only an ideal" (047-008). The conditional is deliberately unreal — were perfect equality achievable, democratic selection would be trivial. The fact that it is not reinforces democracy's practical impossibility.

### The Demanding Conditions

Even were pure democracy attainable, its preconditions are forbidding. Rousseau enumerates them: "neither should the State be so small, nor a people so simple and upright, that the execution of the laws follows immediately from the public will, as it does in a good democracy" (030-009). The state must be small enough for the people to assemble, its citizens virtuous enough to will the general good without faction, and its social conditions egalitarian enough that "thoroughgoing equality" in fortunes and status prevails. These conditions converge so rarely that Rousseau concludes: "Were there a people of gods, their government would be democratic. So perfect a government is not for men" (029-009).

### Scale and Suitability

In Rousseau's tripartite mapping of governmental form to state scale, democracy occupies the smallest extreme: "democracy is possible only in small States" (P01-039). The reasoning is structural: democratic government requires the people's direct participation, which is impossible beyond the scale of a city-state. This principle extends to fiscal capacity: "in a democracy, the people bears the least charge" of taxation, and "democracy [suits] States that are small and poor" (033-006).

The administrative logic follows from the distance between people and government: "the more numerous the public administration, the smaller becomes the relation between the prince and the subjects, and the nearer it comes to equality, so that in democracy the ratio is unity, or absolute equality" (031-007). Democracy maximizes equality between rulers and ruled — a structural virtue that becomes a practical liability at scale.

### Lot as Democratic Method

Rousseau identifies election by lot as uniquely appropriate to democracy: "the election of rulers is a function of government, and not of Sovereignty, [so] the lot is the method more natural to democracy, in which the administration is better in proportion as the number of its acts is small" (047-004). In a true democracy, "magistracy is not an advantage, but a burdensome charge which cannot justly be imposed on one individual rather than another. The law alone can lay the charge on him on whom the lot falls" (047-005). Lot preserves the universality of law by removing human discretion from the selection of magistrates — a mechanism consistent with the general will's indifference to particular persons.

### Degeneration into Ochlocracy

Like all governmental forms, democracy is subject to decay: "democracy degenerates into 'ochlocracy'" (035-009) — mob rule. Government in general "undergoes contraction when it passes from the many to the few, that is, from democracy to aristocracy, and from aristocracy to royalty. To do so is its natural propensity" (035-004). Democracy's gravitational pull toward aristocracy is, for Rousseau, an iron law of political bodies, reversible only by constitutional vigilance.

### The Democratic Moment of Sovereignty

Rousseau identifies one context in which democracy is not only possible but necessary: the founding of the body politic itself. When a people constitutes itself as sovereign, "there is revealed one of the astonishing properties of the body politic, by means of which it reconciles apparently contradictory operations; for this is accomplished by a sudden conversion of Sovereignty into democracy, so that, without sensible change, and merely by virtue of a new relation of all to all, the citizens become magistrates and pass from general to particular acts, from legislation to the execution of the law" (042-006). At the moment of constitution, all citizens act simultaneously as legislators and executors — a fleeting democratic unity that establishes the republic before settling into a more practicable governmental form.

## Historical Context

Rousseau wrote against the backdrop of the Genevan republic — a city-state with strong democratic elements in its General Council but governed in practice by an aristocratic Small Council. His insistence on democracy's impossibility is partly an argument against those who would conflate Geneva's mixed constitution with pure popular rule.

Cole's commentary contextualizes Rousseau's position within the broader democratic tradition: "The justification of democracy is not that it is always right" but that it expresses the community's will (P01-049). For Rousseau, democracy's value lies not in the infallibility of popular judgment — the general will can err — but in the alignment of executive power with sovereign authority. The closer government approaches to identity with the sovereign, the less friction between particular and general wills.
