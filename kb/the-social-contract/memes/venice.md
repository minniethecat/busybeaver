# Venice

Venice, the maritime republic Rousseau sometimes cites as an example of a well-constituted government — though his admiration is qualified and primarily directed at its institutional structure

## Historical Significance

Rousseau references Venice in his analysis of the forms of government, particularly as an example of aristocratic government. The Venetian Republic's complex institutional balance illustrates the principle that government must be suited to the size and character of the State.

## Rousseau's Treatment

### As Political Model

Rousseau uses Venice as a exemplar in his political theory — an illustration of how well-constituted political institutions can sustain civic virtue and the general will.

### Key References

Rousseau's references to Venice appear throughout The Social Contract and his other political writings, serving as concrete illustrations of his abstract principles of political right.
