# Roman Expansion

The expansion of Rome from a small city-state on the Tiber to the dominant power of the Mediterranean world is, for Rousseau, both an achievement and a warning. The Roman Republic's territorial growth demonstrated the extraordinary capacity of well-designed republican institutions — but also revealed, in Rousseau's analysis, the structural contradiction that ultimately undid the Republic: 'the larger the State grows, the weaker the government becomes' (027-005).

## Rousseau's Analysis

### The Engine of Greatness

Rome's expansion was not accidental but institutional. Through the mechanism of the [[popular-assemblies]], 'the Roman people was truly sovereign' (048-039), and this sovereignty generated the civic energy that drove Roman conquest. The [[magistrate|magistrates]] who led Roman armies abroad remained accountable to the assemblies at home — a constitutional discipline that, Rousseau argues, distinguished Roman imperialism from the arbitrary conquests of despotic empires like [[persia|Persia]].

### The Paradox of Growth

> "The larger the State, the more the government must be concentrated; and the more the government is concentrated, the less liberty remains." — Book III, Chapter I (027-005)

Rome's expansion created an insoluble tension. Republican institutions designed for a city-state proved inadequate for governing an empire. The very success of Roman arms eroded the civic equality upon which Roman liberty depended. The concentration of wealth from conquest, the growth of a landless urban populace, and the extension of military commands over distant provinces — all consequences of expansion — progressively undermined the constitutional order.

| Factor | Impact on the Republic |
|--------|----------------------|
| Conquest wealth | Created oligarchic inequality |
| Extended commands | Weakened magisterial accountability |
| Provincial administration | Required powers incompatible with republican norms |
| Urban mob | Displaced citizen-farmers, vulnerable to demagogues |

## Historical Significance

Rousseau uses Rome's trajectory to illustrate a central theorem of his political science: that scale and liberty exist in tension. 'Monarchy is suitable only for great nations' (028-009) — and Rome's transformation from a republic into a de facto monarchy under the emperors was not a moral failure but a structural inevitability. The institutions that served the Republic could not govern the Empire, and the [[decline-of-rome]] began not with barbarian invasions but with this internal contradiction.
