# State of Nature

The state of nature is Rousseau's conception of the human condition prior to the establishment of political society — a hypothetical condition without law, government, or stable moral relations. It is the starting point from which the social compact emerges and the baseline against which civil society is measured.

## Definition

Rousseau envisions the state of nature as a condition where individuals live in "primitive independence" with "no mutual relations stable enough to constitute either the state of peace or the state of war" (006-009). It is not Hobbes's war of all against all, nor Locke's state of inconvenience, but a condition where the obstacles to human preservation eventually exceed individual resources: "That primitive condition can then subsist no longer; and the human race would perish unless it changed its manner of existence" (008-002).

## Rousseau's Account

### Beyond Hobbes

Rousseau distinguishes his account from Hobbes in two crucial ways. First, he rejects the Hobbesian identification of the state of nature with a state of war — war, for Rousseau, is a social phenomenon, not a natural one (006-009). Second, he refuses to equate the dissolution of government with a return to anarchy. Unlike Hobbes, who saw revolution as a return to the state of nature, Rousseau holds that the Sovereign can change its government without dissolving the body politic itself (P01-036).

### The Necessity of Association

The state of nature becomes untenable when "the obstacles in the way of their preservation show their power of resistance to be greater than the resources at the disposal of each individual" (008-002). At this point, humans must either perish or unite: "as men cannot engender new forces, but only unite and direct existing ones, they have no other means of preserving themselves than the formation, by aggregation, of a sum of forces great enough to overcome the resistance" (008-003). The social compact is this act of aggregation.

### The Contrast with Civil Society

The contrast between the state of nature and civil society runs through all of Rousseau's work. His educational treatise *Émile* is a plea for "natural" education; his *Discourses* argue for the "naturalisation" of society; his novel *Julie, or the New Heloise* appeals for more "nature" in human relationships (P01-017). The Social Contract addresses the problem of how to create a form of association that preserves the advantages of civil life without sacrificing the freedom that characterized the natural condition.

### What Is Lost and Gained

By leaving the state of nature, humans lose their natural liberty and unlimited right to everything they can obtain. But they gain civil liberty, proprietary ownership, and moral freedom — "obedience to a law which we prescribe to ourselves is liberty" (008-012). The loss is of a primitive, precarious independence; the gain is the security, equality, and moral agency that only civil society can provide.

## Historical Context

Rousseau's state of nature draws on a century of social contract theory. Hobbes had made it the war of all against all; Locke had softened it into a state of peace governed by natural law, marred only by the inconvenience of each being judge in his own case. Rousseau's innovation is to see the state of nature not as a historical reality but as a methodological device — a thought experiment that reveals what human beings owe to society and what they owe to nature.
