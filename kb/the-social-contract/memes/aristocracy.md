# Aristocracy

A form of government in which sovereign authority is exercised by a restricted number of magistrates — positioned by Rousseau between democracy and monarchy as the form best suited to states of middling size and wealth.

## Definition

Rousseau defines aristocracy as a governmental form in which the sovereign "restrict[s] the government to a small number; so that there are more private citizens than magistrates; and this is named 'aristocracy'" (028-004). In Rousseau's tripartite classification of government forms, aristocracy occupies the middle ground between democracy (government by all or most) and monarchy (government by one), each suited to a different scale of state.

## Rousseau's Account

### The Three Forms of Aristocracy

Rousseau distinguishes three kinds of aristocracy: "natural, elective and hereditary. The first is only for simple peoples; the third is the worst of all governments; the second is the best, and is aristocracy properly so called" (030-005). Natural aristocracy — rule by elders — belongs to primitive societies where age alone confers authority. Hereditary aristocracy Rousseau condemns as the worst of all governments, since it transmits power through patrician families without regard to merit.

The evolutionary trajectory moves from natural to elective to hereditary: "in proportion as artificial inequality produced by institutions became predominant over natural inequality, riches or power were put before age, and aristocracy became elective. Finally, the transmission of the father's power along with his goods to his children, by creating patrician families, made government hereditary, and there came to be senators of twenty" (030-004).

### The Case for Elective Aristocracy

Rousseau's preference for elective aristocracy rests on a practical calculus. It preserves the distinction between sovereign and government — an advantage over democracy, where the same body both legislates and executes — while demanding fewer virtues than popular government. Yet it imposes its own moral conditions: "moderation on the side of the rich and contentment on that of the poor; for it seems that thoroughgoing equality would be out of place, as it was not found even at Sparta" (030-010).

In an elective aristocracy, "the prince chooses the prince, the government is preserved by itself, and voting is rightly ordered" (047-006). This self-perpetuating structure avoids the turbulence of popular election while maintaining accountability through the elective mechanism.

### Scale and Suitability

Rousseau correlates governmental form with the scale of the state. As Cole's introduction summarizes: "democracy is possible only in small States, aristocracy in those of medium extent, and monarchy in great States" (P01-039). Rousseau further links form to fiscal burden: "the more the distance between people and government increases, the more burdensome tribute becomes: thus, in a democracy, the people bears the least charge; in an aristocracy, a greater charge; and, in monarchy, the weight becomes heaviest. Monarchy therefore suits only wealthy nations; aristocracy, States of middling size and wealth; and democracy, States that are small and poor" (033-006).

### Degeneration

Like all governmental forms in Rousseau's theory, aristocracy is subject to decay. Rousseau identifies aristocracy's specific pathology: "aristocracy [degenerates] into 'oligarchy'" (035-009). This degeneration occurs when the few who govern cease to represent the general will and instead pursue their own particular interests. Government in general "undergoes contraction when it passes from the many to the few, that is, from democracy to aristocracy, and from aristocracy to royalty. To do so is its natural propensity" (035-004) — a directional tendency Rousseau regards as irreversible without constitutional vigilance.

### Venice: The False Aristocracy

Rousseau explicitly rejects the Venetian Republic as a model: "it is an error to take the government of Venice for a real aristocracy. If the people has no share in the government, the nobility is itself the people. A host of poor Barnabotes never gets near any magistracy, and its nobility consists merely in the empty title of Excellency, and in the right to sit in the Great Council" (047-007). Venice, in Rousseau's analysis, is not an aristocracy but an oligarchy disguised — a closed caste ruling over a disenfranchised populace.

## Historical Context

Rousseau's treatment of aristocracy draws on classical precedents while engaging contemporary eighteenth-century debates. The Roman Republic provided his principal historical laboratory: "the Comitia Centuriata [were most favorable] to aristocracy" (048-035), where "the division by centuries was so favourable to the aristocracy that it is hard to see at first how the senate ever failed to carry the day" — the first class alone containing ninety-eight of the hundred and ninety-three centuries (048-029).

Cole notes that Rousseau "said that aristocracy was the best form of government for a small State" (P01-060), situating Rousseau within the broader republican tradition that looked to Sparta and Rome as models of mixed constitution. Rousseau's innovation lies not in praising aristocracy as such but in specifying the institutional conditions — election rather than birth, moderation rather than opulence — under which rule by the few can serve the general will.

## Key Passages
