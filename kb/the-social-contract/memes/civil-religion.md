# Civil Religion

Civil religion is Rousseau's controversial doctrine that the State requires a civic creed — not a theological system, but a profession of civic faith that binds citizens' sentiments to their duties toward the community. It addresses the problem that every political society ultimately rests on moral and emotional foundations that law alone cannot supply.

## Definition

Rousseau defines civil religion as a "purely civil profession of faith" whose articles are determined by the Sovereign — not as religious dogmas, but as "sentiments of sociability without which it is impossible to be a good citizen or a faithful subject." The dogmas of civil religion "ought to be simple, few in number, stated with precision, without explanations or commentaries" (052-011).

## Rousseau's Account

### The Problem of Religion and Politics

Rousseau argues that "politics and religion have among us a common object" (019-013). Every political community has historically been founded on religious sentiments — the ancient city-states had their civic gods, and "every religion, therefore, being attached solely to the laws of the State that prescribed it" (052-007). The modern problem arises from Christianity's separation of the spiritual from the temporal, creating a divided loyalty that weakens the State.

### The Social Function of Religion

The positive dogmas of civil religion include "the existence of a mighty, intelligent and beneficent Divinity, possessed of foresight and providence, the life to come, the happiness of the just, the punishment of the wicked, the sanctity of the social contract and the laws." The single negative dogma is the prohibition of intolerance — "intolerance is something which belongs to the religions we have rejected" (052-014).

### Toleration and Its Limits

Rousseau insists on toleration for all religions that tolerate others, but draws a firm line: those who say "outside the Church there is no salvation" cannot be tolerated because they place religious authority above civil authority. "Wherever theological intolerance is admitted, it must inevitably have some civil effect," and "as soon as it has this effect, the Sovereign is no longer Sovereign even in the temporal sphere" (052-015).

### Civil Religion and Conscience

The Sovereign cannot compel anyone to believe the dogmas of civil religion, but it "can banish from the State whoever does not believe them — not for impiety, but for lack of sociability." Civil religion thus operates at the boundary between individual conscience and civic obligation. The citizen must profess the civic creed publicly; private belief remains beyond the State's reach.

## Historical Context

The civil religion chapter was among the most controversial in the Social Contract, condemned by religious authorities and secular liberals alike. It reflects Rousseau's Genevan background (where citizenship required profession of the Reformed faith) and his engagement with Machiavelli's praise of pagan civic religion. The doctrine influenced the French Revolution's Cult of the Supreme Being and continues to inform debates about civic education, national symbols, and the role of religion in public life.
