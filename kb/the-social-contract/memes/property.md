# Property

For Rousseau, the right of property is not natural but civil — it is created and guaranteed by the social compact. What was mere possession in the state of nature, secured only by individual force, becomes genuine property in civil society, "founded only on a positive title" and protected by the whole community (010-003).

## Definition

Rousseau distinguishes possession — "merely the effect of force or the right of the first occupier" — from property, "which can be founded only on a positive title" (010-003). The social compact transforms the former into the latter: by uniting their forces, individuals gain secure ownership of what they possess, guaranteed not by their own strength but by the collective power of the community.

## Rousseau's Account

### The Right of the First Occupier

Rousseau acknowledges a limited "right of the first occupier" but subjects it to strict conditions: the land must not already be inhabited, the occupant must take only what is needed for subsistence, and possession must be established by labour and cultivation, not by idle ceremony. Even this right, however, "is more real than the right of the strongest" only after the establishment of property in civil society.

### Property Under the Social Compact

By the social compact, individuals give their possessions to the community, and the community returns them as legitimate property. "Each man alienates, by the social compact, only such part of his powers, goods and liberty as it is important for the community to control; but it must also be granted that the Sovereign is sole judge of what is important" (016-004). What was precarious individual possession becomes secure civil property, guaranteed by the whole force of the community.

### Property and Equality

Property is closely linked to equality in Rousseau's system. Extreme inequality of property undermines the social compact by creating relations of personal dependence: the rich can buy the poor, and the poor must sell themselves. The legislator must therefore moderate property, ensuring "no citizen shall ever be wealthy enough to buy another, and none poor enough to be forced to sell himself" (023-003). Property must serve liberty, not undermine it.

### The Sovereign's Limits

"The Sovereign never has a right to lay more charges on one subject than on another" (016-010). While the Sovereign determines what property is needed for the community, it must do so through general laws that apply equally. Confiscation of particular property by a particular act is not an exercise of sovereignty but an abuse of power — the Sovereign cannot exceed "the limits of general conventions" (016-010).

## Historical Context

Rousseau's theory of property occupies a middle position between Locke's labour-based natural right to unlimited accumulation and the radical egalitarianism that would call for the abolition of private property. Property is legitimate only insofar as it is established and regulated by the general will — a position that influenced subsequent socialist and social-democratic theories of property as a social institution rather than a natural right.
