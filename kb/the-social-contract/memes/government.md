# Government

Rousseau draws a sharp distinction between sovereignty and government. Sovereignty is the legislative power belonging to the people alone; government is the executive power — an intermediate body charged with executing the laws and maintaining civil and political liberty. Government is not a contract but a commission, revocable at the will of the Sovereign.

## Definition

Government is "an intermediate body set up between the subjects and the Sovereign, to secure their mutual correspondence, charged with the execution of the laws and the maintenance of liberty, both civil and political." It is not sovereign — it receives its authority from the Sovereign and can be modified or revoked at any time. "The power indeed may be transmitted, but not the will" (013-003).

## Rousseau's Account

### Government as Agent, Not Principal

The members of government are "called magistrates or kings, that is to say governors," and the entire body "bears the name of prince" — terminology that emphasizes government's subordinate, commissioned status. Government receives orders from the general will and transmits them to the subjects. It has no legislative authority of its own; what appears to be a governmental decree is, properly understood, "an act, not of sovereignty, but of magistracy" (018-009).

### Forms of Government

Rousseau analyzes three classical forms: democracy (the whole people or most of the people as magistrates), aristocracy (a small number as magistrates), and monarchy (a single magistrate). Each has its advantages and defects depending on the size and character of the State. Democracy, while seemingly the most consistent with popular sovereignty, "is so perfect a government that it is not suited to men" — it requires conditions so demanding that "a government so perfect is not for men."

### The Tendency to Usurpation

Every government has a natural tendency to usurp the Sovereign's authority. "As the particular will acts constantly against the general will, so the government exerts a constant effort against the Sovereignty." The smaller and more concentrated the government, the stronger this tendency. The history of all States is the history of government gradually encroaching on sovereignty until the social compact is dissolved and the State perishes.

### Maintaining the Social Compact

To prevent governmental usurpation, the Sovereign must assemble periodically to reaffirm its authority. These assemblies must open with two questions: "Does it please the Sovereign to preserve the present form of government?" and "Does it please the people to leave its administration to those who are actually charged with it?" This regular renewal of the commission keeps government subordinate to the general will.

## Historical Context

Rousseau's theory of government responds to the absolutist identification of sovereignty with government in Hobbes and the parliamentary sovereignty tradition. By separating the legislative will (sovereignty) from the executive power (government), Rousseau provides a theoretical basis for resisting governmental tyranny without dissolving the body politic — a position that deeply influenced the French Revolution and subsequent republican thought.
