# Jean-Jacques Rousseau

Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712–1778) was a Genevan philosopher, writer, and composer whose political philosophy, particularly *The Social Contract* (1762), shaped the intellectual foundations of modern democracy, republicanism, and the theory of popular sovereignty.

## Life

### Early Years and Background

Born in Geneva in 1712 to a watchmaker father, Rousseau was raised in the Calvinist republic and always identified as "Citizen of Geneva." His mother died shortly after his birth. At sixteen, he left Geneva and eventually settled in France, where he became associated with the *philosophes* of the Enlightenment — though he was often at odds with them.

### Major Works

Rousseau's major writings span political philosophy, education, and literature. The *Discourse on the Sciences and Arts* (1750) and the *Discourse on Inequality* (1755) established his reputation as a critic of civilization. *The Social Contract* and *Émile* (both 1762) were his mature works, the former on political right and the latter on education. Both were condemned — in Geneva and Paris — and Rousseau spent his later years in exile.

## Thought and Influence

Rousseau's thought centers on the tension between nature and society. He argued that humans are naturally good but corrupted by social institutions — a thesis that set him against the Hobbesian view of natural human wickedness. His solution was not a return to nature but the construction of legitimate political institutions through the social compact, by which individuals unite under the general will and "obey themselves alone."

## Relationship to The Social Contract

### Rousseau's Central Argument

Rousseau frames *The Social Contract* as an inquiry into the principles of political right: "Man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains." The work develops the concepts of the general will, popular sovereignty, and the social compact — establishing that legitimate political authority can only derive from the agreement of the governed (008-011).

### Philosophical Context

Rousseau engages with the entire social contract tradition, responding to Hobbes's absolutism, Locke's constitutionalism, and Grotius's natural law theory. He draws on Montesquieu's analysis of law and the classical republican tradition while transforming both into a radical doctrine of popular sovereignty.

## Key Passages

> "Man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains. One thinks himself the master of others, and still remains a greater slave than they." — Book I, Chapter I (005-001)

> "Each of us puts his person and all his power in common under the supreme direction of the general will." — Book I, Chapter VI (008-011)
