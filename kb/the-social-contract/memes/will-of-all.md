# Will of All

The will of all (*volonté de tous*) is Rousseau's term for the mere aggregate of individual private wills — the sum of particular interests, each pursuing its own advantage. It is crucially distinct from the general will, which is directed to the common interest and cannot be reduced to the arithmetic of individual preferences.

## Definition

"There is often a great deal of difference between the will of all and the general will; the latter considers only the common interest, while the former takes private interest into account, and is no more than a sum of particular wills" (015-003). The will of all is what emerges from voting when each citizen asks "what is good for me?" — the general will emerges when each asks "what is good for all?"

## Rousseau's Account

### The Arithmetic of the General Will

Rousseau offers a striking mathematical metaphor: "take away from these same wills the pluses and minuses that cancel one another, and the general will remains as the sum of the differences" (015-003). The will of all contains the general will within it — but the general will is not found by adding preferences together; it is found by removing the opposing private interests that cancel each other out.

### Voting and the General Will

When citizens vote, they do not express the general will directly — they express their opinion about what the general will is. "When, therefore, the opinion contrary to mine prevails, this proves only that I was mistaken, and that what I took to be the general will was not so" (P01-049). The majority does not create the general will; it discovers it.

### Factions and the Corruption of the Will

The formation of factions or partial associations within the State corrupts the process by which the general will emerges. When partial associations arise, "each association's will becomes general in relation to its members and particular in relation to the State." The result is that "there are no longer as many votes as there are men, but only as many as there are associations" — the differences no longer cancel, and the general will is obscured.

## Historical Context

The distinction between the general will and the will of all is Rousseau's most important contribution to democratic theory. It addresses a fundamental problem: how can the outcome of majority voting be legitimate if individuals have conflicting private interests? Rousseau's answer — that the general will is not the sum but the residue of particular wills once opposing interests cancel — influenced Condorcet's jury theorem, Madison's theory of factions in Federalist No. 10, and contemporary deliberative democracy theory.
