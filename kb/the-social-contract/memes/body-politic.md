# Body Politic

By the social compact, 'at once, in place of the individual personality of each contracting party, this act of association creates a moral and collective body' (008-012). The body politic is not a mere aggregate of individuals but a new entity — a public person with its own will (the general will), its own life (the union of its members), and its own interests.

## Rousseau's Account

### Core Doctrine

> "At once, in place of the individual personality of each contracting party, this act of association creates a moral and collective body, composed of as many members as the assembly contains votes, and receiving from this act its unity, its common identity, its life and its will." — Book I, Chapter VI (008-012)

> "If the State is a moral person whose life is in the union of its members, and if the most important of its cares is the care for its own preservation, it must have a universal and compelling force." — Book II, Chapter IV (016-002)

### Significance in Rousseau's System

This concept reflects Rousseau's broader concern with maintaining the integrity of the body politic against the forces that tend to corrupt or dissolve it. It is not an incidental detail of his political theory but a structural element of his constitutional design — part of the institutional architecture that sustains legitimate political order.

## Historical Context

Rousseau draws on classical precedents — the Roman censorship, the Roman tribunate, the medieval concept of the body politic as a *corpus mysticum* — while adapting these institutions to the framework of the social compact. His treatment influenced subsequent constitutional theory, particularly the development of institutions designed to preserve constitutional order.
