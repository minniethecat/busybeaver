# Popular Assemblies

The Roman popular assemblies (*comitia*) are the institutional centerpiece of Rousseau's argument for direct popular sovereignty. Book IV, Chapter IV of [[the-social-contract]] is devoted entirely to the Roman *comitia* — 'no State has ever existed in which the people had so great a share in the government' (048-039). The assemblies exemplify how a large republic could, through careful institutional design, preserve the principle of direct citizen participation.

## Structure and Function

### The Three Assemblies

Rousseau describes three distinct assemblies corresponding to Rome's threefold division of the people:

| Assembly | Basis of Division | Primary Function | PN |
|----------|------------------|-----------------|-----|
| *Comitia Curiata* | Curiae (clans) | Earliest form, confirmation of magistrates | (048-022) |
| *Comitia Centuriata* | Centuries (wealth classes) | Election of senior magistrates, war and peace | (048-028) |
| *Comitia Tributa* | Tribes (geography) | Election of minor magistrates, legislation | (048-039) |

### The Centuriate Innovation

> "Servius Tullius first divided the Roman people into centuries and classes, and gave to the rich, who were less numerous, a greater share of the votes than to the poor." — Book IV, Chapter IV (048-022)

The centuriate assembly distributed voting power by property — a design Rousseau acknowledges as strategically brilliant but normatively ambiguous. The wealthy controlled a majority of the 193 centuries despite being a minority of the population, ensuring that 'the rich had the greater share in the government' (048-028).

## Rousseau's Assessment

Rousseau praises the assemblies as evidence that 'the Roman people was truly sovereign' (048-039). Despite the aristocratic bias of the centuriate organization, the assemblies collectively ensured that no magistrate could govern without popular consent. The [[roman-senate]] proposed, the assemblies disposed, and the [[magistrate|magistrates]] executed — a separation that preserved the sovereignty of the people against both aristocratic and executive encroachment.

> "In Rome, the comitia of the tribes... never ceased to elect magistrates, to pass laws, to judge cases, and to carry on almost all public business with as much ease as the senate itself." — Book IV, Chapter IV (048-039)

## Key Passages

> "No State has ever existed in which the people had so great a share in the government." — Book IV, Chapter IV (048-039)

> "The comitia of the tribes were the most favourable to popular government, and the comitia of the centuries to aristocracy." — Book IV, Chapter IV (048-029)
