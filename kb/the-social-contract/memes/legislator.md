# Legislator

The Legislator occupies a singular position in Rousseau's constitutional theory: neither sovereign nor magistrate, 'the Legislator occupies in every respect an extraordinary position in the State' (019-005). While the [[general-will|general will]] is always right, the people do not always see what is right — 'this makes a Legislator necessary' (018-011). The Legislator gives the people their fundamental constitution, but holds no governmental power.

## Rousseau's Account

### The Legislator's Unique Office

The Legislator's function is to transform human nature itself — to 'change human nature, so to speak' — by converting each individual from a self-sufficient whole into a part of a greater whole. This is a task beyond ordinary political capacity. 'To discover the rules of society best suited to nations, a superior intelligence beholding all the passions of men without experiencing any of them would be needed' (018-011).

### The Difficulty of Persuasion

> "The legislator, being unable to appeal to either force or reason, must have recourse to an authority of a different order, capable of constraining without violence and persuading without convincing." (019-006)

The Legislator must persuade the people to adopt laws they cannot yet fully understand. This requires what Rousseau calls 'the language of the gods' — the appeal to divine authority that founders of nations have historically employed: 'This is what has, in all ages, compelled the fathers of nations to have recourse to divine intervention and to credit the gods with their own wisdom' (019-007).

## Historical Context

Rousseau cites historical legislators as exemplars: Lycurgus of Sparta, who 'gave his countrymen a constitution that still commands admiration'; the Decemvirs of Rome; and Calvin in Geneva, whom Rousseau calls 'the legislator of our republic.' Each faced the challenge of founding institutions for a people not yet formed by those institutions. The Legislator thus represents a paradox at the heart of Rousseau's theory: good laws can only come from a people already formed by good laws — the Legislator resolves this circularity by acting as an external founder who 'brings the laws' and then departs.

## Key Passages

> "The Legislator occupies in every respect an extraordinary position in the State. If he should do so by reason of his genius, he does so no less by reason of his office, which is neither magistracy nor sovereignty." — Book II, Chapter VII (019-005)

> "This makes a Legislator necessary." — Book II, Chapter VI (018-011)

> "The legislator, being unable to appeal to either force or reason, must have recourse to an authority of a different order, capable of constraining without violence and persuading without convincing." — Book II, Chapter VII (019-006)

> "He who has command over men ought not to have command over laws, and he who has command over laws ought not any more to have it over men." — Book II, Chapter VII (019-008)
