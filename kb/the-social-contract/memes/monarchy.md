# Monarchy

A form of government in which the whole executive power is vested in a single natural person — the most powerful and efficient governmental form in Rousseau's classification, yet the one most prone to abuse and least suited to serving the general will.

## Definition

Rousseau defines monarchy as the governmental form in which the sovereign "concentrate[s] the whole government in the hands of a single magistrate from whom all others hold their power. This third form is the most usual, and is called 'monarchy,' or royal government" (028-005). The monarch is defined not by hereditary title but by functional concentration: "So far, we have considered the prince as a moral and collective person, unified by the force of the laws, and the depositary in the State of the executive power. We have now to consider this power when it is gathered together into the hands of a natural person, a real man, who alone has the right to dispose of it in accordance with the laws. Such a person is called a monarch or king" (031-002).

## Rousseau's Account

### Scale and Suitability

In Rousseau's tripartite mapping, monarchy occupies the largest scale: "democratic government suits small States, aristocratic government those of middle size, and monarchy great ones" (028-009). The reasoning is structural — concentrating executive power in a single person maximizes the ratio between prince and subjects, creating the greatest governmental force: "as the government is restricted in numbers the ratio increases and reaches its maximum when the government is in the hands of a single person. There is then too great a distance between prince and people, and the State lacks a bond of union. To form such a bond, there must be intermediate orders" (031-007). Monarchy requires a nobility, a complex social hierarchy to bridge the gulf between the single ruler and the mass of subjects.

Fiscal capacity follows the same gradient: "in a democracy, the people bears the least charge; in an aristocracy, a greater charge; and, in monarchy, the weight becomes heaviest. Monarchy therefore suits only wealthy nations" (033-006). The administrative overhead of monarchy — courts, nobility, royal establishments — makes it the most expensive form of government, viable only in rich states.

### The Inherent Inferiority of Monarchy

Rousseau's most damning assessment of monarchy is comparative: "An essential and inevitable defect, which will always rank monarchical below republican government, is that in a republic the public voice hardly ever raises to the highest positions men who are not enlightened and capable, and such as to fill them with honour; while in monarchies those who rise to the top are most often merely petty blunderers, petty swindlers, and petty intriguers, whose petty talents cause them to get into the highest positions at Court, but, as soon as they have got there, serve only to make their ineptitude public" (031-009). Republics select for competence through public election; monarchies select for courtly intrigue. The structural result is that monarchical administration is systematically staffed by the mediocre.

### The Problem of Succession

Rousseau identifies succession as monarchy's structural weakness. Every interregnum is a crisis; every hereditary transfer risks placing an incompetent or a child on the throne. Regency governments compound the problem by introducing collective rule into a system designed for unitary command. The very efficiency that makes monarchy attractive — unity of decision — becomes its liability when the throne passes to an unfit heir. Rousseau observes that "everything conspires to deprive of justice and reason a man brought up to command others" — the education of a prince is an education in arbitrariness.

### Degeneration into Tyranny

Monarchy's specific pathology is tyranny: "royalty degenerates into 'tyranny'" (035-009). The degeneration is more dangerous than aristocracy's slide into oligarchy or democracy's into ochlocracy because monarchy concentrates all executive force in a single will. When that will separates from the general will — when the king governs for himself rather than for the people — there is no internal check. Rousseau distinguishes the tyrant (who usurps royal authority without right) from the despot (who usurps sovereign authority itself), but both paths lead to the dissolution of the body politic.

### The Roman Caution

Rousseau draws on Roman history to illustrate monarchy's dangers. Romulus, in establishing the Roman constitution, deliberately checked the senate with the people and the people with the senate, "while maintaining his ascendancy over both alike. But, after the spirit of monarchy, he left all the same a greater advantage to the patricians" (048-026). Even Rome's founding monarch, structuring a republic, could not resist tilting the balance toward aristocratic power — an institutional gravity inherent in single-person rule.

## Historical Context

Rousseau wrote under the French monarchy — Cole reminds us that "Neither the French monarchy nor the Genevese aristocracy loved outspoken criticism, and Rousseau had always to be very careful what he said" (P01-006). His critique of monarchy is thus doubly constrained: politically dangerous to express openly, yet central to his republican argument. Rousseau's strategy is to attack monarchy not as illegitimate per se — a monarch can be a legitimate prince if his authority derives from law — but as structurally inferior to republican forms on grounds of competence, stability, and alignment with the general will.
