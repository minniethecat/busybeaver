# Roman Curiae

The curiae represent the oldest stratum of Roman political organization — a division rooted in the original three tribes (Ramnes, Tities, and Luceres) established, according to tradition, by Romulus. Rousseau treats the curiae in Book IV of [[the-social-contract]] as the historical foundation upon which the later [[popular-assemblies|centuriate]] and tribal assemblies were built.

## Structure and Function

### Origins and Composition

The thirty curiae (ten per original tribe) were the earliest voting divisions of the Roman people. Unlike the later centuries — which distributed power by wealth — the curiae were based on clan and kinship. Rousseau notes that 'in the earliest times, the assembly of the Roman people was the comitia of the curiae' (048-022), making it the institutional ancestor of all subsequent Roman popular assemblies.

### The Decline of Curiate Power

> "The comitia of the curiae, being the oldest, gradually lost their importance as the centuries and tribes were established." — Book IV, Chapter IV

As Rome evolved from a clan-based monarchy into a property-based republic, the curiae were progressively supplanted. The [[servius-tullius|Servian]] reforms replaced clan divisions with economic classes, and the *comitia curiata* was reduced to a ceremonial body that confirmed magistrates and ratified adoptions and wills — 'receiving its sanction, but no magistrate was elected save in the comitia of the centuries' (048-022).

### Table: Evolution of Roman Assemblies

| Period | Dominant Assembly | Basis | Key Feature |
|--------|------------------|-------|-------------|
| Kingdom | Curiata | Clan (curiae) | Kinship-based voting |
| Early Republic | Centuriata | Wealth (centuries) | Property-weighted voting |
| Late Republic | Tributa | Geography (tribes) | Most democratic form |

## Rousseau's Assessment

The curiae serve Rousseau's broader argument about constitutional evolution: institutions that suit a people at one stage of development become inadequate as the State grows. The transition from curiae to centuries to tribes illustrates his principle that 'the form of government must change as the State expands' (028-009). The curiae were appropriate for a small city of clans; they could not sustain the political life of a growing republic.
