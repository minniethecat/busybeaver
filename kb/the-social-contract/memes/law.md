# Law

For Rousseau, law is the expression of the general will and the foundation of legitimate political order. Laws are "acts of the general will" — they must come from all the people and apply to all the people, and only the general will can produce genuine legislation (P01-042).

## Definition

Rousseau defines law as an act of the general will that is general in both its source and its object. By the social compact, "we have given the body politic existence and life: we have now by legislation to give it movement and will" (018-002). The compact constitutes the political body; law animates and directs it toward the common good.

## Rousseau's Account

### Law as Expression of the General Will

Rousseau, "agreeing with Montesquieu in making law the 'condition of civil association,' goes beyond him only in tracing it more definitely to its origin in an act of will" (P01-042). Laws can proceed only from the body of citizens who have constituted the State — no decree issued by an individual or a magistrate can be a law, because particular wills cannot produce general acts.

### Generality of Law

The essential characteristic of law is its generality. It must be general in its origin (made by the whole people) and general in its object (applying equally to all). A law that names particular individuals or imposes unequal burdens is not a law but a decree — an act, not of sovereignty, but of magistracy (018-009). The generality of law is the structural guarantee of equality and liberty.

### Law, Sovereign, and Government

The commands of the rulers "cannot pass for general wills, so long as the Sovereign, being free to oppose them, offers no opposition" (013-005). Government executes the laws but does not make them; the legislative power belongs to the Sovereign alone. When the people are silent in the face of government action, "universal silence is taken to imply the consent of the people" (013-005).

### The Legislator

Because the people may not always see the good they will, Rousseau introduces the figure of the Legislator — an extraordinary individual who frames the laws but holds no governmental power. The Legislator "draws up the best rules for society but cannot enforce them" — his role is to persuade without authority and to frame without commanding, a "task beyond human force" that requires "an authority that is nothing."

## Historical Context

Rousseau's theory of law synthesizes the classical republican tradition (where law is the expression of the common good) with modern social contract theory. His distinction between law (general, legislative, sovereign) and decree (particular, executive, governmental) influenced the French Revolution's constitutional theory and Kant's philosophy of right.
