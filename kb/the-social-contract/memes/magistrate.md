# Magistrate

In Rousseau's political lexicon, the magistrate is not a legislator but an executor: 'the members of this body are called magistrates or *kings*, that is to say *governors*, and the whole body bears the name *prince*' (026-007). The magistrate occupies the intermediate space between the [[sovereignty|Sovereign]] and the subjects — administering the [[law]] without making it, and wielding force only in execution of the [[general-will|general will]].

## Rousseau's Account

### The Three Wills of the Magistrate

Rousseau identifies an inherent tension within every magistrate, who simultaneously possesses three distinct wills:

> "In the person of the magistrate we can distinguish three essentially different wills: first, the individual will of the man, which tends only to his personal advantage; secondly, the common will of the magistrates, which is concerned with the advantage of the prince alone — a will which may be called corporate; and thirdly, the will of the people or the sovereign will, which is general both in relation to the State regarded as the whole, and to the government regarded as a part of the whole." (027-006)

This triple will creates a structural tension: 'in a perfect act of legislation, the individual will should be at zero; the corporate will belonging to the government should be quite subordinate; and consequently the general or sovereign will always dominant, and the rule of all the others' (027-008). The magistrate must subordinate personal and corporate interests to the general will — a demand that Rousseau acknowledges is rarely perfectly met.

### Number and Strength

The number of magistrates is inversely proportional to the strength of government. 'The more numerous the magistrates, therefore, the weaker the government' (027-005) — because the corporate will dissipates as it is distributed across more individuals. Rousseau draws a direct arithmetical relationship: 'the ratio of the number of the magistrates to that of the government should be the inverse of the ratio of the number of subjects to that of the sovereign' (028-009).

### Forms Distinguished by Magistrate Count

The number of supreme magistrates determines the form of government:

| Form | Magistrate Count | Principle |
|------|-----------------|-----------|
| Democracy | All or most citizens are magistrates | 'more citizens are magistrates than are mere private individuals' (028-003) |
| Aristocracy | Fewer magistrates than private citizens | 'more private citizens than magistrates' (028-004) |
| Monarchy | A single magistrate | 'government in the hands of a single magistrate from whom all others hold their power' (028-005) |

## Historical Context

Rousseau's theory of magistracy draws on the Roman constitutional tradition, particularly the Roman republic's elaborate system of elected magistrates — consuls, praetors, censors, and tribunes — who exercised executive authority while remaining accountable to the popular assemblies. Rousseau observed that 'in Rome, the comitia of the tribes... never ceased to elect magistrates, to pass laws, to judge cases, and to carry on almost all public business with as much ease as the senate itself' (048-039). This historical model demonstrated that magistrates could be numerous without paralyzing the State — but only under specific constitutional conditions.

The magistrate occupies a conceptually fragile position in Rousseau's system: 'if the Sovereign desires to govern, or the magistrate to give laws, or if the subjects refuse to obey, disorder takes the place of regularity, force and will no longer act together, and the State is dissolved and falls into despotism or anarchy' (026-010). This tripartite boundary — sovereign legislates, magistrate executes, subjects obey — is the constitutional architecture that sustains legitimate government.
