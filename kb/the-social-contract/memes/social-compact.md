# Social Compact

The social compact (*contrat social*) is the foundational act by which individuals, previously living in the state of nature, unite to form a political community. It is the agreement that transforms a multitude of isolated persons into a moral and collective body — the Sovereign — and creates the only legitimate basis for political authority.

## Definition

Rousseau frames the problem the social compact must solve: "to find a form of association which will defend and protect with the whole common force the person and goods of each associate, and in which each, while uniting himself with all, may still obey himself alone, and remain as free as before" (P01-053). The compact is an agreement of each with all — not a contract between ruler and ruled, but a pact among equals that constitutes the people as a political body.

## Rousseau's Account

### The Terms of the Compact

The clauses of the social compact "are everywhere the same and everywhere tacitly admitted and recognised, until, on the violation of the social compact, each regains his original rights and resumes his natural liberty" (008-006). Rousseau insists that these terms are determined by the nature of the act itself — "the slightest modification would make them vain and ineffective" (008-006). Stripped to its essence, the compact reduces to "each of us puts his person and all his power in common under the supreme direction of the general will" (008-011).

### What Is Alienated

By the compact, "each man alienates only such part of his powers, goods and liberty as it is important for the community to control; but it must also be granted that the Sovereign is sole judge of what is important" (016-004). This is a crucial limitation — the compact does not demand total surrender of individual autonomy, only what is necessary for the common good. The Sovereign, constituted by the compact itself, determines the boundary.

### Equality and the Compact

A fundamental consequence of the social compact is equality among citizens: "the social compact sets up among the citizens an equality of such a kind, that they all bind themselves to observe the same conditions and should therefore all enjoy the same rights" (016-009). Every authentic act of sovereignty — every act of the general will — binds or favours all citizens equally.

### Force and Freedom

The compact includes an enforcement mechanism: "whoever refuses to obey the general will shall be compelled to do so by the whole body. This means nothing less than that he will be forced to be free" (009-009). This compulsion is the condition of civil liberty — by enforcing the compact's terms, the community secures each member against personal dependence and arbitrary rule.

### From Compact to Legislation

The social compact gives the body politic "existence and life," but legislation is needed to give it "movement and will" (018-002). The compact is the constitutive act; law is the ongoing expression of the general will that the compact empowers.

## Historical Context

Rousseau's social compact theory draws on and transforms the contract traditions of Hobbes and Locke. Unlike Hobbes, Rousseau does not see the compact as a submission to an absolute sovereign; unlike Locke, he rejects the idea that the compact creates a trust that can be revoked by the people acting against government. For Rousseau, the compact creates the sovereign people itself — an inalienable, indivisible collective body that cannot delegate its legislative authority.
