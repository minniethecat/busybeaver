# England

England occupies a distinctive position in [[the-social-contract]]: it is the modern nation whose political system Rousseau most pointedly critiques. 'The people of England regards itself as free; but it is grossly mistaken; it is free only during the election of members of parliament. As soon as they are elected, slavery overtakes it, and it is nothing' (035-007). This judgment encapsulates Rousseau's radical rejection of representative government.

## Significance in Rousseau's Argument

### The Critique of Representation

England serves as Rousseau's primary example of the illusion of freedom under representative institutions. In Rousseau's analysis, sovereignty cannot be represented — 'the moment a people allows itself to be represented, it is no longer free: it no longer exists' (035-007). The English parliamentary system, which contemporaries celebrated as the pinnacle of liberty, is for Rousseau a mechanism of political alienation.

### The English People as a Cautionary Tale

> "The people of England regards itself as free; but it is grossly mistaken; it is free only during the election of members of parliament." — Book III, Chapter XV (035-007)

The English case demonstrates Rousseau's core thesis about sovereignty: legitimate political authority can only arise from the direct, unmediated expression of the [[general-will]]. Any system that delegates this expression dissolves the political bond.

## Comparative Context

England is contrasted with the classical republics — [[sparta]], [[rome]], and [[geneva]] — where Rousseau believed citizens participated directly in sovereignty. Unlike [[france]], which Rousseau treats as monarchical absolutism, England represents a subtler danger: a system that preserves the forms of freedom while emptying them of substance.
