# French Absolutism

The consolidation of absolute monarchy in France under Louis XIV, representing for Rousseau the complete corruption of legitimate political order — where the will of one replaces the general will

## Historical Account

French Absolutism represents a pivotal moment in the political development of early modern Europe — part of the historical context against which Rousseau developed his critique of existing political institutions.

## Rousseau's Use

### As Political Warning

Rousseau treats French Absolutism as a cautionary example — a historical case that illustrates the structural tendencies that threaten every political community: the usurpation of sovereignty by government, the replacement of the general will by particular interests, and the dissolution of the social compact.

### Theoretical Significance

By analyzing French Absolutism, Rousseau develops his broader argument that political institutions are not arbitrary but must conform to principles of political right — principles that can be discovered through the study of both successful and failed political communities.
