# John Locke

John Locke (1632–1704), English philosopher whose Two Treatises of Government (1689) articulated a social contract theory based on natural rights — an influence on Rousseau that Rousseau also sharply criticizes

## Life

### Early Years and Background

John Locke was born in 1632, into a period of profound political and intellectual transformation in Europe. Their early education and intellectual formation laid the groundwork for their later contributions to political philosophy.

### Major Works and Career

John Locke's major writings established them as one of the foundational thinkers of the social contract tradition. Their work addressed the fundamental questions of political philosophy: the origin of legitimate authority, the nature of law, and the basis of political obligation.

## Thought and Influence

John Locke's political thought centers on the relationship between individual liberty and political authority. Their work developed a distinctive account of how legitimate political institutions can be constructed — an account that Rousseau both draws upon and transforms in The Social Contract.

## Relationship to The Social Contract

### Rousseau's Engagement

Rousseau acknowledges Locke's social contract framework but rejects key Lockean doctrines. Where Locke grounds political right in prepolitical natural rights, Rousseau derives all rights from the social compact itself. Where Locke accepts representative government, Rousseau insists that sovereignty cannot be represented.

### Points of Divergence

Where Rousseau departs from John Locke, he does so not by rejecting the social contract framework but by radicalizing it — pushing the logic of consent, generality, and popular sovereignty further than his predecessor was willing to go.

## Key Passages

> Rousseau's engagement with John Locke appears throughout The Social Contract, most notably in his discussion of the foundations of political right and the nature of legitimate sovereignty.
