# Foundation of the Roman Republic

The establishment of the Roman Republic in 509 BCE — the expulsion of the Tarquin kings and the creation of republican institutions — serves Rousseau as an exemplar of legitimate constitutional founding. Rome's transition from monarchy to republic was not merely a change of rulers but a transformation of the political order: the Roman people became [[sovereignty|sovereign]], and the [[magistrate|magistrates]] became their servants rather than their masters.

## Rousseau's Analysis

### The Expulsion as Founding Act

The Roman Republic was born of an act of collective rejection: the Roman people expelled the Tarquins for the crime of tyranny, and in doing so constituted themselves as a sovereign body. Rousseau treats this event as a real-world approximation of the [[social-compact]] — a moment in which a people, through a shared act of will, dissolved an existing political order and established a new one.

### The Constitutional Architecture

> "The Roman people was truly sovereign." — Book IV, Chapter IV (048-039)

The Republic's founding established the institutional separation that Rousseau considers essential to legitimate government: the [[popular-assemblies]] exercised legislative sovereignty; the [[roman-senate|Senate]] deliberated and proposed; the magistrates executed. No single body held all three powers, and the [[general-will]] — expressed through the assemblies — remained the ultimate source of political authority.

### Table: Republican Institutions at Founding

| Institution | Function | Sovereign/Legislative/Executive |
|------------|----------|-------------------------------|
| Popular assemblies | Law-making, elections, judgments | Sovereign + Legislative |
| Senate | Deliberation, foreign policy, finance | Advisory |
| Consuls | Military command, administration | Executive |
| Tribunes | Protection of plebeians | Veto power |

## The Paradox of Republican Foundation

The founding of the Republic illustrates a paradox at the heart of Rousseau's political theory: the people who are to be formed by good institutions must already be formed to establish those institutions. 'For a young people to be able to relish sound principles of political theory and follow the fundamental rules of statecraft, the effect would have to become the cause' (018-011). The Roman Republic's founding required a people already capable of republican citizenship — a capacity that Rousseau suggests was forged through the very act of founding itself.

> "For a young people to be able to relish sound principles of political theory and follow the fundamental rules of statecraft, the effect would have to become the cause." — Book II, Chapter VII (018-011)
