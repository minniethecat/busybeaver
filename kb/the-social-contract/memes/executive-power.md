# Executive Power

The physical or operative dimension of sovereign authority in Rousseau's political theory — the "brain" that translates the general will (legislation) into particular acts of administration, vested in the government rather than the sovereign people.

## Definition

Rousseau distinguishes executive from legislative power through the analogy of human action: "Every free action is produced by the concurrence of two causes; one moral, i.e. the will which determines the act; the other physical, i.e. the power which executes it. When I walk towards an object, it is necessary first that I should will to go there, and, in the second place, that my feet should carry me. If a paralytic wills to run and an active man wills not to, they will both stay where they are. The body politic has the same motive powers; here too force and will are distinguished, will under the name of legislative power and force under that of executive power. Without their concurrence, nothing is, or should be, done" (026-003).

From this distinction flows Rousseau's formal definition: "I call then 'government,' or supreme administration, the legitimate exercise of the executive power, and prince or magistrate the man or the body entrusted with that administration" (026-008).

## Rousseau's Account

### Why the Sovereign Cannot Execute

The most fundamental constraint on executive power in Rousseau's system is that it cannot belong to the sovereign: "the executive power cannot belong to the generality as legislature or Sovereign, because it consists wholly of particular acts which fall outside the competency of the law, and consequently of the Sovereign, whose acts must always be laws" (026-004). The sovereign wills the general; the government executes the particular. To merge them — to have the same body both legislate and execute — would confound right and fact, law and application, creating a body politic "thus disfigured" that "would soon fall a prey to the violence it was instituted to prevent" (041-002).

Rousseau criticizes earlier theorists who attempted to divide sovereignty by function rather than by nature: "our political theorists, unable to divide Sovereignty in principle, divide it according to its object: into force and will; into legislative power and executive power; into rights of taxation, justice and war" (014-003). This is the error of treating sovereignty as divisible when it is the executive that must be separated.

### The Organic Metaphor

Rousseau deploys a striking organic metaphor to capture the relationship: "The legislative power is the heart of the State; the executive power is its brain, which causes the movement of all the parts. The brain may become paralysed and the individual still live. A man may remain an imbecile and live; but as soon as the heart ceases to perform its functions, the animal is dead" (036-004). The legislative power — the general will — is the life-principle; the executive is the instrument of motion. A state can survive a paralyzed executive (through governmental reform), but not the extinction of sovereign authority.

### The Monarch as Executive

When executive power is concentrated in a single person, that person is the monarch: "So far, we have considered the prince as a moral and collective person, unified by the force of the laws, and the depositary in the State of the executive power. We have now to consider this power when it is gathered together into the hands of a natural person, a real man, who alone has the right to dispose of it in accordance with the laws. Such a person is called a monarch or king" (031-002). The monarch is defined functionally — not by birth or title but by being the sole depositary of executive authority.

### The Necessity of Gradation

No government is truly simple: "Strictly speaking, there is no such thing as a simple government. An isolated ruler must have subordinate magistrates; a popular government must have a head. There is therefore, in the distribution of the executive power, always a gradation from the greater to the lesser number, with the difference that sometimes the greater number is dependent on the smaller, and sometimes the smaller on the greater" (032-002). Executive power is always structured as a hierarchy, even in democracies.

This gradation serves a structural purpose. When "the executive power is not sufficiently dependent upon the legislative power, i.e. when the prince is more closely related to the Sovereign than the people to the prince, this lack of proportion must be cured by the division of the government; for all the parts have then no less authority over the subjects, while their division makes them all together less strong against the Sovereign" (032-005). Dividing executive power among multiple magistrates or bodies prevents any single executive organ from dominating the sovereign — a principle that anticipates later doctrines of separated powers.

### Suspension Before the Sovereign

A crucial constitutional rule governs the relationship: "The moment the people is legitimately assembled as a sovereign body, the jurisdiction of the government wholly lapses, the executive power is suspended, and the person of the meanest citizen is as sacred and inviolable as that of the first magistrate; for in the presence of the person represented, representatives no longer exist" (039-002). When the sovereign convenes, the executive vanishes — all citizens return to their status as equal members of the sovereign body, and government officials hold no authority.

### Representation: Legislative vs. Executive

Rousseau's notorious rejection of representation applies only to the legislative power: "in the exercise of the legislative power, the people cannot be represented; but in that of the executive power, which is only the force that is applied to give the law effect, it both can and should be represented" (040-009). The general will cannot be delegated, but its execution can and must be — this is precisely what government is. The distinction resolves the apparent tension between Rousseau's anti-representative rhetoric and his acceptance of governmental institutions.

### Establishment Order

The executive must follow the legislative in the order of constitution: "The legislative power once well established, the next thing is to establish similarly the executive power; for this latter, which operates only by particular acts, not being of the essence of the former, is naturally separate from it" (041-002). A people must first constitute itself as sovereign — willing the general — before it can create the machinery to execute that will.

## Historical Context

Rousseau's executive-legislative distinction draws on the broader early modern Separation of Powers tradition, particularly Locke and Montesquieu, but with a crucial difference. For Montesquieu, legislative, executive, and judicial powers are co-equal branches checking each other. For Rousseau, the executive is strictly subordinate to the legislative — the will commands, the force obeys. This hierarchy reflects Rousseau's commitment to popular sovereignty: the executive is the servant of the general will, never its partner.
