# Natural Liberty

Natural liberty is the freedom that human beings possess in the state of nature — an unlimited right to everything they can obtain and hold by their own strength. It is what individuals surrender upon entering civil society through the social compact, receiving in exchange the more secure and meaningful civil liberty.

## Definition

"What man loses by the social compact is his natural liberty and an unlimited right to everything he tries to get and succeeds in getting; what he gains is civil liberty and the proprietorship of all he possesses" (010-003). Natural liberty "is bounded only by the strength of the individual" (010-003) — it is freedom without law, which is to say, freedom without security.

## Rousseau's Account

### The Limits of Natural Liberty

Natural liberty sounds absolute, but it is in fact precarious. In the state of nature, one's freedom extends only as far as one's power — the weak have no liberty against the strong. There is no law to appeal to, no community to call upon for protection. "The mere impulse of appetite is slavery," Rousseau writes, "while obedience to a law which we prescribe to ourselves is liberty" (008-012). Natural liberty, driven by appetite rather than reason, is a form of servitude to one's own desires.

### Exchange, Not Loss

The surrender of natural liberty is not a net loss but an exchange. What is given up is "an unlimited right to everything" — a right that in practice cannot be realized because others have the same unlimited right. What is gained is civil liberty, which is "limited by the general will" (010-003) but guaranteed by the whole force of the community. A limited but secure liberty is worth more than an unlimited but unenforceable one.

### Natural Liberty and the Right of Resistance

"When the social compact is violated, each regains his original rights and resumes his natural liberty" (008-006). Natural liberty serves as a permanent backdrop to civil liberty — the right individuals fall back upon when the compact is dissolved by governmental usurpation. This is not a right of rebellion against particular laws but a structural consequence of the compact's violation.

## Historical Context

Rousseau's distinction between natural and civil liberty draws on the natural law tradition while transforming it. For Hobbes, natural liberty is the right of self-preservation that never fully surrenders to the Sovereign. For Locke, natural liberty is governed by natural law and largely preserved in civil society. Rousseau's innovation is to treat natural liberty as something genuinely lost and replaced — civil liberty is not natural liberty plus security; it is a qualitatively different kind of freedom.
