# Slavery

A condition of absolute personal subjection that Rousseau attacks as the paradigmatic example of illegitimate authority — the "right of slavery" being, in his argument, not merely unjust but logically contradictory, since "the words slave and right contradict each other, and are mutually exclusive" (006-015).

## Definition

Rousseau does not define slavery as a political institution in positive terms; rather, he treats it as the limit case of illegitimate power — the reduction of a human being to the property of another through force. The concept appears in the Social Contract primarily as the target of Rousseau's critique of earlier natural-law theorists who sought to justify slavery through conquest, paternal authority, or natural inequality.

Rousseau's moral psychology identifies a broader sense of slavery: "the mere impulse of appetite is slavery, while obedience to a law which we prescribe to ourselves is liberty" (010-004). This definition links political slavery to moral autonomy — the unfree person is governed by external compulsion rather than self-imposed law.

## Rousseau's Account

### The Critique of Conventional Justifications

Rousseau opens his assault on slavery by targeting Grotius: "Grotius denies that all human power is established in favour of the governed, and quotes slavery as an example. His usual method of reasoning is constantly to establish right by fact. It would be possible to employ a more logical method, but none could be more favourable to tyrants" (004-005). The methodological critique is fundamental: deriving normative legitimacy from empirical fact — "slavery exists, therefore it is rightful" — is a category error that serves tyranny.

Rousseau then traces the intellectual genealogy of this error: "The reasoning of Caligula agrees with that of Hobbes and Grotius. Aristotle, before any of them, had said that men are by no means equal naturally, but that some are born for slavery, and others for dominion" (004-008). Against Aristotle's natural-slave doctrine, Rousseau offers a historicized reversal: "Aristotle was right; but he took the effect for the cause. Nothing can be more certain than that every man born in slavery is born for slavery. Slaves lose everything in their chains, even the desire of escaping from them: they love their servitude, as the comrades of Ulysses loved their brutish condition. If then there are slaves by nature, it is because there have been slaves against nature. Force made the first slaves, and their cowardice perpetuated the condition" (004-009).

### The Conquest Argument Refuted

A second conventional justification derives slavery from the supposed right of the conqueror to kill the vanquished: "The victor having, as they hold, the right of killing the vanquished, the latter can buy back his life at the price of his liberty; and this convention is the more legitimate because it is to the advantage of both parties" (006-008). Rousseau dismantles this argument through a series of logical moves.

First, the right of conquest itself reduces to the right of the strongest, which Rousseau has already shown creates no moral obligation. Second, the reasoning is circular: "The right of conquest has no foundation other than the right of the strongest. If war does not give the conqueror the right to massacre the conquered peoples, the right to enslave them cannot be based upon a right which does not exist. No one has a right to kill an enemy except when he cannot make him a slave, and the right to enslave him cannot therefore be derived from the right to kill him. It is accordingly an unfair exchange to make him buy at the price of his liberty his life, over which the victor holds no right. Is it not clear that there is a vicious circle in founding the right of life and death on the right of slavery, and the right of slavery on the right of life and death?" (006-013).

### The Nullity of Slave-Right

Rousseau's conclusion is categorical: "So, from whatever aspect we regard the question, the right of slavery is null and void, not only as being illegitimate, but also because it is absurd and meaningless. The words 'slave' and 'right' contradict each other, and are mutually exclusive. It will always be equally foolish for a man to say to a man or to a people: 'I make with you a convention wholly at your expense and wholly to my advantage; I shall keep it as long as I like, and you will keep it as long as I like'" (006-015). A "convention" in which one party bears all obligations and the other enjoys all rights is not a convention at all — it is a unilateral imposition misdescribed.

### The Paradox of Ancient Liberty

Rousseau confronts an uncomfortable historical fact: the celebrated ancient republics, especially Sparta, depended on chattel slavery. "Is liberty maintained only by the help of slavery? It may be so. Extremes meet. Everything that is not in the course of nature has its disadvantages, civil society most of all. There are some unhappy circumstances in which we can only keep our liberty at others' expense, and where the citizen can be perfectly free only when the slave is most a slave. Such was the case with Sparta" (040-011).

This is not an endorsement but a diagnosis. Ancient liberty was purchased through the unfreedom of a helot class — a bargain Rousseau finds morally repugnant and historically unnecessary for modern peoples. Yet he turns the observation against his contemporaries: "As for you, modern peoples, you have no slaves, but you are slaves yourselves; you pay for their liberty with your own. It is in vain that you boast of this preference; I find in it more cowardice than humanity" (040-011). Modern commercial societies, lacking the slave economy of antiquity, have substituted wage-dependence for chattel slavery — a subtler but equally real unfreedom.

### Representative Government as Political Slavery

Rousseau extends the concept of slavery to the political domain through his critique of representation. The English people "is free only during the election of members of parliament. As soon as they are elected, slavery overtakes it, and it is nothing" (040-006). Political slavery consists not in chains but in delegation of the sovereign will — the moment a people ceases to exercise sovereignty directly, it surrenders its liberty to representatives who govern in their name but not by their will.

## Historical Context

Rousseau's attack on slavery engages a live eighteenth-century debate. Grotius and Pufendorf had constructed natural-law arguments that accommodated slavery as consistent with right reason. Locke, despite his commitment to natural liberty, justified slavery for captives taken in a just war. Rousseau's innovation is to reject the entire framework: there is no "right of slavery" because the concept is internally contradictory. Cole notes that Rousseau's critics "tell you, man is not born free" because "every man born in slavery is born for slavery" — a claim Rousseau refutes by historicizing the appearance of naturalness (P01-016).
