# Equality

Equality is, alongside liberty, one of the two great objects of every system of legislation. For Rousseau, the social compact establishes a moral and legal equality among citizens that replaces the natural inequality of the state of nature with a framework in which all are bound by the same conditions and enjoy the same rights.

## Definition

Rousseau defines equality in civil society not as "the degrees of power and riches being absolutely identical for everybody," but as a condition where "power shall never be great enough for violence, and shall always be exercised by virtue of rank and law; and that, in respect of riches, no citizen shall ever be wealthy enough to buy another, and none poor enough to be forced to sell himself" (023-003). This is structural equality — equality of condition that makes liberty possible.

## Rousseau's Account

### The Compact's Equality

"From whatever side we approach our principle, we reach the same conclusion, that the social compact sets up among the citizens an equality of such a kind, that they all bind themselves to observe the same conditions and should therefore all enjoy the same rights" (016-009). Every authentic act of sovereignty binds or favours all citizens equally — this is the formal equality that the compact guarantees.

### Natural vs. Civil Inequality

In the *Discourse on Inequality*, Rousseau distinguishes natural inequality (differences of strength, intelligence, talent) from moral or political inequality (differences of wealth, status, power established by convention). The social compact does not eliminate natural inequality but renders it politically irrelevant by establishing the supremacy of law over personal advantage. Rousseau constructs "a purely ideal picture" of the state of nature to reveal the extent to which existing inequalities are social artifacts rather than natural necessities (P01-011).

### Equality and Liberty

Equality is the condition of liberty. Where great inequalities of wealth exist, the rich can buy the compliance of the poor, and the poor must sell their liberty to survive. Where power is concentrated, violence replaces law. "Power shall never be great enough for violence" — this is not merely a limit on government but a condition for the freedom of every citizen (023-003). Equality and liberty are therefore not competing values but mutually reinforcing requirements of a well-constituted State.

### The Legislator's Task

Because equality is an object of legislation, the legislator must design institutions that moderate wealth and power. "This implies, on the part of the great, moderation in goods and position, and, on the side of the common sort, moderation in avarice and covetousness" (023-003). The law cannot mandate equal outcomes, but it can establish the conditions under which inequality does not become domination.

## Historical Context

Rousseau's treatment of equality was among his most radical contributions. While Locke defended unequal property as the natural result of industry, and Hobbes saw inequality as inevitable under any sovereign, Rousseau made equality a condition of legitimacy — a State that permits some citizens to buy others is not a genuine political community. This argument directly influenced the egalitarian strand of the French Revolution and subsequent socialist thought.
