# Civil Liberty

Civil liberty is, for Rousseau, the form of freedom that individuals acquire by entering civil society through the social compact. It replaces the unlimited but precarious natural liberty of the state of nature with a freedom defined and secured by law — "obedience to a law which we prescribe to ourselves is liberty" (008-012).

## Definition

In the transition from the state of nature to civil society, "what man loses by the social compact is his natural liberty and an unlimited right to everything he tries to get and succeeds in getting; what he gains is civil liberty and the proprietorship of all he possesses" (008-012). Civil liberty is not mere absence of constraint but the positive freedom of living under laws one has a share in making.

## Rousseau's Account

### Liberty Under the General Will

Civil liberty is "limited by the general will" (010-003). This limitation is not a diminution of freedom but its condition — for without law there is no liberty, only the arbitrary power of the strong over the weak. The general will, by its nature, cannot impose particular burdens: it acts through general laws that apply equally to all, and in obeying such laws, each citizen "obeys himself alone, and remains as free as before" (P01-053).

### Moral Liberty

Beyond civil liberty, Rousseau identifies a higher form: moral liberty. "The mere impulse of appetite is slavery, while obedience to a law which we prescribe to ourselves is liberty" (008-012). This is the freedom of rational self-governance, where the individual is no longer driven by natural impulse but acts according to principles he has helped to establish. It is through this moral liberty that "man becomes truly the master of himself."

### Freedom from Personal Dependence

A central aim of the social compact is to replace personal dependence with dependence on law. In the state of nature, the weak depend on the strong; in corrupt civil societies, the poor depend on the rich. Rousseau's solution is to make every citizen equally dependent on the community as a whole — "by giving each citizen to his country, [the compact] secures him against all personal dependence" (009-009). This is the structural guarantee of liberty.

### Property as Civil Liberty

The social compact transforms precarious natural possession into secure civil property. "Possession, which is merely the effect of force or the right of the first occupier," is replaced by "property, which can be founded only on a positive title" (010-003). This transformation is an essential component of civil liberty — freedom from the insecurity that makes individuals dependent on the power of others.

### Liberty and the Right of Resistance

The limits of civil liberty are defined by the terms of the compact itself. The Sovereign cannot exceed "the limits of general conventions" (016-010), and "every man may dispose at will of such goods and liberty as these conventions leave him" (016-010). When the compact is violated by those in power, "each regains his original rights and resumes his natural liberty" (008-006) — a tacit right of resistance.

## Historical Context

Rousseau's concept of liberty synthesizes and transforms the republican tradition. From the ancients he takes the idea that freedom consists in participation in collective self-governance; from the moderns (particularly Locke) he takes the concern for personal security and property. His innovation is to argue that these two conceptions of liberty are not in tension but mutually reinforcing — participation secures personal freedom, and personal security enables genuine participation.
