# Sovereignty

In Rousseau's political philosophy, sovereignty is the exercise of the general will by the people acting as a collective body. Unlike earlier theorists who located sovereignty in a monarch or parliament, Rousseau insists that sovereignty is inalienable, indivisible, and can belong only to the people themselves.

## Definition

Rousseau defines sovereignty as "nothing less than the exercise of the general will" and holds that it "can never be alienated, and the Sovereign, who is no less than a collective being, cannot be represented except by himself: the power indeed may be transmitted, but not the will" (013-003). This marks a radical departure from both Hobbesian absolutism and Lockean representative government.

## Rousseau's Account

### The Sovereign as Collective Being

The Sovereign is the body politic in its active capacity — the people assembled as a legislative body. Its members "are called citizens, as sharing in the sovereign power, and subjects, as being under the laws of the State" (008-012). This dual role — citizens as both authors and subjects of law — is the distinctive feature of Rousseau's republicanism.

### Inalienable and Indivisible

Sovereignty cannot be transferred to a representative or monarch because the will cannot be represented: "the power indeed may be transmitted, but not the will" (013-003). Similarly, sovereignty is indivisible — the will is either general or it is not. Rousseau rejects the division of sovereignty into legislative, executive, and judicial powers as a confusion of sovereignty (which is legislative) with government (which is executive).

### Limits of Sovereign Power

"The sovereign power, absolute, sacred and inviolable as it is, does not and cannot exceed the limits of general conventions" (016-010). The Sovereign's authority is bounded by the terms of the social compact itself: it must act through general laws that apply equally to all citizens. "The Sovereign never has a right to lay more charges on one subject than on another, because, in that case, the question becomes particular, and ceases to be within its competency" (016-010).

### Sovereignty and Government

Rousseau carefully distinguishes sovereignty from government. Government is merely the executive agent of the Sovereign, "an intermediate body set up between the subjects and the Sovereign" to execute the laws. Whereas sovereignty is inalienable and belongs to the people, government is a commission that can be changed, modified, or revoked by the Sovereign at will (P01-037).

### The Sovereign and the Individual

The Sovereign must guarantee the rights of each citizen against the others: "the sovereign power need give no guarantee to its subjects, because it is impossible for the body to wish to hurt all its members" (009-006). Since the Sovereign is composed of all citizens, it cannot have an interest contrary to theirs — a structural guarantee against tyranny.

## Historical Context

Rousseau's concept of sovereignty draws on Jean Bodin's theory of absolute sovereignty while radically democratizing it. Where Bodin placed sovereignty in the monarch, Rousseau places it in the people. His rejection of representation also challenges the parliamentary sovereignty developed by English jurists such as Blackstone. The doctrine directly influenced the French Revolution's assertion of popular sovereignty and continues to shape democratic theory.
