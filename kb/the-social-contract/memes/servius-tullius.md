# Servius Tullius

Servius Tullius, the sixth king of Rome, is invoked by Rousseau in [[the-social-contract]] as a constitutional reformer who restructured the Roman people into a political order — 'it was Servius, according to tradition, who first divided the Roman people into tribes and centuries' (048-022). His reforms transformed Rome from a city of clans into a republic of property-holders, making him a pivotal figure in the genealogy of republican institutions.

## Role in The Social Contract

### The Servian Constitution

Rousseau presents Servius's constitutional reform as a foundational moment in Roman political development. 'Servius Tullius, according to tradition, was the first to institute the census and to divide the Roman people into six classes' (048-028). This division by property rather than lineage was revolutionary — it created a political order in which wealth, not birth, determined voting power.

### The Centuriate Reform

> "Servius Tullius first divided the Roman people into centuries and classes, and gave to the rich, who were less numerous, a greater share of the votes than to the poor, who were more numerous." — Book IV, Chapter IV (048-022)

The centuriate assembly (*comitia centuriata*) was designed so that the wealthy, though fewer in number, controlled a majority of the voting centuries. Rousseau notes the strategic genius of this arrangement: it gave the appearance of popular participation while preserving the influence of property.

| Reform Element | Description | Rousseau's Assessment |
|---------------|-------------|----------------------|
| Census | Classification by wealth | Created political order |
| Centuries | 193 voting units | Weighted toward property |
| Tribes | Geographic divisions | Later reformed for balance |
| Classes | Six property classes | Determined military and voting obligations |

## Historical Significance

Servius's reforms bridged the transition from monarchy to republic. The census and centuriate organization created the institutional framework that would later serve the Roman Republic. Rousseau uses Servius as an example of the Legislator archetype — a founder who gives a people its fundamental constitution — alongside [[lycurgus-of-sparta]] and [[calvin-of-geneva]].
