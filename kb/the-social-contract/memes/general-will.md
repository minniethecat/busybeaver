# General Will

The general will (*volonté générale*) is the most fundamental concept in Rousseau's political philosophy — the collective will of the sovereign people, always directed toward the common interest, and the sole legitimate source of law and political authority.

## Definition

Rousseau defines the general will as the will of the sovereign body politic directed exclusively to the common good. It is not merely the aggregate of individual preferences, but the shared interest that emerges when citizens deliberate as members of the community rather than as private persons ((015-003)). As Rousseau states in the Social Contract, "the general will alone can direct the State according to the object for which it was instituted, i.e. the common good" ((013-002)).

## Rousseau's Account

### Core Argument

At the foundation of Rousseau's political system lies the distinction between natural liberty and civil liberty. By entering the social compact, "each of us puts his person and all his power in common under the supreme direction of the general will, and, in our corporate capacity, we receive each member as an indivisible part of the whole" ((008-011)). This act transforms a collection of individuals into a moral and collective body — the Sovereign — whose will is the general will.

The general will is always right and tends to the public advantage, but Rousseau cautions that this does not mean the people's deliberations are infallible: "the people is never corrupted, but it is often deceived, and on such occasions only does it seem to will what is bad" ((015-002)).

### General Will vs. Will of All

Rousseau draws a crucial distinction between the general will and the will of all. "There is often a great deal of difference between the will of all and the general will; the latter considers only the common interest, while the former takes private interest into account, and is no more than a sum of particular wills" ((015-003)). The general will is not discovered by aggregation but by subtraction — removing the "pluses and minuses that cancel one another" from particular wills reveals the common interest that remains.

### Sovereignty and Law

Sovereignty, for Rousseau, "being nothing less than the exercise of the general will, can never be alienated, and the Sovereign, who is no less than a collective being, cannot be represented except by himself" ((013-003)). Laws are "acts of the general will," proceeding only from the body of citizens who have constituted the State ((P01-042)). This makes the general will the exclusive source of legitimate legislation.

### Lesser General Wills

Rousseau recognizes that every political society contains smaller associations, each with its own general will. These lesser general wills are legitimate in relation to their members but "wrong in relation to" the broader general will of the State ((P01-047)). The supremacy of the great general will is "the first principle of public economy and the fundamental rule of government" ((P01-047)).

### Compulsion to Freedom

The enforcement mechanism of the general will contains one of Rousseau's most striking formulations: "whoever refuses to obey the general will shall be compelled to do so by the whole body. This means nothing less than that he will be forced to be free" ((009-009)). This is not tyranny but the condition of civil liberty — by submitting to the general will, each citizen is secured "against all personal dependence" and gains the freedom that comes from living under self-imposed law.

## Historical Context

Rousseau's concept of the general will draws on a tradition of political thought stretching from ancient notions of the common good through medieval theories of corporate personality. He was influenced by Diderot's earlier use of the term in the *Encyclopédie*, but transformed it from a universal moral principle into a specifically political concept tied to the sovereign authority of a particular people. The idea also reflects Rousseau's Genevan background, where the collective sovereignty of the General Council of citizens was a living political practice.
