# Hereditary Succession

## Definition

Hereditary succession is the practice by which [[authority|political authority]] passes from one ruler to the next by birthright rather than by merit, election, or consent. For [[thomas-paine|Paine]], this mechanism compounds the evil of monarchy: if monarchy is a degradation of [[equality|human equality]], hereditary succession is "an insult and imposition on posterity" that presumes to bind [[posterity|future generations]] to rulers they never chose (003-001). It transforms a temporary misfortune into a permanent injustice.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s critique of hereditary succession is both rationalist and scriptural. On rational grounds, he argues that no person has the right to determine who shall govern their [[posterity|descendants]]. On scriptural grounds, he traces the invention of [[monarchy|kingship]] to the Israelites' rejection of divine rule — a sin, not a blessing — and notes that even the biblical patriarchs did not practice hereditary rule (003-001).

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] devotes sustained attention to hereditary succession because it is the mechanism that perpetuates monarchy. Without hereditary succession, monarchy would be a one-generation aberration; with it, the original [[usurpation|usurp]]ation becomes a permanent condition. He identifies hereditary succession as one of "the two first" components of the [[british-constitution|English constitution]] that are "independent of the people" and thus tyrannical in principle (002-002).

The practical consequences of hereditary succession are devastating. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] catalogs [[wars-of-the-roses|the civil wars]] that have resulted from disputed successions: "In short, monarchy and succession have laid (not this or that [[monarchy|king]]dom only) but the world in blood and ashes" (003-001). The [[wars-of-the-roses|Wars of the Roses]], fought entirely over competing hereditary claims, serve as his primary English example — a conflict that devastated the nation for the sake of deciding which family would rule.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] also deploys a memorable naturalistic argument: if hereditary right were truly natural, "nature... would not so frequently turn it into ridicule by giving mankind an *ass for a lion*" (003-001). The intellectual incapacity of many hereditary monarchs is, for [[thomas-paine|Paine]], nature's own refutation of the principle.

Finally, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] extends the critique to virtue itself: "virtue is not hereditary" (005-002). Even if a virtuous monarch happens to reign, there is no guarantee — indeed no likelihood — that their heir will share their qualities. Political competence cannot be transmitted through bloodlines.

## Key Passages

> To the evil of monarchy we have added that of hereditary succession; and as the first is a degradation and lessening of ourselves, so the second, claimed as a matter of right, is an insult and imposition on posterity.
> (003-001)

> One of the strongest natural proofs of the folly of hereditary right in kings, is, that nature disapproves it, otherwise she would not so frequently turn it into ridicule by giving mankind an *ass for a lion*.
> (003-001)
