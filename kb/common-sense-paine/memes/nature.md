# Nature

## Definition

Nature, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s political framework, is the normative order of creation — the way things are and ought to be when not distorted by artificial human institutions. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] draws on the Enlightenment [[custom|tradition]] of [[natural-rights|natural law]], which holds that reason can discern moral and political truths by examining the natural world and human nature. "Good and bad are the distinctions of nature" (003-001): moral categories are not arbitrary conventions but reflections of a natural order accessible to human reason.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] uses "nature" in two intertwined senses. First, as a descriptive account of how humans are — "mankind being originally equals in the order of creation" (003-001). Second, as a normative standard for how society should be organized — political arrangements that contradict nature are illegitimate by that very fact. [[monarchy|Monarchy]], for [[thomas-paine|Paine]], is unnatural because "nature knows them not" (006-001); [[monarchy|king]]s exist only by human contrivance.

The appeal to nature is central to [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s rhetorical and philosophical strategy. By grounding his arguments in nature rather than scripture or precedent, he makes them accessible to any reasoning person — no priest or lawyer is needed to interpret what nature teaches.

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s appeal to nature runs through every part of *Common Sense*, serving as the foundation for his critique of monarchy and his case for independence.

**Nature and reason as twin authorities.** [[thomas-paine|Paine]] pairs nature with reason throughout the pamphlet. "The simple voice of nature and of reason will say, it is right" (002-002). Nature provides the facts — [[equality|human equality]], the distinctions between good and bad — while reason draws the correct conclusions from those facts. Together they constitute an epistemic foundation independent of scripture, [[custom|tradition]], or [[monarchy|king]]ly [[power|authority]].

**Nature as the standard for government.** In Part 3, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] asks his readers to evaluate British rule "on the principles of nature and common sense" (004-001). The implication is clear: if an institution fails the test of nature — if it contradicts the [[equality|natural equality]] of humankind, if it serves the few at the expense of the many — it is illegitimate, regardless of how long it has existed or what precedents support it.

**Nature against monarchy.** [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s most pointed use of nature is his attack on monarchy as unnatural. "As nature knows them not, they know not nature" (006-001) — [[monarchy|king]]s, being artificial creations, are cut off from the natural order that should guide political life. Hereditary [[hereditary-succession|succession]] is particularly unnatural because it assumes that wisdom and virtue can be transmitted through bloodlines, a claim that nature plainly contradicts.

**Nature and [[america|America]]'s capacity.** In Part 4, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] argues that "where nature hath given the one, she has withheld the other" (005-002) — meaning that [[america|America]]'s natural endowments (resources, population, geographic [[independence|separation]] from [[europe|Europe]]) make it capable of self-government. Nature itself has prepared [[america|America]] for independence.
