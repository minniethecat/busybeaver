# Sir William Meredith

## Biography

Sir William Meredith (c. 1725–1790) was a British Whig politician who served in [[british-parliament|Parliament]] and held various ministerial positions. He was known for his reformist views and critical assessments of English governance.

## Role in Common Sense

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] cites Meredith as a witness against the British constitution: "Sir William Meredith calls it a republic; but in its present state it is unworthy of the name" (003-001). By invo[[monarchy|king]] a British parliamentarian's own description, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] turns the defenders of the English system against themselves. If even a British MP calls the government a republic, what becomes of the claim that monarchy is essential to liberty?

The rhetorical move is characteristic of [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s argumentative style: use the opponent's own authorities against them. Meredith's word "republic" becomes ammunition for [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s claim that the monarchical elements of the [[british-constitution|English constitution]] are unnecessary — even the English themselves, in their more honest moments, admit as much.
