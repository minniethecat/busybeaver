# Oppression

## Definition

Oppression, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s political vocabulary, is the unjust use of governmental power to burden, constrain, or crush the people. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] distinguishes oppression from mere misfortune: oppression is *inflicted* by human agents wielding [[power|political power]], not by natural circumstance. It is the predictable consequence of illegitimate government — of monarchy, of hereditary [[hereditary-succession|succession]], of the concentration of power in hands unaccountable to the people.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] makes a subtle analytical distinction early in *Common Sense*: "Oppression is often the *consequence*, but seldom or never the *means* of riches" (003-001). That is, the rich do not typically become rich by oppressing others; rather, once rich, they become oppressors. This distinction matters because it locates the cause of oppression not in economic inequality as such but in the corruption that inequality enables when combined with unchecked [[power|political power]].

For [[thomas-paine|Paine]], oppression is not merely a misfortune to be endured but a condition that justifies resistance and, ultimately, revolution. The experience of oppression is evidence that the social contract has been broken.

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s treatment of oppression in *Common Sense* operates on historical, analytical, and rhetorical levels.

**The Norman yoke.** [[thomas-paine|Paine]] draws on the radical Whig [[custom|tradition]] of the "Norman yoke" to argue that [[england|England]] itself was founded on oppression. He describes "the wretched Britons under the oppression of [[william-the-conqueror|the Conqueror]]" (004-002), invo[[monarchy|king]] [[william-the-conqueror|William the Conqueror]]'s subjugation of Anglo-Saxon [[england|England]] as the original sin of the British constitution. If [[england|England]]'s own political order began in conquest and oppression, what claim can it make to legitimate rule over [[america|America]]?

**Oppression and freedom.** [[thomas-paine|Paine]] sets up a stark contrast between the Old World and the New: "the old world is overrun with oppression. [[freedom|Freedom]] hath been hunted round the globe" (004-003). [[america|America]], in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s framing, is the last refuge of freedom — the place where the cycle of oppression can be broken. This is not mere rhetoric; it is a substantive argument that staying under British rule means participating in a system defined by oppression.

**The trajectory of oppression.** [[thomas-paine|Paine]] warns that British policies, "if continued would grow into oppressions" (005-002). The insight here is dynamic: oppression is not static. What begins as mild imposition tends toward full-blown tyranny. The colonists must resist not only present oppressions but the trajectory toward worse ones.
