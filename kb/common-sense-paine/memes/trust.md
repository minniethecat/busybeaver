# Trust

## Definition

Trust, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s political vocabulary, is the conditional delegation of [[power|authority]] from the people to their representatives and governors. It is a fiduciary concept: those who hold power hold it in trust for those who granted it, and the trust can be revoked when abused. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] uses "trust" to reframe the relationship between rulers and ruled — governors are not masters but trustees, and the people are not subjects but the grantors of a revocable trust.

The concept is fundamental to [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s contractarian political theory. Where monarchists claimed that [[monarchy|king]]s held power by divine right or inheritance, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] insisted that all [[authority|political authority]] is held in trust from the people. This transforms political obligation: obedience is owed not to the person of the ruler but to the office, and only so long as the officeholder honors the terms of the trust.

## In Paine's Argument

**Trust as the basis of government.** In Part 1, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] establishes the trust relationship as foundational to all legitimate government. He argues that individuals in the [[natural-liberty|state of nature]] "surrender up a part of [their] property to furnish means for the [[security|protection]] of the rest" (002-002), creating a trust that government must honor. The government is not a sovereign but a trustee.

**The [[monarchy|king]] as untrustworthy trustee.** [[thomas-paine|Paine]] applies the trust concept most pointedly to monarchy. He argues that hereditary [[hereditary-succession|succession]] is incompatible with trust: "the [[monarchy|king]] is not to be trusted without being looked after" (002-002). The very structure of monarchy — in which power passes by birth rather than merit — demonstrates that it cannot be a genuine trust relationship. A trustee who inherits the position has not earned the trust of the governed.

**Trust and the decision for independence.** In Part 3, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] appeals to trust as a practical consideration in the independence debate. He asks [[america|America]]ns "to see what we have to trust to, if separated, and what we have to expect, if dependant" (004-001). The framing is significant: [[thomas-paine|Paine]] treats the political future as a matter of what is trustworthy — the known dangers of British rule versus the manageable risks of self-government.

**Continental trustees.** In the Appendix, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] proposes that the new [[america|America]]n government be structured around "continental trustees" (006-001), explicitly using the language of trust for the offices of the new republic. This is the positive application of the trust concept: having demolished the untrustworthy trust of monarchy, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] constructs a genuinely fiduciary republic.
