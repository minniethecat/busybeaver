# England

## Overview

England in 1776 was the world's dominant naval and commercial power, ruling an empire that stretched across the Atlantic. Its constitutional monarchy — the result of the [[Revolution of 1688]] — was widely admired as a model of balanced government. [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s *Common Sense* systematically dismantles this admiration.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] refers to England throughout the pamphlet — as "[[george-iii|the King]] of England" (001-001), "the much boasted [[british-constitution|constitution of England]]" (002-002), and "the people of England" (005-002, 006-001). England is both the adversary and the object of [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s constitutional critique.

## In Common Sense

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s treatment of England serves two rhetorical purposes.

First, he demystifies the [[british-constitution|English constitution]], arguing that its vaunted balance of King, Lords, and Commons is a disguise for monarchical tyranny. The English system is "the base remains of two ancient tyrannies" compounded with "some new republican materials" (002-002).

Second, he distinguishes between the English people and the English government. "The people of England are presenting addresses against the war" (005-002) — ordinary English citizens recognize the injustice of the [[america|America]]n war, even if their government does not.

This distinction opens space for future reconciliation between the [[america|America]]n and English peoples while justifying [[independence|separation]] from the English state.


[[thomas-paine|Paine]] also traces England's institutional history, noting that "England, since the conquest, hath known some few good monarchs, but groaned beneath a much larger number of bad ones" (003-001). The problem is not individual rulers but the institution of monarchy itself. This historical argument connects England's fate to the example of [[William the Conqueror]].
