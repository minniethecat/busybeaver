# Happiness

## Definition

Happiness, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s political vocabulary, is not a private emotion but a public standard. It is the criterion by which institutions are measured: does a form of government promote human happiness or diminish it? This question runs through *Common Sense* as a decisive test of legitimacy.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s conception of happiness reflects Enlightenment assumptions about human nature and the purpose of political association. [[government|Government]] exists not merely to prevent harm — to provide [[freedom|security]] — but to positively promote the conditions in which human beings can flourish. [[society|Society]], in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s opening distinction, "promotes our happiness positively by uniting our affections" (002-002), while government merely restrains our vices. The contrast is not between happiness and misery but between two different sources of well-being: the natural and the institutional.

## In Paine's Argument

The happiness theme enters early, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s fundamental distinction between society and government. [[society|Society]], he writes, "promotes our happiness positively by uniting our affections" (002-002). [[government|Government]], by contrast, is at best a necessary evil — it "prevents our vices" but does not itself generate happiness. This asymmetry between society (good) and government (necessary) is the conceptual architecture of the entire pamphlet.

In Part 2, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] sharpens the criterion. When evaluating monarchy, the question is "whether they are the means of happiness or of misery to mankind" (003-001). The answer, of course, is misery — the wars, the taxes, the hereditary [[hereditary-succession|succession]] of incompetents. A system that produces misery for the many while benefiting the few is, by [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s utilitarian logic, self-condemned.

The argument reaches its most forward-looking expression in Part 4, where [[thomas-paine|Paine]] discusses [[america|America]]'s choice. The decision for independence, he argues, is "necessary towards her future happiness" (004-001). [[posterity|Future]] happiness — not present comfort, not the avoidance of conflict — is the proper standard of political judgment. This temporal orientation, toward the generations to come, gives the happiness criterion its moral weight.

The Appendix closes with a sardonic benediction that returns to the theme: "Whose peace and happiness, may God preserve, Amen" (004-002). The wish is offered for a British official, but the irony is unmistakable. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] has spent the entire pamphlet arguing that the British system destroys the very happiness invoked in this conventional prayer.

## Related Concepts
