# Consent of the Governed

## Definition

Consent of the governed is the principle that [[Government]] derives its [[authority|legitimate authority]] from the agreement — whether explicit or tacit — of those subject to its rule.

In *Common Sense*, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] adopts this Lockean premise not as an abstract philosophical commitment but as a practical weapon: if [[england|Britain]] governs [[america|America]] without [[america|America]]n consent, then British rule is not legitimate government but [[usurpation|usurp]]ation.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] sharpens the principle beyond Locke's formulation.

For Locke, consent could be tacit — continuing to live under a government implied acceptance of its [[power|authority]].

[[thomas-paine|Paine]], writing in a revolutionary context, demands more: consent must be active and continuous, and when a government becomes destructive of the ends for which it was instituted, the people have not only the right but the [[Duty]] to withdraw their consent and establish a new government.

## In Paine's Argument

The principle of consent runs through *Common Sense* like a structural beam, holding together arguments that might otherwise appear disconnected.

When [[thomas-paine|Paine]] traces government to its origins — a small colony electing representatives "by the consent of every individual" — he is establishing that consent is the only legitimate source of [[authority|political authority]].

The British government's claim to rule [[america|America]] by inheritance, conquest, or parliamentary supremacy all fail the consent test.

This argument has immediate constitutional implications. The King "can make no laws without his consent" in the British system (004-001), yet he claims the power to bind [[america|America]]ns who never consented to his [[power|authority]].

The contradiction is not a legal technicality but a moral void at the heart of the colonial relationship.

[[america|America]]ns cannot be said to have consented to a government three thousand miles away in which they have no representation — "until we consent, that the seat of government... be in [[america|America]]" (005-002), [[authority|legitimate authority]] remains in suspension.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s innovation is to turn consent from a philosophical justification of existing government into a justification for revolution. Consent is not something [[america|America]]ns once gave and cannot retract; it is something they have never given to [[british-parliament|the British Parliament]] or King, and therefore must give to a new [[america|America]]n government of their own ma[[monarchy|king]].
