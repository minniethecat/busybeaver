# Lord North

## Biography

Frederick North, 2nd Earl of Guilford (1732–1792), known as Lord North, served as Prime Minister of [[england|Great Britain]] from 1770 to 1782. His ministry presided over the escalation of the [[American Revolution|American conflict]], including the passage of the Coercive Acts (Intolerable Acts) in 1774 and the prosecution of the war following the [[lexington-and-concord|Battles of Lexington and Concord]].

North was a personally affable but politically loyal servant of [[George III]], who relied on him to manage [[british-parliament|Parliament]] through the turbulent years of the [[america|America]]n crisis. He resigned in March 1782 following the British defeat at Yorktown, ma[[monarchy|king]] way for the short-lived [[Marquess of Rockingham|Rockingham]] ministry that began peace negotiations.

## Role in Common Sense

Lord North appears in *Common Sense* only once, in the opening paragraph of Part 3 (004-001), where [[thomas-paine|Paine]] groups him with the entire British administration as unworthy of [[america|America]]n attention:

> "The removal of North, or the whole detestable junto, is a matter unworthy the millions we have expended." (004-001)

| Reference | Context | PN |
|-----------|---------|-----|
| "The removal of North, or the whole detestable junto" | [[thomas-paine|Paine]] dismisses hope that a change in British ministry would resolve the conflict | 004-001 |

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s argument in this passage is strategic: some [[america|America]]ns hoped that replacing North's ministry with a more conciliatory one might restore relations. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] rejects this as naive — the problem is not the personnel of the British government but the system of [[Monarchy|monarchical rule]] itself. By calling North's ministry "the whole detestable junto" (a junto being a self-interested political faction), [[thomas-paine|Paine]] delegitimizes the British administration as a cabal rather than a legitimate government.
