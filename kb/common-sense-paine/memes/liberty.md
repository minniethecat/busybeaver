# Liberty

## Definition

Liberty, for [[thomas-paine|Paine]], is the natural human condition — the state in which individuals are free to pursue their wants without arbitrary restraint. It is not a grant from government but a birthright that government exists to protect. In the opening pages of *Common Sense*, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] describes "a state of natural liberty" in which society forms spontaneously to meet human needs (002-002). [[government|Government]] enters only later, as a necessary constraint on the vices that threaten liberty itself.

This conception distinguishes [[thomas-paine|Paine]] from thinkers who treated liberty as a privilege conferred by law. For [[thomas-paine|Paine]], liberty is prior to law; law exists to secure liberty, not to define it. The test of any government is whether it protects or diminishes the liberty of the governed — and a government that diminishes liberty has forfeited its claim to obedience.

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] consistently frames [[america|the American cause]] as a struggle for liberty. He describes [[america|America]] as a refuge for "the lovers of civil and religious liberty from every part of [[europe|Europe]]" who fled persecution to settle the new continent (004-001). This framing transforms the independence movement from a colonial tax dispute into a universal fight for human freedom — a rhetorical strategy essential to [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s success.

The connection between liberty and [[freedom|security]] is central to [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s political theory. [[government|Government]]'s purpose, he states plainly, is "[[freedom|freedom and security]]" (002-002). These two goods are inseparable: liberty without [[freedom|security]] is chaos, while [[freedom|security]] without liberty is tyranny. The art of constitutional design lies in achieving both simultaneously.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] also links liberty to representation, praising "the liberty of choosing a house of commons" as one of the few genuine rights Englishmen possess under their constitution (003-001). But he immediately notes that this liberty is hollow without a corresponding liberty to choose the other branches of government. [[representation|Representation]], for [[thomas-paine|Paine]], is liberty made operational — the mechanism by which the governed participate in their own governance.

In his closing appeal, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] addresses the [[america|America]]n people as "guardians of the public liberty" and urges them to preserve their freedom through decisive action (006-001). The preservation of liberty requires vigilance and courage; it will not survive passivity or compromise with tyranny.
