# Constitution

## Definition

For [[thomas-paine|Paine]], a constitution is not merely a description of how a [[Government]] happens to operate, but a deliberate act of the people establishing the form and limits of their government. It is the written expression of the social contract — the fundamental [[Law]] that precedes and authorizes government, rather than emerging from it. This distinction is crucial: "A constitution is not the act of a government, but of a people constituting a government" (004-002). A government without a constitution is merely [[Power]] without principle.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s conception of a constitution was revolutionary for its time. In the British system, the "constitution" was understood as the accumulated body of statutes, precedents, and customs — an organic growth rather than a deliberate design. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] rejected this entirely. A genuine constitution must be written, explicit, and enacted by the sovereign people; anything less is merely a "form of government without a constitution" (002-002).

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] applies this constitutional critique directly to the English system. He dismisses the vaunted [[british-constitution|English constitution]] as no constitution at all — merely a patchwork of "the base remains of two ancient tyrannies... compounded with some new republican materials" (002-002). Because the [[british-constitution|English constitution]] was never deliberately established by the people, it lacks the fundamental legitimacy that only popular sovereignty can confer.

The practical urgency of constitutional thin[[monarchy|king]] becomes clear in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s prescriptions for [[america|America]]. He calls for the colonies to "form a constitution of our own in a cool deliberate manner" (004-002), emphasizing that constitutional design requires calm reflection, not the heat of crisis. The form he sketches — a continental conference to draft a [[Charter]], followed by ratification — anticipates the actual process of [[america|America]]n constitution-ma[[monarchy|king]] with remarkable precision.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] insists on the connection between constitution and [[Independence]]: without "a settled form of government, an independant constitution of its own," [[america|America]] cannot secure its liberty (005-002). The constitution is the institutional embodiment of independence — the mechanism by which a people transforms from subjects of a foreign power into citizens of a self-governing republic.

The constitution also serves as a bulwark against future tyranny. By fixing the rules of government in writing, it prevents the gradual accumulation of [[tyranny|arbitrary power]]. A "constitutional spirit" — vigilance toward governmental overreach — is the citizen's proper posture (006-001).
