# Virtue

## Definition

Virtue, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s political lexicon, is the moral quality that sustains free government. It is the capacity of citizens to prioritize the common good over private interest, and of rulers to exercise power with restraint and justice. The insufficiency of virtue is, paradoxically, the reason government exists at all: government is "rendered necessary by the inability of moral virtue to govern the world" (002-002). If all people were virtuous, no government would be needed; because they are not, political institutions must supply the defect.

Yet virtue is not merely the negative condition that government compensates for — it is also the positive quality that makes [[republic|republican government]] possible. A republic depends on virtuous citizens who are willing to participate in self-government, hold their representatives accountable, and resist the temptations of corruption and faction.

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s most stri[[monarchy|king]] claim about virtue is that it cannot be inherited. "Virtue is not hereditary" (005-002) — a simple statement with radical implications. If virtue cannot be transmitted through bloodlines, then hereditary monarchy is structurally incapable of ensuring virtuous rule. The accident of birth may produce a wise [[monarchy|king]] or a fool; there is no mechanism to guarantee the former or prevent the latter.

This argument transforms virtue from a personal quality into a political criterion. The test of any political system is whether it tends to select for virtue in its rulers. [[monarchy|Monarchy]] fails this test systematically; [[republic|republicanism]], with its mechanisms of election and accountability, at least creates incentives for virtuous behavior.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] also invokes virtue as a historical bequest: "Can we but leave posterity with a settled form of government, an independant constitution of its own... as a glorious memento of our virtue" (005-002). The [[america|America]]n revolutionaries have an opportunity to demonstrate their virtue to [[posterity|future generations]] by establishing free institutions. To fail in this task would be not merely a political mistake but a moral failure.

The [[british-constitution|English constitution]], by contrast, has "eaten out the virtue of the house of commons" through corruption and royal influence (003-001). The structural decay of English liberty is, at bottom, a corruption of virtue — the replacement of public-spiritedness with private interest.
