# American Revolution

## Overview

The American Revolution (1775–1783) was the political and military conflict through which thirteen British colonies in North [[america|America]] achieved independence and established the United States of [[america|America]]. *Common Sense*, published in January 1776, was the most influential pamphlet advocating for this revolution — it transformed a colonial tax dispute into a universal struggle for human liberty.

## In Common Sense

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] wrote *Common Sense* at the decisive moment of the Revolution, when the colonies had already fought at [[lexington-and-concord|Lexington]], [[lexington-and-concord|Concord]], and [[battle-of-bunker-hill|Bunker Hill]] but had not yet declared independence. His argument was that the revolution was already underway in fact — the only question was whether [[america|America]]ns would formalize it in principle. "Every thing that is right or natural pleads for [[independence|separation]]. The blood of the slain, the weeping voice of nature cries, 'TIS TIME TO PART" (005-002).

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] frames the Revolution not as a rejection of British identity but as an affirmation of universal human rights. The [[america|America]]n cause is the cause of "the lovers of civil and religious liberty from every part of [[europe|Europe]]" who sought refuge in the new continent (004-001). By universalizing the conflict, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] transforms what might have been a provincial rebellion into an event of world-historical significance — the first time a people deliberately constituted their own government on principles of liberty and equality.
