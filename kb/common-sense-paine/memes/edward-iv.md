# Edward IV

## Biography

Edward IV (1442–1483) was King of [[england|England]] from 1461 to 1470 and again from 1471 until his death. He was the first Yorkist [[monarchy|king]], having deposed [[henry-vi|Henry VI]] during the [[wars-of-the-roses|Wars of the Roses]]. His reign was marked by the continuation of that dynastic conflict.

## Role in Common Sense

Edward appears in *Common Sense* as one half of the bloody dyad that [[thomas-paine|Paine]] invokes to illustrate the absurdity of hereditary [[hereditary-succession|succession]]. "Battles and sieges, were fought between Henry and Edward" (003-001) — the very names reduced to mere counters in a dynastic struggle. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] does not trouble to distinguish between Lancaster and York because, from his perspective, the distinction is meaningless: both represent the same illegitimate principle of hereditary rule.

The passage in which Edward appears is one of [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s most compressed historical arguments. In a single sentence, he compresses decades of civil war into evidence for a single proposition: hereditary [[hereditary-succession|succession]] produces bloodshed, not stability. The monarchical claim to ensure peaceful transitions of power is belied by the entire history of English dynastic conflict.

## Notable Quotations

> …The contention for monarchy and succession, between the houses of York and Lancaster, laid England in a scene of blood for many years. Twelve battles, besides skirmishes and sieges, were fought between Henry and Edward.
>
> (003-001)
