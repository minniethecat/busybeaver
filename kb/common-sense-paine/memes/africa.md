## Overview

Africa in the 18th century was a continent increasingly shaped by [[europe|Europe]]an colonialism, particularly the transatlantic slave [[commerce|trade]]. For [[europe|Europe]]an powers, Africa was a source of wealth and a theater of imperial competition.

In *Common Sense*, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] references Africa in the context of [[england|Britain]]'s global reach and its moral failures. His mentions are brief but pointed, using Africa to illustrate the scope of British imperialism and the barbarism it produces.

## In Common Sense

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] references Africa three times, each in a different rhetorical context. He notes that [[england|Britain]] has deployed "British arms in either [[asia|Asia]], Africa, or [[europe|Europe]]" (004-001), using Africa as one coordinate in a global map of British military reach.

In a more charged passage, he declares that "[[asia|Asia]], and Africa, have long expelled her" (004-003), referring to [[england|Britain]]'s diminished standing — a claim that even distant continents have rejected British influence.

His most stri[[monarchy|king]] reference appears in the discussion of [[america|America]]n naval potential, where he states that "Africa is in a state of barbarism" (005-002) as a consequence of [[europe|Europe]]an exploitation. This passage ties Africa's suffering directly to the colonial system that [[thomas-paine|Paine]] urges [[america|America]] to escape.


## Notable Quotations

> …Asia and Africa have long expelled her — Europe regards her like a stranger, and England hath given her warning to depart. O! receive the fugitive, and prepare in time an asylum for mankind.
>
> (004-003)

> Africa is in a state of barbarism; and no power in Europe, hath either such an extent of coast, or such an internal supply of materials. Where nature hath given the one, she has withheld the other; to America only hath she been liberal of both.
>
> (005-002)
