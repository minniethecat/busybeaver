# Usurpation

## Definition

Usurpation, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s political vocabulary, is the ta[[monarchy|king]] of [[authority|political authority]] without right — the seizure of power that belongs, by nature and reason, to the people. It is the opposite of legitimate government, which derives its [[power|authority]] from consent. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] uses the term to characterize both the origin of monarchy as an institution and the specific character of British rule over the [[america|American colonies]].

The concept carries moral as well as political weight. A usurper does not merely govern poorly; a usurper governs without title. The distinction is crucial to [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s argument: it is not enough to reform a usurping government — it must be replaced entirely. Reform implies the regime has legitimate foundations that can be improved; usurpation implies the foundations themselves are corrupt.

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] deploys "usurpation" at two key moments in *Common Sense*. In the Introduction, he frames the entire pamphlet as a rejection of "the usurpation of either" party in the [[america|America]]n debate — both those who would submit to [[england|Britain]] and those who would exploit the crisis for personal power (001-001). The word establishes his rhetorical position: he speaks for neither faction but for reason.

The more sustained argument appears in his analysis of monarchy's origins. "If the first [[monarchy|king]] was taken by lot, it was a form of election; if by election, it was a form of republic; if by usurpation, it was a form of tyranny" (003-001). [[thomas-paine|Paine]] runs through the logical possibilities and concludes that monarchy, in every possible origin story, is either elective (and therefore not truly monarchical) or usurping (and therefore illegitimate). There is no third option that would give monarchy a legitimate foundation.

This analysis connects to the [[america|America]]n situation: British rule over the colonies is usurpation because it lacks [[america|America]]n consent. The King's [[power|authority]] in [[america|America]] is no more legitimate than the first [[monarchy|king]]'s seizure of power — both rest on force rather than right. [[america|America]]ns who accept British rule are not submitting to [[authority|legitimate authority]] but acquiescing to usurpation.
