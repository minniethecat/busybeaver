# Norman Conquest

## Overview

The Norman Conquest of 1066 was the invasion and occupation of [[england|England]] by Norman, Breton, and French forces led by [[william-the-conqueror|William the Conqueror]]. It resulted in the replacement of the Anglo-Saxon elite with a Norman aristocracy and fundamentally transformed English society, language, and governance. For [[thomas-paine|Paine]], it represents the original sin of English monarchy — the violent, non-consensual act from which all subsequent [[monarchy|royal authority]] derives.

## In Common Sense

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] returns to the Norman Conquest repeatedly as the definitive proof that the English monarchy is illegitimate in its origins. "[[england|England]], since the conquest, hath known some few good monarchs, but groaned beneath a much larger number of bad ones" (003-001). The Conquest is the founding moment that defines all subsequent English history — a history of [[usurpation|usurp]]ation, oppression, and occasional respites under good rulers who cannot remedy the structural defect of their office.

The rhetorical power of the Conquest reference lies in its concreteness. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] is not ma[[monarchy|king]] an abstract philosophical argument against monarchy; he is pointing to a specific historical event that every English schoolchild knew. The present [[monarchy|king]], [[george-iii|George III]], traces his [[power|authority]] to [[william-the-conqueror|William the Conqueror]] — and William's [[power|authority]], in turn, traces to nothing more noble than armed invasion. "It certainly hath no divinity in it" (003-001).
