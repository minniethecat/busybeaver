# Commerce

## Definition

Commerce in *Common Sense* operates on two levels.

On the surface, it is a practical argument: [[england|Britain]] restricts [[america|America]]n trade for its own benefit, and independence would free [[america|America]]n commerce to flourish globally.

Below the surface, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] treats commerce as a moral phenomenon — dependency on British trade "diminishes the spirit" (005-002), while commercial self-sufficiency is an expression of national maturity.

Commerce is not merely an economic activity but a measure of political health.

This dual treatment reflects [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s broader method: material arguments ([[america|America]] will prosper independently) are always intertwined with moral arguments (dependence corrupts the character of a people). Commerce is the point where these two lines of reasoning converge most visibly.

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s commercial argument proceeds in three movements. First, he establishes the injustice of the existing arrangement: [[england|Britain]] "hath enriched herself" through [[america|America]]n commerce while restricting it "in every branch" (004-001). The colonial relationship is not mutual benefit but extraction — [[england|Britain]] prospers, [[america|America]] is "cramped and fettered" (006-001).

Second, he argues that independence would improve [[america|America]]'s commercial position. Freed from British restrictions, [[america|America]]n ships could trade directly with [[europe|Europe]] — "the commerce by which she hath enriched herself" need not flow through British ports. This is the argument from self-interest: even those who care nothing for liberty should support independence because it makes economic sense.

Third, and most distinctively Painite, is the moral dimension: "Commerce diminishes the spirit, both of patriotism and military defence" (005-002). A nation absorbed in trade risks losing the civic virtue necessary to defend its liberty.

This is not an argument against commerce — [[thomas-paine|Paine]] enthusiastically endorses [[america|America]]n commercial potential — but a warning against allowing commercial interests to override political judgment.

The [[america|American colonies]] must not remain dependent on [[england|Britain]] simply because the trade relationship is familiar and profitable.
