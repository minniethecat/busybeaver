## Overview

France was one of the dominant [[europe|Europe]]an powers in the 18th century, governed as an [[absolute-government|absolute monarchy]] under the Bourbon dynasty.

In *Common Sense*, [[thomas-paine|Thomas Paine]] repeatedly references France alongside [[spain|Spain]] as a strategic counterweight to British power. France represented both a potential military ally and an example of monarchy no less tyrannical than [[england|Britain]]'s.

## In Common Sense

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] invokes France in several roles. He observes that the French government is "as monarchical as that of France or [[spain|Spain]]" (003-001), underscoring that hereditary rule is arbitrary regardless of which crown wears it.

He notes that the law of the land operates similarly in both nations, with differences in land tenure between [[england|Britain]] and France. (002-002)

He argues that the colonies would naturally be "at peace with France and [[spain|Spain]] were they at war with [[england|Britain]]" (004-001), freeing [[america|America]] from [[europe|Europe]]an conflicts tied to [[england|England]].

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] contends that "France or [[spain|Spain]] will give us any kind of assistance" (005-003) should the colonies declare independence.

In the Appendix, he urges that [[america|America]]n states should be "as independent of each other as France and [[spain|Spain]]" (006-001), using [[europe|Europe]]an sovereign equality as a model.
