# Saul

## Biography

Saul was the first [[monarchy|king]] of Israel, anointed by [[samuel-prophet|the prophet Samuel]] at the people's demand. His reign was marked by military successes against Israel's enemies but also by conflicts with [[samuel-prophet|Samuel]] and eventual divine rejection. He was succeeded by [[david-biblical|David]], not through [[hereditary-succession|hereditary right]] but through divine selection.

## Role in Common Sense

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] invokes Saul to undermine the principle of hereditary [[hereditary-succession|succession]]. "Saul was by lot, yet the [[hereditary-succession|succession]] was not hereditary" (003-001). The observation is precise and damaging: even Israel's first [[monarchy|king]] did not establish a dynasty. If the very prototype of biblical [[monarchy|kingship]] was non-hereditary, what basis can there be for the claim that hereditary [[hereditary-succession|succession]] is divinely ordained?

The Saul reference also serves [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s broader argument that monarchy in scripture is always a mixed phenomenon — never purely endorsed, always accompanied by warnings and qualifications. Saul was chosen reluctantly by a prophet who had just warned against [[monarchy|kingship]]; his reign was troubled; his dynasty failed. The biblical record, properly read, is an argument against monarchy, not for it.

## Notable Quotations

> …Saul was by lot, yet the succession was not hereditary, neither does it appear from that transaction there was any intention it ever should.
>
