# Reconciliation Debate of 1775

## Overview
In 1775, even after [[lexington-and-concord|Lexington]] and [[lexington-and-concord|Concord]], many colonial leaders still hoped for [[reconciliation|reconciliation with Britain]]. The [[continental-congress|Second Continental Congress]] sent the Olive Branch Petition to [[george-iii|George III]], see[[monarchy|king]] a peaceful resolution. The [[monarchy|king]] refused even to read it, declaring the colonies in rebellion.

## In Common Sense
[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s pamphlet is an intervention in this live debate. He systematically dismantles the case for reconciliation, arguing that "much hath been said of the advantages of reconciliation, which, like an agreeable dream, hath passed away and left us as we were" (004-001). The dream of reconciliation is seductive but dangerous — it delays the necessary work of establishing independent government while leaving [[america|America]]ns vulnerable to renewed British oppression.

## Notable Quotations
