# Obedience

## Definition

Obedience, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s political framework, is the obligation of citizens to comply with the directives of government. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] distinguishes between **conditional obedience** — owed to a legitimate government that protects rights and serves the public good — and **blind obedience**, which is demanded by tyrannical regimes and rooted in habit, fear, or superstition rather than reason.

For [[thomas-paine|Paine]], obedience is never an absolute duty. It is contingent upon the government fulfilling its proper purpose: the [[security|protection]] of life, liberty, and property. When a government ceases to serve these ends, the obligation of obedience dissolves and the people may rightfully withdraw their consent.

The concept is particularly significant in *Common Sense* because [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s argument for [[america|America]]n independence rests on the claim that the colonists' obedience to the [[british-crown|British Crown]] is no longer justified. He must persuade his readers that their habitual loyalty is misplaced.

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] addresses obedience on multiple levels throughout *Common Sense*, weaving it into his broader case for independence.

**Obedience to Continental government.** [[thomas-paine|Paine]] observes that "the colonies have manifested such a spirit of good order and obedience to continental government" (004-002) as should reassure any reasonable person. This natural willingness to obey a government of their own ma[[monarchy|king]] stands in contrast to the forced obedience demanded by [[england|Britain]].

**The problem of foreign obedience.** In Part 4, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] challenges the notion that [[america|America]]ns owe obedience to a distant [[monarchy|king]] and [[british-parliament|Parliament]]. He asks whether it is reasonable for a continent to submit "its own countrymen to a foreign obedience" (005-002). For [[thomas-paine|Paine]], obedience to a government three thousand miles away, unaccountable to the people it rules, is not a duty but a form of subjugation.

**Obedience enforced by arms.** [[thomas-paine|Paine]] addresses the British resort to military force in the Appendix, noting that [[george-iii|the King]]'s response to colonial grievances has been "the ta[[monarchy|king]] up arms to enforce obedience" (006-001). When a government must compel obedience through violence rather than earn it through justice, it has already lost its moral claim to rule.
