# Independence

## Definition

Independence, in the context of *Common Sense*, is the complete political separation of the [[america|American colonies]] from [[england|Great Britain]] — not merely autonomy within the empire, but the establishment of a sovereign [[america|America]]n republic. For [[thomas-paine|Paine]], independence is not one option among many but the necessary conclusion of reason and the inevitable outcome of history. "Independency means no more, than, whether we shall make our own laws, or whether [[george-iii|the King]]... shall tell us 'there shall be no laws but such as I like'" (004-001). The question is stark: self-government or subjection.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s rhetorical strategy is to make independence seem not radical but obvious — the conclusion that any reasonable person, unencumbered by prejudice and interest, would reach. He presents independence as the natural terminus of a process that began with the first English settlements in [[america|America]].

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] frames the choice between reconciliation and independence as exhaustive and urgent. "Every thing that is right or natural pleads for separation," he insists (005-002). The blood shed at [[lexington-and-concord|Lexington]] and [[lexington-and-concord|Concord]] has already created a de facto state of independence; the only question is whether [[america|America]]ns will formalize what events have already accomplished.

The case for independence rests on both principle and pragmatism. On principle, continued [[obedience|allegiance]] to the British crown violates the natural rights of [[america|America]]ns and perpetuates a system of hereditary rule that is illegitimate in its very origins. On pragmatism, reconciliation is impossible because [[england|Britain]] has demonstrated that it will always prioritize its own interests over those of the colonies. "We act consistently," [[thomas-paine|Paine]] writes, "because for the first time in history, a people have the opportunity to form a government for themselves" (006-002).

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] also addresses the fear that independence would lead to chaos or civil war. He argues that a continental government, established through a constitutional convention, would provide stability and order. The alternative — continued dependence on a distant and hostile parliament — is the true recipe for disorder.
