# Robert Barclay

## Biography

Robert Barclay (1648–1690) was a Scottish Quaker theologian and apologist, best known for his *Apology for the True Christian Divinity* (1676), a systematic defense of Quaker beliefs. He also wrote an address to [[charles-i|Charles I]]I urging religious toleration. [[thomas-paine|Paine]], writing the Appendix to *Common Sense*, addresses the [[quakers|Quakers]] of [[william-penn|Penn]]sylvania directly and invokes Barclay as a model of moral courage.

## Role in Common Sense

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s reference to Barclay appears in the context of his address "to [[quakers|the people called Quakers]]" (006-002). The [[quakers|Quakers]], prominent in [[william-penn|Penn]]sylvania, were generally pacifist and reluctant to support the revolutionary cause. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] challenges them by invo[[monarchy|king]] their own [[custom|tradition]]: "Had ye the honest soul of Barclay, ye would preach repentance to your [[monarchy|king]]" rather than urging submission (006-002).

The rhetorical strategy is sophisticated. Rather than attac[[monarchy|king]] Quaker beliefs, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] appropriates them — arguing that true Quaker principles, as exemplified by Barclay's willingness to speak truth to power, actually support the revolutionary cause. A faith that demands moral witness cannot consistently demand political passivity.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] also includes the header "Barclay's address to [[charles-i|Charles I]]I" (006-003), framing the Appendix as continuing in the [[custom|tradition]] of Quaker moral witness. By positioning his own argument alongside Barclay's, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] suggests continuity between Quaker religious witness and revolutionary political action.

## Notable Quotations
