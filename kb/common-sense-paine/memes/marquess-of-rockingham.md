# Marquess of Rockingham

## Biography

Charles Watson-Wentworth, 2nd Marquess of Rockingham (1730–1782), served twice as Prime Minister of [[england|Great Britain]] — first from 1765 to 1766, and briefly again in 1782.

His first ministry is best remembered for the [[stamp-act-repeal|repeal of the Stamp Act]] in 1766. This conciliatory gesture toward the [[america|American colonies]] was accompanied by the Declaratory Act, which asserted [[british-parliament|Parliament]]'s right to legislate for [[america|America]] "in all cases whatsoever."

Rockingham led the Whig opposition during [[lord-north|Lord North]]'s ministry, advocating for reconciliation with the colonies. He returned to power in March 1782 after North's resignation, but died in office that July after only fourteen weeks. His brief second government initiated peace negotiations to end the [[American Revolution|American war]].

## Role in Common Sense

Rockingham appears in *Common Sense* only once, in the Appendix (006-001). There [[thomas-paine|Paine]] quotes [[sir-john-dalrymple|Sir John Dalrymple]]'s argument that the [[stamp-act-repeal|repeal of the Stamp Act]] was an act of royal prerogative, not parliamentary action:

> "If you are inclined to pay compliments to an administration, which we do not complain of, (meaning the Marquis of Rockingham's at the [[stamp-act-repeal|repeal of the Stamp Act]]) it is very unfair in you to withhold them from that prince, *by whose* nod alone *they were permitted to do any thing*." (006-001)

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] cites this passage not to discuss Rockingham himself, but to expose the logic of [[Monarchy|Tory ideology]]. Dalrymple's argument — that even a favorable act of [[british-parliament|Parliament]] was really the [[monarchy|king]]'s doing — demonstrates the absurdity, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s view, of a system where all power ultimately flows from a hereditary monarch.

The Rockingham ministry, despite repealing an unpopular tax, is reduced in this framing to an instrument of royal will.
