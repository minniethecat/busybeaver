# Freedom

## Definition

Freedom, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s usage, is consistently paired with [[Security]] as the dual purpose of legitimate [[Government]]. "Freedom and security" is the formula he uses to describe what government exists to provide (002-002). This pairing is significant: freedom without security is vulnerable to external threat and internal disorder; security without freedom is tyranny. Only when both are present can government be said to fulfill its purpose.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s conception of freedom is both negative (freedom from [[tyranny|arbitrary power]]) and positive (freedom to participate in self-government). It encompasses [[liberty|civil liberty]], religious liberty, and economic liberty — the freedom to worship, to speak, to [[commerce|trade]], and to govern oneself through elected representatives.

## In Paine's Argument

The language of freedom runs through [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s case for [[america|America]]n [[Independence]]. He describes the proposed continental government as capable of "securing freedom and [[Property]] to all men" (004-002) — a formulation that anticipates the [[american-revolution|American Revolution]]'s famous trinity of "life, liberty, and the pursuit of [[Happiness]]."

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] warns that continued submission to [[england|Britain]] will cost [[america|America]]ns their freedom. "Where will be our freedom? where our property?" he asks, if [[america|America]] remains dependent on a distant parliament that has already demonstrated its willingness to sacrifice colonial interests (005-002). The rhetorical questions are designed to force the reader to confront the stakes directly: this is not a debate about tax policy but a struggle for the fundamental conditions of free life.

The connection between freedom and action is central to [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s rhetoric. Freedom is not a passive condition to be enjoyed but an active project to be undertaken. [[america|America]]ns must "receive their portion of freedom from the event of a few months" of decisive action, not wait for it to be granted by a reluctant parliament (006-001).
