# Prerogative

## Definition

Prerogative, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s distinctive usage, is a theological-political concept: the exclusive right to establish and depose [[government|governments]]. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] argues that this power belongs to God alone, ma[[monarchy|king]] any human claim to sovereign [[power|authority]] an act of impious [[usurpation|usurp]]ation — an invasion of "the prerogative of heaven" (003-001).

This is not the conventional legal meaning of royal prerogative ([[british-crown|the Crown]]'s discretionary powers). [[thomas-paine|Paine]] deliberately reappropriates the term, transferring it from monarch to deity to undermine the theological pretensions of [[monarchy|kingship]]. By relocating prerogative in heaven, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] strips earthly rulers of their most potent ideological weapon: divine sanction.

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] introduces the concept in Part 2, his systematic attack on [[monarchy]]. After surveying biblical history to demonstrate God's disapproval of [[monarchy|king]]s, he delivers the theological verdict: monarchy is "a form of government which so impiously invades the prerogative of heaven" (003-001). The language is deliberately provocative — "invades," "impiously" — framing monarchy not as a political error but as a religious offense.

The biblical grounding is essential to [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s rhetorical strategy. He traces Israel's demand for a [[monarchy|king]] as a rejection of divine rule, citing [[gideon-biblical|Gideon (Biblical)]]'s refusal ("the Lord shall rule over you") and [[samuel-prophet|Samuel (Prophet)]]'s warning. [[monarchy|Monarchy]] thus becomes not merely imprudent but sinful — a [[usurpation|usurp]]ation of [[power|authority]] that belongs exclusively to God.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] returns to the concept in the Appendix, responding to the [[Quakers]]' political testimony. The [[quakers|Quakers]] had argued that "the setting up and putting down [[monarchy|king]]s and [[government|governments]], is God's peculiar prerogative" (006-004), claiming this principle required them to remain politically neutral. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] turns their own argument against them: if [[government|governments]] are God's prerogative, then all [[government|governments]] — including the [[america|America]]n revolutionaries' — must be accepted as divinely ordained. He exposes the [[quakers|Quakers]]' inconsistency: they invoke God's prerogative to oppose revolution while actively publishing political testimonies.

The argument culminates in a pragmatic rebuttal: "Kings are not taken away by miracles, neither are changes in [[government|governments]] brought about by any other means than such as are common and human; and such as we are now using" (006-004). God's prerogative does not preclude human agency — it works through it.

## Related Concepts

- [[Monarchy]] — the institution [[thomas-paine|Paine]] condemns as an invasion of divine prerogative
- Divine Right — the doctrine [[thomas-paine|Paine]] refutes by relocating prerogative from [[monarchy|king]] to God
- [[Hereditary Succession]] — a human claim to perpetuity that compounds the [[usurpation|usurp]]ation of divine prerogative
- [[Authority]] — under [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s framework, [[authority|legitimate authority]] derives from consent, not from divine delegation
- [[Government]] — "God's peculiar prerogative" to establish, but executed through human reason and consent

## Key Passages

> "When a man seriously reflects on the idolatrous homage which is paid to the persons of Kings, he need not wonder, that the Almighty ever jealous of his honor, should disapprove of a form of government which so impiously invades the prerogative of heaven."
> (003-001)

> "It hath ever been our judgment and principle, since we were called to profess the light of Christ Jesus, manifested in our consciences unto this day, that the setting up and putting down kings and governments, is God's peculiar prerogative; for causes best known to himself."
