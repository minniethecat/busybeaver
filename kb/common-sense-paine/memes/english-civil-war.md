# English Civil War

## Overview

The English Civil War (1642–1651) was a series of [[war|armed conflict]]s between [[british-parliament|Parliament]]arians and Royalists over the governance of [[england|England]]. It resulted in the trial and execution of [[charles-i|Charles I]], the abolition of the monarchy, and the establishment of the Commonwealth under [[oliver-cromwell|Oliver Cromwell]]. The monarchy was restored in 1660, but the constitutional questions raised by the conflict were never fully resolved.

## In Common Sense

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s argument draws on the English Civil War to refute the claim that monarchy ensures stability. "In short, monarchy and [[hereditary-succession|succession]] have laid (not this or that [[monarchy|king]]dom only) but the world in blood and ashes" (003-001). The Civil [[war|War]] is the English exhibit in this catalog of monarchical destruction — a conflict that devastated the nation for the sake of competing hereditary claims.

The deeper point is that monarchy does not prevent civil war but causes it. "It preserves a nation from civil wars; and were this true, it would be weighty" — but it is not true (003-001). The English Civil War, fought between competing claims to sovereign [[power|authority]], demonstrates that monarchy is a source of conflict, not a remedy for it.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] also uses the Civil [[war|War]] to draw a pointed lesson for [[america|America]]ns. If the English themselves could not resolve their [[british-colonial-conflict|constitutional crisis]] without civil war, how can [[america|America]]ns expect [[england|Britain]] to govern the colonies justly from three thousand miles away? The structural tendencies that produced the Civil [[war|War]] are still present in the British constitution — they have merely been directed outward, against the colonies, rather than inward.
