# Society

## Definition

Society, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s foundational distinction, is the natural product of human wants and social affections. It is the spontaneous organization that emerges when people cooperate to meet their needs.

Unlike [[Government]], which is an artificial institution created to restrain vice, society is "produced by our wants" and "promotes our happiness positively by uniting our affections" (002-002).

Society is prior to government, both chronologically and normatively. People form societies first, and only later institute government when the imperfections of social life become apparent.

This distinction is perhaps the single most important conceptual move in *Common Sense*. By separating society from government, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] undermines the [[custom|tradition]]al identification of [[authority|political authority]] with social order.

The [[monarchy|king]] is not the father of the nation; the government is not the soul of the body politic. Society can flourish without monarchy, and [[Monarchy]] often harms the society it claims to protect.

| 1st (Natural) | Society | Wants and affections | Cooperative, positive |
| 2nd (Artificial) | [[government|Government]] | Wickedness and vice | Restraining, negative |
> The two-stage model: society precedes and outranks government (002-002).

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] illustrates the society-government distinction through a thought experiment. A small group of people, "unconnected with the rest," settle a new land (002-002). Their first thought is society — mutual aid, cooperation, shared labor.

Only later, as numbers grow and [[virtue|moral virtue]] proves insufficient, do they institute government. This imagined [[natural-liberty|state of nature]] is not a historical claim but a conceptual demonstration: society is natural, government is a supplement.

The practical implication is revolutionary. If society can exist without government — and specifically without monarchical government — then dissolving political ties with [[england|Britain]] does not mean dissolving social order.

[[america|America]]ns will still form communities, [[commerce|trade]], cooperate, and flourish. They will simply be doing so under their own government rather than under a distant parliament. This directly answers the Tory objection that independence would plunge the colonies into chaos.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] returns to the distinction throughout the pamphlet. When he warns that monarchy is "the bane of all good society" (005-002), he draws on the same framework: monarchy corrupts the natural bonds of society by introducing artificial hierarchies.

A healthy society requires a government that serves rather than dominates it. The [[Quakers]] provide an interesting case — a religious society organized around peace and equality, functioning largely without coercive government.

The distinction also underwrites [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s argument about commerce and political order. Trade and exchange are functions of society, not government. When [[thomas-paine|Paine]] argues that "our plan is commerce" (005-002), he means [[america|America]]ns already possess a functioning society with its own economic life.

All they need is a government that protects rather than disrupts that society. This is why [[thomas-paine|Paine]] can confidently assert that [[america|America]] will prosper under independence — society is already in place.

## Key Passages

> Society is produced by our wants, and government by our wickedness; the former promotes our happiness *positively* by uniting our affections, the latter *negatively* by restraining our vices. The one encourages intercourse, the other creates distinctions. The first is a patron, the last a punisher.
> (002-002)

> In this state of natural liberty, society will be their first thought. A thousand motives will excite them thereto; the strength of one man is so unequal to his wants, and his mind so unfitted for perpetual solitude, that he is soon obliged to seek assistance and relief of another.
> (002-002)

> Monarchy is the bane of all good society, and hath a tendency to dissolve the bonds of social affection.
> (005-002)
