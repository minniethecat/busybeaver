# Oliver Cromwell

## Biography

Oliver Cromwell (1599–1658) was an English military and political leader who led the [[british-parliament|Parliament]]ary forces to victory in the [[English Civil War]]. After the execution of [[Charles I]] in 1649, he ruled [[england|England]], Scotland, and Ireland as Lord Protector from 1653 until his death.

His rule was marked by strict Puritan governance and military authoritarianism. Though Cromwell opposed the tyranny of the [[Monarchy]], his own regime demonstrated that replacing one form of arbitrary rule with another does not produce legitimate [[Government]].

## Role in Common Sense

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] mentions Cromwell in the Appendix, in a passage reflecting on divine providence and [[power|political power]]. "Oliver Cromwell thanks you," [[thomas-paine|Paine]] writes ironically — suggesting that those who attribute all political events to divine will unwittingly credit [[usurpation|usurper]]s with God's approval (006-004).

The reference is brief but pointed. Cromwell, for all his Puritan piety, was a military dictator who replaced one form of arbitrary rule with another. This serves [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s broader argument about the danger of conflating divine providence with political outcomes.

If every ruler who seizes power can claim to be God's instrument, then no tyranny can ever be resisted on principle. [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s point — developed more fully in the [[Divine Providence Debate]] — is that political legitimacy must rest on consent and constitution, not on claims of divine favor.

Neither [[charles-i|Charles I]], nor Cromwell, nor [[george-iii|George III]] can meet this standard. All three represent [[Tyranny]] in different forms: hereditary monarchy, military dictatorship, and distant imperial rule. The [[america|America]]n experiment in self-government offers the genuine alternative.

Cromwell also illustrates a structural problem with revolution. Overthrowing a [[tyranny|tyrant]] is not enough — what replaces him matters. [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s argument for a written [[Constitution]] and a representative [[Continental Congress]] is designed to prevent [[america|America]] from trading one Cromwell for another.
