# Honor

## Definition

In *Common Sense*, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] subjects honor to a republican revaluation. Where monarchy treats honor as something conferred by a sovereign — a title, a decoration, an inherited distinction — [[thomas-paine|Paine]] locates true honor in the defense of liberty and the practice of self-government.

The pamphlet's treatment of honor runs parallel to its treatment of virtue: both are values claimed by monarchy but, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s analysis, more authentically realized under republican institutions. A hereditary monarch who rules without consent can neither give nor receive genuine honor. Honor requires freedom.

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] introduces the theme early, in his attack on monarchy's historical origins. The pagan nations "paid divine honors to their deceased [[monarchy|king]]s" (003-001), a practice that [[thomas-paine|Paine]] treats as both superstitious and politically revealing. The honors accorded to monarchs are, in origin, not marks of genuine merit but of fear and flattery elevated to religious ritual.

The concept takes on a more constructive role when [[thomas-paine|Paine]] appeals to "continental union, faith and honor" (004-001). Here honor joins faith as a binding force — what [[america|America]]ns owe one another in their common cause. This is honor as mutual obligation, not as hierarchical distinction.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] sharpens the contrast between true and false honor when he denounces those who would submit to tyranny. Such a course is "unworthy a man of honor" (005-002). The "man of honor" in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s lexicon is not the courtier who enjoys royal favor but the citizen who refuses to be a subject. Honor is defined by resistance to unjust power, not by proximity to it.

The Appendix extends the image: independence will place [[america|America]] "on a firm, secure, and honorable basis" (006-001). The triad — firm, secure, honorable — links structural stability with moral legitimacy. A nation cannot be honorable while it depends on another nation's will.
