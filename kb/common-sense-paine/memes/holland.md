## Overview

Holland, the Dutch Republic, was one of the few non-monarchical states in 18th-century [[europe|Europe]]. A commercial and maritime power, it had a republican form of government that stood in marked contrast to the hereditary monarchies dominating the continent.

In *Common Sense*, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] uses Holland as a key example to support his argument against monarchy. The Dutch Republic serves as living proof that a nation can be stable, prosperous, and free without a [[monarchy|king]].

## In Common Sense

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] invokes Holland at three critical points in his argument. In his critique of hereditary [[hereditary-succession|succession]], he notes that "Holland without a [[monarchy|king]] hath enjoyed more peace" (003-001) than monarchical nations, directly challenging the claim that monarchy brings stability.

He groups Holland with other [[europe|Europe]]an nations to argue that shared liberty, not shared monarchy, creates true bonds: people are countrymen "for [[england|England]], Holland, Germany, or Sweden, when compared with [[france|France]] or [[spain|Spain]]" (004-001) based on their relative freedoms.

Most pointedly, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] observes that "Holland and Swisserland are without wars" (004-002), while monarchies are perpetually in conflict. This contrast forms a central pillar of his argument that republics are inherently more peaceful.


## Notable Quotations

> Holland without a king hath enjoyed more peace for this last century than any of the monarchial governments in Europe.
>
> (003-001)

> Holland and Swisserland are without wars, foreign or domestic: Monarchical governments, it is true, are never long at rest.
>
> (004-002)
