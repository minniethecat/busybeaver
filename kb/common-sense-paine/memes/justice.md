# Justice

## Definition

Justice, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s framework, is not primarily a legal concept but a moral one. It is the standard against which all political institutions must be measured — the principle that government exists to do "justice to others" (002-002) rather than to serve the interests of rulers.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] distinguishes sharply between the sword of justice and the sword of war. The latter decides by force; the former decides by right. When he observes that in the British system "the sword, not of justice, but of war, decides the suit" (006-001), he indicts the entire monarchical order as fundamentally unjust — not because it produces wrong verdicts, but because its method of decision is force rather than reason.

The connection between justice and the social contract is explicit: if the "solemn compact" of government "would dissolve, and justice be extirpated the earth" (004-003), then the moral universe itself depends on the right ordering of political institutions. Justice is not a luxury of settled government; it is the condition of government's legitimacy.

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] deploys justice as both a diagnostic and a prescriptive tool. Diagnostically, he uses it to reveal the injustice at the heart of the British constitutional system: "without manifest injustice to their children," no generation can bind [[posterity|future generations]] to a particular form of government (003-001). Hereditary [[hereditary-succession|succession]] is not merely imprudent — it is unjust, because it denies each generation the right to choose its own government.

In calling for independence, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] appeals to justice as the standard that [[england|Britain]] has failed to meet. He calls on "[[england|Britain]] to do this continent justice" (004-001), but the phrasing implies that [[england|Britain]] cannot — that the relationship between metropole and colony is structurally incapable of justice because it is founded on domination rather than consent.

The constructive dimension of [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s argument is that [[america|America]]n independence will restore justice to political life. A government founded on a written charter, with separated powers and regular elections, can administer justice impartially. [[monarchy|Monarchy]], by contrast, is inherently unjust because its "sword" is always ultimately that "of war" — of force — rather than of right.
