# Republic

## Definition

A republic, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s political vocabulary, is a form of government in which power is exercised by elected representatives rather than by hereditary monarchs or aristocratic elites. It is government by the people, conducted through institutions designed to secure the public good rather than private interest. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] uses "republic" and "republican" not merely as descriptive labels but as terms of praise — the republic is the form of government that reason and nature alike endorse.

Crucially, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] distinguishes the [[america|America]]n republic from the [[europe|Europe]]an republics he dismisses. "The republics of [[europe|Europe]] are all (and we may say always) in peace" — but this peace, he implies, is the peace of insignificance (004-002). A genuine republic, on the [[america|America]]n model, would be large, diverse, and energetic — capable of defending itself while preserving liberty at home.

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s republicanism is built on the ruins of his critique of monarchy. Having demonstrated that monarchy is illegitimate in origin and destructive in operation, he naturally turns to the question of what should replace it. The answer is a republic — specifically, a continental republic with a [[constitution|written constitution]], [[representation|equal representation]], and regular elections.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] finds a biblical precedent for republicanism in the period before the Israelite monarchy. The government of Israel under the judges, he argues, "was a kind of republic administred by a judge and the elders of the tribes" (003-001). This was, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s reading, the form of government that God originally intended — the monarchy came later, as a punishment for the people's ingratitude. By grounding republicanism in scripture, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] preempts the royalist argument that monarchy is divinely ordained.

The practical superiority of republican government lies in its capacity for self-correction. "Where a republican government, by being formed on more natural principles," the people can remedy defects through the ordinary political process rather than through revolution (004-002). The republic does not require perfection at its founding; it requires institutions capable of peaceful improvement over time.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] also emphasizes that republican government is cheaper and less prone to war than monarchy. "The [[security|safety]] of a republic depends essentially on the energy of a common national sentiment; on a uniformity of principles and habits" (005-002). A republic, grounded in shared values rather than hereditary privilege, can draw on the loyalty and energy of its citizens in ways that monarchy cannot.
