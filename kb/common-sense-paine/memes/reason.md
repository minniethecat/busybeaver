# Reason

## Definition

Reason, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s philosophical vocabulary, is the human capacity for rational judgment and logical inference — the faculty by which individuals can assess political claims without relying on custom, [[custom|tradition]], or [[power|authority]]. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] treats reason as the proper foundation for all political argument. He opens *Common Sense* by contrasting reason with the inertia of habit: "Time makes more converts than reason" (001-001).

For [[thomas-paine|Paine]], reason is not merely an abstract Enlightenment ideal but a practical tool for political deliberation. He repeatedly appeals to his readers' reason as the arbiter of his arguments, as[[monarchy|king]] them to set aside prejudice and evaluate his case on its merits. The appeal to reason is central to [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s rhetorical strategy — he presents himself not as a prophet or [[power|authority]] but as a fellow reasoner.

The concept distinguishes [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s political philosophy from both divine-right monarchism (which appeals to scripture and [[custom|tradition]]) and from mere emotional rhetoric. Reason, for [[thomas-paine|Paine]], is what makes self-government possible: citizens capable of reasoning can judge their own interests without needing a [[monarchy|king]] to think for them.

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] deploys the concept of reason throughout *Common Sense* as both a methodological principle and a substantive argument.

**Reason against custom.** The opening of *Common Sense* frames the entire pamphlet as an exercise in reason against habit. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] observes that "a long habit of not thin[[monarchy|king]] a thing wrong, gives it a superficial appearance of being right" (001-001). The antidote to this cognitive inertia is reason — the willingness to examine beliefs rather than inherit them.

**Reason and nature together.** [[thomas-paine|Paine]] frequently pairs reason with nature as twin sources of political truth. "The simple voice of nature and of reason will say, it is right" (002-002). This pairing is significant: nature provides the facts of [[equality|human equality]], while reason draws the correct political conclusions from those facts. Together they form an epistemic foundation that requires neither priests nor [[monarchy|king]]s to interpret.

**The reader as reasoner.** Throughout Part 3, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] addresses his reader as a reasoning subject. He asks the reader to "divest himself of prejudice and [[prejudice|prepossession]], and suffer his reason and his feelings to determine for themselves" (004-001). This is both a rhetorical move and a political claim — if ordinary people are capable of reasoning about government, then they are capable of self-government.

**Reason and independence.** In Part 4, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] uses reason to dismantle objections to independence. He examines the claim that [[america|America]] is not yet "ripe" for independence and finds it unreasonable: "many strong and stri[[monarchy|king]] reasons may be given, to shew, that nothing can settle our affairs so expeditiously as an open and determined declaration for independance" (005-003).
