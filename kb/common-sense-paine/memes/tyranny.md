# Tyranny

## Definition

Tyranny, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s political vocabulary, is the condition in which government exceeds its proper bounds and exercises arbitrary power over the governed. It is the state against which all legitimate government is erected as a defense. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] traces tyranny to the same source as monarchy — the corruption of power when unchecked by [[consent-of-the-governed|popular consent]]. The natural tendency of power, he warns, is toward tyranny; "the natural disease of monarchy" is the accumulation of unchecked [[power|authority]] (002-002).

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s concept of tyranny is both structural and personal. Structurally, any government that operates without a constitution is inherently tyrannical, because its power is unlimited by law. Personally, the tyrant is the individual — whether [[monarchy|king]], minister, or parliament — who wields this unchecked power. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] calls on his readers to oppose "not only the tyranny, but the tyrant" (004-003), refusing the comfortable distinction between good [[monarchy|king]]s and bad advisors that had long protected monarchy from criticism.

## In Paine's Argument

The specter of tyranny haunts every page of *Common Sense*. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] argues that the British government has become tyrannical in its treatment of the [[america|American colonies]], and that reconciliation would merely restore a relationship of systematic oppression. He traces the lineage of English tyranny all the way back to [[william-the-conqueror|William the Conqueror]]: "A French bastard landing with an armed banditti, and establishing himself [[monarchy|king]] of [[england|England]] against the consent of the natives, is in plain terms a very paltry rascally original" (003-001).

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] warns that the distance between [[england|Britain]] and [[america|America]] makes tyranny structurally inevitable. A government ruling from three thousand miles away cannot be responsive to the needs of the governed; distance itself becomes a form of tyranny, insulating rulers from the consequences of their decisions. [[america|America]]ns who accept British rule are "opening a door to eternal tyranny, by keeping vacant the seat of government" (004-002).

The climax of [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s anti-tyranny argument is his appeal to action. "O ye that love mankind! Ye that dare oppose, not only the tyranny, but the tyrant, stand forth!" (004-003). This is not merely a call to resistance but to revolution — the total replacement of tyrannical institutions with legitimate ones. For [[thomas-paine|Paine]], half-measures against tyranny are self-defeating; only the complete establishment of self-government can secure liberty against the return of arbitrary power.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] concludes by warning that the British character itself has become tyrannical: "Brutality and tyranny appear on the face of it" (006-001). The corruption is not merely institutional but cultural — the British people, accustomed to dominating others, have lost the capacity for just governance.
