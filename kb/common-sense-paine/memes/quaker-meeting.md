# Quaker Meeting

## Overview
Quaker Meetings were the local congregations of the [[quakers|Religious Society of Friends]], organized through a system of monthly, quarterly, and yearly meetings. In [[william-penn|Penn]]sylvania, the Quaker Meeting exercised significant political influence due to the colony's Quaker origins.

## In Common Sense
[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s Appendix is addressed "to [[quakers|the people called Quakers]]" — specifically the Quaker Meeting in [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]] and its broader network (006-002). He treats the Meeting as a political as well as religious body, arguing that its pacifist principles are being used to justify political quietism in the face of British oppression. The appendix is, in effect, an appeal from one dissenting [[custom|tradition]] ([[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s radical Protestant individualism) to another (Quaker moral witness).

## Notable Quotations

> The principles of Quakerism have a direct tendency to make a man the quiet and inoffensive subject of any, and every government *which is set over him*.
>
> (006-004)
