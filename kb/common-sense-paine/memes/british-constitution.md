# British Constitution

## Definition

The British [[Constitution]], as [[thomas-paine|Thomas Paine]] engages with it in *Common Sense*, refers to the unconstitutional settlement of 1688 — the mixed [[Government]] of King, Lords, and Commons that eighteenth-century Britons celebrated as the model of balanced [[Liberty]].

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s project is to dismantle this admiration piece by piece, revealing the constitution not as a safeguard of [[Freedom]] but as "the base remains of two ancient tyrannies" compounded with "some new republican materials" (002-002).

Where his contemporaries saw equilibrium, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] saw deception.

The King and [[british-house-of-lords|the Lords]] — the monarchical and aristocratic components — dominate; [[british-house-of-commons|the Commons]], the sole republican element, is "the only republican part in the constitution" (003-001) and even it is checked into impotence.

The constitution's reputation for balance is, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s analysis, a rhetorical achievement rather than a structural one.

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s critique of the British Constitution serves as the hinge between his theory of government (Part I) and his attack on [[Monarchy]] (Part II).

He begins by conceding the constitution's reputation — "the so much boasted constitution of England" (002-002) — before systematically exposing its flaws. The constitution is "noble for the dark and slavish times in which it was erected," not for the enlightened age of 1776.

The argument operates on two levels. First, historically: the constitution's components — monarchy, aristocracy, and a weak commons — are not products of wisdom but of conquest and [[usurpation|usurp]]ation.

Second, functionally: even if the constitution once served a purpose, it is now "merely temporary" (004-001), a placeholder that [[america|America]]ns must replace with "a constitution of our own in a cool deliberate manner" (004-002).

The distinction between a temporary expedient and a permanent form is central — [[thomas-paine|Paine]] insists [[america|America]]ns must not mistake the former for the latter.

This critique culminates in a practical recommendation: [[america|America]] must establish "an independant constitution of its own" (005-002) rather than accept the British model by default. The British Constitution is not a universal template but a specific historical artifact, and a flawed one at that.
