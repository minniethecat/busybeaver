## Overview

Turkey, the Ottoman Empire, was one of the great powers of the 18th century, controlling vast territories across southeastern [[europe|Europe]], the Middle East, and North [[africa|Africa]]. To [[europe|Europe]]an observers, the Ottoman system represented the archetype of despotic rule.

In *Common Sense*, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] references Turkey twice as a comparative case in his broader argument against monarchy and hereditary government.

## In Common Sense

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] makes a provocative comparison early in the pamphlet, arguing that government can be "as oppressive in [[england|England]] as in Turkey" (002-002). By likening [[england|England]] to what [[europe|Europe]]ans considered the epitome of tyranny, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] undermines the claim that [[england|Britain]]'s mixed constitution guarantees liberty.

In the Appendix, he extends this comparative logic to foreign policy, noting that [[england|Britain]] "would have defended Turkey from the same motive" (004-001) that she defends other nations — namely, self-interest in [[commerce|trade]] rather than any principled commitment to freedom.

This supports [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s argument that British [[security|protection]] of the colonies is motivated by commerce, not care.

## Notable Quotations

> …That the crown is not as oppressive in England as in Turkey.
