# Henry VI

## Biography

Henry VI (1421–1471) was King of [[england|England]] from 1422 to 1461 and again from 1470 to 1471. His reign was dominated by the [[wars-of-the-roses|Wars of the Roses]], the dynastic conflict between the Houses of Lancaster and York. His mental instability and military defeats led to his deposition by [[edward-iv|Edward IV]].

## Role in Common Sense

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] references Henry VI in his catalog of the disasters caused by hereditary [[hereditary-succession|succession]]. "Twice was Henry placed between the contending parties" in the [[wars-of-the-roses|Wars of the Roses]] (003-001). The image is of a [[monarchy|king]] who is not a ruler but a pawn — tossed between warring factions who fight not over policy but over which bloodline should occupy the throne.

This is the practical consequence of hereditary [[hereditary-succession|succession]] that [[thomas-paine|Paine]] wants his [[america|America]]n readers to contemplate. The [[wars-of-the-roses|Wars of the Roses]] devastated [[england|England]] for decades — not over any question of justice or liberty, but merely over which descendant of Edward III had the better claim. The absurdity of the principle is matched only by the bloodshed it produced.

## Notable Quotations

> Twelve pitched battles, besides skirmishes and sieges, were fought between Henry and Edward. Twice was Henry prisoner to Edward, who in his turn was prisoner to Henry. And so uncertain is the fate of war and the temper of a nation, when nothing but personal matters are the ground of a quarrel, that Henry was taken in triumph from a prison to a palace, and Edward obliged to fly from a palace to a foreign land; yet, as sudden transitions of temper are seldom lasting, Henry in his turn was driven from the throne, and Edward recalled to succeed him. The parliament always following the strongest side.
>
> (003-001)
