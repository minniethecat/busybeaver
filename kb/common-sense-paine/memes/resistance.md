# Resistance

## Definition

Resistance, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s political thought, is the active opposition to government when it exceeds its [[authority|legitimate authority]] and becomes destructive of the ends for which it was instituted. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] treats resistance not merely as a desperate last resort but as a principled response to tyranny — an exercise of natural right grounded in the same reasoning that justifies government itself.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] distinguishes resistance from mere rebellion or disorder. Legitimate resistance is measured, purposeful, and aimed at restoring the proper relationship between government and the governed. It is, in his framework, the people's ultimate safeguard against the slide from [[power|authority]] into tyranny.

The concept is central to *Common Sense* because [[thomas-paine|Paine]] is, in effect, calling the [[america|America]]n colonists to an act of collective resistance against British rule. He must demonstrate that resistance is not only permissible but necessary.

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s treatment of resistance in *Common Sense* operates on two levels: the philosophical justification for resistance as a right, and the practical argument that the moment for resistance has arrived.

**Resistance as paradox.** [[thomas-paine|Paine]] acknowledges the apparent contradiction in combining resistance with subjection. He writes of the difficulty "to unite resistance and subjection" (005-003), observing that it "requires an idea too refined for common understanding." The paradox points to a deeper truth: once resistance becomes necessary, the relationship of subjection has already broken down. The people cannot be both subjects and resisters; they must choose.

**The stakes of resistance.** [[thomas-paine|Paine]] warns that if the colonists do not resist now, they risk being "at last cheated into slavery" after "a long and brave resistance" (005-002). The warning is pointed — half-hearted or delayed resistance is worse than none at all. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] urges his readers to recognize that the moment for decisive action has come and that further delay will only make resistance costlier.

**Resistance and independence.** Throughout Part 4, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] frames the call for independence as the logical culmination of resistance. Once the colonists have resisted British arms at [[lexington-and-concord|Lexington]] and [[lexington-and-concord|Concord]], and once they have recognized the impossibility of reconciliation, the only coherent path forward is to declare independence and establish a government they need not resist.
