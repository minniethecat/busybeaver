# Thomas Paine

## Biography

Thomas Paine (1737–1809) was an English-born [[america|America]]n political activist, philosopher, and revolutionary. Born in Thetford, Norfolk, [[england|England]], to a Quaker father and Anglican mother, he worked variously as a stay-maker, teacher, and excise officer before emigrating to [[america|America]] in 1774 with a letter of introduction from Benjamin Franklin.

Arriving in [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]], Paine became editor of the *[[william-penn|Penn]]sylvania Magazine* and quickly established himself in revolutionary circles. In January 1776, he published *Common Sense* anonymously — a 47-page pamphlet that made the case for [[america|America]]n independence in plain, forceful prose accessible to ordinary citizens rather than political elites.

The pamphlet was an unprecedented success, selling an estimated 500,000 copies across the colonies at a time when the total population was about 2.5 million. George Washington himself credited it with "working a powerful change in the minds of many men." (P01-003)

During the [[american-revolution|Revolutionary War]], Paine served as a soldier and wrote *The [[america|America]]n Crisis* papers, whose opening line — "These are the times that try men's souls" — Washington ordered read to his troops at Valley Forge.

After the war, Paine returned to [[europe|Europe]], where he wrote *[[natural-rights|Rights]] of Man* (1791–92) in defense of the French Revolution, and *The Age of [[reason|Reason]]* (1794), a deist critique of organized religion. He returned to the United States in 1802 and died in New York City in 1809.

## Role in Common Sense

Paine is the author and narrator of the entire pamphlet, and his rhetorical persona is integral to its argument. He positions himself not as a political theorist addressing elites, but as a plain-speaking citizen appealing to common sense — a deliberate framing that separates his argument from the learned treatises of John Locke or Montesquieu.

The structure of *Common Sense* reflects Paine's methodical approach. He begins with a theoretical distinction between [[Society]] and [[Government]] — the former a blessing produced by human wants, the latter a "necessary evil" produced by human wickedness (002-002).

From this foundation, he attacks the English [[Constitution]] as a "house divided against itself" (002-002), then dismantles the scriptural basis for [[Monarchy]] through a close reading of the Old Testament — invoking [[gideon-biblical|Gideon]] and the prophet [[samuel-prophet|Samuel]] to show that God himself disapproved of [[monarchy|king]]s (003-001).

Paine's central rhetorical move is to treat monarchy not as a form of government to be reformed, but as a [[usurpation|usurp]]ation to be resisted. "A long habit of not thinking a thing wrong, gives it a superficial appearance of being right," he writes, "and raises at first a formidable outcry in defense of custom." (001-001)

This reframing — that custom is not [[power|authority]], and ancient wrong is not right — pervades the entire work.

> Paine's five-part structure moves from philosophical foundation through scriptural critique to practical policy — a rhetorical architecture designed to persuade both reason and sentiment.

In Part III, Paine turns to the immediate crisis, arguing that [[reconciliation|reconciliation with Britain]] is no longer possible because "the blood of the slain, the weeping voice of nature cries, 'Tis time to part'" (004-001).

In Part IV, he outlines [[part-4-present-ability-of-america|America's capacity]] for self-government, including proposals for a [[Continental Congress]] and a written [[Constitution]].

Throughout the pamphlet, Paine avoids naming contemporary [[america|America]]n figures — George Washington, John [[samuel-adams|Adams]], Franklin, Jefferson — a strategic choice that keeps the argument focused on principles rather than personalities.

The only living ruler he directly challenges is [[George III]], whom he never names but repeatedly condemns as the "Royal Brute of [[england|Britain]]" and the "hardened, sullen-tempered Pharaoh of [[england|England]]." (004-002)
