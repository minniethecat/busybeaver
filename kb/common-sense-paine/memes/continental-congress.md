# Continental Congress

## Overview

The Continental Congress was the convention of delegates from the thirteen [[america|American colonies]] that served as the governing body of the United States during the [[American Revolution]]. The First Congress met in 1774 in response to the [[Boston Tea Party]] and the Coercive Acts.

The Second Congress, beginning in 1775, managed the [[War]] effort, issued the Declaration of [[independence|Independence]], and served as the de facto national [[Government]] until the Articles of Confederation took effect. For [[Thomas Paine]], it was both his primary audience and the institutional vehicle for his constitutional vision.

## Role in Common Sense

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] wrote *Common Sense* with the Continental Congress as his primary audience. He calls on the Congress to take the decisive step of declaring independence and establishing a "continental form of government" with a [[constitution|written constitution]].

The Congress's conduct during the crisis demonstrates [[america|America]]n readiness for self-government. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] praises its "spirit of good [[Order]] and obedience to continental government" as evidence that [[america|America]]ns are capable of managing their own affairs (004-002).

Crucially, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] argues that the Congress should act by the [[power|authority]] of the people, not by the sufferance of the British crown. Its legitimacy derives from its representative character — the delegates are chosen by colonial conventions, which in turn represent the people.

This is the contractarian moment [[thomas-paine|Paine]] envisions. Just as [[Society]] precedes [[Government]] in his conceptual scheme, the Congress represents [[america|America]]n society organizing itself to institute a new government. It is not a rebellion against order but the establishment of legitimate order.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] also addresses the practical dimensions of Congress's role. "Now is the seed time of continental union, faith and [[Honor]]" (004-001) — the moment for bold action is now, before the window of opportunity closes. Delay risks losing the momentum that makes independence possible.

The Congress's ultimate test will be whether it can translate revolutionary energy into a durable constitutional structure. [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s vision is of a body that does not merely manage a war but founds a republic — the first in modern history to be established on the principle of [[consent-of-the-governed|popular consent]].

> Now is the seed time of continental union, faith and honor. The least fracture now will be like a name engraved with the point of a pin on the tender rind of a young oak; the wound will enlarge with the tree, and posterity read it in full grown characters.
>
> (004-001)
