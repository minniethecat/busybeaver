# House of Commons

## Overview
The House of Commons was the lower house of [[british-parliament|the British Parliament]], composed of elected members representing counties and boroughs. In constitutional theory, it represented the common people of [[england|Britain]]. In practice, its membership was controlled by the aristocracy through patronage and rotten boroughs.

## In Common Sense
[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s treatment of the Commons is nuanced. He acknowledges it as "the republican part in the constitution" — the sole element of the English system with any claim to democratic legitimacy (003-001). Yet he immediately notes that the crown and the lords have "eaten out the virtue of the house of commons" through corruption and influence (003-001). The Commons, in theory a check on monarchy, has in practice become an instrument of it.

This analysis serves [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s larger argument: even the best element of the British constitution is fatally compromised. Reform from within is impossible because the institutions of reform have themselves been captured by the interests they are supposed to check. Only independence — the creation of entirely new institutions on republican principles — can secure genuine representation.

## Notable Quotations

> …The new republican materials, in the persons of the commons, on whose virtue depends the freedom of England.
