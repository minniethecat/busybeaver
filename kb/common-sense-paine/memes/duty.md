# Duty

## Definition

Duty, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s *Common Sense*, is a concept under contest. The pamphlet's opponents invoked duty to argue that [[america|America]]ns owed loyalty to the [[british-crown|British Crown]]. [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s response is not to dismiss duty but to redefine it: true duty is not obedience to a hereditary sovereign but mutual obligation among free citizens.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] draws on the social contract [[custom|tradition]] to distinguish between duties that arise from genuine consent and those imposed by force or custom. Citizens have duties to one another and to the institutions they have created. They have no duty to a monarch who rules without their consent, regardless of how many generations have submitted before them.

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s treatment of duty begins with his account of society's formation. In the small group of settlers he imagines, individuals "will begin to relax in their duty and attachment to each other" (002-002) as their immediate needs are met — a natural observation that leads to the necessity of government. Duty, in this primal scene, is the web of mutual obligation that holds society together before law exists.

The concept becomes polemical when [[thomas-paine|Paine]] addresses the reconciliation argument. "To say that reconciliation is our duty," he declares, "is truly farcical" (004-001). The word "farcical" is chosen with care: [[thomas-paine|Paine]] is not merely disagreeing with the duty-to-[[england|Britain]] argument but moc[[monarchy|king]] it. A duty that requires self-destruction is, in his vocabulary, no duty at all.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] extends the argument by implication throughout the text. When he discusses [[america|America]]'s naval capacity, he notes that "a few guardships on constant duty" (005-002) would suffice — a practical use of the term that subtly reinforces his larger point. Duty in the republican sense is steady, practical, and directed toward [[security|protection]]. It is not a grand abstraction invoked to justify submission.

The Appendix completes the argument by redefining [[america|America]]'s duty in familial terms. [[england|Britain]], [[thomas-paine|Paine]] writes, is like a parent "whom it is more her duty to take care of" (006-001) — but the relationship has been inverted by British abuse. When a parent becomes a predator, the child's duty shifts: self-preservation becomes the primary obligation.
