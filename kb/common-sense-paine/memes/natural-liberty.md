# Natural Liberty

## Definition

Natural liberty, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s *Common Sense*, is the condition of human beings before the establishment of government — a state of freedom constrained only by individual physical limitations, not by law or [[authority|political authority]]. In this state, individuals possess full liberty of action but lack the [[freedom|security]] that only collective organization can provide. "In this state of [[liberty|natural liberty]], society will be their first thought" (002-002).

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s account of [[liberty|natural liberty]] is notably unsentimental. Unlike Rousseau, he does not idealize the state of nature as a lost paradise. Natural liberty is real but insufficient — it is the starting point from which human beings, driven by practical necessity, build society and eventually government. The key point is that government is posterior to liberty: liberty is not a grant from government but a pre-existing condition that government is instituted to protect.

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] deploys the concept of [[liberty|natural liberty]] to establish the proper relationship between government and freedom. If individuals in their natural state are free, then government can only be legitimate insofar as it preserves rather than diminishes that freedom. [[government|Government]] is not the source of rights but their guardian.

This has immediate political implications. A government that destroys liberty is not a government but a tyranny — it has abandoned its purpose. The British government's treatment of the [[america|American colonies]] — imposing laws without consent, quartering troops, restricting [[commerce|trade]] — is not an exercise of [[authority|legitimate authority]] but a violation of the [[liberty|natural liberty]] that government exists to protect.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] also uses [[liberty|natural liberty]] to explain why society precedes and is distinct from government. [[society|Society]] is produced by "our wants" — the natural desire of free individuals to cooperate for mutual benefit. [[government|Government]] is produced by "our wickedness" — the need to restrain those who would abuse their [[liberty|natural liberty]] to harm others. This distinction limits government's scope: it exists only to address the problem of wickedness, not to manage the full range of human association.
