# British Crown

## Overview
The British Crown is the institution of monarchy in [[england|Great Britain]], tracing its lineage to [[william-the-conqueror|William the Conqueror]]. In the 18th century, the Crown claimed [[power|authority]] over the [[america|American colonies]] through royal charters, governors, and a network of patronage that extended across the Atlantic.

## In Common Sense
[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s attack on the British Crown is comprehensive. He traces its origins to the [[norman-conquest|Norman Conquest]] — a violent [[usurpation|usurp]]ation — and argues that "the present line ([[william-the-conqueror|William the Conqueror]]) was a Frenchman" with no legitimate claim to English, let alone [[america|America]]n, [[obedience|allegiance]] (004-001). The Crown's [[power|authority]] rests not on consent but on conquest, and conquest confers no right.

More fundamentally, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] argues that monarchy as an institution is incompatible with liberty. "Absolute power is the natural disease of monarchy" (002-002) — the structural incentives of crown government inevitably produce tyranny, regardless of the personal character of any individual monarch. The British Crown, for all its constitutional limitations, remains an engine of [[tyranny|arbitrary power]] that no reform can remedy short of abolition.
