# Boston Tea Party

## Background

The Boston Tea Party (December 16, 1773) was a political protest in which [[america|America]]n colonists destroyed an entire shipment of tea sent by the [[British East India Company]]. The protest defied the Tea Act of 1773, which granted [[british-east-india-company|the Company]] a monopoly on tea sales in the [[america|American colonies]].

The Tea Act was not a new tax — it actually lowered the price of tea — but it preserved the principle of [[british-parliament|Parliament]]ary taxation without colonial representation. For the colonists, this was the core grievance: not the amount of the tax, but the [[power|authority]] that imposed it.

The British response — the Coercive Acts (called the "Intolerable Acts" by colonists) — closed [[boston|Boston]] Harbor, revoked [[america|Massachusetts]]'s charter, and imposed military rule. These measures transformed a tax dispute into a [[british-colonial-conflict|constitutional crisis]].

## Events

On the night of December 16, a group of colonists disguised as Mohawk warriors boarded three ships in [[boston|Boston]] Harbor. Over the course of three hours, they dumped 342 chests of tea — worth approximately £10,000 — into the water.

The action was carefully targeted: no other cargo was damaged, no one was injured, and the participants even swept the decks afterward. It was destruction of property as political speech — a calculated act of civil disobedience designed to force the British government to choose between concession and coercion.

[[england|Britain]] chose coercion. The Coercive Acts united the colonies in opposition to what they saw as collective punishment. The [[Continental Congress]] was convened in direct response, and the cycle of action and reaction accelerated toward war.

## Key Figures

- [[Samuel Adams]] — leader of the Sons of [[liberty|Liberty]], organizer of [[boston|Boston]] resistance

- Thomas Hutchinson — royal governor of [[america|Massachusetts]], whose refusal to let the tea ships leave precipitated the crisis
- [[lord-north|Lord North]] — British Prime Minister, architect of the Coercive Acts

## Aftermath

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] does not discuss the Tea Party directly in *Common Sense*, but his argument assumes its context. The cycle of British punishment and colonial resistance had reached a point where reconciliation was structurally impossible.

The Tea Party and the British response demonstrated that the British government would always respond to colonial resistance with coercion, not constitutional reform. This is precisely the situation [[thomas-paine|Paine]] addresses: when the choice is between permanent subjugation and independence, independence is the only rational course.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s pamphlet was published in January 1776, just over two years after the Tea Party. The intervening events — the Coercive Acts, the First [[Continental Congress]], [[lexington-and-concord|Lexington]] and [[lexington-and-concord|Concord]], [[battle-of-bunker-hill|Bunker Hill]] — had transformed the political landscape. The Tea Party was the spark that made *Common Sense* possible.
