# Quakers

## Overview

The Religious Society of Friends (Quakers) was a Christian denomination known for its commitment to pacifism, simplicity, and the doctrine of the "inner light." Founded by George Fox in 1652, the movement spread rapidly in [[england|England]] and the [[america|American colonies]].

Quakers were prominent in [[william-penn|Penn]]sylvania, which had been founded by [[William Penn]] as a Quaker colony in 1681. Their pacifist principles created tension with the revolutionary movement — a tension [[thomas-paine|Paine]] addresses directly in the Appendix of *Common Sense*.

| Quaker Principle | [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s Use | Rhetorical Effect |
|-----------------|-------------|-------------------|
| Inner light (direct revelation) | Each person can discern God's will directly — including on political questions | Undermines clerical [[power|authority]] to command obedience |
| Speaking truth to power | Cites [[robert-barclay|Barclay]] as model of moral courage | Reframes dissent as religious duty |
| Pacifism | Implies passive subjects enable tyranny | Turns Quaker virtue into political liability |
| [[equality|Equality]] | All persons equal before God — including [[monarchy|king]]s and subjects | Aligns Quakerism with republican principles |

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s strategy: turn Quaker principles against Quaker quietism.

## Role in Common Sense

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] addresses the Quakers directly in the Appendix, recognizing their political influence in [[william-penn|Penn]]sylvania. Their pacifism posed a challenge to the revolutionary cause: if Quakers refused to fight, they effectively sided with the status quo.

His strategy is to appeal to Quaker principles themselves. "The principles of Quakerism have a direct tendency to make a man the quiet and inoffensive subject of any, and every government which is set over him" — but this passivity, he argues, is a corruption of true religious witness (006-004).

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] distinguishes between the whole body of Quakers and "a factional and partial" group that had issued a declaration urging submission. (006-002) He appeals to the broader Quaker [[custom|tradition]] of dissent against unjust [[power|authority]], citing [[Robert Barclay]] as a model of moral courage.

The address is a masterful piece of political rhetoric. Rather than attacking Quakerism from outside, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] claims to defend its true spirit against its timid leaders. True Quakerism, he argues, demands active resistance to injustice, not passive submission to tyranny.

This argument connects to [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s broader theory of [[Society]]: religious communities are natural social formations that should support liberty, not monarchy. The Quakers, properly understood, should be allies of the revolution, not obstacles to it.

> The principles of Quakerism have a direct tendency to make a man the quiet and inoffensive subject of any, and every government *which is set over him*.
>
> (006-004)
