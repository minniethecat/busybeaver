# Samuel Adams

## Biography

Samuel Adams (1722–1803) was an [[america|America]]n revolutionary leader, political philosopher, and one of the Founding Fathers of the United States. Born in [[boston|Boston]], [[america|Massachusetts]], he graduated from Harvard College and initially worked as a tax collector before turning to political organizing.

Adams was the principal architect of colonial resistance in [[america|Massachusetts]]. He organized the Sons of [[liberty|Liberty]], helped coordinate the [[Boston Tea Party]] (1773) in response to the Tea Act, and served as a delegate to the [[Continental Congress]] from 1774 to 1781. His political writings — including the *[[america|Massachusetts]] Circular Letter* (1768) — articulated colonial grievances in constitutional terms that anticipated many of the arguments of the Declaration of [[independence|Independence]].

A skilled propagandist and organizer, Adams built the committees of correspondence that knit the colonies together in opposition to British policy. John Adams, his younger cousin and fellow revolutionary, called him "the most essential character" in the movement for independence. After the Revolution, Samuel Adams served as Lieutenant Governor (1789–94) and Governor (1794–97) of [[america|Massachusetts]].

## Role in Common Sense

Samuel Adams is never mentioned by name in *Common Sense*. Like George Washington, Benjamin Franklin, and other [[america|America]]n revolutionary leaders, he belongs to the deliberate silence [[thomas-paine|Paine]] maintains about contemporary [[america|America]]n figures — a strategy that keeps the argument focused on principles of government rather than on personalities or factional alignments.

However, Adams's political work forms the essential backdrop to [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s pamphlet. The resistance movement Adams built in [[america|Massachusetts]] — through the Sons of [[liberty|Liberty]], the committees of correspondence, and the [[boston|Boston]] Town Meeting — created the political conditions in which *Common Sense* could have the explosive effect it did. The pamphlet was published in January 1776; by July, the [[Continental Congress]] had adopted the Declaration of [[independence|Independence]], which Adams signed.

> Paine provided the public argument that Adams's organizing made politically possible — a division of revolutionary labor between the agitator and the pamphleteer.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] and Adams never collaborated directly, and their political philosophies diverged after the Revolution — Adams remained a Congregationalist [[custom|tradition]]alist, while [[thomas-paine|Paine]] was a religious freethinker. But in the critical months of early 1776, their efforts converged: Adams's decade of political groundwork gave [[thomas-paine|Paine]] an audience ready to be persuaded, and [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s prose gave that audience the argument it needed.
