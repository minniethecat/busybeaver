# Power

## Definition

Power, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s political vocabulary, denotes the capacity to govern — the authority to make and enforce laws and to direct the affairs of a nation. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] is concerned above all with the *source* of legitimate power. He rejects power held by birth or inheritance, arguing that all just political power originates in the consent of the governed.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] distinguishes between the power that nature gives — the universal human capacity "of feeling" (001-001) — and the power men claim over one another through artificial institutions. The former is equal and shared; the latter, when not derived from consent, is [[usurpation|usurp]]ation.

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] opens *Common Sense* by appealing to a universal human faculty: [[nature|Nature]] has given every person "the Power of feeling" (001-001), grounding both the perception of injustice and the impulse to remedy it. The reader's natural powers are as legitimate as any [[monarchy|king]]'s.

The core of [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s argument concerns government's origins. In the [[natural-liberty|state of nature]], individuals delegate power to representatives for mutual convenience. Power flows upward from the people, not downward from a sovereign. This makes monarchy a fundamental inversion — a [[monarchy|king]] claiming power by heredity has no legitimate source for it (003-001).

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] extends this to the [[british-constitution|British Constitution]]. The Commons, meant to represent the people's power, is a mere check on [[british-crown|the Crown]] rather than the true seat of sovereignty. In practical terms, the "custom of nations, when any two are at war, for some other powers ... to step in as mediators" (005-003) is unavailable to [[america|America]] while it remains a British subject.
