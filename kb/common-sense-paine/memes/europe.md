## Overview

Europe in the late 18th century was a patchwork of monarchies, principalities, and a handful of republics, all competing for power through shifting alliances.

For [[thomas-paine|Thomas Paine]], Europe was not merely a geographic designation but a political category — the old world whose institutions and corruptions [[america|America]] needed to transcend. Throughout *Common Sense*, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] uses Europe as a foil.

## In Common Sense

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] opens his critique by noting that "the monarchial [[government|governments]] in Europe" (003-001) all rest on the same dubious foundation of hereditary [[hereditary-succession|succession]] — antiquity alone is their only defense.

He dismisses the notion that any "European power" (004-001) would intervene on [[england|Britain]]'s behalf, since European nations have commercial interests that align more naturally with a free [[america|America]].

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] acknowledges that "the republics of Europe are all (and we may say always) in peace" (004-002), a pointed contrast with the war-prone monarchies, though he stops short of idealizing them.

He declares that Europe now "regards her like a stranger" (004-003), referring to [[england|Britain]]'s diminished standing. He contends that "no power in Europe" (005-002) possesses the reach to oppose [[america|America]]n independence.

In the Appendix, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] warns that the new nation must remain "uncontaminated by European corruption" (006-001), framing [[america|America]] as a deliberate break with the old world's political vices.

## Notable Quotations

> Europe, and not England, is the parent country of America. This new world hath been the asylum for the persecuted lovers of civil and religious liberty from every part of Europe. Hither have they fled, not from the tender embraces of the mother, but from the cruelty of the monster.
>
> (004-001)

> Our plan is commerce, and that, well attended to, will secure us the peace and friendship of all Europe; because, it is the interest of all Europe to have America a free port. Her trade will always be a protection, and her barrenness of gold and silver secure her from invaders.
>
> (004-001)
