# Divine Providence Debate

## Background

One of the most potent arguments against [[america|America]]n independence was theological. Some religious leaders, particularly among the [[Quakers]] and Anglicans, argued that the colonies' connection to [[england|Britain]] was part of God's providential plan. To separate would be impious — a rebellion against divine order.

This argument carried weight in a deeply religious society. If God had placed [[america|America]]ns under British rule, then resistance was not merely treasonous but sinful. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] recognized that the political case for independence could not succeed without addressing this theological objection.

| Pro-Providence Argument | [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s Refutation |
|------------------------|-------------------|
| [[england|Britain]]'s power shows God's favor | "The Heathens paid divine honors to their deceased [[monarchy|king]]s" — every empire claims divine sanction (003-001) |
| [[america|America]]n setbacks prove God opposes independence | Attributing outcomes to God is circular; any [[usurpation|usurper]] could claim the same |
| Scripture commands obedience to rulers | Scripture commands justice; tyranny violates divine law (006-001) |
| The [[monarchy|king]] is God's anointed | [[monarchy|Monarchy]]'s origin is "paltry rascally" — no divinity in conquest (003-001) |
> Paine's four-part rebuttal of the providentialist argument.

## Events

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] devotes the Appendix of *Common Sense* largely to refuting the providentialist argument. He engages directly with a declaration issued by a faction of [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]] [[quakers|Quakers]] who urged submission to [[england|Britain]] on religious grounds.

His rhetorical strategy is twofold. First, he argues that divine providence cannot be read from political outcomes — if every victor claims God's favor, then "[[oliver-cromwell|Oliver Cromwell]] thanks you," and any [[tyranny|tyrant]] can claim divine sanction (006-004).

Second, he flanks the [[quakers|Quakers]] by appealing to their own [[custom|tradition]] of dissent. He cites [[Robert Barclay]], an esteemed Quaker theologian, as a model of moral courage in spea[[monarchy|king]] truth to power. True religious witness, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] argues, requires active resistance to injustice, not passive submission.

The real enemy of divine providence, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] insists, is not the revolutionaries but the [[british-crown|British monarchy]]. British rule violates divine law by its "unwarrantable" exercise of power (006-001). If God's will is to be discerned in human affairs, it favors liberty, not tyranny.

> But as nothing but heaven is impregnable to vice, it will unavoidably happen, that in proportion as they surmount the first difficulties of emigration, which bound them together in a common cause, they will begin to relax in their duty and attachment to each other; and this remissness will point out the necessity of establishing some form of government to supply the defect of moral virtue.
>
> (002-002)


## Aftermath

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s argument helped shift the terms of debate. After *Common Sense*, the providentialist case lost much of its force — not because [[america|America]]ns became less religious, but because [[thomas-paine|Paine]] successfully claimed the theological high ground for the revolutionary cause.

The connection between political liberty and divine favor became a recurring theme in [[america|America]]n revolutionary rhetoric. The Declaration of [[independence|Independence]] appeals to "[[nature|Nature]]'s God" and "divine Providence" in language that echoes [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s reframing. [[independence|Independence]] was now cast not as rebellion against God's order but as fulfillment of it.
