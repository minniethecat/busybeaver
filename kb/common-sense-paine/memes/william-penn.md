# William Penn

## Biography

William Penn (1644–1718) was an English [[Quakers|Quaker]], religious dissenter, and the founder of the [[pennsylvania|Province of Pennsylvania]]. Born in London to Admiral Sir William Penn — a naval hero of the Anglo-Dutch [[war|War]]s — the younger Penn converted to Quakerism in the 1660s, a decision that led to repeated imprisonment for his nonconformist religious beliefs.

In 1681, [[Charles II]] granted Penn a vast tract of land in North [[america|America]] in settlement of a debt owed to Penn's late father. Penn named the territory [[pennsylvania|Pennsylvania]] — "Penn's Woods" — and designed it as a "holy experiment" in religious liberty and [[representation|representative government]]. Its Frame of [[government|Government]] (1682) established an elected assembly, guaranteed freedom of conscience, and limited the death penalty to cases of murder and treason.

Penn's commitment to peaceful relations with Native [[america|America]]ns — he negotiated the famous Treaty of Shackamaxon with the Lenape — distinguished [[pennsylvania|Pennsylvania]] from most other colonies. He died in 1718 after a series of financial setbacks and a stroke that incapacitated his final years.

## Role in Common Sense

William Penn does not appear by name in *Common Sense*. The corpus references to "[[pennsylvania|Pennsylvania]]" — the colony he founded — are the closest connections. [[thomas-paine|Paine]], who arrived in [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]] in 1774 and published *Common Sense* there in 1776, was himself a beneficiary of Penn's legacy of religious toleration. [[Philadelphia]], the city where the pamphlet was printed and where the [[Continental Congress]] met, was Penn's "City of Brotherly Love."

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s relationship to Penn's Quaker legacy is complex. While [[thomas-paine|Paine]] appreciated Quaker pacifism and their history of resisting state persecution, he sharply criticized those [[quakers|Quakers]] who opposed the [[american-revolution|Revolutionary War]] on religious grounds — seeing their neutrality as a form of de facto loyalism. This tension is reflected in the pamphlet's [[Quakers]] discussion.

Penn's broader significance to the *Common Sense* argument lies in the principle his colony embodied: that civil government could be founded on consent and religious liberty, rather than on conquest and ecclesiastical coercion — principles [[thomas-paine|Paine]] extends to the case for [[america|America]]n independence.
