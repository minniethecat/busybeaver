# Religious Liberty

## Definition

Religious liberty, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s *Common Sense*, is the natural right of individuals to hold and practice their religious beliefs without coercion by the state.

Unlike later formulations that treat religious liberty as a constitutional guarantee, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] grounds it in the broader argument for natural rights — government exists to protect individuals' pre-existing freedoms, not to dictate conscience.

Religious belief is, by its nature, outside the legitimate scope of government power.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s conception is distinctly Enlightenment in character: religious liberty is not a grant from the state but a right the state is obliged to respect.

This framing places religious freedom alongside [[liberty|civil liberty]] — both are expressions of the same underlying principle that "government by our wickedness" must not encroach upon the [[natural-liberty|natural freedom]]s individuals possessed before government existed.

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] invokes religious liberty at a strategic point in his argument for [[america|America]]n independence.

He describes [[america|America]] as a refuge for "persecuted lovers of civil and religious liberty from every part of [[europe|Europe]]" (004-001), transforming the colonies' religious diversity from a potential weakness into a moral strength.

The image is deliberate: [[america|America]] is not merely a place of economic opportunity but a sanctuary for conscience.

This argument serves two rhetorical purposes. First, it distinguishes [[america|America]] from [[england|Britain]], where religious conformity was enforced by law — the Test Acts, the established [[church-of-england|Church of England]], and the penal laws against dissenters all testified to a state that claimed [[power|authority]] over conscience.

Second, it makes religious liberty a reason for independence rather than a side issue: [[reconciliation|reconciliation with Britain]] would mean submission to a government that recognizes no principled limit on its [[power|authority]] over religious life.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] does not develop a full theory of religious toleration in *Common Sense* — that would come later in *The Age of [[reason|Reason]]*. But the seed is present: government's legitimate sphere is civil order, not the salvation of souls.
