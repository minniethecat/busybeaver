# Monarchy

## Definition

Monarchy, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s analysis, is the rule of one person over others by claim of [[hereditary-succession|hereditary right]] — a form of government that is both historically illegitimate in origin and practically destructive in operation. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] traces monarchy not to divine ordination or social contract, but to conquest and [[usurpation|usurp]]ation: the first kings were simply "the principal of some band of ruffians" who seized power through violence (003-001). The institution has no basis in nature, reason, or scripture; it is a human invention designed to exalt one family above all others at the expense of the common good.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s critique of monarchy is not merely historical but theological. Drawing on the Old Testament, he argues that monarchy was a punishment inflicted on the Israelites for rejecting divine rule — "a matter of sorrow, not of glory" to have kings (003-001). This argument, tailored to a Bible-reading colonial audience, transforms monarchy from a sacred institution into a mark of divine disfavor.

## In Paine's Argument

The attack on monarchy occupies the rhetorical center of *Common Sense*. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] devotes the entirety of Part II ("Of Monarchy and [[hereditary-succession|Hereditary Succession]]") to demonstrating that monarchy is incompatible with liberty, equality, and good government. He begins with the [[natural-liberty|state of nature]]: "Mankind being originally equals in the order of creation, the equality could only be destroyed by some subsequent circumstance" (003-001). Monarchy, by creating artificial distinctions of rank, violates this original equality.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] identifies a structural flaw in monarchy: the isolation of the monarch from the people. "The state of a king shuts him from the world," preventing him from knowing the true condition of his subjects (003-001). This epistemic barrier means monarchy is systemically incapable of good governance — the king simply cannot acquire the knowledge necessary to rule wisely.

The practical consequences are equally damning. Monarchy breeds war, corruption, and civil strife. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] traces the history of [[england|England]]'s civil wars directly to monarchical claims: "In short, monarchy and [[hereditary-succession|succession]] have laid (not this or that kingdom only) but the world in blood and ashes" (003-001). The [[wars-of-the-roses|Wars of the Roses]], the [[english-civil-war|English Civil War]], and the conflict with [[america|America]] all stem from the same source — the claim of one family to rule by birthright.

## Key Passages

> To the evil of monarchy we have added that of hereditary succession; and as the first is a degradation and lessening of ourselves, so the second, claimed as a matter of right, is an insult and imposition on posterity.
> (003-001)

> One of the strongest natural proofs of the folly of hereditary right in kings, is, that nature disapproves it, otherwise she would not so frequently turn it into ridicule by giving mankind an *ass for a lion*.
> (003-001)
