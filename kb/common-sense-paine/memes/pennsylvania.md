## Overview

Pennsylvania was one of the thirteen British colonies in North [[america|America]] at the time [[thomas-paine|Thomas Paine]] wrote *Common Sense* in 1776. Founded by [[william-penn|William Penn]] in 1681 as a haven for [[quakers|Quakers]] and [[religious-liberty|religious freedom]], the colony had grown into a prosperous and populous province centered on [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]] — the largest city in the colonies and the meeting place of the [[continental-congress|Continental Congress]].

By the time of the [[american-revolution|American Revolution]], Pennsylvania occupied a strategic position both geographically and politically, situated between New [[england|England]] and the southern colonies. Its economy was diverse, encompassing agriculture, [[commerce|trade]], and early manufacturing, and its paper currency was widely recognized.

## In Common Sense

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] references Pennsylvania in three distinct contexts, each reinforcing a different facet of his argument for independence.

First, on the question of colonial relationships, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] observes that Pennsylvania and New Jersey are "sister colonies by the way of [[england|England]]" — related to each other only through their common connection to the mother country (004-001). This roundabout lineage, he suggests, is an unnatural way to conceive of [[america|America]]n unity, which should instead flow directly from shared continental interests.

Second, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] uses a land dispute between Pennsylvania and Connecticut as evidence of British governmental inadequacy. The disagreement over unlocated western lands "shews the insignificance of a British government, and fully proves, that nothing but Continental [[power|authority]] can regulate Continental matters" (005-002). The inability of distant London to adjudicate local boundary conflicts demonstrates the practical necessity of independent [[america|America]]n governance.

Finally, in the Appendix, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] presents Pennsylvania's economic substance as a fiscal argument for independence. He calculates that western lands, if properly managed, are worth "upwards of twenty-five millions, Pennsylvania currency" with quit-rents yielding two millions yearly (006-001) — a sum sufficient to service the war debt without burdening the colonists with taxes. This use of Pennsylvania's currency as the monetary frame underscores the colony's centrality to the colonial economy.

## Notable Quotations
