# Property

## Definition

Property, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s political vocabulary, refers to the material possessions and economic resources of individuals — but his treatment of it is primarily political rather than economic. Property serves two functions in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s argument: it is the primary motivation for individuals to enter into [[society|civil society]], and it is the material stake that gives citizens a genuine interest in the stability and justice of their government.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] follows Locke in treating property as a natural right that government exists to protect, not to create. However, [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s treatment of property is more instrumental than philosophical — he is less interested in theorizing the origins of property than in demonstrating that the [[security|protection]] of property requires good government and that bad government destroys property.

## In Paine's Argument

**Property as the reason for government.** In Part 1, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] argues that individuals form government precisely because they need [[security|protection]] for their property: each person "surrender[s] up a part of his property to furnish means for the [[security|protection]] of the rest" (002-002). This makes property the foundational motive for political association — without the need to protect property, there would be no reason to create government.

**[[america|America]]n property and British depredation.** In Part 3, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] makes property central to the case for independence. He asks [[america|America]]ns to consider "the precariousness with which all [[america|America]]n property is possessed" (004-001) under British rule. The argument is that British policy renders [[america|America]]n property insecure — and a government that cannot protect property has forfeited its claim to obedience.

**[[freedom|Freedom]] and property together.** [[thomas-paine|Paine]] pairs freedom with property as the twin objects of good government, writing that the aim is "securing freedom and property to all men" (004-002). The pairing is not accidental: freedom is the condition of self-direction, and property is the material basis that makes freedom meaningful. A person without secure property is not genuinely free.

**Property as a practical consideration for independence.** In Part 4, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] addresses a practical objection: would independence not drive away "emigrants of property"? His answer is that the opposite is true — "emigrants of property will not chuse to come to a country whose form of government hangs but by a thread" (005-003). The argument turns the objection on its head: only independence can create the stability that property-holders seek.

**Property and the cost of reconciliation.** In the Appendix, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] warns that [[reconciliation|reconciliation with Britain]] would drain [[america|America]]n property to "support a power who is become an enemy to all mankind" (006-001). The economic argument for independence is that remaining in the empire means paying for one's own oppression.
