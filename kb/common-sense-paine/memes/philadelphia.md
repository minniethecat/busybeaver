# Philadelphia

## Overview

Philadelphia was the largest and most politically significant city in colonial [[america|America]] in 1776. It served as the meeting place of the [[Continental Congress]] and was the de facto capital of the revolutionary movement. The city was a center of printing, commerce, and political debate — the natural birthplace for a pamphlet like *Common Sense*.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] published *Common Sense* in Philadelphia on January 10, 1776, signing the preface with "Philadelphia, February 14, 1776" (001-001). The city's printers, booksellers, and coffee houses ensured the pamphlet spread rapidly through the colonies.

## In Common Sense

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] references Philadelphia directly in the context of military vulnerability. He warns that a British force could sail up the Delaware River "and laid the city of Philadelphia under instant contribution" — holding the capital hostage (005-002). This threat underscores the urgency of establishing a continental navy and army.

The choice of Philadelphia as the [[continental-congress|Continental Congress]]'s seat made it the symbolic as well as practical center of [[america|America]]n resistance. When [[thomas-paine|Paine]] calls for a "continental form of government," he is addressing the delegates assembled in Philadelphia's State House.
