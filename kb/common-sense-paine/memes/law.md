# Law

## Definition

Law, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s political thought, occupies an intermediate position between the raw power of the state and the higher moral standard of justice. Law is the mechanism through which government operates — but it is only legitimate when it conforms to the principles of natural right and is enacted through proper representative processes.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] distinguishes law from arbitrary will. The King "can make no laws without his consent" (004-001) — but in the British system, [[george-iii|the King]]'s consent is what transforms a bill into law, ma[[monarchy|king]] the monarch the gatekeeper of legislation. This arrangement, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] argues, subordinates law to a single human will, defeating the purpose of law as an impersonal standard.

The alternative [[thomas-paine|Paine]] envisions is law that "nothing may pass into" except "what is satisfactorily just" (004-002). Law should be the product of deliberation, not command — the crystallization of public reason, not the expression of royal pleasure. This is the constitutional ideal that [[america|America]]n independence would make possible.

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s treatment of law is inseparable from his critique of the British constitution. The British system claims to be government by law, not by men — but [[thomas-paine|Paine]] shows that the law itself is subject to monarchical will at every turn.

The absence of law is one of [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s most powerful images of political dissolution. When he writes that without proper government man is "without law, without government, without any mode of justice" (006-001), he presents lawlessness as a kind of civil death — the condition from which government rescues humanity.

But law can also be an instrument of oppression. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] notes that [[america|America]]ns who seek "redress for ourselves in the civil law" (006-002) find no remedy because the same system that claims to administer law is the source of their grievances. The law that should protect becomes the law that imprisons.

The solution is not to abolish law but to reconstitute its foundation. Law must be made by representatives of the people, subject to review and rejection, and answerable to a written charter that defines the limits of legislative power. "Those who are lawfully placed in [[power|authority]]" (006-004) — the emphasis on "lawfully" is [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s — can govern, but only within the bounds that law itself establishes.
