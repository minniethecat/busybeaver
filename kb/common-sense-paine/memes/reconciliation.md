# Reconciliation

## Definition

Reconciliation, in the context of *Common Sense*, refers to the political strategy of restoring harmonious relations between [[england|Britain]] and the [[america|American colonies]] while preserving British sovereignty. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] devotes sustained attention to dismantling this position, which was advocated by many colonial leaders in 1775–76. For [[thomas-paine|Paine]], reconciliation is not merely impractical but fundamentally incoherent: the injuries [[america|America]] has suffered are not accidents of policy but structural consequences of monarchical government, and no amount of negotiation can remedy a structural defect.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] frames reconciliation as a dangerous illusion — "like an agreeable dream" from which [[america|America]]ns must awaken before it is too late (004-001). The dream is seductive precisely because it appeals to shared heritage and economic interest, but it ignores the irreconcilable conflict between liberty and [[tyranny|arbitrary power]].

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s case against reconciliation rests on several pillars. First, he argues that the breach between [[england|Britain]] and [[america|America]] is not a temporary quarrel but the result of systematic oppression. "The same tyranny which drove the first emigrants from home, pursues their [[posterity|descendants]] still" (004-001). The pattern of British abuse is not a deviation from normal governance but its essence; reconciliation would merely restore the conditions that produced the crisis.

Second, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] argues that reconciliation is temporally impossible — the damage is done. He asks his readers directly: "Ye that tell us of harmony and reconciliation, can ye restore to us the time that is past?" (004-003). The blood shed at [[lexington-and-concord|Lexington]] and [[lexington-and-concord|Concord]], the lives lost at [[battle-of-bunker-hill|Bunker Hill]], cannot be undone by diplomatic accommodation. Reconciliation would require [[america|America]]ns to forget injuries "which nature cannot forgive" (004-003).

Third, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] insists that reconciliation would leave [[america|America]] permanently vulnerable to future oppression. A reconciled [[america|America]] would remain subject to a distant parliament that has already demonstrated its willingness to sacrifice colonial interests. The structural incentives that produced the current crisis would remain unchanged, guaranteeing future conflicts.

Finally, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] presents reconciliation and independence as an exhaustive choice: "the most material and practicable plan, reconciliation or independance?" (006-001). There is no middle ground, no third option. By framing the question this way, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] forces his readers to confront the stakes directly rather than hiding behind vague hopes of compromise.
