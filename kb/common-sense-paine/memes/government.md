# Government

## Definition

For [[Thomas Paine]], government is an artificial institution — a "necessary evil" created to restrain human wickedness. It stands in sharp contrast to [[Society]], which arises naturally from human wants and promotes happiness positively.

This foundational distinction, drawn in the opening pages of *Common Sense*, frames [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s entire political argument: government is not a blessing but a burden. It is to be tolerated only insofar as it protects [[Liberty|freedom]] and [[freedom|security]] (002-002).

The legitimacy of any government depends entirely on its performance, not on [[custom|tradition]], divine mandate, or [[hereditary-succession|hereditary right]]. [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s view places him within the [[Social Contract]] [[custom|tradition]], though his argument is more radical than most contract theorists in its willingness to dissolve existing ties.

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] opens *Common Sense* with his famous distinction: "[[society|Society]] is produced by our wants, and government by our wickedness; the former promotes our happiness positively by uniting our affections, the latter negatively by restraining our vices" (002-002).

This distinction is both descriptive and normative. It tells us what government is — a restraint on vice — and what it should never become: a substitute for the natural bonds of society.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] applies this critical lens to the [[british-constitution|English constitution]], arguing it fails even on its own terms. The remnants of monarchical power are "the base remains of two ancient tyrannies" compounded with "some new republican materials" (002-002).

The English system, far from being a balanced constitution, is a patchwork that preserves the very abuses it claims to check. In contrast, the ancient republics and the [[Continental Congress]] offer models of government without hereditary privilege.

The practical implications emerge most clearly in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s discussion of [[america|America]]n independence. He argues the colonies require "a form of government, which sooner or later must happen" on the continent (004-001). The existing ad-hoc arrangements are insufficient.

Crucially, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] insists [[america|America]]ns must establish their own "settled form of government, an independant constitution of our own" through deliberate choice, not accident (005-002). This is the contractarian moment: the people design the government that will govern them.

The legitimacy of the new [[america|America]]n government will derive from this act of collective self-constitution. It will not rest on inherited claims or military conquest. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] cites [[William the Conqueror]], whose claim rested on conquest — the opposite of consent-based government.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] also addresses the practical question of expense. The cost of maintaining a government "matters not" compared with the cost of subjugation (006-001). A self-governing [[america|America]] would be wealthier and freer than a subjugated one.

## Key Passages

> Some writers have so confounded society with government, as to leave little or no distinction between them; whereas they are not only different, but have different origins.
> (002-002)

> Here then is the origin and rise of government; namely, a mode rendered necessary by the inability of moral virtue to govern the world. Here too is the design and end of government, viz. freedom and security.
> (002-002)

> The safety of a republic depends essentially on the energy of a common national sentiment; on a uniformity of principles and habits; on the exterior of permanent and respectable form.
> (005-002)

> The government of a free people ought to be so regulated as to prevent the abuse of power; for power is of a grasping nature, and has a tendency to enlarge its bounds.
> (005-002)
