# Peace

## Definition

In *Common Sense*, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] treats peace not as a passive default but as a positive condition that requires the right political arrangements to sustain. The question is not whether peace is desirable — [[thomas-paine|Paine]] takes that as given — but what form of government is capable of delivering it.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] distinguishes between the false peace of submission and the durable peace of self-governance. When [[america|America]]ns remain subjects of the [[british-crown|British Crown]], they purchase a temporary absence of open conflict at the price of their liberty. True peace, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s analysis, flows from independence and the capacity to govern one's own affairs without the interference of a distant monarch.

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] opens his case by challenging monarchy's claim to provide stability. He observes that the nations of [[europe|Europe]] "without a [[monarchy|king]] hath enjoyed more peace for this last century than any of the monarchial [[government|governments]] in [[europe|Europe]]" (003-001). The historical record — [[holland|Holland]], Swisserland — demonstrates that republics are not the chaotic experiments their critics allege. They are, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s telling, the most peaceful polities.

The strategic dimension of peace appears in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s discussion of foreign relations. [[independence|Independence]], he argues, would make [[america|America]] a neutral power: "we should be at peace with [[france|France]] and [[spain|Spain]] were they at war with [[england|Britain]]" (004-001). Dependence on [[england|Britain]], by contrast, drags [[america|America]] into [[europe|Europe]]an conflicts that serve no [[america|America]]n interest. Peace is not merely a domestic concern — it is a matter of survival in a world of competing empires.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] returns to the theme when discussing [[america|America]]'s naval capacity. A modest navy, maintained "in time of peace" (005-002), serves as both a deterrent and a [[security|protection]] of commerce. The argument is characteristically practical: peace requires preparation, not passivity.

The Appendix broadens the argument. [[independence|Independence]], [[thomas-paine|Paine]] writes, will place [[america|America]] "on that guardian of our peace and [[security|safety]]" (006-001) — the constitutional order that protects liberty. Peace and [[security|safety]] are twinned throughout the text, suggesting that one cannot exist without the other.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] also engages Quaker pacifism directly. While acknowledging that "the love and desire of peace is not confined to Quakerism" (006-002), he refuses to let peace become an argument for submission. A peace purchased by surrendering the capacity to resist tyranny is, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s calculus, no peace at all.
