# Slavery

## Definition

Slavery, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s political vocabulary, does not refer primarily to chattel slavery but to a condition of political subjection — the loss of self-government and civic freedom. It is the antithesis of [[liberty]], the inevitable consequence of failed republican institutions and the default condition of any people who lack the power to make their own laws.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] uses slavery as both a diagnostic and a polemical concept. Diagnostically, it names what happens when constitutional safeguards collapse: "when [[virtue|republican virtue]] fails, slavery ensues" (003-001). Polemically, it is the outcome [[thomas-paine|Paine]] warns [[america|America]]ns they face if they accept [[reconciliation|reconciliation with Britain]] — not merely subordination but outright enslavement.

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] deploys slavery at three critical junctures in *Common Sense*, each escalating the stakes of his argument.

In Part 2, slavery is the structural consequence of monarchy's erosion of republican institutions. After analyzing the [[british-constitution|English constitution]], [[thomas-paine|Paine]] concludes that the crown has "swallowed up the power" of [[british-house-of-commons|the Commons]], and "when [[virtue|republican virtue]] fails, slavery ensues" (003-001). Here slavery is not imposed by a conqueror but generated internally by institutional decay.

In Part 3, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] introduces a subtler form of enslavement: legal vacuum. [[america|America]]ns "may be as effectually enslaved by the want of laws in [[america|America]], as by submitting to laws made for us in [[england|England]]" (004-001). This reframes slavery from physical coercion to structural powerlessness — a people without legislative autonomy are slaves regardless of their material conditions.

In Part 4, slavery becomes the hidden cost of reconciliation. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] warns that "Conquest may be effected under the pretence of friendship; and ourselves, after a long and brave resistance, be at last cheated into slavery" (005-002). The language of deception — "cheated," "pretence" — frames British overtures as a trap, not a genuine offer.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] also extends the metaphor to psychological subjection: "The rich are in general slaves to fear, and submit to courtly power with the trembling duplicity of a Spaniel" (005-002). This internalization of servitude is, for [[thomas-paine|Paine]], as dangerous as external coercion.
