# Wars of the Roses

## Overview

The Wars of the Roses (1455–1487) were a series of English civil wars fought between the rival Houses of Lancaster and York for control of the throne. The conflict arose entirely from disputed hereditary claims — both houses were descended from Edward III and each claimed the superior right. The wars ended with the victory of Henry Tudor ([[henry-vi|Henry VI]]I), who united the houses by marriage.

## In Common Sense

For [[thomas-paine|Paine]], the Wars of the Roses are the definitive demonstration that hereditary [[hereditary-succession|succession]] produces bloodshed, not stability. "Battles and sieges, were fought between Henry and Edward" (003-001) — decades of [[war|warfare]], not over any question of justice or policy, but merely over which branch of a family tree should occupy the throne.

The absurdist quality of [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s summary is deliberate. He reduces the grand dynastic conflict to a family quarrel — "Henry and Edward," as if they were squabbling neighbors rather than rival [[monarchy|king]]s. The effect is to strip monarchy of its mystique and reveal it as a system that generates conflict by its very structure. "Twice was Henry placed between the contending parties" — a [[monarchy|king]] who was not a ruler but a pawn in a game of dynastic chess.

The [[america|America]]n implication is clear: why would the colonies, which have no stake in the hereditary claims of any [[europe|Europe]]an dynasty, submit themselves to a system that has proven so destructive even to those who participate in it?
