# Parliamentary Representation Crisis

## Overview
The [[america|America]]n colonists' rallying cry of "no taxation without representation" reflected a fundamental constitutional dispute: could a [[british-parliament|Parliament]] in which [[america|America]]ns had no elected representatives legitimately legislate for the colonies? The British doctrine of "virtual representation" — that [[british-parliament|Parliament]] represented all British subjects regardless of who elected it — satisfied nobody in [[america|America]].

## In Common Sense
[[thomas-paine|Paine]] rejects virtual representation as a fiction. He notes that the [[monarchy|king]] "can make no laws without his consent" in the form of royal assent, yet the people have no equivalent veto (004-001). The structural asymmetry is the point: the British system is designed to represent the interests of the crown and the aristocracy, not the people — and certainly not the distant [[america|America]]n people.
