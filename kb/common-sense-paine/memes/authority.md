# Authority

## Definition

Authority, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s political vocabulary, refers to the rightful power to govern — the legitimate basis upon which one person or body may make binding decisions for others. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] draws a sharp distinction between **legitimate authority**, which flows from the consent of the governed, and **illegitimate authority**, which rests on heredity, conquest, or claims of divine sanction.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] rejects the idea that [[power|authority]] can be inherited. He argues that "mankind being originally equals in the order of creation" (003-001), no person is born with a natural right to rule over another. Any claim to [[power|authority]] must be justified by reason and the public good, not by [[custom|tradition]] or force.

The concept of [[power|authority]] runs through every part of *Common Sense*. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] subjects each form of [[power|authority]] claims — monarchical, parliamentary, scriptural — to the test of reason and natural rights, finding most of them wanting.

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] deploys the concept of [[power|authority]] on multiple fronts throughout *Common Sense*. His central argument is that British [[power|authority]] over the [[america|American colonies]] lacks any legitimate foundation. He offers "simple facts, plain arguments, and common sense" (004-001) to demonstrate that neither the [[monarchy|king]] nor [[british-parliament|Parliament]] possesses rightful [[power|authority]] over [[america|America]].

**Against hereditary [[power|authority]].** [[thomas-paine|Paine]] devotes Part 2 of *Common Sense* to dismantling the claim that [[monarchy|king]]s possess [[power|authority]] by birthright. He traces monarchy to its origins in conquest and pagan idolatry, arguing that hereditary [[hereditary-succession|succession]] has no basis in reason or scripture. The "violence ever attendant on regal [[power|authority]]" (004-002) is, for [[thomas-paine|Paine]], evidence of its illegitimacy — legitimate authority does not require force to sustain itself.

**Against scriptural [[power|authority]] for monarchy.** [[thomas-paine|Paine]] addresses the argument that monarchy is authorized by the Bible. He challenges proponents of divine-right monarchy: "unless ye can produce divine [[power|authority]], to prove, that the Almighty" (006-004) has sanctioned [[monarchy|king]]ly rule, the claim collapses.

**For Continental [[power|authority]].** In Part 4, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] argues that only a Continental government — one deriving its [[power|authority]] from the consent of the [[america|America]]n people — can legitimately govern the colonies. "Nothing but Continental [[power|authority]] can regulate Continental matters" (005-002). This positive vision of [[power|authority]], rooted in representation and the common good, stands in opposition to the [[usurpation|usurp]]ed [[power|authority]] of the [[british-crown|British Crown]].

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] also critiques the [[quakers|Quakers]] for presuming to speak with [[power|authority]] on political matters "without a proper [[power|authority]] for so doing" (006-002), extending his concern with rightful [[power|authority]] from the governmental to the civic sphere.
