# Necessity

## Definition

Necessity, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s political vocabulary, is not mere convenience or preference. It is the force of unavoidable circumstance — the condition in which action is compelled because the alternative is destruction.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] likens necessity to "a gravitating power" (002-002), suggesting it operates with the same inescapable logic as [[natural-rights|natural law]]. Just as objects fall toward the earth, nations are drawn toward independence when continued dependence becomes unsustainable.

This concept bridges [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s theoretical arguments about the origins of government and his practical case for revolution. [[government|Government]] itself originates in necessity — human wickedness makes it "necessary to surrender up a part of his property to furnish means for the [[security|protection]] of the rest" (002-002). By the same logic, when government becomes the source of harm rather than [[security|protection]], necessity compels its replacement.

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] deploys necessity at critical junctures throughout *Common Sense*, each time to foreclose the option of inaction. In Part 4, he argues that [[america|America]]'s population and commerce have grown to a point where naval [[security|protection]] is "a matter of necessity" (005-002) — not a luxury or strategic preference.

The argument escalates in the same section: [[america|America]]ns have been "driven to the necessity of brea[[monarchy|king]] off all connection" with [[england|Britain]] (005-003). The verb "driven" is significant — [[thomas-paine|Paine]] presents independence not as a bold choice but as the only path remaining after all others have been foreclosed by British intransigence.

In the Appendix, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] sharpens the argument further: the "unboundedness" of the [[british-crown|British Crown]]'s claims "shew the necessity of pursuing the doctrine of the constitution" (006-001). Here necessity becomes a teacher — extreme circumstances reveal truths that comfortable times obscure.

Even [[george-iii|the King]] himself, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] notes, is "under the necessity, of putting himself in the place" of God (006-002) — a rhetorical move that turns necessity against monarchy, showing that the logic which supposedly justifies [[monarchy|royal authority]] actually exposes its absurdity.
