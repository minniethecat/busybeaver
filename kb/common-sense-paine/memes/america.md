# America

## Definition
In *Common Sense*, "America" is far more than a geographic designation. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] elevates it to a moral and political cause — the "cause of America" that he declares "is in a great measure the cause of all mankind" (001-001). America represents the possibility of a new kind of political order, founded on [[natural-rights|natural rights]] rather than hereditary privilege, and free from the corruptions of [[monarchy|monarchical]] [[england|Europe]].

## In Paine's Argument
[[thomas-paine|Paine]] frames the struggle between [[england|England]] and America as an irreconcilable conflict — not a temporary dispute over taxation or representation, but a structural incompatibility between [[liberty]] and [[tyranny]]. "Men of all ranks have embarked in the controversy, from different motives, and with various designs" (004-001), yet the logic of events points toward independence.

The breach is final: "Neither can ye reconcile [[england|Britain]] and America. The last cord now is broken" (004-003). [[thomas-paine|Paine]] rejects the [[reconciliation]] position held by many colonial leaders, arguing that the injuries America has suffered are not accidents of policy but consequences of [[monarchy|monarchical]] government itself.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] also emphasizes the global significance of the American experiment. "While America calls herself the Subject of [[england|Great Britain]], she is a stranger to herself, and a pauper to the world" (005-003). [[independence|Independence]] would not only free America but inspire [[freedom|liberty]] movements worldwide. The pamphlet's appendix addresses "the people of [[england|England]] to the inhabitants of America" (006-001), underscoring that the argument concerns all who live under the American cause. (006-002)(006-004)
