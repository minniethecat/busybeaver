# Social Contract

## Definition

The social contract is the Enlightenment political theory that legitimate government arises from an agreement among free individuals to form a political society, rather than from divine right, conquest, or hereditary [[hereditary-succession|succession]]. [[thomas-paine|Thomas Paine]] deploys this concept throughout *Common Sense* to argue that the [[british-crown|British monarchy]] lacks [[authority|legitimate authority]] over the [[america|American colonies]], because government must rest on the consent of the governed — a consent that monarchy and hereditary rule cannot provide.

Unlike some Enlightenment thinkers who treated the social contract as a literal historical event, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] treats it as an ongoing test of legitimacy: any government that ceases to serve the interests of the governed has broken the compact and may be rightfully dissolved. This radical interpretation makes the social contract a tool for revolution, not merely a theory of government's origin.

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] opens *Common Sense* by drawing a critical distinction between society and government. [[society|Society]], he argues, is produced by our wants and promotes our happiness positively — it unites our affections. [[government|Government]], by contrast, is a necessary evil produced by our wickedness; it restrains our vices (002-002). This distinction is foundational to the social contract [[custom|tradition]]: society is natural and prior; government is artificial and subordinate.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] explicitly invokes the language of compact when discussing monarchy. He describes hereditary [[hereditary-succession|succession]] as "an unwise, unjust, unnatural compact" — one that no rational people would have consented to in the first place (003-001). The monarchical claim to rule by birthright, without the consent of the governed, violates the very principle on which legitimate government must rest.

The contractarian logic reaches its fullest expression when [[thomas-paine|Paine]] warns that without legitimate government, "the social compact would dissolve, and justice be extirpated from the earth" (004-003). This passage reveals [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s core argument: justice depends on government by consent, and when that consent is withdrawn — as he argues the [[america|America]]n colonists have done — the existing government loses all moral claim to obedience.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] also deploys the language of consent in practical constitutional terms. He notes that in the British system, the [[monarchy|king]] "can make no laws without his consent" in the form of royal assent, yet the people's representatives require no equivalent consent from the governed (004-001). This asymmetry exposes the entire constitutional order as a sham — a compact in name only, designed to preserve power rather than to protect liberty.

For [[thomas-paine|Paine]], [[america|the American cause]] represented an opportunity to establish government on proper contractarian principles for the first time. He called for "a settled form of government, an independant constitution of our own" (005-002), one that would derive its just powers from the consent of the governed rather than from the accidents of history and inheritance. This vision connects the social contract directly to the [[america|America]]n project of constitutional self-government.

## Key Passages

> Society is produced by our wants, and government by our wickedness; the former promotes our happiness *positively* by uniting our affections, the latter *negatively* by restraining our vices.
> (002-002)

> Mankind being originally equals in the order of creation, the equality could only be destroyed by some subsequent circumstance... the distinctions of rich and poor may be accounted for, without having recourse to the harsh, ill-sounding names of oppression and avarice.
> (002-002)

> To the evil of monarchy we have added that of hereditary succession; and as the first is a degradation and lessening of ourselves, so the second, claimed as a matter of right, is an insult and imposition on posterity.
> (003-001)
