# Civil Liberty

## Definition

Civil liberty, as [[thomas-paine|Thomas Paine]] invokes the term in *Common Sense*, is the [[Freedom]] of individuals to participate in civic life without arbitrary interference by the state — the right to be governed by [[Law]]s rather than by the will of rulers. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] almost always pairs [[liberty|civil liberty]] with religious liberty, treating them as two expressions of a single principle: [[Government]] exists to protect pre-existing human freedoms, not to create or grant them.

The pairing is significant. For [[thomas-paine|Paine]], a government that claims [[power|authority]] over conscience (religious liberty) will inevitably claim [[power|authority]] over civic life ([[liberty|civil liberty]]). The two freedoms stand or fall together. When he describes [[america|America]] as a refuge for "the persecuted lovers of civil and religious liberty from every part of [[europe|Europe]]" (004-001), he is describing a single class of people united by their commitment to both dimensions of freedom.

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] does not develop a separate theory of [[liberty|civil liberty]] distinct from his broader argument about government. Rather, [[liberty|civil liberty]] is the practical expression of his theoretical framework: if government is "a necessary evil" instituted to protect individuals from each other, then [[liberty|civil liberty]] is what government exists to preserve.

The British government's failure to respect [[america|America]]n [[liberty|civil liberty]] is one of [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s central charges. The King "can make no laws without his consent" in the British system (004-001) — yet [[america|America]]ns, who never consented to his [[power|authority]], are bound by laws they had no role in ma[[monarchy|king]]. This is not merely a [[Constitution]]al defect but a violation of [[liberty|civil liberty]] itself: the right to be governed only by laws to which one has consented.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s argument connects [[liberty|civil liberty]] to representation. Without representation, there can be no consent; without consent, there can be no [[liberty|civil liberty]]. The British constitution's failure to represent [[america|America]]ns makes their subjection to British law an act of force, not of governance.
