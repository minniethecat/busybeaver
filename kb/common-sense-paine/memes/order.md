# Order

## Definition

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s use of "order" in *Common Sense* spans several distinct but connected meanings. In the natural sense, order refers to the original equality of human beings "in the order of creation" (003-001) — a divinely established arrangement in which no person is born with the right to rule another. This [[nature|natural order]] is [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s foundational premise: monarchy and hereditary [[hereditary-succession|succession]] violate it.

In the political sense, order refers to the structured governance that enables a society to function. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] contrasts the good order of republican institutions with the disorder produced by monarchy — a system built on the very violation of [[nature|natural order]] that ought to guarantee stability.

A third sense — civic order — appears in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s praise of [[america|America]]n conduct: the colonists have "manifested such a spirit of good order and obedience to continental government" (004-002). Here order is not imposed from above but voluntarily maintained by citizens who recognize the legitimacy of their own institutions.

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] deploys the concept of [[nature|natural order]] at a critical juncture in Part 2, where he attacks the scriptural basis of monarchy. Human beings, he insists, were "originally equals in the order of creation" (003-001). The equality could only have been broken by some subsequent event — yet no such event is recorded. The implication is clear: monarchy is a [[usurpation|usurp]]ation of the original divine order.

This natural-order argument carries through to [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s vision of government. He seeks "to gain a clear and just idea" of government's design by examining it from first principles — "in order to" understand (002-002) — a phrase that recurs throughout the text, mar[[monarchy|king]] each step of his logical method. Order is not only what [[thomas-paine|Paine]] argues about; it is how he argues.

The civic dimension of order emerges most clearly in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s account of [[america|America]]n political conduct. The colonists, he notes with approval, have shown a "spirit of good order and obedience to continental government" (004-002). This is a rebuke to those who fear that independence would unleash chaos. [[america|America]]ns have already demonstrated their capacity for ordered self-governance.

Even in the Appendix, [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s critique of monarchy returns to the theme of order. Those who passively accept hereditary rule have "resigned up the ordering, altering, and disposal" of their affairs to a power they did not choose (006-004). To refuse to order one's own political life is, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s telling, a form of moral abdication.
