## Overview

Asia in the 18th century was a vast continent of diverse empires, [[monarchy|king]]doms, and trading networks. For [[europe|Europe]]an powers like [[england|Britain]], Asia represented both commercial opportunity and imperial ambition, particularly through the [[british-east-india-company|East India Company]]'s expanding influence in the Indian subcontinent.

In *Common Sense*, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] references Asia as a coordinate of British global power — and as proof that this power was already waning.

## In Common Sense

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] mentions Asia twice, both times in the context of British military reach. He notes [[england|Britain]]'s deployment of "British arms in either Asia, [[africa|Africa]], or [[europe|Europe]]" (004-001), using the three continents to illustrate the global scale of British militarism.

In a passage celebrating [[england|Britain]]'s declining reputation, he declares that "Asia, and [[africa|Africa]], have long expelled her" (004-003). This claim — that even distant continents have rejected British influence — supports [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s broader argument that the colonies have nothing to gain from remaining tied to a power that the rest of the world has already turned against.

## Notable Quotations

> …Asia and Africa have long expelled her — Europe regards her like a stranger, and England hath given her warning to depart.
>
> (004-003)
