# Natural Rights

## Definition

Natural rights, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s political philosophy, are the rights that belong to every individual by virtue of their existence as a human being — not granted by government, charter, or custom, but inherent in the order of creation. These rights precede and stand above all political arrangements. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] opens *Common Sense* by invoking "the natural rights of all Mankind" as the standard against which all [[government|governments]] must be judged (001-001). Any government that violates these rights has forfeited its legitimacy.

This conception draws on the Enlightenment [[custom|tradition]] of natural law, but [[thomas-paine|Paine]] gives it a distinctly revolutionary edge. Natural rights are not abstract philosophical propositions; they are actionable claims that justify resistance to tyranny. The [[america|America]]n cause is legitimate precisely because [[england|Britain]] has violated the natural rights of the colonists — a violation so fundamental that submission would be moral cowardice.

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] grounds natural rights in the original equality of mankind. "Mankind being originally equals in the order of creation," he writes, inequality arises from subsequent circumstances, not from nature (003-001). The distinction between [[monarchy|king]] and subject, noble and commoner, is a human artifice — a violation of the [[nature|natural order]], not an expression of it.

The language of rights pervades [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s argument for independence. He insists that no generation has the power "to give away the right of posterity" (003-001), establishing an intergenerational dimension to natural rights that would later become central to [[america|America]]n constitutional thought. Each generation must secure its own liberty; the decisions of ancestors cannot bind [[posterity|descendants]] to submit to tyranny.

In his closing argument, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] appeals to the reader's identity as "a virtuous supporter of the rights of mankind and of the FREE AND INDEPENDANT STATES of AMERICA" (006-001). This rhetorical move ties natural rights directly to the cause of [[america|America]]n independence — to support the revolution is to support the rights of all mankind, not merely the interests of one colony or continent.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] also connects natural rights to the simple voice of "nature and of reason," arguing that the most basic human faculties testify to the wrongness of hereditary rule (002-002). The appeal to nature is both philosophical and rhetorical: it allows [[thomas-paine|Paine]] to bypass complex legal arguments and speak directly to the common sense of ordinary readers.
