# War

## Definition

War, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s political vocabulary, is armed conflict between political communities — but his treatment of the concept goes far beyond a neutral description. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] presents war as a systematic consequence of monarchical government, a violation of natural rights, and the primary evil that republican independence would remedy. Unlike the classical [[custom|tradition]] that sometimes celebrated martial virtue, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] treats war as fundamentally destructive and almost always avoidable under a just political order.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] does not analyze war in the abstract; he always ties it to the question of government form. Kings make war for personal aggrandizement; republics, by contrast, have no structural incentive for war. This makes the concept of war central to [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s argument for independence: [[england|Britain]]'s monarchical system drags [[america|America]] into [[europe|Europe]]an wars, while an independent [[america|America]]n republic could stay out of them.

## In Paine's Argument

**War as the natural state of monarchy.** In Part 2, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] traces monarchy's connection to war through biblical history, arguing that "it is the pride of [[monarchy|king]]s which throws mankind into confusion" (003-001). The passage situates war not as an occasional failure of monarchy but as its expected output — [[monarchy|king]]s seek glory through conquest, and subjects pay the price.

**War and the case for independence.** The most sustained treatment of war appears in Part 3, where [[thomas-paine|Paine]] makes the pragmatic case that remaining tied to [[england|Britain]] means perpetual entanglement in [[europe|Europe]]an conflicts. He argues that [[america|America]]'s "connexion" to [[england|Britain]] "tends directly to involve this continent in [[europe|Europe]]an wars and quarrells" (004-001). The logic is straightforward: [[england|Britain]] is a [[europe|Europe]]an power, and [[europe|Europe]]an powers fight wars; therefore [[america|America]], if attached to [[england|Britain]], will be drawn into wars that have nothing to do with its own interests.

**The counterexample of republics.** [[thomas-paine|Paine]] contrasts monarchical belligerence with the peacefulness of republics, citing [[holland|Holland]] and Switzerland: "[[holland|Holland]] and Swisserland are without wars, foreign or domestic" (004-002). These examples serve as proof that the form of government determines the likelihood of war — monarchies breed conflict, republics cultivate peace.

**War and naval power.** In Part 4, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] discusses the practical question of [[america|America]]n naval capacity, noting that "no country on the globe is so happily situated, or so internally capable of raising a fleet as [[america|America]]" (005-002). He frames military preparation not as aggression but as deterrence — a defensive posture that a virtuous republic may adopt without becoming warlike.

**The moral framing of war.** In the Appendix, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] returns to war in explicitly moral terms. He distinguishes between defensive and offensive war, arguing that "the first going to war must be more so, by all the difference between the aggressor and the defendant" (006-002). This moral asymmetry matters for [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s broader argument: [[england|Britain]] initiated the conflict, so [[america|America]]'s armed resistance is justified.
