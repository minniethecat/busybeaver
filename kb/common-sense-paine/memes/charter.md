# Charter

## Definition

A charter, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s constitutional vocabulary, is a written document that establishes the framework of [[Government]] — defining its structure, powers, and limits. It is distinct from ordinary [[law|legislation]]: where a law regulates conduct, a charter constitutes the [[power|authority]] that makes laws.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] draws a sharp distinction between a charter and a mere grant from a [[monarchy|king]]. A true charter emanates from the people and binds the government; a royal charter emanates from the sovereign and binds the people. The difference is one of sovereignty — who is the source, and who is the subject.

The practical implication is that a charter must precede government. As [[thomas-paine|Paine]] argues, "the articles or charter of government, should be formed first" (005-002), and only then should the government begin to operate under its terms. This temporal priority reflects a logical priority: the people constitute the government; the government does not constitute the people.

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s discussion of charters appears most prominently in his practical proposals for [[america|America]]n [[Independence]]. He argues that the colonies should hold a Continental Conference to draft "a charter of government" (004-002) that would serve as the foundation for the new [[america|America]]n polity.

The charter [[thomas-paine|Paine]] envisions is not merely advisory. It must contain everything "necessary for a charter to contain" (004-002) — a comprehensive framework that leaves no essential question of power unresolved. Ambiguity in a charter is an invitation to [[usurpation|usurp]]ation.

This argument draws on the colonial experience with written charters (Connecticut, Rhode Island, the various proprietary grants) but transforms the concept. Colonial charters had been granted by [[british-crown|the Crown]]; the new [[america|America]]n charter would be created by the people themselves.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s emphasis on the charter serves a tactical purpose as well. By insisting that the form of government be settled before independence is declared, he reassures cautious readers that revolution will not lead to chaos. The charter is the mechanism that transforms revolution from a destructive act into a constructive one.
