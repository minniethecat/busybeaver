# Samuel

## Biography

Samuel is a major figure in the Hebrew Bible, serving as the last of the judges and the first of the prophets. According to the biblical narrative, the Israelites demanded a [[monarchy|king]] to rule over them "like all the nations"; Samuel warned them of the oppressions monarchy would bring, but the people insisted. He anointed [[saul-biblical|Saul]] as Israel's first [[monarchy|king]], and later [[david-biblical|David]].

## Role in Common Sense

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] cites Samuel alongside [[gideon-biblical|Gideon]] as a divine witness against monarchy. "The Almighty, as declared by [[gideon-biblical|Gideon]] and the prophet Samuel, expressly disapproves of government by [[monarchy|king]]s" (003-001). The appeal to Samuel is strategically important: as the prophet who actually anointed Israel's first [[monarchy|king]], Samuel's anti-monarchical warnings carry special weight. Even the man who crowned the [[monarchy|king]] warned against [[monarchy|kingship]].

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] uses the Samuel narrative to refute the claim that monarchy enjoys divine sanction. Far from being God's preferred form of government, monarchy in the Bible is a concession to human sinfulness — a punishment for rejecting divine rule. Samuel's warnings about the oppressions of [[monarchy|kingship]] (conscription, taxation, confiscation) anticipate precisely the grievances of the [[america|America]]n colonists against [[george-iii|George III]].
