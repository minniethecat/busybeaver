# Boston

## Overview

Boston was the epicenter of colonial resistance to British [[power|authority]] in the 1770s. The [[Boston Tea Party]] of 1773 and the subsequent Coercive Acts (which closed Boston Harbor) transformed the city into a symbol of British oppression. By the time [[thomas-paine|Paine]] wrote *Common Sense*, Boston had been under military occupation and siege.

The city's suffering gave emotional weight to the independence argument. If [[america|America]]ns needed a vivid example of what [[reconciliation|reconciliation with Britain]] meant, Boston — "that seat of wretchedness" (004-001) — provided it.

## In Common Sense

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] invokes Boston with calculated rhetorical force: "let us transport ourselves for a few moments to Boston, that seat of wretchedness" (004-001). The image is of a city broken by British military rule — its harbor closed, its commerce destroyed, its people subjected to martial law.

This reference anchors [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s argument in lived experience rather than abstract principle. Boston proves that reconciliation is not a return to peaceful cooperation but submission to armed coercion. The choice facing [[america|America]]ns is not between peace and war but between resistance and subjugation.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] also references the British military commanders stationed "at Boston" (006-002), ma[[monarchy|king]] the city the concrete location where the abstract conflict between liberty and tyranny takes material form.
