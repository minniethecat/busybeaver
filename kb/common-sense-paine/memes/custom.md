# Custom

## Definition

Custom, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s usage, refers to the unthin[[monarchy|king]] adherence to established practices and inherited institutions. It gives wrong ideas "a superficial appearance of being right" (001-001). Unlike reason, which examines principles on their merits, custom relies on longevity as proof of validity.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] treats custom as an active obstacle to political judgment. It operates most dangerously in government, where inherited forms — monarchy, hereditary [[hereditary-succession|succession]], colonial subordination — persist because people have grown accustomed to them, not because they are defensible.

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] deploys custom as a diagnostic tool to explain why sensible people accept indefensible arrangements. In the Introduction, he predicts "a formidable outcry in defense of custom" (001-001), but remains confident that "time makes more converts than reason."

In Part 2, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] traces monarchy to a custom borrowed from the Heathens. "[[government|Government]] by [[monarchy|king]]s was first introduced into the world by the Heathens, from whom the children of Israel copied the custom" (003-001). This reduces monarchy from divine institution to imitative practice.

In Part 3, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] attacks the reconciliation argument as custom-based fallacy. [[america|America]]'s past flourishing under [[england|Britain]] does not justify continued connection: "We may as well assert that because a child has thrived upon milk, that it is never to have meat" (004-001).

In Part 4, the "custom of nations" and "the custom of all courts" become diplomatic obstacles. While [[america|America]] remains nominally British, no foreign power can mediate in her disputes (005-003). [[independence|Independence]] alone can break this diplomatic custom.

- [[Monarchy]] — [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s prime example of an institution sustained by custom rather than merit
