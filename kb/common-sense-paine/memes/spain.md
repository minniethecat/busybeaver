## Overview

Spain in the 18th century was a major [[europe|Europe]]an imperial power, though its global influence had declined from its 16th-century zenith. It remained a significant colonial presence in the [[america|America]]s.

In *Common Sense*, [[thomas-paine|Thomas Paine]] treats Spain almost invariably as a paired entity with [[france|France]] — the two Catholic monarchies that together could counterbalance British power.

## In Common Sense

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] groups Spain with [[france|France]] as examples of how monarchy functions no better abroad than in [[england|Britain]]. He writes that [[england|England]]'s government is "as monarchical as that of [[france|France]] or Spain" (003-001), depriving the British constitution of any claim to exceptional virtue.

He argues that [[america|America]]n independence would transform [[france|France]] and Spain from neutral observers into potential allies. The colonies "should be at peace with [[france|France]] and Spain were they at war with [[england|Britain]]" (004-001), no longer bound to [[england|Britain]]'s quarrels.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] suggests that "[[france|France]] or Spain will give us any kind of assistance" (005-003) in the event of a formal break. In the Appendix, he invokes the sovereign equality of [[france|France]] and Spain — "as independent of each other as [[france|France]] and Spain" (006-001) — as the model for [[america|America]]n states under a continental government.
