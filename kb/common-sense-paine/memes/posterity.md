# Posterity

## Definition

Posterity, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s *Common Sense*, refers to future generations — those not yet born whose interests are bound up in present political decisions. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] treats posterity not as an abstraction but as a stakeholder with genuine moral claims on the living.

The present generation has no right to bind future generations to defective government. The cause of independence is accordingly framed as a duty owed to those who will inherit the consequences. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] condemns hereditary institutions as impositions on posterity while framing free government as the greatest bequest one generation can leave to the next.

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] introduces posterity through his attack on hereditary [[hereditary-succession|succession]]. The practice is "an insult and an imposition on posterity" because "all men being originally equals, no one by birth could have a right to set up his own family in perpetual preference to all others for ever" (003-001).

The givers of public honors "could have no power to give away the right of posterity" — declaring that one family's children "shall reign over ours for ever" is "manifest injustice" (003-001). Hereditary rule steals from posterity the right to choose its governors.

In Part 3, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] expands the scope. The [[america|America]]n struggle "is not the concern of a day, a year, or an age; posterity are virtually involved in the contest, and will be more or less affected, even to the end of time, by the proceedings now" (004-001). Delay amounts to "bringing ruin upon posterity" (004-001).

Part 4 contains [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s strongest posterity arguments. Debt incurred for independence "will serve as a glorious memento of our virtue. Can we but leave posterity with a settled form of government, an independant constitution of its own, the purchase at any price will be cheap" (005-002).

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] adds practical urgency: "Youth is the seed time of good habits, as well in nations as in individuals" (005-002). Acting while [[america|America]] is young is essential. He closes with a moral observation: "When we are planning for posterity, we ought to remember, that virtue is not hereditary" (005-002).
