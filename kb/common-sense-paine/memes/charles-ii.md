# Charles II

## Biography

Charles II (1630–1685) was [[george-iii|King of England]], Scotland, and Ireland from the Restoration of the monarchy in 1660 until his death. The son of the executed [[Charles I]], he spent the [[english-civil-war|English Civil War]] and Commonwealth periods in exile before being invited to reclaim the throne after [[Oliver Cromwell]]'s death and the collapse of the Protectorate.

His reign — known as the Restoration — saw the reopening of theaters, the founding of the Royal [[society|Society]], and a reaction against the Puritan austerity of the [[oliver-cromwell|Cromwell]] years. Politically, Charles II navigated between an assertive [[british-parliament|Parliament]] and his own Catholic and absolutist sympathies, converting to Catholicism on his deathbed. He had no legitimate children, and the throne passed to his Catholic brother, James II — a [[hereditary-succession|succession]] crisis that led ultimately to the [[revolution-of-1688|Glorious Revolution]] of 1688.

## Role in Common Sense

Charles II appears in *Common Sense* only once, and indirectly, in the Appendix. [[thomas-paine|Paine]] quotes — or rather, cites — an address by the Quaker [[Robert Barclay]] to Charles II (006-003). The reference occurs in a passage discussing the persecution of [[Quakers]] and the failure of established churches to practice the toleration they demand for themselves.

| Reference | Context | PN |
|-----------|---------|-----|
| "[[robert-barclay|Barclay]]'s address to Charles II" | [[thomas-paine|Paine]] invokes [[robert-barclay|Barclay]]'s principled appeal to the [[monarchy|king]] as an example of Quaker integrity | 006-003 |

> The single reference is a footnote to a larger argument about religious sincerity — Paine's real target is the hypocrisy of state-backed churches.

[[robert-barclay|Barclay]], a prominent Quaker theologian, had addressed Charles II in *An Apology for the True Christian Divinity* (1676), urging the [[monarchy|king]] to end the persecution of [[quakers|Quakers]]. [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s citation of this appeal serves his broader argument: that those who profess Christianity should act consistently with its principles, and that established [[power|authority]] — whether a Stuart [[monarchy|king]] or the [[Church of England]] — has no monopoly on moral truth.

Charles II thus functions in the pamphlet as a historical reference point rather than a live political target. Unlike [[George III]], whom [[thomas-paine|Paine]] attacks relentlessly without naming, Charles II is cited neutrally — a [[monarchy|king]] from the prior century whose reign provides an illustrative anecdote rather than a present threat.

> Had ye the honest soul of Barclay, ye would preach repentance to your king; ye would tell the Royal Wretch his sins, and warn him of eternal ruin.
>
> (006-003)
