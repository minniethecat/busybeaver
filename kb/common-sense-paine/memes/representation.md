# Representation

## Definition

Representation, for [[thomas-paine|Paine]], is the mechanism by which the people participate in government without directly exercising power themselves. It is the institutional expression of popular sovereignty — the means through which the governed give or withhold their consent. In a properly constituted republic, the people elect representatives who make laws on their behalf; these representatives remain accountable to the electorate, ensuring that government remains responsive to the governed.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] distinguishes genuine representation from the sham representation offered by the British system. In [[england|Britain]], the [[british-house-of-commons|House of Commons]] is nominally representative, but its members are chosen by a tiny fraction of the population and are subject to royal influence and corruption. The "republican part" of the [[british-constitution|English constitution]] — [[british-house-of-commons|the Commons]] — is the only element with any claim to legitimacy, but even it is compromised by its subordination to monarchy and aristocracy (003-001).

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s positive case for representation centers on the [[america|American colonies]]. He proposes a continental conference to draft a charter, followed by regular elections to a continental congress. The representation must be "more equal" than the British model — each colony, and within each colony each district, should have representation proportionate to its population (004-002). This principle of equal representation anticipates the core democratic commitments of the [[america|America]]n founding.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] explicitly connects representation to the necessity of large republics. "There is no political matter which more deserves our attention than the necessity of a large and equal representation" (005-002). Without equal representation, the republic becomes an oligarchy — rule by the few who control the levers of electoral power. Representation is thus not merely a practical convenience for large territories; it is a moral imperative of [[republic|republican government]].

The alternative to representation is direct democracy, which [[thomas-paine|Paine]] (like most eighteenth-century thinkers) considers impractical for any territory larger than a town. Representation solves the problem of scale while preserving the principle of popular sovereignty. It is "the representation more equal" that distinguishes [[america|America]]n [[republic|republicanism]] from the corrupted republics of [[europe|Europe]] (004-002).
