# William the Conqueror

## Biography

William the Conqueror (c. 1028–1087) was the Duke of Normandy who invaded [[england|England]] in 1066. He defeated King Harold at the Battle of Hastings, initiating the [[Norman Conquest]] and the Norman dynasty.

His reign transformed English society, law, and language. For [[Thomas Paine]], however, William's significance lies not in his accomplishments but in his method: he was a foreign conqueror who seized the English throne by force, not by consent.

## Role in Common Sense

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] invokes William the Conqueror repeatedly as definitive proof that the English monarchy rests on an illegitimate foundation. "A French bastard landing with an armed banditti, and establishing himself [[monarchy|king]] of [[england|England]] against the consent of the natives, is in plain terms a very paltry rascally original" (003-001).

The bluntness of [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s language is calculated. He wants his readers to see monarchy not as a sacred institution but as the legacy of a criminal enterprise. This directly undermines the aura of legitimacy that surrounds the [[British Crown]].

The relevance to the [[america|America]]n crisis is direct. If the English monarchy itself originated in conquest rather than consent, what claim can it possibly have to the [[obedience|allegiance]] of the [[america|American colonies]]? [[thomas-paine|Paine]] traces the royal line directly to William: "the present line (William the Conqueror) was a Frenchman, and half the peers of [[england|England]] are his [[posterity|descendants]]" (004-001).

The British crown, far from being a native English institution, is a foreign imposition. It has never been legitimated by [[consent-of-the-governed|popular consent]] — the very standard [[thomas-paine|Paine]] established in his discussion of [[Government]] as a necessary evil that must answer to the people.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] also draws a practical lesson from William's conquest. "When William the Conqueror subdued [[england|England]], he gave them law at the point of the sword" (005-002). This is how conquerors govern — by force, not by right. [[america|America]]ns must not wait for a conqueror to impose law upon them.

The alternative to self-government is not benign monarchy but submission to force. This connects directly to [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s constitutional argument: the [[Continental Congress]] must establish a government before someone else does, just as William established his by conquest.

## Key Passages

> A French bastard landing with an armed banditti, and establishing himself king of England against the consent of the natives, is in plain terms a very paltry rascally original. It certainly hath no divinity in it.
> (003-001)

> When William the Conqueror subdued England, he gave them law at the point of the sword; and until we consent, that the seat of government, in America, be legally and authoritatively occupied, we shall be in danger of having it filled by some fortunate ruffian, who may afterwards take the same method with us, as William the Conqueror did with them.
> (005-002)
