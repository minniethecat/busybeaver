# Stamp Act Repeal

## Overview

The Stamp Act of 1765 imposed a direct tax on the [[america|American colonies]], requiring printed materials to bear a revenue stamp. Colonial resistance was fierce and effective; [[british-parliament|Parliament]] repealed the act in 1766 but simultaneously passed the Declaratory Act, asserting its [[power|authority]] to legislate for the colonies "in all cases whatsoever." The repeal was thus a tactical retreat, not a constitutional concession.

## In Common Sense

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] references the Stamp Act repeal in the Appendix, quoting a British official's complaint about colonial ingratitude: "it is very unfair in you to make no concessions" after the repeal (006-001). [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s response is that the repeal proves nothing about British good intentions — it merely demonstrates that [[england|Britain]] will retreat when resistance is costly and reassert control when resistance subsides.

The Stamp Act episode is, for [[thomas-paine|Paine]], a microcosm of the entire British-colonial relationship: a cycle of oppression, resistance, temporary concession, and renewed oppression. [[reconciliation|Reconciliation]] would not break this cycle but perpetuate it. Only independence — the complete removal of British legislative [[power|authority]] — can prevent the next Stamp Act.

## Notable Quotations
