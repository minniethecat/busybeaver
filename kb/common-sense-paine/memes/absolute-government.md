# Absolute Government

## Definition

Absolute government, in [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s analysis, is any political system where [[power|sovereign power]] is unlimited by [[Law]] or institutional constraint. Its defining feature is the inversion of the proper relationship between law and ruler: "in absolute [[government|governments]] [[george-iii|the King]] is law" (004-002), whereas in free countries "the law is [[monarchy|king]]."

[[thomas-paine|Paine]] distinguishes absolute government from [[monarchy]] as such — [[england|England]] is a monarchy but not (in theory) absolute — and from [[tyranny]], which describes the abuse of power rather than its [[Constitution]]al structure. Absolute government is structural: it describes a system where no institutional barriers prevent the ruler from acting arbitrarily. "A thirst for absolute power is the natural disease of monarchy" (002-002) — the tendency toward absolutism is inherent in unchecked executive [[power|authority]].

## In Paine's Argument

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s treatment of absolute government runs through all four parts of *Common Sense*, forming a structural critique that culminates in the case for [[america|America]]n [[independence]].

In Part 1, he introduces the concept as monarchy's inherent pathology: "a thirst for absolute power is the natural disease of monarchy" (002-002). This frames absolutism not as an aberration but as the predictable endpoint of monarchical logic — the disease toward which monarchy naturally tends.

In Part 2, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] contrasts absolute and limited monarchy through the lens of function. "In absolute monarchies the whole weight of business, civil and military, lies on the [[monarchy|king]]" (003-001). This concentration is simultaneously inefficient and dangerous — one fallible human bears the entire burden of state, with no institutional mechanism to correct error.

In Part 3, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] identifies the British crown's ambition to extend absolutism to [[america|America]]. He warns that [[europe|Europe]]an monarchs have conspired to "make [[george-iii|the King]]s of [[europe|Europe]] absolute" (004-001), citing Denmark and Sweden as recent victims. The [[america|American colonies]] face the same threat — not conquest by a foreign power but absorption into an absolutist system that would extinguish their [[custom|tradition]]al liberties.

The argument culminates in Part 4 with [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s most quoted formulation: "in absolute [[government|governments]] [[george-iii|the King]] is law, so in free countries the law ought to be [[monarchy|king]]" (004-002). This is the conceptual keystone of *Common Sense* — the distinction between the rule of men and the rule of law, between arbitrary will and constitutional [[Order]].

## Related Concepts

- [[Monarchy]] — the institutional form most susceptible to absolutism; "the natural disease of monarchy"
- [[Tyranny]] — the behavioral manifestation of absolute power; what absolute government enables
- [[Law]] — the defining opposite: under absolutism the [[monarchy|king]] is law, under [[Freedom]] law is [[monarchy|king]]
- [[Liberty]] — the condition absolute government extinguishes
- [[Government]] — in its proper (non-absolute) form, government is "a necessary evil" restrained by law
- [[British Constitution]] — [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s case study of a mixed system corrupted toward absolutism by the crown

## Key Passages

> "A thirst for absolute power is the natural disease of monarchy."
> (002-002)

> "In absolute monarchies the whole weight of business, civil and military, lies on the king; the children of Israel in their request for a king, urged this plea 'that he may judge us, and go out before us and fight our battles.' But in countries where he is neither a judge nor a general, as in England, a man would be puzzled to know what is his business."

> "For as in absolute governments the King is law, so in free countries the law ought to be king; and there ought to be no other."
