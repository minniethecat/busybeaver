# George III

## Biography

George III (1738–1820) was King of [[england|Great Britain]] and Ireland from 1760 until his death, and the third British monarch of the House of Hanover. His reign of nearly sixty years was one of the longest in British history, spanning the [[American Revolution]], the French Revolutionary and Napoleonic [[war|War]]s, and the transformation of [[england|Britain]] into a global industrial and imperial power.

George III came to the throne at age twenty-two, determined to restore [[british-crown|the Crown]]'s political influence after the Whig dominance of the early Hanoverian period.

His assertive approach to colonial governance — particularly the imposition of direct taxes on the [[america|American colonies]] through the [[stamp-act-repeal|Stamp Act]] (1765) and the Townshend Acts (1767) — precipitated the [[british-colonial-conflict|constitutional crisis]] that led to the [[American Revolution]].

By the time *Common Sense* appeared in January 1776, the conflict had already erupted into open [[war|warfare]] at [[Lexington and Concord]] and [[battle-of-bunker-hill|Bunker Hill]]. The King's proclamation of August 1775 declaring the colonies in open rebellion marked the point of no return for many [[america|America]]ns — including, crucially, [[Thomas Paine]].

## Role in Common Sense

George III occupies a peculiar position in *Common Sense*: he is the pamphlet's central antagonist, yet [[thomas-paine|Paine]] never once names him directly. Instead, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] refers to him as "the King of [[england|England]]" (001-001), "the Royal Brute of [[england|Britain]]" (004-002), and most memorably, "the hardened, sullen-tempered Pharaoh of [[england|England]]" (001-001).

This calculated anonymity is a rhetorical strategy — by refusing to dignify the monarch with his name, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] treats George III not as a legitimate sovereign entitled to respect, but as a generic embodiment of the tyranny inherent in monarchy itself.

> Paine's treatment of George III is unique in political pamphleteering: he attacks the office by attacking the man, and attacks the man by refusing to name him.

[[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s argument against monarchy — developed at length in [[part-2-monarchy-and-succession|Part II]] — uses George III as its implicit case study.

The doctrine of [[hereditary-succession|hereditary succession]] is absurd, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] argues, not merely in theory but in its concrete result: a distant [[monarchy|king]] who regards [[america|America]] as nothing more than a source of revenue and who has "undertaken in his own Right" to sustain parliamentary tyranny over the colonies (001-001).

By the pamphlet's end, [[thomas-paine|Paine]] has transformed George III from a distant sovereign into a personal enemy of [[america|America]]n liberty. "The blood of the slain, the weeping voice of nature cries, 'Tis time to part" (004-001) — and the one from whom [[america|America]] must part is, above all, George III.

## Key Passages

> "As the King of England hath undertaken in his own Right, to support the Parliament in what he calls Theirs, and as the good people of this country are grievously oppressed by the combination, they have an undoubted privilege to inquire into the pretensions of both, and equally to reject the usurpation of either."
> (001-001)

> "But where says some is the King of America? I'll tell you Friend, he reigns above, and doth not make havoc of mankind like the Royal Brute of Britain."
> (004-002)
